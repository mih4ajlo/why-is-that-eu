## What is it?

The Adequate Minimum Wages Directive (2022/2041/EU) requires all EU Member States to ensure their minimum wages are "adequate" — defined as enabling a decent standard of living. It does not set a uniform EU minimum wage. Instead, it establishes two binding pathways: countries with statutory minimum wages must use transparent criteria (including purchasing power, wage distribution, and economic growth) to set and regularly update them, while all Member States must promote collective bargaining coverage to at least 80% of workers.

The directive introduces mandatory reporting, involvement of social partners, and limits on deductions and variations from the minimum wage. It also prohibits enforcement actions that undermine collective bargaining. Adopted in October 2022, it gave Member States until November 2024 to transpose it into national law, with compliance deadlines extending to 2025 for the collective bargaining provisions.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's roots lie in a structural tension exposed by the 2008 financial crisis and the subsequent austerity policies. Between 2010 and 2019, in-work poverty rose across the EU, reaching 9.4% of employed people by 2019, according to Eurostat. Low-wage sectors — cleaning, retail, care work, hospitality — saw stagnant or falling real wages even as productivity and corporate profits grew. The existing EU framework, based on voluntary coordination through the European Semester, had failed to reverse this trend.

The immediate trigger was a political crisis in Germany. In 2019, the European Court of Justice's *Bauer* ruling (C-569/16) confirmed that EU fundamental rights could limit national wage policy, setting a legal precedent. But the real breakthrough came from a coalition of northern and southern Member States, pushed by the Von der Leyen Commission's promise to implement the European Pillar of Social Rights — proclaimed in 2017 by the Juncker Commission.

Key actors included Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, who made the Pillar her signature initiative, and Social Rights Commissioner Nicolas Schmit, a Luxembourgish socialist who had witnessed his country's success with collective bargaining coverage above 80%. The proposal was published in October 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, when low-paid essential workers — care home staff, delivery drivers, supermarket cashiers — were suddenly visible as critical to society. Public support spiked: a 2020 Eurobarometer survey found 90% of EU citizens supported a minimum wage that guaranteed a decent living.

The directive faced fierce opposition from Denmark, Sweden, and Austria — countries with no statutory minimum wage, where trade unions and employer organisations jointly set wages through collective agreements. They argued the 80% coverage target was a disguised imposition of statutory minimums. The Swedish Trade Union Confederation (LO) warned it would "destroy the Nordic model" of autonomous social partnership. Employer group BusinessEurope warned of job losses and lost competitiveness, claiming in a 2020 position paper that statutory minimums in Germany (introduced in 2015) had reduced employment in the low-wage sector.

A compromise was brokered by the Portuguese Council Presidency in early 2021: the directive explicitly exempted wage setting from EU harmonisation (Article 153 TFEU), and the 80% target became a "promotion" obligation rather than a rigid floor. Denmark, Sweden, and Austria secured opt-outs for sectors where collective bargaining already covered over 80% of workers. The final vote in the Council on 4 October 2022 saw 21 Member States in favour, with Denmark and Sweden voting against, and Austria and Germany abstaining. The European Parliament adopted it on 14 September 2022 by 505 votes to 92, with 44 abstentions.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact was forcing changes in how countries with low collective bargaining coverage approach wage setting. In Poland, where only 15% of workers were covered by collective agreements in 2021 (Eurostat), the government passed a law in November 2024 establishing a new Social Dialogue Council with binding obligations to consult on minimum wage updates. In Bulgaria, with coverage below 10%, the government committed to a national action plan to promote sectoral bargaining.

By early 2025, five Member States — Belgium, Czechia, Hungary, Italy, and Slovakia — faced infringement proceedings for failing to transpose the directive on time. The Commission's 2024 annual report on the European Semester found that minimum wages in 12 Member States remained below 60% of the national median wage, the threshold many experts consider adequate.

Employer federations in Germany and France criticised the additional administrative burden, particularly the requirement to report annually on the adequacy criteria. However, major trade unions — the German DGB and French CFDT — hailed the directive as a "historic first step" toward EU-level wage coordination. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) noted in a November 2024 statement that the directive had already "broken the taboo" of EU involvement in wage setting, despite Article 153's limits.

The directive's indirect effects included a renewed push for the EU to legislate on platform work (the Platform Work Directive, adopted in 2024) and on pay transparency, building on the 2023 Pay Transparency Directive. Some legal scholars have argued the directive opens the door to future EU-level minimum wage floors, though the Commission insists this remains a national competence.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU have no minimum wage law before 2022?

The EU's legal framework for social policy (Article 153 TFEU) explicitly excludes "pay" from EU harmonisation. Previous attempts, including a 1993 Commission recommendation on wage protection, had no binding effect. The 2008 crisis and subsequent austerity shifted public opinion, while the 2017 Social Pillar provided a political framework. The COVID-19 pandemic finally created the political momentum to act, as low-paid essential workers became visible and public support surged.

### Why did Denmark and Sweden oppose it so strongly?

Both countries have no statutory minimum wage. Instead, wages are set entirely through collective agreements between trade unions and employer organisations, covering over 80% of workers in each country. Their governments and union confederations feared the directive would inevitably lead to EU-level statutory minimums, undermining the Nordic model where wage determination is a matter for autonomous social partners. They secured opt-outs in the final text but still voted against.

### Does the directive set a specific minimum wage amount for all EU countries?

No. The directive requires Member States to use transparent criteria to set adequate minimum wages, referencing purchasing power, wage distribution, and economic growth as benchmarks. It does not set a percentage of median or average wage, though the Commission's accompanying recommendation suggested (non-binding) that 60% of the median wage is a useful adequacy indicator. The directive also does not require countries without statutory minimums to introduce them.

### How is the 80% collective bargaining target enforced?

The directive requires Member States to promote collective bargaining coverage and create action plans where coverage falls below 80%. This is a "promotion" obligation, not a mandatory floor. Countries like Poland and Bulgaria must develop national plans to increase coverage. Enforcement relies on the Commission monitoring through the European Semester process and potential infringement procedures, though sanctions are unlikely unless a Member State takes no action at all.
