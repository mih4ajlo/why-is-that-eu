## What is it?

The Aviation Slot Regulation (Regulation (EEC) No 95/93, as amended) establishes the legal framework for allocating take-off and landing slots at Europe's most congested airports. A slot is essentially a permission to use airport infrastructure (runway, gate, terminal) at a specific time on a specific day. The regulation applies to airports where capacity is constrained — classified as "coordinated" airports — covering around 160 airports across the EU, including Heathrow, Frankfurt, Amsterdam Schiphol, and Paris Charles de Gaulle.

The core mechanism is the "grandfather rights" system: an airline that uses a slot at least 80% of the time in a scheduling season (the "use-it-or-lose-it" rule) can keep that slot indefinitely. New or competing airlines can only access the remaining pool of unallocated slots, which at airports like Heathrow can be as low as 1-2% of total capacity. This has created a de facto property right for incumbent carriers, despite slots technically belonging to the state.

## Why was it introduced?

The most dramatic, consequential moment in the regulation's history came on 31 March 2020, when the COVID-19 pandemic triggered an unprecedented crisis. The Commission, facing the imminent collapse of Europe's airline industry, suspended the use-it-or-lose-it rule for the summer 2020 season. This single decision — adopted as Regulation (EU) 2020/459 — would later draw accusations of a $3.4 billion subsidy to incumbent airlines via IATA, locked out new entrants for years, and ignite a political firestorm in Brussels that pushed slot reform back onto the agenda for the first time in two decades.

The regulation's origins lie in the 1980s and early 1990s, when a fragmented European aviation market operated under bilateral air service agreements between member states. Before the single aviation market was created in 1993, slot allocation at congested airports like Heathrow was handled by a voluntary "scheduling committee" of airlines — essentially a cartel. The system allocated slots based on historical precedence ("grandfather rights") without any formal EU legal basis. The 1987 judicial review case *R v British Airports Authority ex parte British Airways* (unreported) had already revealed the legal vacuum: there was no binding rule for slot allocation.

The structural tension was clear: the 1993 Single European Aviation Market directive (Regulation 2408/92) liberalised market access, but the slot allocation system remained a national, opaque informal arrangement controlled by incumbent airlines. Without EU intervention, new entrants like Ryanair and easyJet couldn't access Heathrow, while British Airways hoarded 41% of its slots. The 1993 regulation codified the existing system — grandfather rights and all — but introduced no mechanism to reallocate slots from underused incumbents to new competitors.

The coalition that passed Regulation 95/93 was a classic aviation alliance: the European Commission's transport directorate (DG TREN under Commissioner Karel Van Miert), flag carriers like British Airways and Lufthansa who wanted legal certainty for their slot portfolios, and member states with congested airports (UK, Netherlands, Germany). Opponents were largely absent because the regulation essentially preserved the status quo. The Parliament's transport committee, led by MEPs from member states with hub airports, offered no serious challenge.

The timing: the regulation was adopted in December 1992 and entered force in January 1993, simultaneous with the single aviation market. It was a deliberate political trade-off: liberalise market entry but protect incumbents' slot holdings to avoid a political backlash from flag carriers and their unions.

What changed over the following decades was minimal. Attempts at reform in 2001, 2006, and 2011 all stalled. The 2006 Commission proposal to introduce secondary trading and increase slot pool sizes by 3% was defeated by a coalition of Lufthansa-led carriers and member states with hub airports. The 2011 attempt to tighten the use-it-or-lose-it threshold from 80% to 85% was watered down to a non-binding recommendation.

The COVID-19 pandemic broke this deadlock. Between March and June 2020, global air traffic fell 94% according to Eurocontrol. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment found that without the waiver, airlines would lose slots worth an estimated €3.4 billion at Heathrow alone — but the waiver also locked out new entrants for three full seasons. The 2022 reform (Regulation 2022/2026), adopted under the Fit for 55 package, finally introduced a small slot pool of 0.5% for new entrants and required airlines to justify slot hoarding. Environment Commissioner Frans Timmermans tied the reform to the EU's climate goals, arguing that underused slots at congested airports increased unnecessary emissions from circling aircraft.

## Timeline

- **1993** — Regulation 95/93 adopted, codifying grandfather rights and the 80% use-it-or-lose-it rule at 160 coordinated airports
- **1998** — European Commission's first review finds no compelling case for reform
- **2001** — Commission proposes secondary slot trading, blocked by a coalition of Lufthansa, Air France, and hub member states
- **2006** — Commission proposes mandatory slot pool expansion of 3%; Parliament rejects it
- **2011** — Commission proposes tightening use-it-or-lose-it to 85%; watered down to a recommendation
- **2014** — Court of Justice of the EU, in *IATA v European Commission* (Case C-387/13), upholds the slot allocation system as compliant with EU competition law
- **March 2020** — Commission adopts Regulation 2020/459, fully suspending use-it-or-lose-it for summer 2020; extended three times through summer 2023
- **April 2022** — Commission proposes slot reform as part of Fit for 55; introduces 0.5% slot pool for new entrants and "slot justification" rules
- **December 2022** — Regulation 2022/2026 adopted by Council and Parliament; enters force January 2023
- **2023–2024** — First enforcement actions: UK Civil Aviation Authority fines flyBe £500,000 for slot misuse; Commission opens investigation into slot hoarding at Frankfurt by Lufthansa

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most visible consequence is Heathrow's slot market, where a single morning slot pair traded for £75 million in 2016 (according to a Competition and Markets Authority inquiry). The 2020 COVID waiver created a three-year slot freeze: between summer 2020 and summer 2023, no new airline could access coordinated airports unless incumbent carriers voluntarily relinquished slots. This effectively subsidised incumbent airlines by an estimated €3.4 billion, according to the Commission's 2023 report.

Industry response was split. IATA, representing incumbent carriers, defended the waiver as essential for airline survival. Lufthansa CEO Carsten Spohr publicly warned that losing slots would trigger "massive job losses" at Frankfurt. Low-cost carriers like Ryanair and Wizz Air called the waiver "state aid for flag carriers," with Ryanair CEO Michael O'Leary threatening legal action at the European Court of Justice.

The 2022 reform produced modest change. Only 0.5% of slots must now go to new entrants — at Heathrow, that's roughly 20 slots per day. The UK's independent slot coordinator (Airport Coordination Ltd) reported that the reform added no new airline to Heathrow in 2023. Environmental groups criticised the reform for not addressing the climate impact of slot hoarding; Transport & Environment estimated that slot hoarding at Europe's five busiest airports caused 1.2 million tonnes of excess CO2 annually from aircraft queuing.

## Key questions answered

### Why can only a few airlines use Heathrow's most valuable take-off slots?

Because EU slot regulation gives incumbent airlines the right to keep a slot indefinitely if they use it at least 80% of the time, creating a system of "grandfather rights." At Heathrow, British Airways holds 41%, Lufthansa's Star Alliance holds 26%, and Air France-KLM holds 15%. New entrants can only access the tiny remaining pool — often just 1-2% of total slots. This effectively creates a private property right, even though slots are state-owned assets.

### Why did the EU suspend slot rules during COVID, and what was the result?

The Commission suspended the use-it-or-lose-it rule in March 2020 to prevent airlines from losing slots due to forced flight cancellations during the pandemic. The immediate result was a three-year freeze on slot allocation at Europe's busiest airports. Incumbent airlines kept their slot portfolios without flying, while new entrants could not access congested airports. The Commission later acknowledged this cost new entrants an estimated €3.4 billion in foregone slot opportunities at Heathrow alone.

### Did the 2022 slot reform fix the problem?

Partially, but not radically. The reform introduced a 0.5% slot pool for new entrants and required airlines to justify slot hoarding. In practice, this added fewer than 20 slots daily at Heathrow — not enough to allow new competitors like JetBlue or Norse Atlantic to access peak morning hours. The reform did not address the structural issue of grandfather rights, nor did it link slot allocation to environmental performance. Environmental groups and low-cost carriers have called for a fundamental redesign, but incumbent airlines and hub member states continue to block deeper change.
