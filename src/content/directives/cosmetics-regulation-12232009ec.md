## What is it?

Regulation (EC) no. 1223/2009 sets the legal framework for all cosmetic products marketed in the European Union. It replaced the earlier Cosmetics Directive (76/768/EEC) and transformed what had been a set of loosely harmonised national rules into a single, directly applicable regulation. The core requirement is that every cosmetic product must have a safety assessment carried out by a qualified professional before it can be placed on the market, and that assessment must be documented in a Product Information File kept on EU territory.

The regulation establishes a 'responsible person' – usually the manufacturer, importer, or distributor – who is legally liable for each product's compliance. All products must be notified to the EU's Cosmetic Products Notification Portal (CPNP) before sale. It also contains a dynamic list of substances that are banned or restricted (Annexes II–VI), with strict rules for preservatives, colourants, and UV filters. Labelling must include an ingredients list (INCI), batch number, function of the product, and any special precautions. A key innovation was the explicit ban on animal testing for finished products and ingredients, with a marketing ban that came fully into force in 2013.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation’s most dramatic element is its prohibition of animal testing – the culmination of two decades of activism and political battles. In the 1980s and 1990s, animal welfare groups such as the European Coalition to End Animal Experiments (ECEAE) and the British Union for the Abolition of Vivisection ran sustained campaigns against the use of animals to test cosmetic ingredients. A 1993 European Commission report had estimated that about 30,000 animals were used per year in cosmetic testing, though industry groups disputed the figure. The first legislative breakthrough came in 2003 with the 7th Amendment to the Cosmetics Directive, which set a phased ban: a testing ban on finished products from 2004, a ban on ingredient testing from 2009, and a marketing ban (i.e., selling products tested on animals outside the EU) from 2009 with a possible extension to 2013. This phased approach caused deep rifts. The European Commission, under the then-Enterprise Commissioner Erkki Liikanen, faced heavy lobbying from cosmetics giants such as L'Oréal, Beiersdorf, and Procter & Gamble, who argued that alternatives were not ready and that a marketing ban would be illegal under WTO rules. At the same time, the UK government (with the Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency) and the German Bundesinstitut für Risikobewertung resisted the full marketing ban until the very last Council vote in 2003. In the final compromise, the marketing ban was delayed until 2013, with an opt-out clause allowing the Commission to extend it if no valid alternatives emerged. The Court of Justice of the European Union later confirmed the legality of the ban in Case C-497/10 (2012), dismissing industry claims of WTO incompatibility.

Structural tensions also drove the regulation. The old directive had been amended 55 times, creating a fragmented patchwork of national interpretations. The rise of nanotechnology – with ingredients such as titanium dioxide and zinc oxide nano-particles in sunscreens – highlighted gaps: the 1976 directive had no risk assessment framework for nanoparticles. A 2005 Commission impact assessment noted that national authorities lacked a central database to track product safety, leading to uneven enforcement. Incidents such as the detection of high lead levels in lipsticks (breathlessly covered by consumer magazines and NGOs like the European Consumer Organisation, BEUC) heightened public concern, even though the EU already had limits. The Commission’s Directorate-General for Health and Consumer Protection (DG SANCO), under Commissioner Meglena Kuneva from 2007, made a modernised cosmetics law a priority. The timing was perfect: the animal-testing ban was politically untouchable after years of public support, the January 2009 marketing deadline was approaching, and the European Parliament’s Environment and Public Health Committee (led by rapporteur Dagmar Roth-Behrendt, a German Socialist) was determined to close loopholes. The Parliament adopted the regulation with 597 votes in favour, 19 against, and 19 abstentions on 24 March 2009. Industry had largely accepted the inevitable, shifting their lobbying to securing the transition period until 2013.

## Timeline

- **1976** — Cosmetics Directive (76/768/EEC) adopted; harmonises essential safety rules but leaves many details to member states.
- **1993** — Commission report estimates 30,000 animals used yearly in cosmetic testing; animal welfare groups intensify campaigns.
- **2003** — 7th Amendment to the Cosmetics Directive (2003/15/EC) adopted: phase-out of animal testing agreed, with marketing ban delayed to 2013.
- **2005** — European Commission publishes impact assessment for a new regulation; highlights fragmentation, nanotechnology gaps, and weak enforcement.
- **2007** — Meglena Kuneva takes office as EU Consumer Commissioner; commits to a single, modernised cosmetics law.
- **2008** — Commission proposal for the Cosmetics Regulation (COM(2008) 49 final) tabled.
- **2009, March** — European Parliament adopts the regulation by an overwhelming majority (597-19-19).
- **2009, November** — Council of the EU formally adopts Regulation 1223/2009.
- **2010, January** — Regulation enters into force (application phase started).
- **2013, July** — Full marketing ban on animal-tested cosmetic products takes effect, ending the transition period.
- **2019** — First major update: Commission releases new guidelines on nanomaterials following a 2017 opinion from the Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (SCCS).
- **2021** — European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) publishes additional guidance on the use of substances classified as carcinogenic, mutagenic, or toxic for reproduction (CMRs) in cosmetics.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation made the EU the world’s first major market to ban the sale of cosmetics developed through animal testing. This forced multinationals such as Unilever, Procter & Gamble, and L'Oréal to invest heavily in alternative testing methods (e.g., reconstructed human skin models like EpiSkin) and to restructure global supply chains. By 2020, the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) reported that over 50 alternative methods had been validated, though some continued to be challenged by the OECD. The ban also had extraterritorial effects: producers in China – which still requires animal testing for imported cosmetics – faced a dilemma. China’s 2021 reform exempting "ordinary" cosmetics from animal testing was partly driven by the desire to access the EU market.

The CPNP now holds over 2 million product notifications (as of 2022, according to the Commission’s Single Market Scoreboard). This centralisation allowed national authorities to track and recall unsafe products more quickly. For example, 60 products containing the prohibited preservative methylisothiazolinone above the new limits were withdrawn in 2016–2018 after the SCCS lowered the threshold. Small and medium-sized enterprises faced higher upfront compliance costs: a 2015 Commission study estimated the average cost of a single safety assessment at €1,500–€3,000, which critics say disadvantages artisan producers. However, the regulation eliminated the need to comply with 28 different sets of national rules, reducing longer-term regulatory burden.

## Key questions answered

### Is animal testing for cosmetics banned in the EU?

Yes, completely. Since 2013, it is illegal to sell in the EU any cosmetic product where the final product or its ingredients have been tested on animals—regardless of where the testing occurred. The ban also applies to imports, which led to a long-running dispute with China and other countries that still require animal testing.

### Which chemicals are banned from cosmetics in Europe?

The regulation’s Annex II lists over 1,300 substances that are prohibited entirely, including carcinogens such as certain tar-derived colours (Coal Tar dyes), lead acetate, and some parabens. A further 200+ substances are restricted in concentration or use (Annex III), and preservatives, colourants, and UV filters must be specifically authorised (Annex IV–VI). The list is updated regularly based on opinions from the Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (SCCS).

### How do I know a cosmetic product is safe in the EU?

The regulation requires every product to have a safety assessment report, signed by a qualified toxicologist or pharmacist. The responsible person – typically the brand owner or importer – must hold that report and submit product notifications to a centralised EU database. Labels must list ingredients using standard INCI names, so consumers can avoid allergens. If a serious safety issue appears (e.g., contamination or allergic reaction), the national authority can order an immediate recall.

### Who is liable if a cosmetic product causes harm?

The "responsible person" (the manufacturer, importer, or EU-based distributor named on the label) is legally liable. They must keep a Product Information File for 10 years after the last batch was sold. If a defect is found, they must notify authorities and can face fines, product bans, or civil damages. Several member states, including France and Italy, have imposed multi-million-euro penalties on companies that fail to report adverse effects.
