## What is it?

The Screening Regulation (EU 2024/1356) introduces a standardised, pre-entry procedure for all third-country nationals who are apprehended crossing the EU’s external borders without meeting entry conditions, or who are disembarked after a search-and-rescue operation. The procedure—lasting up to seven days—obliges national authorities to carry out identity verification, health checks (for contagious diseases), fingerprinting, facial image capture, and security screening against EU databases (including the Schengen Information System and Eurodac).

If the person indicates an intention to apply for international protection, the screening is immediately followed by a referral to the asylum procedure. If not, the person may be subjected to a return procedure. The regulation also requires Member States to establish independent monitoring mechanisms to ensure fundamental rights are respected during screening, including the principle of non-refoulement.

The regulation was adopted as part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, a legislative package proposed by the European Commission in September 2020 and approved by the European Parliament and the Council in May 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the 2015–2016 migration crisis, when more than 1.3 million asylum applications were filed in the EU—most in Greece, Italy, and Germany—and national border authorities lacked uniform procedures to systematically record who was entering. A 2017 report by the European Court of Auditors found that most Member States did not systematically screen irregular arrivals, and fingerprinting rates in several frontline states remained below 50%. This allowed people to move undetected across internal borders, a phenomenon known as “secondary movement” that the European Commission argued undermined the integrity of the Schengen area.

The structural tension was built into the old Dublin system: the country of first entry was responsible for processing the asylum claim, but without mandatory screening, many migrants simply avoided registration in the frontline state and travelled north. The situation became both a security concern (the perpetrators of the 2015 Paris and 2016 Brussels attacks had fraudulently used the migrant route) and a political crisis that paralysed EU migration policy for years.

Coalition dynamics shifted after the European Parliament elections of 2019. The von der Leyen Commission, led by Home Affairs Commissioner Ylva Johansson (Sweden, S&D), made migration reform a top priority. Johansson brokered a compromise between the Mediterranean frontline states (Greece, Italy, Malta, Spain) and the countries of secondary destination (Germany, Sweden, France). The Parliament’s rapporteur for the Screening Regulation was S&D MEP Birgit Sippel (Germany), who pushed for binding fundamental-rights safeguards and independent monitoring. The key opposition came from the Visegrád Group (Poland, Hungary, Czechia, Slovakia) and Austria, which rejected any mandatory solidarity mechanism and argued that screening would create a legal entry point for asylum-seekers. Their blocking power in the Council was eventually overcome by the use of qualified majority voting and side payments in the form of increased border-control funding.

Timing was crucial. The 2019–2021 COVID-19 pandemic temporarily sealed external borders and reduced arrivals, giving the Commission political space to negotiate without daily press images of overcrowded reception centres. Then the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine triggered the Temporary Protection Directive, demonstrating that the EU could act rapidly when a crisis hit. This broke a long-standing deadlock and made hesitant Member States more willing to accept a binding framework for external border management.

What finally unblocked the Screening Regulation was the inclusion of an independent monitoring mechanism, a demand of the European Parliament that both the Council and the Commission ultimately accepted in early 2024. Without that concession, the Parliament had threatened to reject the entire Pact package.

## Timeline

- **2015–2016** — Migration crisis; EU-Turkey statement of March 2016 reduces irregular arrivals via the Eastern Mediterranean.
- **2016** — Commission proposes to revise the Dublin Regulation; negotiations stall.
- **2017** — European Court of Auditors reports that fewer than half of irregular arrivals are systematically fingerprinted in frontline states.
- **2019** — European Parliament elections; Ursula von der Leyen becomes Commission President, promises a “New Pact on Migration”.
- **September 2020** — Commission publishes the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, including a proposal for a Screening Regulation.
- **2021–2023** — Trilogue negotiations; Parliament insists on an independent monitoring body; Council and Commission resist initially.
- **December 2023** — Political agreement reached between co-legislators after the Parliament’s LIBE committee adopts its position with 47 votes in favour, 17 against, 1 abstention.
- **April 2024** — European Parliament votes to approve the entire Pact package (Screening Regulation included) with 322 votes in favour, 266 against, 55 abstentions.
- **May 2024** — Council formally adopts the regulation.
- **June 2024** — Regulation enters into force; Member States have until June 2026 to fully implement the screening procedures and monitoring bodies.

## Impact and consequences

The Screening Regulation is expected to affect several hundreds of thousands of people annually: in 2023, 380,000 irregular border crossings were detected by Frontex, and over 150,000 people were rescued at sea and disembarked in Italy, Greece, Spain, and Malta. The regulation mandates that each of these individuals undergo the full screening process.

Industry impacts are concentrated on border-control authorities, Frontex, health services, and IT database managers. Member States are required to deploy biographic and biometric equipment, medical staff, and security-clearance personnel at all designated crossing points and centres. Frontex’s budget was increased to €1 billion for the 2024–2027 period to support implementation.

Fundamental-rights organisations, including Amnesty International and the Council of Europe’s Commissioner for Human Rights, warned that the seven-day detention period (which can be extended) risks leading to de facto imprisonment of asylum-seekers and violates the right to liberty. The European Ombudsman opened an own-initiative inquiry in 2024 to monitor whether national monitoring bodies are sufficiently independent.

Greece and Italy, which already operated screening facilities under EU-funded hotspot schemes, are expected to transition to the new legal framework. Hungary refused to implement the regulation; the Commission opened infringement proceedings in July 2024. Poland also signalled resistance, but the new pro-EU government under Donald Tusk (elected December 2023) reversed the previous administration’s opposition and began piloting screening procedures at the Polish-Belarusian border in early 2025.

## Key questions answered

### Are all people crossing the EU's external borders now screened?

The regulation applies to anyone who crosses an external border illegally or is rescued at sea and does not meet entry conditions. It does not apply to arrivals at legal border crossing points (e.g. airports with valid visas) unless the traveller is refused entry. Nationals of the EU, EEA, and Switzerland are exempt. Asylum-seekers who present themselves at a border crossing point and request protection are still subject to a separate, accelerated border procedure under amendments to the Asylum Procedure Regulation, not the screening procedure—though the two may be combined in practice.

### What happens if a migrant refuses to cooperate with the screening?

When a person resists fingerprinting, identity checks, or security questioning, Member State authorities may apply coercive measures only where strictly necessary and proportionate, and in accordance with national law. The regulation explicitly prohibits measures that violate human dignity or amount to torture. Non-cooperation can result in an entry ban or a return decision issued without an individual assessment of protection needs—though the prohibition of refoulement remains absolute.

### Does the new screening system apply to people rescued at sea?

Yes. Any third-country national who is disembarked on the territory of an EU Member State after a search-and-rescue operation is subject to screening. This includes rescue operations conducted by the coastguard, NGOs, or commercial vessels. The regulation does not prescribe duties for the rescuing ship before disembarkation, but once on land, screening is mandatory. Italy and Greece had argued that NGOs carrying out rescues should be obliged to follow a standard coordination protocol; the regulation instead relies on post-disembarkation checks.

### Why did EU countries disagree for so long about border screening?

The main point of contention was whether screening should be mandatory or voluntary. Frontline states (Greece, Italy) wanted it mandatory for all arrivals to prevent migrants from immediately leaving for other Member States. Countries like Germany and Sweden agreed, because they wanted to know who was entering. However, a group of Member States led by Hungary and Poland feared that mandatory screening would create a binding legal gateway for asylum-seekers, effectively forcing them to accept a minimum number of applications. The compromise attaches screening to existing obligations under Schengen law and explicitly states that it does not constitute a formal application for international protection, thereby decoupling screening from any commitment to process all claims on the territory.
