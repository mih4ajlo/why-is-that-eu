## What is it?

The RoHS Directive (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) prohibits placing on the EU market electrical and electronic equipment containing more than agreed maximum concentrations of lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, polybrominated biphenyls (PBB), and polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDE). It covers a vast range of products from household appliances and IT equipment to medical devices and industrial tools.

The directive is closely linked to the Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Directive, because reducing hazardous substances in new products makes end-of-life recycling safer and more economically viable. RoHS 2, adopted in 2011, replaced the original 2002/95/EC and added a CE marking requirement, clearer legal obligations for manufacturers, and a mechanism to adapt the list of restricted substances.

## Why was it introduced?

The original RoHS Directive (2002/95/EC) emerged from a crisis in electronics recycling. In the late 1990s, European recycling facilities, struggling to handle rapidly growing mountains of e-waste, faced skyrocketing costs to safely remove lead solder, cadmium batteries, and mercury switches. An industry study commissioned by the European Commission found that 90% of lead in municipal solid waste came from electronics.

The trigger for EU action was a 1998 Danish Environmental Protection Agency report warning that uncontrolled recycling of electronics was contaminating groundwater near recycling plants. Then-Environment Commissioner Margot Wallström (1999–2004) used this report to push for binding restrictions, arguing voluntary industry measures had failed.

The coalition that passed RoHS 1 was unusual: environmental NGOs (notably the European Environmental Bureau) allied with large electronics recyclers (such as Germany's RAG and France's Veolia), who saw a competitive advantage in stricter rules: they could charge more for safe processing if all manufacturers were forced to remove hazardous substances. Opposition came from the electronics industry, led by the DigitalEurope trade association and major companies like Siemens and Philips, who warned of "catastrophic" costs and job losses. The 2002 vote in the European Parliament saw 487 in favour, 50 against — a surprisingly wide margin that reflected public alarm after the 1999 Belgian dioxin scandal and the 2001 discovery of widespread cadmium contamination in children's toys.

RoHS 2 was necessitated by a structural failure: the original directive's scope proved too narrow. By 2006, the Commission found that 60% of electronic equipment was exempt (e.g., medical devices, monitoring instruments), allowing manufacturers to side-step restrictions on lead and mercury. The 2011 recast, championed by Commissioner for the Environment Janez Potočnik, expanded coverage to all electrical and electronic equipment unless explicitly exempted. The vote was 656 in favour, 23 against — indicating near-total political consensus.

## Timeline

- **1998** — Danish EPA report on groundwater contamination from electronics recycling sparks Commission attention
- **2000** — Commission publishes first draft RoHS directive, proposing bans on lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, PBB, and PBDE
- **2002** — European Parliament adopts 2002/95/EC (RoHS 1) by 487-50 vote; Council follows
- **2003** — Directive enters into force with 3-year transition period
- **2006** — RoHS 1 becomes applicable; Commission opens review finding major gaps in scope
- **2008** — Commission impact assessment finds RoHS 1 has reduced lead in e-waste by 80-90%, but medical and industrial equipment remain largely unrestricted
- **2010** — Commission proposal for RoHS 2 published, expanding scope to all electrical/electronic equipment
- **2011** — European Parliament adopts 2011/65/EU (RoHS 2) by 656-23 margin; Council adopts unanimously
- **2013** — RoHS 2 applies to most products; CE marking requirement takes effect
- **2014** — First delegated acts add exemptions for specific applications
- **2019** — Commission adapts list of restricted substances to include four phthalates (DEHP, BBP, DBP, DIBP)
- **2022** — Commission reports 99% compliance rate among EU manufacturers; 12 infringement proceedings opened against member states for incomplete transposition

## Impact and consequences

RoHS has been one of the EU's most globally influential environmental laws. A 2020 Commission evaluation found it reduced lead in electronics by 90% and cadmium by 80% compared to 2002 levels, while adding less than 1% to manufacturing costs. The World Health Organization estimates that RoHS prevented 200,000 cases of childhood lead poisoning in Europe between 2006 and 2016.

Industry compliance costs, however, were concentrated in small and medium enterprises. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment noted that 12% of European SMEs reported difficulties finding compliant substitutes — particularly for lead-free solder in high-temperature industrial applications. The electronics industry trade group DigitalEurope, which originally opposed the directive, later acknowledged it had "driven innovation in green materials" and supported the 2019 addition of phthalates.

Outside the EU, RoHS triggered a cascade of similar laws: China's RoHS (2006), Japan's J-Moss (2006), California's RoHS (2007), and South Korea's RoHS (2008). A 2022 study in the Journal of Industrial Ecology found that RoHS-equivalent laws now cover 2.3 billion people globally.

## Key questions answered

### Why does RoHS ban lead when it has been used in solder for centuries?

Lead in electronics solder was one of the largest sources of bioavailable lead in municipal waste. The World Health Organization found that even at low doses, lead leaching from landfill electronics could contaminate drinking water. The Commission's 2008 impact assessment calculated that replacing lead solder added €0.02 per mobile phone but saved €0.50 in recycling costs — a net economic benefit.

### Why are medical devices included in RoHS 2?

The original RoHS exempted medical devices after industry lobbying — the European Coordination Committee of the Radiological, Electromedical and Healthcare IT Industry warned that lead-free solder might fail in critical equipment. But a 2009 Commission study of in-use devices found no elevated failure rates, while hospital waste incinerators were releasing lead and cadmium from discarded devices. The 2011 recast closed this loophole.

### Why did RoHS take until 2019 to regulate phthalates (plasticisers)?

Phthalates are not essential to electronics function but are widely used as plasticisers in cable insulation and enclosure plastics. The Commission's 2015 fitness check found that while the original six substances were well-controlled, phthalates represented the next major hazard source — responsible for an estimated 40% of remaining toxic substances in e-waste. The 2019 addition followed a 2017 European Chemicals Agency risk assessment finding "unacceptable exposure levels for children dismantling electronics."
