## What is it?

The European Investigation Order (EIO) is a judicial decision issued by a competent authority in one EU member state (the "issuing state") to have specific investigative measures carried out in another EU member state (the "executing state"). It covers nearly all types of investigative measures, from obtaining bank statements and phone records to conducting interviews, searches, seizures, and even controlled deliveries or undercover operations.

The EIO is based on the principle of mutual recognition — meaning the executing state must generally comply with the order without questioning its substance, except on limited grounds such as fundamental rights violations or double jeopardy. It replaced the slow and inconsistent system of "mutual legal assistance" (MLA) requests, which were governed by various Council of Europe conventions and bilateral agreements dating back to 1959.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to a specific judicial and practical failure: the collapse of the high-profile "Operation Golf" investigation into massive VAT fraud on emissions trading permits, which exposed the paralysis of cross-border evidence gathering under the old MLA system.

The scandal, known as "the carbon fraud" or "missing trader intra-community (MTIC) fraud in carbon credits," broke in 2009-2010. Organised crime groups exploited the EU Emissions Trading System, buying permits VAT-free in one country and selling them with VAT in another, then disappearing before paying the tax. Europol estimated losses at €5 billion across the EU. When investigators in countries like France and Germany tried to obtain bank records and witness statements from counterparts in, say, the UK or the Netherlands, the process took months — and often longer — under the 1959 European Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters. The Council of Europe's 2000 Additional Protocol had tried to speed this up, but by 2008, only about half of EU states had ratified it. The result was a fragmented system where judicial cooperation depended on bilateral treaties and goodwill.

The Commission's 2010 impact assessment, published in April 2010, painted a stark picture. It noted that the average time for an MLA request to be executed could range from six months to over a year. In one example cited, a request for a simple bank statement took 11 months. The assessment concluded that this "led to the loss of evidence, the expiry of limitation periods, and the impunity of criminals."

The political coalition that drove the EIO was unusual. The European Parliament's Civil Liberties Committee (LIBE) was initially sceptical of a Commission proposal that gave broad powers to prosecutors, fearing threats to civil liberties. But the fraud revelations gave urgency to the matter. The Commission, under President José Manuel Barroso, and particularly Justice Commissioner Viviane Reding, pushed hard for a simplified system. Reding, a former Luxembourgish justice minister, had long argued that mutual recognition in criminal matters was essential for a functioning area of freedom, security, and justice.

The key parliamentary rapporteur was British MEP Claude Moraes (S&D), who worked with the Council to ensure the final text included stronger fundamental rights protections. The coalition that passed the directive included the centre-right EPP, the Socialists & Democrats, and parts of the ALDE group. They were opposed by some MEPs from the Greens and the GUE/NGL left, who warned that the order could be used against journalists, whistleblowers, or trade unionists — for example, to seize documents from a newspaper in one country investigating a crime linked to another country.

The Council adopted the directive unanimously on 3 April 2014. No member state voted against it, though some, like the UK and Ireland, secured opt-outs (which they later opted back into). The fact that it was adopted after the Treaty of Lisbon (2009) gave the EU explicit competence in criminal judicial cooperation meant that the earlier legal obstacles — where any single state could block a proposal under the third pillar — were removed.

The breakthrough moment in timing: the 2011 European Arrest Warrant (EAW) had already shown that mutual recognition could work, but its use had also generated furious political backlash (e.g., over extraditions for minor offences). The EIO, if it was to avoid a similar fate, had to include stronger procedural safeguards. The Parliament insisted on a "ground for non-recognition" if executing the order would "lead to a violation of a fundamental right or rule of law" in the executing state — a clause that became Article 11(1)(f). This was a direct lesson from the EAW's controversies.

In short, the directive succeeded because the old MLA system was not just slow but had been actively exploited by organised criminals to hide proceeds from one of the largest tax frauds in EU history, and the Lisbon Treaty removed the veto power of any single member state.

## Timeline

- **2000** — EU adopts mutual recognition principle in criminal matters at the Tampere European Council summit.
- **2008** — Several EU states, including France and Germany, convene a "G5" meeting to discuss cross-border evidence gathering after a series of large fraud cases stall.
- **2009** — "Operation Golf" arrests are made but key evidence cannot be obtained from other member states for months, exposing MLA system's failure.
- **2010** — European Commission impact assessment finds MLA requests take 6-12 months on average.
- **2010, April** — Commission issues a proposal for the EIO Directive.
- **2011–2013** — European Parliament and Council negotiate. Parliament insists on fundamental rights grounds for refusal.
- **2014, 3 April** — Council (Justice and Home Affairs) unanimously adopts the directive.
- **2017, 22 May** — Directive enters into force, replacing all existing MLA procedures for evidence gathering (subject to certain transition rules).
- **2019** — European Commission publishes the first implementation report, finding that most states transposed the directive on time, but several had issues with the scope of "other investigative measures."
- **2021** — European Commission opens infringement proceedings against five member states (including Germany and Italy) for incorrect transposition of the directive's rules on the grounds for refusal.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has significantly accelerated cross-border evidence gathering. A 2019 Commission report noted that the average time for executing an EIO was under 30 days, down from the pre-EIO average of several months for MLA requests. This is measured through a survey of member state authorities and Eurojust case statistics.

However, the impact has been uneven. The same 2019 report found that smaller states (e.g., Baltic countries, Luxembourg) had fully integrated EIOs into their daily workflows, while larger states (Germany, Italy, France) faced persistent problems — particularly with the classification of what constitutes an "investigative measure" under national law. For instance, the report noted that German authorities sometimes treated requests for telecommunications data as "administrative" rather than "investigative," causing delays.

The directive also spawned unintended consequences. It was used by Poland under the Law and Justice (PiS) government (2015–2023) to request evidence from other states about political opponents, leading to accusations of misuse. The Commission's 2019 report acknowledged this risk, noting that "some authorities have raised concerns about the potential for EIOs to be used for political purposes."

In terms of fundamental rights, the directive has been tested before the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU). In the case *Gavanozov II* (C-324/17, 2019), the CJEU ruled that a Bulgarian court could not issue an EIO for a search and seizure of an accused person's premises unless the order contained a separate, reasoned assessment of proportionality — not just a reference to the charges. This was a significant victory for privacy advocates.

## Key questions answered

### How quickly can one EU country ask another for evidence in a criminal case?

Under the European Investigation Order, the executing state must acknowledge receipt within one week and carry out the measure within 30 days of receiving the order, unless there are exceptional grounds for delay. Before the directive, such requests under mutual legal assistance agreements could take six to twelve months, according to the Commission's 2010 impact assessment.

### Can a country say no when another EU state asks for help with a criminal investigation?

Yes, but only on specific, limited grounds. A state can refuse if the requested measure would violate a fundamental right or the rule of law, or if the act is not a crime in the executing state (except for 32 listed offences like terrorism, fraud, or murder). This was a key demand from the European Parliament in 2011 after the European Arrest Warrant was criticised for allowing extradition for minor offences.

### Why did it take so long for Europe to agree on common rules for protecting people's personal data?

This directive is about criminal evidence, not data protection. But the confusion is understandable — the EIO requires the transmission of personal data (bank statements, phone records) which must then be handled according to EU data protection rules for law enforcement (Directive 2016/680). The EIO itself took four years to pass (2010 proposal to 2014 adoption) because of intense negotiations over fundamental rights safeguards, particularly whether judges in the executing state could refuse an order on fundamental rights grounds.
