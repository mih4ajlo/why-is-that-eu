## What is it?

The In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices Regulation (IVDR) replaced the 1998 In Vitro Diagnostic Directive (98/79/EC) and took full effect on May 26, 2022 for most devices, with phased deadlines extending to 2028 for some categories. It applies to any medical device that tests human samples outside the body — from HIV and hepatitis blood tests to glucose monitors, pregnancy tests, cancer companion diagnostics, and genetic predisposition tests.

Under the old directive, most diagnostic tests were self-certified by manufacturers without independent review of their clinical evidence. The IVDR introduces a risk-based classification system (Class A, B, C, D, from lowest to highest risk), requiring notified bodies (independent certification agencies) to scrutinize clinical performance and safety data for all but the lowest-risk tests. Class D devices (e.g., tests for Ebola, HIV, or blood transfusion safety) face the most stringent review, including mandatory batch testing by EU reference laboratories.

The regulation also tightens oversight of notified bodies themselves, requires manufacturers to demonstrate "clinical performance" and "scientific validity" through dedicated studies or published literature, and mandates a new EU-wide database (EUDAMED) for device registration and incident reporting.

## Why was it introduced?

The IVDR was a direct regulatory response to several scandals that shattered trust in the EU's medical device oversight system, culminating in the Poly Implant Prothèse (PIP) breast implant scandal — but specifically for diagnostics, the trigger was the realisation that the old "self-certification" regime was dangerously inadequate for high-risk tests.

The 1998 In Vitro Diagnostic Directive (IVDD) was a "baby of its time" — written before the genomics revolution, before direct-to-consumer genetic testing, and before the European Court of Justice had to grapple with the boundaries of what a "medical device" even meant. Under the IVDD, over 80% of diagnostic tests — including many cancer and infectious disease tests — were classified as "low risk" and could be placed on the market with a simple manufacturer declaration of conformity. Notified bodies rarely if ever reviewed actual clinical data.

The first major jolt came with a 2010 European Commission investigation into the pharmaceutical manufacturer Roche. The Commission fined Roche €3.4 million for abusing its dominant position in the HIV and hepatitis B diagnostic testing markets in Greece and Cyprus (Commission case COMP/39.847). While a competition case, it exposed how a single company could control access to essential diagnostics and raised broader questions about market surveillance.

Far more consequential was the 2011-2012 Poly Implant Prothèse (PIP) scandal in France, where a manufacturer was found to have used industrial-grade silicone in breast implants, leading to ruptures and health complications affecting hundreds of thousands of women across Europe. The PIP scandal was about implantable devices, not diagnostics, but it triggered a full-scale re-examination of the entire EU medical device regulatory framework. The European Parliament launched an inquiry, and in 2012, European Commissioner for Health and Consumer Policy John Dalli (2010–2012) initiated a revision process that his successor Tonio Borg (2012–2014) continued.

During the legislative process, a separate 2014 investigation by the journal BMJ found that several high-risk diagnostic tests — including a test for a genetic mutation linked to breast cancer (BRCA) — had been marketed without any clinical validation of their performance in European populations. The investigation noted that the UK's Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) had no authority to require manufacturers to submit clinical evidence before placing tests on the market.

The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Glenis Willmott (S&D, UK), pushed for much tougher rules than the Commission's initial 2012 proposal. A key fight centred on whether genetic tests would require pre-market authorisation by notified bodies — the Parliament insisted they should. An even more contentious battle was over "companion diagnostics" — tests that determine whether a specific drug will work for a patient (e.g., Herceptin for HER2-positive breast cancer). Industry groups including MedTech Europe and individual companies such as Roche, Abbott, and Siemens argued that the new system would be too slow and costly, potentially delaying patient access to innovative tests. They warned that the costs of re-certifying hundreds of thousands of existing tests could drive smaller manufacturers out of the market.

A critical moment came in April 2014 when the European Court of Justice issued a judgment in Case C-219/11 (Brain Products vs. BioSemi), clarifying that software used to process medical data could itself be considered a medical device — a decision that broadened the regulation's scope to include diagnostic algorithms and digital health apps.

The final regulation was adopted by the European Parliament on April 5, 2017 and by the Council of the European Union on May 5, 2017. The vote in Parliament was 591 in favour, 29 against, and 13 abstentions. The Council adopted it unanimously. The transition period was set at five years — intended to give the industry time to adapt — but the COVID-19 pandemic caused major delays.

## Timeline

- **1998** — The In Vitro Diagnostic Directive (98/79/EC) enters into force, allowing manufacturers to self-certify most diagnostic tests.
- **2010** — European Commission fines Roche €3.4 million for abusing its dominant position in the Greek HIV and hepatitis B diagnostic testing market.
- **2012** — The PIP breast implant scandal leads the European Commission to propose comprehensive reform of the medical devices regulatory framework. Commissioner John Dalli resigns in October 2012; Tonio Borg takes over.
- **2014** — The BMJ investigation reveals that high-risk genetic tests (including BRCA tests) are being marketed without clinical validation in Europe. The European Court of Justice's Brain Products ruling (Case C-219/11) extends device regulation to diagnostic software.
- **April 2017** — European Parliament adopts the IVDR by 591 votes to 29, with 13 abstentions. Council adopts it unanimously in May.
- **May 2017** — Regulation (EU) 2017/746 officially published in the Official Journal of the European Union.
- **May 2022** — Original date of application; partial enforcement begins but COVID-19 disrupts implementation.
- **January 2024** — Revised transitional deadlines take effect: Class D devices must comply by May 2025, Class C by May 2026, Class B by May 2027, Class A sterile devices by May 2028. EUDAMED database still not fully operational as of 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The IVDR has profoundly restructured the European diagnostic testing market. By 2024, the number of designated notified bodies under the regulation had collapsed — from over 20 under the old directive to only 6 fully designated under the IVDR. This bottleneck has created severe delays: the Commission's own 2023 report estimated that "several thousand" existing certificates under the old directive had not yet transitioned, with some notified bodies reporting waiting times of over 18 months for a certification audit.

Small and medium-sized manufacturers have been hit hardest. In 2022, MedTech Europe estimated that compliance costs for a typical Class C test could exceed €500,000 — a sum that many small diagnostic firms cannot absorb. Several companies, particularly in the rare disease diagnostics sector, have ceased marketing tests in the EU altogether, moving them instead to the United States or markets with less stringent requirements. The European Society of Human Genetics warned in 2021 that "many thousands" of genetic tests for rare diseases could disappear from the EU market.

The European Commission attempted to mitigate this with a series of transitional extensions (December 2022, January 2024), but the fundamental tension remains between safety oversight and market access. The 2024 transitional deadlines were the Commission's response to the crisis, but have drawn criticism from patient groups who argue they delay stronger protections.

Notably, the IVDR's stricter evidence requirements have improved the quality of clinical data available for diagnostic decisions. A 2023 study in the *Journal of Clinical Pathology* found that the number of IVDR-compliant clinical performance studies registered in the EU clinical trials database had increased by 40% since 2021, indicating that manufacturers are conducting more rigorous testing — but the trade-off is that fewer new tests reach the European market compared to the US or Asia.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe overhaul its rules for medical diagnostic tests?

The old system let manufacturers self-certify most diagnostic tests — including those for cancer, HIV, and genetic disorders — without any independent review of their clinical accuracy. This was exposed as dangerously lax after investigations found that high-risk genetic tests were being sold in Europe without any proof they actually worked properly. The Poly Implant Prothèse breast implant scandal in 2012 broke the political dam, as it showed that the entire EU medical device oversight system — not just for implants — was built on trust in manufacturers rather than independent verification.

### What changed for a company that makes a COVID-19 test or a blood glucose monitor?

Under the old rules, most tests were "self-declared" by the manufacturer — they could slap a CE mark on the product and start selling it across Europe without anyone checking their clinical data. Under the new rules, the company must now submit detailed evidence of clinical performance and scientific validity to an independent "notified body" for review. A COVID-19 antigen test, for example, would likely be Class C (high risk) and require an audit of its sensitivity and specificity data before it can be sold.

### Why are some diagnostic tests disappearing from European markets?

Compliance with the new regulation is expensive — estimates from industry associations suggest certifying a single test can cost over half a million euros. Many smaller companies, particularly those offering tests for rare diseases that only sell a few hundred kits a year, cannot afford this. They've withdrawn their products from the EU market entirely, sometimes leaving patients and doctors without access to specialised diagnostic tools. The European Commission has acknowledged this is an unintended consequence, but insists the trade-off is necessary for patient safety.

### Are the rules applied the same way across all EU countries?

Not entirely. While the regulation is directly binding, its implementation depends on the capacity of each country's "notified bodies" and local competent authorities. As of 2024, Germany and the Netherlands had designated notified bodies, while several smaller EU member states had none. This creates an uneven playing field: a manufacturer in Spain must send its device to a notified body in another country, adding time and cost. The Commission's EUDAMED database, designed to centralise registration and surveillance, was still not fully operational in 2024, further complicating cross-border oversight.
