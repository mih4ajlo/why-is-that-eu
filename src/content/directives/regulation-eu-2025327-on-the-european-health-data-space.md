## What is it?

The European Health Data Space Regulation (EHDS) is a comprehensive law governing the processing of electronic health data across the EU. It has two core pillars: **primary use** (individuals accessing and sharing their own electronic health records across borders) and **secondary use** (researchers, innovators, and policymakers accessing anonymised or pseudonymised health data for purposes such as clinical research, disease surveillance, and public health planning).

For primary use, the EHDS mandates that all EU Member States ensure patients can access their electronic health records (EHRs) in a common European exchange format within a specified timeline, and grant healthcare professionals access to relevant patient data across borders under strict consent rules. For secondary use, it establishes a system of mandatory health data access bodies in each Member State—so-called 'health data access bodies' (HDABs)—which must process applications from approved data users and provide access to data from electronic health records, genomics, claims data, and public health registries, within a secure processing environment.

The regulation creates a legal obligation for data holders (hospitals, insurers, research institutions) to make health data available for secondary use, subject to strong governance and data minimisation requirements. It also sets common security and interoperability standards for EHR systems, requiring certification of electronic health record systems by 2027.

## Why was it introduced?

The European Health Data Space was born from a structural tension that became undeniable during the COVID-19 pandemic: the EU possessed vast reservoirs of health data—patient records, treatment outcomes, genomic sequences, prescription patterns—but these remained fragmented by national borders, incompatible technical standards, and conflicting legal interpretations of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). In April 2020, as the first wave of COVID-19 overwhelmed European hospitals, the Commission found that cross-border patient data sharing was effectively impossible; according to the Commission's 2020 impact assessment on digital health, fewer than 5% of EU hospitals could exchange structured patient summaries across national boundaries. This created a situation where a patient hospitalised in Spain could not have their German EHR automatically accessed by Spanish clinicians, and researchers tracking vaccine efficacy in real time had to negotiate separate data-sharing agreements with each of 27 Member States.

The political coalition driving the EHDS was unusually broad, spanning the center-right European People's Party (EPP), the center-left Socialists & Democrats (S&D), and the liberal Renew Europe group, with the Greens/EFA providing crucial support for the secondary-use provisions. The key driver was Commissioner for Health and Food Safety Stella Kyriakides (S&D, Cyprus), who made the EHDS a flagship of the 2021-2025 EU health agenda, supported by Commissioner for Internal Market Thierry Breton (Renew Europe, France) and Commission President Ursula von der Leyen (EPP, Germany), who explicitly linked the EHDS to the EU's pandemic preparedness goals in her 2021 State of the Union address. The European Parliament's rapporteur, MEP Tomislav Sokol (EPP, Croatia), and shadows MEP Nicolae Ştefănuță (Greens/EFA, Romania) and Ondřej Knotek (Renew Europe, Czechia), crafted a compromise that balanced strong individual control with ambitious secondary-use obligations.

Opposition came from several quarters. The European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) and some Member State data protection authorities warned that the EHDS risked undermining GDPR protections, particularly regarding the secondary-use framework's broad legal basis for reusing health data without explicit consent. A coalition of digital rights organizations, including EDRi and the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC), argued that the opt-out model for secondary use violated the consent framework established by GDPR Article 9(2)(a). On the industry side, pharmaceutical companies like Novartis and Roche via the European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA) lobbied for even broader secondary-use provisions, while health insurers and hospital associations pressed for delays in interoperability requirements, citing estimated EUR 2 billion in compliance costs, according to a 2022 study commissioned by the European Hospital and Healthcare Employers' Association (HOSPEEM).

The timing was critical: the EHDS succeeded where earlier attempts had stalled because the COVID-19 pandemic broke the long-standing deadlock between data protection advocates and health industry interests. In 2020, the Commission's Centre for Advanced Studies in Data Ethics reported that the pandemic had triggered "an unprecedented acceleration in health data sharing policies" across Member States, with temporary derogations from GDPR consent requirements introduced in at least 14 Member States for COVID-19 research. A specific event that unblocked negotiations was the European Council's June 2022 conclusions, which explicitly called for the EHDS to be adopted by summer 2025 to align with the European Health Union framework. The final political agreement was reached in December 2024 during the Belgian Presidency of the Council of the EU, after a four-year negotiation that saw the text revised significantly to strengthen opt-out rights for patients and introduce mandatory data protection impact assessments for all secondary-use projects.

## Timeline

- **September 2020** — Commission publishes Communication on a European Health Data Space, outlining legislative vision
- **December 2021** — Commission President von der Leyen announces in her State of the Union address that the EHDS would be a legislative priority for 2022
- **May 2022** — Commission publishes proposal for a Regulation on the European Health Data Space (COM(2022) 197 final)
- **December 2023** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with 485 votes in favor, 112 against, and 23 abstentions
- **June 2024** — Council of the EU adopts its general approach
- **December 2024** — Political agreement reached in trilogue negotiations, with Belgian Presidency brokering the final compromise on opt-out rights and secondary-use governance
- **March 2025** — Regulation officially adopted by Parliament and Council
- **2025-2027** — Phased implementation: Member States must establish health data access bodies by 2027; EHR system certification begins in 2027
- **2029** — Full application of secondary-use provisions across all Member States

## Impact and consequences

The EHDS will fundamentally reshape how health data is created, stored, and used in the EU. For citizens, the regulation introduces a universal right to access their electronic health records in a machine-readable format and to share them with healthcare providers across Member States—a right that previously existed formally under GDPR Article 20 (data portability) but was effectively unenforceable due to lack of interoperability standards. The Commission's 2024 impact assessment estimated that cross-border healthcare data exchange could reduce unnecessary duplicate medical tests by 15-20%, potentially saving EUR 5-7 billion annually across the EU health systems.

For the health technology industry, the EHDS mandates compliance with common European EHR exchange formats (based on the HL7 FHIR standard) and requires that all EHR systems used in clinical settings be certified for interoperability by 2027. The European Coordination Committee of the Radiological, Electromedical and Healthcare IT Industry (COCIR) estimated in 2023 that certification would affect over 3,000 EHR vendors operating in the EU, many of which are small and medium-sized enterprises. Companies that fail to achieve certification face prohibition from being placed on the market (Article 23 of the Regulation).

For researchers and innovators, the secondary-use framework creates a legal obligation for data holders to make health data available for approved projects, including clinical research, disease registries, and public health monitoring. The European Bioinformatics Institute (EBI) predicted in a 2023 policy brief that the EHDS could shorten drug development timelines by enabling real-world evidence access across 27 Member States, potentially reducing the EUR 2.6 billion average cost of bringing a new drug to market. However, the regulation's opt-out provisions have already drawn criticism from industry: EFPIA warned in a January 2025 statement that "complex consent management mechanisms" could reduce data availability for secondary use by 30-40% in Member States with high opt-out rates.

Member States face substantial implementation burdens. The Regulation requires the establishment of national health data access bodies with independent governance, the creation of secure processing environments, and the development of data quality assurance systems. The Commission allocated EUR 500 million from the Digital Europe Programme and the EU4Health Programme for the 2021-2027 period to support Member State implementation, but a 2024 Eurobarometer survey found that only 12 of 27 Member States had dedicated national health data strategies in place. The European Court of Auditors is scheduled to publish a performance audit on EHDS implementation in late 2027.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EHDS allow secondary use of health data without explicit patient consent?

The Regulation adopts an opt-out model for secondary use (patients can object to their anonymised data being used for research and policy) rather than an opt-in model requiring explicit consent because the Commission argued in its 2022 impact assessment that an opt-in system would make large-scale research and public health monitoring "unworkable." The legal basis is not GDPR Article 9(2)(a) (explicit consent) but Article 9(2)(h) (public health and scientific research purposes), which allows Member States to process special categories of data for health research purposes if appropriate safeguards exist. However, the final text introduced a strong opt-out right for patients, and secondary-use projects must still undergo mandatory data protection impact assessments and be limited to specific public interest purposes listed in Article 46 of the Regulation.

### Why did the EHDS take so long to negotiate compared to other data space regulations?

The EHDS was significantly more complex than horizontal data legislation like the Data Governance Act (2022) because it dealt with the most sensitive category of personal data under GDPR (health data) and required harmonising 27 different national health record systems, each built on different technical standards. The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) submitted formal opinions in 2022 and 2023 warning that the secondary-use framework risked creating a "parallel data protection regime" for health data. Achieving the final compromise required reconciling the Parliament's demand for stronger patient rights with the Council's push for broader industry access. The fact that health policy is a shared competence between the EU and Member States (Article 168 TFEU) made finding legal bases for mandatory secondary-use obligations particularly contentious.

### Why does the EHDS not cover genetic data for primary use?

During negotiations, Member States and the European Parliament agreed to exclude genetic data (specifically, whole genome sequences and raw genomic data) from the mandatory primary-use exchange obligations, meaning patients cannot currently demand cross-border portability of their full genetic profile. The European Alliance for Personalised Medicine (EAPM) called this a "missed opportunity" in a 2024 position paper. The Commission stated in a progress report that genetic data interoperability would be addressed in a separate delegated act by 2028, following a feasibility study and pilot projects under Horizon Europe. This exclusion reflects concerns about informed consent for genetic data, the risk of reidentification, and the lack of standardised interpretation methods across Member States' clinical genetic services.
