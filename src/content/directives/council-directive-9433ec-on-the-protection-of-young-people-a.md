## What is it?

Directive 94/33/EC, commonly called the Young Workers Directive, establishes a minimum age of 15 for employment in the European Union and lays down specific protections for workers under 18. It distinguishes between "children" (under 15, or still in full-time compulsory schooling) and "young people" (ages 15 to 17). Children may perform only light work — such as babysitting, newspaper delivery, or light agricultural tasks — under strict conditions, and are generally prohibited from working during school hours. Young people aged 15–17 are allowed to work but face limits on night work (banned between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m., or 11 p.m. to 7 a.m. in some cases), a maximum eight-hour day and 40-hour week, and a ban on work that exceeds their physical or psychological capacity, exposes them to toxic substances, or involves dangerous machinery.

The directive applies to all sectors of economic activity, including agriculture, retail, and services, though member states may exempt occasional family-based work or cultural performances with permits. It obliges employers to assess risks specific to young workers and to provide adequate training.

## Why was it introduced?

Until the early 1990s, EU law offered young workers no dedicated protection. The 1989 Framework Directive on health and safety (89/391/EEC) covered all workers, but did not address the particular vulnerabilities of children and adolescents. National laws varied widely: the United Kingdom and Ireland allowed part-time work from age 13 under some conditions, while Germany and Denmark had higher minimum ages and stricter hour limits. The European Parliament had long called for harmonisation, and in 1991, Social Affairs Commissioner Padraig Flynn, under the Delors Commission, proposed the directive partly as a response to the 1989 UN Convention on the Rights of the Child and the ILO’s Minimum Age Convention (No. 138).

The immediate political trigger was the adoption of the Maastricht Treaty (1992), which gave the EU explicit competence in social policy. The Commission used this new mandate to push for minimum harmonisation. Opposition came from business federations (such as UNICE, now BusinessEurope) and some member states — particularly the UK under John Major, which argued that the directive would threaten traditional part-time jobs for teenagers and increase costs for small employers. The UK government also objected to extending EU social policy via qualified majority voting. Despite this, the directive was adopted by the Council of Ministers (unanimity was still required for social policy at the time) in June 1994, with the UK eventually supporting after obtaining exemptions for armed forces, police, and civil protection services. The European Parliament, whose opinion was consultative only, had backed a stronger ban on work for under-15s and limits on cultural performances, but those amendments were rejected by the Council.

The directive filled a gap: before 1994, a 14-year-old working illegally in a factory in one member state could be exposed to machinery that was banned for anyone under 18 in a neighbouring country. The Commission’s 1992 impact assessment noted that workplace injuries among under-18s in some member states were up to three times higher than for adult workers. The directive aimed to create a level floor — not a uniform ceiling — allowing member states to maintain stricter rules while ensuring no child could be legally exploited below the agreed minimum.

## Timeline

- **1989** — EC adopts Framework Directive 89/391/EEC on occupational safety and health, but young workers are not specifically addressed.
- **1991** — European Parliament passes a resolution calling for stronger protection of children at work.
- **December 1991** — Commission (Commissioner Padraig Flynn) issues a first proposal for a directive on the protection of young people at work (COM(91) 543).
- **1992** — Maastricht Treaty enters into force, expanding EU social policy competence.
- **June 1994** — Directive 94/33/EC adopted by the Council of Ministers (unanimity), with the UK securing exemptions for military and emergency services.
- **22 June 1994** — Directive enters into force.
- **22 June 1996** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2005** — European Commission publishes a report on the implementation of the directive (COM(2005) 479), finding that all member states had transposed it but highlighting persistent problems with light work definitions and enforcement in agriculture and family businesses.
- **2010s** — Several infringement cases brought by the Commission against member states for incorrect transposition (e.g., concerning night work exemptions; specific cases not publicly detailed).

## Impact and consequences

All 15 EU member states at the time (since expanded to 27) had to amend or replace national laws. Countries with previously weak protections — such as Greece and Portugal — raised their minimum ages and introduced bans on hazardous work. The United Kingdom, which had allowed 13-year-olds to work in light agriculture and retail, tightened hour limits and introduced a risk-assessment requirement for employers. A 2005 Commission report found that the directive had reduced the number of 15–17-year-olds working in heavy industry and construction across the EU, though precise figures were not collected centrally. The directive also spurred national campaigns: in Ireland, for example, the Health and Safety Authority began targeted inspections of part-time youth employment in the late 1990s.

Employer groups warned of job losses for teenagers, but a 2002 study by the European Foundation for the Improvement of Living and Working Conditions found no evidence of a significant reduction in youth employment rates attributable to the directive. Instead, the main effect was a shift away from dangerous sectors (e.g., manufacturing, agriculture at night) to retail and hospitality, where risks are lower. The directive has been relatively uncontroversial since transposition; no major revision has been proposed, as the minimum age of 15 remains widely accepted. However, the rise of platform work and gig economy jobs for teenagers (such as delivery apps) has raised new questions about applicability — these were not covered in the 2005 evaluation.

## Key questions answered

### Can a 14-year-old get a part-time job in Europe?

Yes, but only light work — such as babysitting, newspaper delivery, or light shop work — and only outside school hours and with a permit in some countries. The directive allows children aged 13–14 to do light work, and member states may set a higher minimum (e.g., 14 in some). The key restriction is that the work must not harm their health, safety, or education.

### What kinds of work are banned for teenagers under EU law?

Teenagers under 18 are banned from night work (generally 10 p.m. to 6 a.m.), from working more than 8 hours a day or 40 hours a week, and from any job involving dangerous machinery, toxic chemicals, extreme temperatures, or physical strain beyond their capacity. Specific lists of prohibited activities vary by country but cover sectors like mining, demolition, and slaughterhouses.

### Do EU rules apply to all children, even those working in family businesses?

The directive contains an important exemption: member states may exclude work in family undertakings (e.g., a child helping in a family farm or shop) provided it is not harmful or dangerous. Many countries, such as Italy and Spain, have used this exemption. However, the exemption does not apply to industrial work or work that exceeds light duties. In practice, family exemptions have been criticised for making enforcement difficult in agriculture and small retail.

### Why does European law allow 13-year-olds to work at all, if the minimum school-leaving age is often higher?

The directive reflects a political compromise between countries like the UK and Germany, which already permitted light work from age 13, and countries like Belgium, where the minimum was 15 for any paid work. The 13–14 light work category was seen as a way to preserve cultural traditions (e.g., summer berry picking, newspaper rounds) without forcing children into the adult labour market. The condition is strictly that the work must not interfere with education.
