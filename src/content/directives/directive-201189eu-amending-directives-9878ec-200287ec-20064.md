## What is it?

Directive 2011/89/EU tightened the supplementary supervision of financial conglomerates — groups like Allianz, BNP Paribas, or ING that combine banking, insurance, and investment services under one corporate roof. It amended four existing directives to fix loopholes that allowed such sprawling groups to escape consolidated oversight.

Before 2011, national regulators typically supervised banks and insurers separately, even when they belonged to the same parent company. A bank supervisor checked the banking arm; an insurance supervisor checked the insurance arm. Neither had full visibility of risks building up at the group level — intra-group loans, capital parked in unregulated subsidiaries, or double-counting of capital across sectors. The directive forced supervisors to look at the whole group, not just its parts, and gave them tools to demand more capital or restructure when risks crossed sector boundaries.

The core requirements included: mandatory identification of all financial conglomerates by national authorities; calculation of group-wide capital adequacy using a single method; elimination of "double gearing" where the same capital was used to support both banking and insurance risks; and enhanced reporting on intra-group transactions and risk concentrations.

## Why was it introduced?

The collapse of the Belgian-Dutch banking and insurance group Fortis in 2008 was the specific, dramatic event that broke a decade of political deadlock on conglomerate supervision. Fortis had EUR 900 billion in assets and employed 85,000 people across banking and insurance. When it failed, no single regulator could see the full picture — the Belgian Banking Commission oversaw the bank; the Belgian Insurance Supervisory Authority oversaw the insurance arm; neither had consolidated data. The Dutch central bank, which also had supervisory responsibilities, had only partial visibility.

The European Commission's 2010 impact assessment on the directive explicitly cited Fortis as demonstrating that "the current supplementary supervision of financial conglomerates has not been sufficiently effective." The assessment noted that Fortis had used intra-group transactions to shift capital between its banking and insurance entities, masking the group's true vulnerability. When Lehman Brothers collapsed in September 2008, Fortis could not unwind these internal positions quickly enough — and it required an EUR 11.2 billion government bailout by Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg, the largest state rescue of a private company in European history at that point.

Lehman Brothers itself was not a conglomerate under EU law, but the crisis it triggered exposed a structural tension: the original Financial Conglomerates Directive (2002/87/EC) had been written in the aftermath of the dot-com crash and the Enron scandal, when regulators were focused on old-fashioned fraud and accounting tricks. It allowed national supervisors to exempt groups from supplementary supervision if they judged the risk low — and most did. By 2007, according to the Joint Forum of international banking and insurance regulators, only about 60 financial conglomerates worldwide had been formally identified as such, despite thousands of multi-sector groups operating across EU markets.

The coalition that pushed through the 2011 update was unusual. The European Central Bank, freshly empowered post-crisis, demanded tighter rules. So did the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA), established in 2010. The European Parliament's economic committee, under rapporteur Swiss MEP Sven Giegold (Greens/EFA), pushed for even tougher provisions than the Commission proposed, including mandatory stress tests for conglomerates — though these were watered down in final negotiations. The industry opposition came primarily from large universal banks with insurance arms — Deutsche Bank, ING, and others — who argued that consolidated capital requirements would make them less competitive versus US and Asian rivals. The Association of German Banks warned in a 2010 position paper that the directive would "lead to a considerable increase in capital requirements for financial conglomerates without any proven benefit for financial stability."

The timing succeeded because of the unique political moment after the 2008–2009 financial crisis. The de Larosière Report, delivered to the Commission in February 2009, had explicitly called for "strengthened oversight of financial conglomerates" as a key lesson of the crisis. Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier took office in February 2010 with a mandate to overhaul financial regulation. The Commission's original proposal for the directive came in May 2010, just months after the Greek debt crisis erupted — another event that demonstrated how interconnected and opaque European finance had become. The Parliament and Council adopted the text in October 2011, less than eighteen months after the proposal, which was unusually fast for EU financial legislation.

What changed decisively was the shift from voluntary to mandatory identification of conglomerates. The 2002 directive had left it to national authorities to decide what constituted a conglomerate. The 2011 update imposed clear mathematical thresholds: groups must be identified as conglomerates if they have combined assets exceeding EUR 6 billion, have both banking and insurance activities that each represent more than 10% of the group's total, and have cross-sector activities that the lead supervisor deems significant. This closed the loophole that had allowed dozens of groups to escape supplementary oversight.

## Timeline

- **2002** — Original Financial Conglomerates Directive (2002/87/EC) adopted, requiring supplementary supervision but with wide exemptions
- **September 2008** — Lehman Brothers collapse triggers global financial crisis; Fortis is bailed out weeks later at a cost of EUR 11.2 billion
- **February 2009** — de Larosière Report recommends strengthening conglomerate oversight
- **February 2010** — Michel Barnier becomes Internal Market Commissioner, prioritises financial regulation reform
- **May 2010** — European Commission publishes proposal for Directive 2011/89/EU
- **2010–2011** — Parliament and Council negotiations; European Parliament pushes for stress test requirements, eventually dropped
- **16 October 2011** — Directive adopted by the European Parliament and Council
- **December 2011** — Published in the Official Journal of the European Union
- **1 January 2013** — Member states required to transpose into national law (with extensions for some provisions)

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced national supervisors to formally identify all financial conglomerates operating within their jurisdictions. By 2014, the Joint Committee of the European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs) had listed 62 financial conglomerates in the EU — up from essentially none under the voluntary 2002 regime. The largest identified groups included Allianz, AXA, BNP Paribas, Crédit Agricole, Deutsche Bank, Generali, ING, Intesa Sanpaolo, and Société Générale.

Industry compliance costs were significant. A 2015 Commission report on the directive's application found that banks and insurers had to implement new IT systems to track intra-group transactions, hire dedicated compliance teams for conglomerate-level reporting, and in some cases restructure their legal entities to eliminate capital double-counting. The Commission estimated implementation costs at EUR 5–15 million per large group, with ongoing annual costs of EUR 1–3 million.

The directive also triggered a wave of corporate restructuring. ING broke itself up in 2012, separating its banking and insurance arms into ING Bank and NN Group — partly in response to EU state aid conditions from its 2008 bailout, but also to simplify its compliance with the new conglomerate rules. Deutsche Bank and Allianz maintained their conglomerate structures but had to hold significantly more capital than before.

Enforcement was initially weak. Germany and Italy were late transposing the directive, and the Commission launched infringement proceedings against Germany in 2014. A 2017 European Court of Justice ruling (Case C-209/15) confirmed that the Commission could fine member states for incomplete transposition, though the case concerned a different financial directive and set an important precedent.

## Key questions answered

### Why do large financial groups need special oversight beyond normal banking and insurance rules?

Because risks can hide in the cracks between sectors. A bank owned by an insurance company might make loans that the insurance parent quietly guarantees, shifting risk off the bank's balance sheet. Capital that looks adequate for the bank alone and adequate for the insurer alone might be the same capital counted twice — "double gearing." The 2008 crisis showed that no single national authority was looking at the whole picture. Fortis had both a bank and an insurer under the same roof, but the bank regulator didn't know what the insurer was doing, and vice versa. When the bank ran into trouble, the insurer's capital was already committed, and the whole structure collapsed.

### What actually happened with Fortis that triggered this rule change?

Fortis was a Belgian-Dutch financial group with both a major bank and a major insurer. During the 2008 crisis, Fortis had used its insurance arm to provide credit guarantees to its banking arm, effectively shifting risk internally without reporting it to either the banking or insurance supervisor. When credit markets froze, those guarantees were called, and the insurance arm couldn't cover them because its capital was already supporting the bank. No single regulator had the legal authority to demand consolidated data on intra-group exposures. Fortis needed an EUR 11.2 billion state rescue split between three countries — then collapsed anyway and was nationalised and broken up. That demonstrated that voluntary, sector-by-sector supervision was dangerously inadequate.

### Does every European country have to apply these rules the same way?

In principle yes, but in practice enforcement varies. The directive sets minimum standards, and national authorities can apply stricter rules. The UK, before Brexit, was the most aggressive enforcer, requiring conglomerates to hold additional capital buffers beyond the directive's minimums. Germany and Italy were slower to implement, and the European Commission had to push them with infringement procedures. Smaller countries like Luxembourg, which hosts many financial holding companies, were initially reluctant to identify all conglomerates operating from their territory because of competitive pressures — until the European Banking Authority and EIOPA began publishing common lists of identified groups.
