## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2019/6 is the main EU law governing the marketing, manufacturing, distribution and use of veterinary medicinal products – drugs designed for animals. It replaced the previous directive from 2001 (2001/82/EC) and introduced a tougher regime aimed squarely at reducing the risk of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) emerging from animal husbandry.

The regulation bans the routine preventive use of antibiotics in groups of healthy animals (prophylaxis), tightly restricts mass treatment (metaphylaxis), and requires veterinary prescriptions for all antimicrobials. It also introduces cascade rules that push vets to use human medicines only as a last resort, and mandates EU-level collection of sales and use data for antibiotics.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the 2015 revelation by Chinese researchers that a gene conferring resistance to colistin – a last-line human antibiotic – had spread from pigs to people via a mobile piece of DNA (the mcr-1 gene). Colistin was widely used as a growth promoter and preventive treatment in EU pig and poultry farms. The finding, published in *The Lancet Infectious Diseases* in November 2015, created a crisis of confidence. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) had already flagged colistin as critically important for humans. Suddenly, the status quo – where farmers could dose entire herds with colistin for weeks without a vet’s diagnosis – looked reckless.

The structural tension was clear: the old directive (2001/82/EC) allowed routine group prophylaxis, creating a perverse incentive for livestock producers to buy antibiotics cheaply and use them as a substitute for hygiene, biosecurity, and better housing. Meanwhile, the EU’s own 2011 Action Plan on AMR had set voluntary reduction targets, but national measures were patchy. The Netherlands and Denmark had slashed use by over 50% since 2007, while countries like Spain, Italy and Poland saw little change. Without binding EU rules, the internal market allowed low-cost meat from high-use countries to undercut producers who had invested in better practices. A 2016 Commission report found that 30-50% of antibiotics used on EU farms were not prescribed for individual sick animals.

The coalition that secured passage brought together the European Parliament’s Environment and Public Health (ENVI) committee – led by rapporteur Françoise Grossetête (EPP, France) – and key member states including Denmark, Sweden, and the Netherlands, which had long pushed for EU-wide restrictions. They were buoyed by the UN General Assembly’s 2016 high-level meeting on AMR, which committed all 193 member states to action. The European Commission’s 2015 proposal had initially been less stringent – it allowed prophylactic use in animals with a proven risk of disease. But the Parliament, in its first reading in 2018, voted 583 to 66 to ban routine preventive use entirely, aligning with the European Food Safety Authority’s (EFSA) opinion that such use was not needed if husbandry conditions improved.

Opposition came from livestock-heavy member states (Spain, Italy, Poland) and feed-additive manufacturers. The European Federation of Feed Additive Manufacturers (FEFANA) argued that a ban would force farmers to return to less effective zinc oxide treatments. The livestock industry warned of higher mortality and production costs. But the final compromise – brokered under the Bulgarian and Austrian presidencies in 2018 – retained the ban on routine prophylaxis while allowing metaphylaxis (treating a group after diagnosis in some animals) only under strict veterinary oversight.

Timing was decisive. The earlier attempt to revise the veterinary medicines directive had stalled in 2014 after an impact assessment (2013) showed insufficient evidence to go beyond voluntary measures. Two events broke the deadlock: the colistin scandal, and the publication of the EU’s One Health Action Plan on AMR in June 2017. The plan, spearheaded by Health Commissioner Vytenis Andriukaitis, elevated AMR from a veterinary niche to a cross-sector political priority, linking animal, human and environmental health. Andriukaitis’ predecessor, Tonio Borg, had been cautious; Andriukaitis made AMR a personal mission.

## Timeline

- **2011** – European Commission launches the first EU Action Plan against AMR, calling for voluntary reductions.
- **2013** – Commission impact assessment on veterinary medicines reform flags weak evidence for mandatory bans; reform shelved.
- **November 2015** – Chinese scientists report the first plasmid-mediated colistin resistance gene (mcr-1) in pig farms. The gene is later found in EU livestock.
- **2015** – Commissioner Andriukaitis announces the Commission will propose new veterinary medicines legislation.
- **September 2016** – UN General Assembly holds a high-level meeting on AMR; 193 nations commit to national action plans.
- **June 2017** – The European Commission publishes the EU One Health Action Plan on AMR.
- **October 2018** – European Parliament adopts its first-reading position (583-66) banning routine prophylactic antibiotic use.
- **December 2018** – Council of the EU agrees a general approach; trilogue negotiations begin.
- **February 2019** – Provisional political agreement reached between Parliament and Council.
- **11 December 2018** – Coreper (ambassadors) endorses the final compromise text.
- **17 April 2019** – Regulation (EU) 2019/6 formally adopted by the European Parliament (vote: 556-25) and Council.
- **28 January 2022** – Regulation becomes applicable (Article 156), replacing Directive 2001/82/EC.
- **2022–2023** – First European-wide reports on antimicrobial sales under the new system (Article 57) published by EMA.
- **2023** – European Commission publishes first implementation report, noting uneven national compliance.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation delivered the first EU-wide ban on routine preventive group antibiotic use in livestock. The biggest operational change: farmers can no longer keep antibiotics on the farm for "just in case" dosing. Each treatment must be justified by a veterinary diagnosis. In practice, this forced many farms to improve biosecurity, ventilation, and stocking density – especially in pig breeding and poultry broiler sheds.

Industry response was mixed. The largest animal health companies – Zoetis, Boehringer Ingelheim, Elanco – had already been shifting portfolios toward vaccines and diagnostic tools. They largely adapted. Smaller feed-additive producers, particularly those selling medicated premixtures, faced a market contraction. The EU feed-additives sector, represented by FEFANA, noted that prophylactic antibiotic sales fell by roughly 40% between 2011 and 2020 even before the regulation fully applied, according to EMA’s European Surveillance of Veterinary Antimicrobial Consumption (ESVAC) report.

Consumption data shows variation. By 2021, sales of veterinary antibiotics in 31 European countries had dropped 43% since 2011 (EMA ESVAC, 2022). Countries that already had strict national rules (Denmark, Sweden, Netherlands) saw little further reduction, while southern and eastern member states – notably Spain, Italy, and Poland – had to accelerate transition. Spain, the EU’s largest pig producer, struggled with compliance: a 2022 Commission inspection found that many Spanish pig farms were still using prophylactic metaphylaxis without proper veterinary oversight.

The regulation also tightened the cross-border trade in veterinary medicines. Previously, farmers in one EU country could often order antibiotics from a neighbouring state with looser rules. The new law required all antimicrobial prescriptions to be valid only in the prescribing country, creating friction for cross-border livestock movements – a problem that the 2023 Commission report flagged as unresolved.

One unintended consequence: the ban on prophylactic use pushed some farmers to zinc oxide (a heavy metal additive) as an alternative for pre-weaning diarrhoea in piglets. The Commission, in its 2023 implementation report, noted that zinc oxide use rose in several member states, prompting calls for restriction under the Sustainable Use of Pesticides and the new Animal Health Law.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU ban routine antibiotics for healthy farm animals?

Routine preventive use of antibiotics in healthy animals – for example, giving all piglets antibiotics to prevent diarrhoea – created a low-pressure environment that accelerated the development of resistant bacteria. Resistant bacteria can spread to humans through meat, direct contact, or the environment. The ban aims to break that cycle by forcing farmers to improve hygiene and biosecurity instead of relying on drugs.

### How does the regulation affect the cost of meat in the EU?

Producers who previously relied on cheap prophylactic antibiotics face higher costs for veterinary visits, diagnostics, and farm improvements. Those who already had good biosecurity see little change. The EU Commission’s 2018 impact assessment estimated a 0.3-0.5% increase in production costs for pig and poultry, but with significant variation by farm type. In practice, consolidation in the livestock sector has accelerated as smaller farms struggle to meet the new standards.

### Can a farmer still treat a whole herd when only some animals are sick?

Yes, but only under strict conditions. Metaphylaxis – treating an entire group after a veterinary diagnosis in some animals – remains allowed, but only when there is a confirmed disease present and when alternatives (vaccines, improved hygiene) are not sufficient. The vet must write a prescription covering the entire group, and the farmer must keep records. This is a tightening from the previous law, which allowed blanket treatment without a prior diagnosis.

### What happens if a veterinarian prescribes a human antibiotic to a farm animal?

The regulation bans the use of antimicrobials that are critically important for human medicine (such as colistin, carbapenems, and third-generation cephalosporins) for prophylaxis in animals. They can only be used for individual animals after a specific sensitivity test. This “cascade” rule ensures human-critical drugs are reserved for humans unless no animal-specific alternative exists. Vets who violate the rule face penalties set by each member state; fines in some countries can reach €100,000 and licence suspension.
