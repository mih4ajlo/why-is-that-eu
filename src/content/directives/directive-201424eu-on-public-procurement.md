## What is it?

Directive 2014/24/EU is the main EU law governing how public authorities buy goods, works, and services. It replaced the earlier 2004/18/EC directive, aiming to make public procurement more efficient, flexible, and strategic. It covers contracts above certain monetary thresholds (e.g., €5.35 million for public works, €140,000 for most supplies and services) and sets out procedures for advertising, awarding, and reviewing contracts.

The directive introduced new tools like the innovation partnership procedure, simplified rules for smaller contracts, and explicit provisions to allow contracting authorities to take environmental, social, and innovation criteria into account when awarding contracts. It also included measures to fight corruption and conflicts of interest, such as mandatory exclusion of bidders convicted of specific crimes and obligations to report fraud to the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF).

## Why was it introduced?

The 2014 reform was triggered not by a single scandal but by a slow-burning crisis: the 2008-2009 financial crash and the subsequent sovereign debt crisis exposed deep inefficiencies in how EU member states spent public money. Public procurement accounted for roughly 14% of EU GDP — around €2 trillion annually — according to the European Commission's 2011 impact assessment. But surveys conducted by the Commission and Eurobarometer showed that only 16% of procurement procedures published in the Official Journal of the EU attracted cross-border bids, meaning the Single Market for public contracts was essentially national.

The structural tension was clear: while the 2004 directive had standardised basic procedures, it did not address the three biggest failures. First, procurement was overwhelmingly focused on the cheapest price, not best value, locking out innovative small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and discouraging green investment. Second, corruption and bid-rigging were endemic — a 2012 Commission staff working paper found that 57% of public procurement procedures in some member states had only one bidder. Third, the administrative burden was crushing: the Commission's own 2011 evaluation estimated that the cost of tendering for SMEs was 3-5% of contract value, a barrier that effectively excluded them from 60% of public contracts.

The political coalition for reform was unusual but powerful. The European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO) rapporteur, Swedish MEP Anna Hedh (S&D), pushed hard for green and social criteria, backed by environmental NGOs and trade unions. On the business side, large European contractors like Strabag (Austria) and Bouygues (France) quietly lobbied for more flexibility, while SME associations like UEAPME argued for lighter procedures. The strongest opposition came from several member states — particularly Germany and the UK — who feared losing control over national procurement policies. The UK's government, under coalition prime minister David Cameron, argued that Brussels should not dictate how local councils buy cleaning services.

What broke the deadlock was the eurozone crisis. By 2011-2012, the Commission, led by President José Manuel Barroso and Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier, framed procurement reform as a key part of the Europe 2020 strategy for smart, sustainable, and inclusive growth. The argument was brutally simple: if member states could not cut deficits and spend taxpayer money efficiently, they could not afford to ignore the €2 trillion procurement market. The Italian government under Mario Monti — a former competition commissioner — was a particularly vocal advocate, citing corruption scandals in the Mafia-controlled public works sector.

The legislative process was fast by EU standards: the Commission published its proposal in December 2011, the Parliament and Council reached a first-reading agreement in June 2013, and the directive was adopted in February 2014. In the Council, the directive passed with a qualified majority; the UK and the Netherlands voted against, arguing it went too far in allowing "buy European" preferences. Germany abstained.

## Timeline

- **2004** — The previous public procurement directive (2004/18/EC) is adopted, consolidating earlier rules.
- **2008-2009** — Financial crisis hits EU member states, exposing inefficiencies in public spending.
- **2010** — The Europe 2020 strategy identifies public procurement as a tool for innovation and green growth.
- **December 2011** — The European Commission publishes its proposal for a new directive, including the innovation partnership and green criteria.
- **2012** — The Commission's evaluation finds that 57% of procurement procedures in some member states receive only one bid, highlighting corruption concerns.
- **June 2013** — The European Parliament and Council reach a first-reading agreement after trilogues.
- **February 2014** — The directive is formally adopted (2014/24/EU). The UK and the Netherlands vote against; Germany abstains.
- **April 2014** — The directive enters into force.
- **April 2016** — The deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2017** — The Commission launches infringement proceedings against several member states (including Poland and Greece) for late or incomplete transposition.
- **2020** — The Commission issues guidance on using procurement for strategic autonomy during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- **2022** — The Commission publishes a review assessing implementation; it finds that only 40% of member states have fully transposed the directive correctly.

## Impact and consequences

The directive had a mixed impact. On the positive side, the explicit inclusion of environmental and social criteria — including life-cycle costing — allowed forward-looking contracting authorities to reward sustainability. The City of Vienna, for instance, used the new rules to award contracts for energy-efficient lighting, cutting emissions by 15% according to its 2019 sustainability report. SMEs benefited from simplified procedures: the Commission's 2020 evaluation found that 12% more SMEs won public contracts between 2016 and 2019 compared to the previous period, though the absolute share remained low at around 30%.

But implementation was patchy. By 2022, the Commission had launched 14 infringement procedures against member states for incorrect or incomplete transposition. The 2022 review by the Commission found that only 40% of member states had correctly applied the rules on e-procurement — which the directive made mandatory as of 2018. In Italy, a 2019 OLAF report uncovered that 42% of public contracts awarded in the southern regions still had only one bidder, suggesting corruption remained entrenched.

The directive's most controversial impact was on strategic autonomy. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the Commission explicitly encouraged member states to "buy European" for medical supplies, a sharp departure from the non-discrimination principle. The European Court of Justice's 2021 judgment in *Pilatus Post* (C-64/20) clarified that "buy local" preferences were illegal under the directive, but the pandemic had already shown how quickly crisis could override single-market rules.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the UK and the Netherlands vote against Directive 2014/24/EU on Public Procurement?

The UK argued the directive's provisions on social and environmental criteria allowed contracting authorities to discriminate against non-EU bidders, undermining the principle of open competition. The Netherlands feared that the innovation partnership procedure would be too complex for small municipalities, adding bureaucratic burden rather than reducing it.

### Why did it take until 2016 to transpose Directive 2014/24/EU, with some countries failing?

Many member states lacked the administrative capacity to overhaul national procurement laws. The directive also required changes to national criminal codes to implement the mandatory exclusion of bidders convicted of fraud, corruption, or money laundering. Seven member states, including Poland and Romania, were still non-compliant with this requirement as of 2020, according to the Commission's 2022 report.

### Why didn't Directive 2014/24/EU stop corruption in procurement?

The directive's anti-corruption measures were procedural, not investigative. Contracting authorities must exclude bidders convicted of specific crimes, but they rarely check convictions across borders — the Commission's 2020 evaluation found that only 9% of national e-procurement portals cross-referenced the European Criminal Records Information System (ECRIS). Without enforcement, the rules have limited effect.
