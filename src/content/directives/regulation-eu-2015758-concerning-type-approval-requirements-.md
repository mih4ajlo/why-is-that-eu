## What is it?

The eCall Regulation (2015/758/EU) requires that all new passenger cars and light vans sold in the EU are equipped with an in-vehicle emergency call system that automatically dials the single European emergency number 112 in the event of a serious road crash. The system, often built into a vehicle’s telematics unit, transmits a minimum set of data – the vehicle’s location, direction of travel, timestamp, and vehicle type – to the nearest public safety answering point (PSAP). The call can also be triggered manually by a passenger.

The regulation only covers type-approval of the eCall device for new vehicle models; it does not mandate retrofitting existing cars. To protect privacy, the eCall system is designed to be dormant until a crash occurs – no continuous tracking is permitted. The regulation also lays down strict rules on data transmission, storage, and deletion. The technical specifications were adopted in delegated acts, notably the Delegated Regulation (EU) 2017/78 which details the eCall’s performance requirements and the data it must send.

## Why was it introduced?

The push for a mandatory EU-wide eCall system originated from a fundamental structural tension: Europe had one of the world’s best emergency infrastructures, but a fragmented patchwork of national eCall proposals risked creating incompatible, proprietary systems that would waste time and money at precisely the moment when every second counted. Between 2005 and 2012, EU road fatalities hovered around 28,000–30,000 per year (Eurostat data). The European Commission estimated in its 2012 impact assessment that a well-functioning eCall system could reduce emergency response times by 40% in rural areas and up to 50% in built‑up areas, potentially saving between 1,500 and 2,500 lives annually and reducing the severity of injuries for another 15,000–20,000 victims.

The idea had been floating since the 1999 eSafety initiative, and voluntary deployment began around 2009, but take‑up was negligible. By 2013, only around 0.4% of vehicles were equipped with an eCall device, according to the European Emergency Number Association (EENA). The main obstacle was the classic chicken‑and‑egg problem: “private” eCall services (often subscription‑based) existed, but carmakers had little incentive to install the free 112‑based system as long as the public PSAPs weren’t ready, and member states didn’t upgrade PSAPs because almost no cars carried the device.

The coalition that pushed through mandatory legislation was unusual. On one side, the European Parliament’s Committee on Transport and Tourism, led by rapporteur Philippe De Backer, argued that only a binding regulation could break the deadlock. On the other, the European Commission – particularly Vice‑President Neelie Kroes (Digital Agenda) and Commissioner for Industry Ferdinando Nelli Feroci – saw eCall as a flagship of the “connected car” policy. The European Automobile Manufacturers’ Association (ACEA) initially resisted a mandatory deadline, warning of high costs and technical complexity; however, several large carmakers (including Volvo, BMW, and Ford) had already begun developing their own proprietary eCall systems, and the prospect of a single harmonised standard – rather than a panoply of national rules – won them over.

Timing was critical. The crash of the *Costa Concordia* cruise ship in 2012 and the subsequent debate on maritime emergency systems had no direct link to road safety, but it did raise the political salience of all emergency‑response mechanisms. More directly, the 2013 European Parliament resolution on road safety set a political target of halving road deaths by 2020, and the Council of the EU – after years of hesitation – adopted a general approach in December 2013 that accepted mandatory eCall for all new cars. The final deal was reached in November 2014, and the regulation was published in May 2015.

Opposition came from a handful of member states (notably Germany, which feared a clash with its own premium car industry’s proprietary systems) and from privacy advocates, who warned that eCall could become a surveillance tool. The compromise: the regulation explicitly prohibits any tracking data beyond the minute of the crash and requires that no further processing occur without the driver’s consent. This settled a key tension that had blocked earlier attempts.

## Timeline

- **2001** – Commission launches the eSafety initiative, calling for telematics to improve road safety.
- **2009** – Memorandum of Understanding signed with carmakers to voluntarily deploy eCall from 2010; almost no uptake.
- **2012** – Commission publishes an impact assessment estimating life‑saving potential of up to 2,500 lives per year.
- **July 2013** – European Parliament votes for mandatory introduction by 2015 (resolution P7_TA(2013)0334).
- **December 2013** – Council adopts general approach on the eCall regulation.
- **November 2014** – European Parliament and Council reach a trilogue agreement.
- **May 2015** – Regulation (EU) 2015/758 is published in the Official Journal; enters into force 20 days later.
- **March 2018** – Mandatory eCall compliance becomes applicable for all new type‑approvals of passenger cars and light vans.
- **2019** – The Commission reports that the majority of PSAPs in the EU are now eCall‑compatible; only Cyprus, Malta, and a few regions of Greece lag behind.
- **2021** – Commission issues a delegated act updating data transmission standards.

## Impact and consequences

The eCall regulation reshaped the automotive telematics market. By 2020, almost every new car sold in the EU came with an embedded eCall module. According to the Commission’s 2021 review, the system is activated automatically in around 15–20% of all serious crashes (those with airbag deployment), and manual activation occurs in another 5–10% of cases. The review estimated that eCall had contributed to a 9–12% reduction in road fatalities compared to a baseline scenario without the system – though attributing causality precisely has been difficult due to simultaneous improvements in vehicle crashworthiness.

Industry response was mixed. Smaller carmakers initially complained about the per‑unit cost (estimated at €100–€200 by the Commission) but the scale of production quickly drove costs down to about €50 per unit by 2020. The European Automobile Manufacturers’ Association (ACEA) reported that the regulation gave European telematics vendors a first‑mover advantage in the connected‑car market.

The most significant unintended consequence was the delayed roll‑out of “private” eCall services (such as BMW’s Assist or Mercedes‑Benz’s mbrace). Several carmakers argued that the 112‑based system duplicated what they already offered as premium features. The Commission allowed private eCall systems to remain but required them to be fully interoperable with the free 112‑based system – a rule that caused some technical friction but ultimately ensured that all cars could reach a PSAP without a subscription.

Privacy remains a contested area. The European Data Protection Supervisor welcomed the regulation’s strict data‑minimisation provisions, but civil society groups (notably EDRi) have pointed out that the infrastructure can be used for emergency call data – and that the type‑approval framework does not prevent carmakers from repurposing the eCall module for other connected services if they obtain separate consent. This tension resurfaced in the 2020–2021 debate on the ePrivacy Regulation.

## Key questions answered

### Does my car automatically call emergency services if I crash?

Yes, if your car was first registered after March 31, 2018, and is a passenger car or light van in the EU. The eCall system activates automatically when in‑vehicle sensors (such as airbag deployment) detect a severe impact. It dials 112 and sends a “Minimum Set of Data” – location, direction, and vehicle type – to the nearest public safety answering point. You can also press a button to call manually.

### Can the police or my insurance company track my car through eCall?

No. By design, the eCall system is dormant; it does not collect or store any location data before a crash. It only transmits data at the moment of the emergency call and automatically deletes that data after the call ends. The regulation explicitly prohibits continuous tracking or profiling. However, if you have separately opted into another service (e.g., stolen‑vehicle tracking), that data is governed by different rules – it is not part of the mandatory eCall.

### Why did Europe make eCall mandatory instead of leaving it to the market?

Because voluntary deployment failed. By 2013, only 0.4% of cars carried the 112‑based eCall system. Car makers had little incentive to install it until public‑safety answering points were equipped, and member states didn’t upgrade PSAPs until most cars carried the technology – a classic collective‑action problem. The regulation broke this deadlock by setting a single deadline for all new cars, making interoperability mandatory, and using type‑approval as the legal lever.

### Does eCall work when I drive outside my home country?

Yes. The system is based on the single European emergency number 112, which is available in all EU member states plus several non‑EU countries (Norway, Switzerland, Iceland, UK). The mandatory data format is harmonised, so the PSAP in any member state can interpret the sent data. The Commission has also negotiated bilateral agreements with some neighbouring countries to extend interoperability.
