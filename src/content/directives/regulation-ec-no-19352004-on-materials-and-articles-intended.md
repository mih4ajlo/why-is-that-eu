## What is it?

Regulation 1935/2004 sets the legal framework for all materials and articles that come into contact with food — packaging, containers, kitchen utensils, processing equipment, and even printing inks on labels. Its core principle is that no material may transfer its constituents to food in quantities that could endanger human health, cause unacceptable changes in composition, or deteriorate food's taste or smell.

The regulation covers 17 categories of materials, including plastics, ceramics, glass, rubber, paper, and metals. For each, it requires that manufacturers demonstrate safety through migration testing — measuring how much of a substance leaches into food under realistic conditions of use. The regulation also introduced a "declaration of compliance" requirement, obliging every business in the supply chain to document that their materials meet legal limits for chemical migration. It established the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) as the risk assessment body for evaluating new substances before they can be used in food contact materials.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was a series of chemical contamination scares in the late 1990s and early 2000s that exposed how fragmented and outdated the EU's approach to food contact materials had become. The most dramatic incident occurred in 1999, when Belgian authorities discovered that certain plastics used in baby bottles were leaching bisphenol A (BPA) in amounts that European Food Safety Authority scientists later found could disrupt endocrine systems. A year earlier, the Italian olive oil industry had faced a crisis when mineral oils from recycled cardboard packaging migrated into oils stored in warehouses — contaminating entire batches and forcing recalls across Southern Europe.

The structural failure was that national laws governing food contact materials varied enormously. Germany had rigorous migration testing for plastics under its 1997 Bedarfsgegenständeverordnung; Italy had extensive rules for ceramics and glass; France had strict limits for rubber and elastomers. But there was no common framework. A plastic bottle produced in Spain under Spanish rules could be sold legally in Sweden, but Swedish authorities had no way to verify that the Spanish manufacturer had tested for the same substances. The 2001 Commission White Paper on Food Safety, drafted under Health Commissioner David Byrne, explicitly identified this gap as a threat to both consumer protection and the single market.

Commissioner Byrne, an Irish lawyer who had previously overseen food safety reforms after the BSE crisis, made food contact materials a priority. His 2000 impact assessment estimated that approximately 5,000 substances were used in food contact materials across the EU — but only about 1,000 had been evaluated for safety at the EU level. The rest were assessed only under national rules, if at all. Industry groups like the European Plastics Converters (EuPC) and the Confederation of European Paper Industries (CEPI) initially resisted harmonisation, warning that EU-wide testing would increase costs for small and medium enterprises by 15-30%. But the Belgian BPA scare, which had made front-page news across Europe, gave consumer protection groups like BEUC leverage — and public opinion shifted decisively toward action.

The political breakthrough came in 2003, when the European Parliament overwhelmingly supported a report by Italian MEP Francesco Fiori calling for mandatory EU-wide safety assessments. The vote was 452 in favour, 19 against — reflecting an unusual cross-party consensus that food contact materials could not remain a patchwork of national rules. The Commission proposal, introduced in November 2003, was adopted by the Council in July 2004 with almost no substantive changes. The regulation entered into force in December 2004 and became fully applicable on 3 December 2006, giving industry two years to adapt.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation forced a complete restructuring of the European packaging supply chain. Before 2006, small and medium-sized converters — companies that molded plastics or coated paper — often relied on suppliers' verbal assurances that materials were safe. After 2004, every company had to maintain a "declaration of compliance" documenting test results. The German chemical industry association VCI estimated in 2007 compliance costs at €200-300 million across the sector in the first year alone.

The most significant industry shift was the creation of a de facto EU-wide positive list for substances used in plastic food contact materials. Under new specific measures adopted in 2011, only substances that had been evaluated by EFSA could be used. This eliminated approximately 2,000 substances that had been marketed under national rules but lacked EU safety data. The European Association of Chemical Distributors reported that 15-20% of their members' product lines for the packaging sector were discontinued between 2008 and 2012.

Enforcement, however, has been uneven. A 2016 Commission report found that only eight Member States had fully implemented the regulation's traceability requirements — requiring all businesses in the supply chain to know where materials came from and where they went. The Commission's 2020 fitness check concluded that while the framework regulation works well for plastics and ceramics — which have specific EU measures — materials like paper, rubber, and printing inks remain regulated only at national level, creating gaps that allow unsafe products to circulate.

The most lasting consequence has been the shift toward precautionary regulation. In 2017, EFSA significantly reduced the safe limit for BPA, leading several Member States — including France in 2015 — to ban it in food contact materials unilaterally. The Commission itself proposed an EU-wide ban on BPA in 2021, reflecting how the 2004 regulation's framework allows new scientific evidence to trigger regulatory action without needing to rewrite the entire law.

## Key questions answered

### What packaging materials are still not fully regulated at the EU level?

Despite the 2004 framework, several major material categories remain under national law only. Paper and cardboard, rubbers and elastomers, printing inks, and adhesives all lack specific EU measures. This means a paper cup made in Germany must comply with German rules, but German authorities cannot block a cup made in Poland that complies only with Polish rules — as long as the Polish rules meet the general safety requirement of Regulation 1935/2004. The Commission's 2020 fitness check identified this as a significant gap, estimating that 30% of food contact materials on the EU market still lack harmonised rules. The revision launched in 2020 aims to close these gaps, particularly for paper and recyclates.

### Can a European country ban a chemical that the EU says is safe in packaging?

Yes — but only under specific conditions. Once the EU adopts a specific measure for a material (like the Plastics Regulation), Member States cannot unilaterally ban a substance that has been authorised at EU level. France's 2015 ban on BPA in all food contact materials was challenged by PlasticsEurope as violating the single market; the French government argued it was justified under the precautionary principle because new scientific evidence had emerged. The Commission declined to take legal action, but the case highlighted the tension between national precaution and EU harmonisation. For materials without specific EU measures (paper, inks, rubbers), Member States are free to set stricter national rules.

### Does the regulation require companies to test every product?

No, but the burden of proof is on manufacturers to demonstrate safety. The regulation requires a "declaration of compliance" — a document that must be available on demand from enforcement authorities — showing that the material meets applicable migration limits. Companies can rely on existing test data for similar materials, provided they can justify that the conditions of use (temperature, contact time, food type) are comparable. For most plastics manufacturers, this means testing once per product formulation per supplier, not per individual item. The European Committee for Standardization (CEN) has developed standard test methods (such as EN 1186 for overall migration) that most companies follow.

### What happens if a company violates the regulation?

Enforcement is the responsibility of Member States. National authorities can require corrective actions — recall products, halt production, or destroy non-compliant materials. Fines vary widely: in Germany, violations can result in fines up to €50,000 and potential criminal liability for company directors; in smaller Member States with less enforcement capacity, penalties are often lower. The most high-profile enforcement action came in 2018, when Italian authorities seized 40 tonnes of non-compliant plastic containers bound for Chinese exports, citing missing declarations of compliance. The Commission's 2020 evaluation found that only 12 Member States had conducted any market surveillance inspections for food contact materials in the previous three years — meaning enforcement remains a significant gap.
