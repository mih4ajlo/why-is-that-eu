## What is it?

The Cybersecurity Act (2019/881/EU) is a regulation that gives the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) a permanent role and expanded resources, replacing its previous temporary status. More importantly, it creates a voluntary EU-wide cybersecurity certification framework for information and communications technology (ICT) products, services, and processes.

Under the framework, certified products receive an EU-wide certificate valid in all member states—eliminating the need for multiple national certifications. Certification schemes can be developed at three assurance levels: basic, substantial, and high (for high-risk uses like critical infrastructure). The European Commission, with input from ENISA and a stakeholder group (European Cybersecurity Certification Group), defines the specific schemes. The first such schemes covered common criteria for ICT security (EUCC) and cloud services (EUCS).

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst was a fundamental structural tension: the EU had no common cybersecurity certification system. Instead, member states ran their own national schemes—often incompatible, expensive, and overlapping. A manufacturer wanting to sell a connected device across Europe could face 28 separate certifications, each with different standards and costs. This fragmented market stifled digital trade and created security gaps.

The political moment crystallised in 2016–2017. Two massive cyberattacks—WannaCry (May 2017) and NotPetya (June 2017)—crippled hospitals, ports, and multinationals across Europe. In the UK alone, WannaCry shut down one-third of NHS trusts. The attacks exposed that many connected products lacked basic security and that no EU-wide baseline existed. The European Commission, under President Jean-Claude Juncker and Digital Single Market Commissioner Andrus Ansip, saw cybersecurity as a market-integration and consumer-protection priority.

The coalition driving the Act was unusual. Large technology firms like Microsoft and Siemens publicly called for EU-wide certification to simplify compliance. Consumer groups (BEUC) pushed for mandatory security standards. On the other side, some member states—particularly those with mature national schemes (e.g., Germany, France, the UK before Brexit)—feared losing control. They argued that voluntary certification would be ignored and that a permanent ENISA could override national security agencies. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Angelika Niebler (EPP), largely supported the Commission’s proposal but insisted on adding the high assurance level and stronger provisions for national involvement.

Timing was everything. The earlier attempt to give ENISA a permanent mandate had stalled in 2013, blocked by member states reluctant to cede sovereignty. But the 2016 Network and Information Security (NIS) Directive (2016/1148/EU)—the EU’s first cybersecurity law—had already created a cooperative framework. Its adoption showed that member states could agree on common rules. The 2017 ransomware attacks broke the remaining resistance; even sceptical states like Germany and Poland accepted that fragmented certification was a liability.

What finally unblocked the permanent mandate was the 2018 – 2019 budgeting round. The Commission proposed doubling ENISA’s budget to €23 million per year, and the Council agreed—conditional on member states retaining a strong role in the certification governance. The compromise: a European Cybersecurity Certification Group composed of national representatives would oversee scheme development, while ENISA would coordinate and execute. The regulation passed the European Parliament on 12 March 2019 with 600 votes in favour, 47 against, and 26 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **2013** — Commission proposal for a permanent ENISA mandate fails due to member state opposition. ENISA continues on temporary five-year mandates.
- **July 2016** — NIS Directive (2016/1148/EU) enters force, establishing baseline cybersecurity requirements and cross‑border cooperation.
- **May–June 2017** — WannaCry and NotPetya attacks disrupt critical services across Europe; political urgency surges.
- **September 2017** — European Commission unveils the Cybersecurity Act as part of a broader "Cybersecurity Package".
- **March 2019** — European Parliament adopts the Act with overwhelming majority (600 in favour).
- **April 2019** — Council of the EU formally approves.
- **June 2019** — Regulation enters into force; ENISA becomes a permanent agency.
- **January 2021** — First certification scheme (EUCC) proposed; public consultation opens.
- **December 2023** — Implementing Regulation for EUCC adopted (Commission Implementing Regulation 2023/2699).

## Impact and consequences

The Act transformed ENISA from a temporary project office with roughly 60 staff into a permanent agency of over 100 staff by 2024, with a budget exceeding €30 million per year. For businesses, the certification framework reduced duplication: a smart meter manufacturer that used to apply for certificates in five member states can now obtain one EU‑wide certificate. By 2024, the Commission had launched work on ten certification schemes, covering everything from cloud services to 5G components.

However, the real impact has been slower than hoped. Because certification is voluntary, many companies have not bothered—especially small and medium enterprises that do not sell cross‑border. The Commission’s 2023 evaluation found that only eight products had received EUCC certification by mid‑2023. In response, the 2022 NIS 2 Directive (2022/2555/EU) made certification mandatory for certain critical product categories, and the 2023 Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) will require cybersecurity certification for all connected products sold in the EU by 2027.

Industry responses were mixed. Large firms with compliance departments welcomed the harmonisation; smaller ones complained about cost and complexity. The German IT industry association Bitkom warned in 2020 that voluntary certification would lead to a “two‑tier market”—big firms certified, small firms not—exactly what happened.

Member states also adapted unevenly. By 2024, only seven had fully implemented the governance structures for national certification bodies, according to a Commission progress report. The rest cited resource constraints.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU now have a permanent cybersecurity agency?

Yes. Before the Cybersecurity Act, the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) was reauthorised every few years on a temporary basis. The Act made ENISA a permanent agency with a stable budget and expanded responsibilities, including managing the EU’s cybersecurity certification framework.

### What does EU cybersecurity certification mean for products I buy?

If a product—like a smart thermostat or a connected medical device—carries an EU cybersecurity certificate, it means the manufacturer has had it independently tested against a common set of security requirements valid in all 27 member states. The certification is voluntary for most products currently, but new laws will soon make it mandatory for many categories.

### Why did the EU create a certification framework instead of just setting rules?

The EU’s core problem before 2019 was not a lack of rules—it was a patchwork of incompatible national certification schemes. A product tested in Germany might need re‑testing in France. The Cybersecurity Act creates a single trusted certificate that works across Europe, removing that duplication. Over time, the framework also aims to raise the baseline security level for all certified products.
