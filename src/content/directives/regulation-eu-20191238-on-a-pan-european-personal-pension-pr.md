## What is it?

The Pan-European Personal Pension Product (PEPP) Regulation, adopted in 2019, creates a voluntary, standardized personal pension scheme that can be sold across the European Union. It allows savers to accumulate savings for retirement through a single product regardless of where they move within the bloc, addressing the lack of portability in national pension systems. PEPP can be offered by a range of financial institutions, including banks, insurance companies, asset managers, and occupational pension funds, subject to regulatory approval by the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA).

The product is subject to common rules on transparency, investment options, and consumer protection. PEPP providers must offer a default "basic" investment option designed to preserve capital, with a maximum annual charge of 1% of accumulated capital under a specific calculation method introduced by the regulation. Savers can choose from up to five alternative investment options. Accumulated savings are fully portable: a saver can continue contributing to the same PEPP after moving to another Member State, unlike most national private pensions.

## Why was it introduced?

The PEPP Regulation was a direct response to a structural failure: Europe's fragmented pension markets left millions of citizens unable to build portable retirement savings. According to the European Commission's 2017 impact assessment, only 27% of EU citizens between 25 and 59 had any form of personal pension, and cross-border provision was virtually nonexistent — fewer than 1% of personal pensions were sold across borders. The problem was particularly acute for the estimated 17 million EU citizens living in a Member State other than their country of origin, who faced losing tax advantages or being forced to cash out pensions when moving.

The political catalyst was the Capital Markets Union (CMU) initiative launched by Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker in 2014. After the 2008 financial crisis, European policymakers recognized that Europe's over-reliance on bank lending and underdeveloped capital markets hindered economic growth. The CMU aimed to unlock private capital for investment. But retail investment — including pensions — remained stubbornly national, with deep structural barriers: 28 different regulatory regimes, no passporting mechanism for personal pensions, and incompatible tax treatments.

The decisive push came from an unlikely coalition. On one side, the European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Sophia in 't Veld (ALDE, Netherlands), argued that an EU-level pension product would boost competition and reduce costs. On the other, industry associations including Insurance Europe and the European Fund and Asset Management Association (EFAMA) lobbied for a flexible framework that would allow existing products to qualify as PEPPs without costly restructuring. The insurance sector was particularly active: according to a 2017 study, over 95% of personal pension contracts in the EU were sold with capital guarantees, a feature the PEPP's default option mirrored.

Opposition came from several quarters. Germany's Bafin and several national regulators warned that a mandatory 1% fee cap would make the product unprofitable for lower-income savers, who would instead be pushed toward more expensive national products. France's financial supervisory authority (ACPR) expressed concern that the PEPP's tax treatment would remain under national control, creating confusion. Consumer groups, including BEUC, criticized the cap as too high, noting that average annual fees of national products in Sweden and the Netherlands were below 0.5%.

The timing was critical. The European Commission's proposal in June 2017 came just weeks after the European Court of Justice's May 2017 ruling on the "Müller" case (C-451/16), which affirmed that cross-border pension rights could not be restricted by national legislation. According to Commissioner Jonathan Hill (2014-2016), the "demographic time bomb" — with the EU's old-age dependency ratio projected to rise from 29.6% in 2015 to 51.2% by 2070, according to Eurostat — made reform unavoidable.

The political negotiation took 18 months. The European Parliament adopted its position in April 2019 by 519 votes to 84. The Council reached a political agreement in December 2018, with only two Member States — Denmark and the Netherlands — voting against, arguing the product would not offer sufficient value compared to their well-developed national occupational pension systems. The final text was adopted on June 20, 2019.

- **May 2017** — European Court of Justice ruling in Müller (C-451/16) affirms cross-border pension portability rights

- **December 2018** — EU Council (Economic and Financial Affairs Council) reaches political agreement
- **April 16, 2019** — European Parliament adopts its position (519 in favour, 84 against, 40 abstentions)
- **June 20, 2019** — Regulation (EU) 2019/1238 adopted by the European Parliament and Council
- **November 29, 2019** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after publication in the Official Journal)
- **March 22, 2022** — Application date: financial institutions can begin offering PEPP products
- **2022-2024** — First PEPP products registered: as of 2024, EIOPA reported that only a handful of firms had applied, with no products yet reaching the market in most Member States

## Impact and consequences

As of 2024, the PEPP Regulation has had limited practical impact. According to EIOPA's 2023 annual report, only three products had been registered by financial institutions across the entire EU. The European Commission's 2023 review noted that "few market participants have taken up the PEPP" and that "the 1% fee cap has been cited as a disincentive." Industry groups like Insurance Europe argued the cap made the product uneconomic for medium- and low-income savers, particularly in Central and Eastern Europe where national personal pension fees averaged 2-3% annually.

The product's slow adoption reflects deeper structural barriers the regulation did not resolve. National tax treatment remains a crucial factor: in most Member States, tax advantages for PEPP contributions are limited or nonexistent, creating a significant disadvantage compared to national products that benefit from favorable tax regimes. The European Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated that PEPP subscribers in France would receive a tax subsidy worth 0.2% of contributions, compared to 4.5% for national products. Germany's Riester pension, by contrast, offers annual subsidies of up to €175 per saver, plus child allowances.

Consumer awareness remains low. According to a 2022 Eurobarometer survey, only 8% of EU citizens had heard of PEPP. National regulators, whose cooperation is required for cross-border distribution, have been slow to adapt their supervisory frameworks. The regulation does not harmonize tax rules — explicitly prohibited by Article 114 TFEU — leaving a patchwork of 27 different tax regimes that constrains the product's appeal.

A notable consequence has been the pressure on the European Commission to revisit the fee cap. In its 2023 review, the Commission proposed replacing the fixed 1% cap with a "value-for-money" test that would allow higher fees if a product demonstrated superior performance or lower management costs. This change was incorporated into the 2024 Retail Investment Strategy proposal, but as of 2025, no legislative text has been adopted.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU cap fees on a new type of cross-border pension at 1% when pension companies said it was too low?

The 1% cap was a political compromise between consumer groups who wanted lower fees and industry who wanted none. According to the Commission's 2017 impact assessment, the average annual management fee for personal pensions across the EU was 0.9%, but this masked enormous variation: fees in some Member States exceeded 3% annually. The European Parliament's ECON committee initially proposed a 0.75% cap, while the Council proposed 1.5%. The final 1% figure emerged from trilogue negotiations in late 2018. Insurers warned that the cap would make PEPP unprofitable for smaller providers and certain customer segments, but the Commission and Parliament prioritized consumer protection after studies showed that even 1% annual fees could reduce total retirement savings by 20% over a 40-year career.

### Why hasn't the EU's plan for a single Europe-wide personal pension taken off?

PEPP's failure to gain traction stems from a fundamental design flaw: the regulation created a product but did not harmonize the tax environment in which it operates. According to the European Commission's 2023 review, national tax rules effectively determine the product's attractiveness. In 22 of 27 Member States, PEPP contributions are not tax-deductible or are subject to less favorable treatment than national products. Additionally, the regulation left distribution and advice in national hands, meaning PEPP providers must still comply with 27 different advisory regimes and marketing rules. The product's portability, while theoretically valuable, applies only to the accumulation phase — payout structures remain subject to national rules, creating further friction.

### Why did Denmark and the Netherlands vote against the EU's new Europe-wide personal pension product?

Both Denmark and the Netherlands already had mature, high-penetration occupational pension systems that cover 80-95% of their working populations. According to the European Commission's 2017 impact assessment, their existing systems offered fees well below the PEPP's 1% cap (average 0.3-0.5% annually in occupational plans). Dutch and Danish pension funds, represented by PensionsEurope, argued that a new EU product would not add value for their members but could undermine the efficiency of their collective systems by drawing assets away from low-cost, professionally managed funds. Their opposition reflected a broader tension in the PEPP negotiation between countries with well-developed private pension systems (Germany, Denmark, Netherlands, Sweden) and those with weaker coverage (particularly Southern and Eastern European Member States).
