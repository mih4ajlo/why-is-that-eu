## What is it?

The Data Governance Act (DGA) is a horizontal regulation that aims to increase trust in and availability of data for re-use across the European Union. It creates legal frameworks for three key mechanisms: 1) the reuse of data held by public sector bodies that is subject to others' rights (e.g., commercial confidentiality, intellectual property), 2) notification and oversight of data intermediation services that connect data holders with data users, and 3) registration of "data altruism" organisations that voluntarily share data for general interest purposes. It also establishes a European Data Innovation Board to advise the Commission on data-sharing best practices.

The DGA is a cornerstone of the European Strategy for Data, which the Commission introduced in February 2020. While the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) focuses on personal data, the DGA covers all types of data — including non-personal data like industrial sensor data, trade secrets, and public sector geo-information. It operates as a framework that enables voluntary data sharing, distinct from the Data Act (2023/2854), which imposes mandatory obligations for data generated by connected products.

## Why was it introduced?

The DGA was born out of a structural tension in the EU data economy: a growing mountain of data sat unused while European companies and public services suffered from data scarcity. The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment estimated that the volume of data created in the EU would multiply five-fold between 2018 and 2025, from 33 zettabytes to 175 zettabytes. Yet only a fraction was being reused — the Commission cited a 2018 study finding that 80% of industrial data was never used. Meanwhile, non-EU platforms had amassed dominant positions by hoarding user data, creating a dependency the Commission deemed strategically unsustainable.

The coalition that pushed the DGA through was broad but fragile. Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton was the driving force, building on President Ursula von der Leyen’s political commitment to "European digital sovereignty" in her 2019-2024 mandate. BusinessEurope, the main pan-European business lobby, supported the reuse of public sector data but fought hard to limit liability for data intermediaries and to ensure that commercial confidentiality protections were not weakened. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) pushed for stronger safeguards against data exploitation. The European Parliament's rapporteur on the file was Angelika Niebler (EPP, Germany), who helped steer a compromise that satisfied both business and consumer interests. Opposition came primarily from member states with strong commercial data-protection traditions, particularly Germany and France, who feared that DGA rules on data reuse might undermine existing trade-secret protections.

The timing was driven by three converging factors. First, the COVID-19 pandemic exposed the value of data sharing — health data, mobility data, and economic data became critical for crisis management, and the EU had no common framework to enable it. Second, the Court of Justice of the European Union’s Schrems II ruling (July 2020) had invalidated the Privacy Shield for transatlantic data flows, creating urgency for a coherent European data space that could reduce dependence on non-EU data holders. Third, the Commission had already laid the groundwork with the Open Data Directive (2019/1024) — which focused on making certain public sector and publicly funded data reusable — and needed a complementary framework to govern the reuse of data not covered by open-data rules.

Earlier attempts had stalled. The Commission's 2017 "Building a European Data Economy" communication had proposed a data ownership framework that would have created new property rights for non-personal data — but this was abandoned in 2018 after fierce opposition from industry, which argued it would disrupt data flows, and from civil society, which warned it could be used to bypass data protection rights. The DGA took a different, less prescriptive approach: instead of defining who owns data, it focuses on enabling data sharing through trusted intermediaries.

A specific incident broke the deadlock on the public-sector reuse provisions. The 2019 "Google Spain v. AEPD" case (C-136/17), while about the right to be forgotten, had revealed how unevenly data reuse rights were implemented across member states. The Commission’s 2020 evaluation of the Public Sector Information (PSI) Directive found that only 9 of 27 member states had fully transposed the 2013 rules. The DGA introduced mandatory horizontal rules for public sector bodies holding data that falls outside open-data regimes — for example, public transport timetables that include commercially sensitive carrier contracts, or health registries containing trade secrets. It required these bodies to put in place technical and legal safeguards for reuse, and to charge transparent fees. The alternative — a fully voluntary system — was rejected by the Commission’s 2020 impact assessment, which found that voluntary data-sharing schemes across the EU had achieved "very limited" uptake.

## Timeline

- **2014** — The PSI Directive (2013/37/EU) is transposed; the Commission begins monitoring its implementation.
- **2017** — The Commission publishes "Building a European Data Economy" communication, proposing data ownership rights for non-personal data — later abandoned.
- **February 2020** — The Commission releases the "European Strategy for Data" communication, announcing the DGA as a legislative priority.
- **November 2020** — The Commission formally proposes the Data Governance Act (COM/2020/767).
- **December 2021** — The Council of the European Union and the European Parliament reach political agreement on the DGA after trilogue negotiations.
- **May 2022** — The DGA is formally adopted by the Council and Parliament; it enters into force on 23 June 2022.
- **September 2023** — The DGA begins to apply (Article 48 sets application from 24 September 2023, except for data altruism provisions becoming applicable from 23 June 2024).
- **2024** — The Commission reports that several member states have designated competent authorities for data intermediation services; no major enforcement actions are public by end of 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate impact of the DGA has been to create a regulatory ecosystem for data intermediaries. By late 2024, the Commission had received notifications from a number of data intermediation services across the EU, though the total number remained limited — partly because the rules only began to apply in September 2023. The European Data Innovation Board was launched in 2024 to coordinate practices.

Industry response was cautious. European data cooperatives and a handful of start-ups welcomed the framework as providing legal certainty, but larger players — particularly in the automotive and manufacturing sectors — expressed concerns about administrative costs of notification and about potential conflicts with trade-secret protections. The Commission’s 2024 review of the "Data Governance Act: First Implementation Steps" found that around 15 data intermediation services had been notified across the EU by mid-2024, with Germany and France accounting for the majority.

The DGA has had a more significant structural effect on public sector bodies. Previously, many held data (e.g., transport flow data, energy grid data, public procurement contract details) that they lacked the mandate to share because of third-party rights. The DGA provided a clear legal basis for reuse, though implementation has been uneven. A 2024 Commission survey cited in the 2024 progress report found that only 40% of public sector bodies across the EU had updated their internal data-sharing procedures to comply with the DGA by the end of its first year of application.

The most concrete consequence is the establishment of the European data altruism framework. The first "data altruism organisations" were registered in 2024, mostly in the health and environmental sectors. The European Data Protection Board intervened in 2023, warning that the DGA’s data altruism provisions must not undermine GDPR rights — a clash of frameworks that the Commission acknowledged would need further guidance.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a new law if the GDPR already governs data?

The GDPR focuses on *personal* data protection — data that can identify a living individual. The DGA covers *all* types of data, including industrial machine data, commercial trade secrets, and anonymised public records. Moreover, the GDPR regulates use of personal data; the DGA governs the *sharing* of data held by public bodies or through intermediaries. They are complementary: the DGA explicitly states it does not affect the GDPR.

### Why didn’t the EU just mandate data sharing outright instead of creating the voluntary framework of the Data Governance Act?

The Commission considered mandatory data-sharing obligations but rejected them for the DGA because they would have required defining data ownership rights — an approach that sparked the 2017 failure. Instead, the DGA focuses on removing barriers to voluntary sharing by creating trust mechanisms (intermediaries, altruism registration) and uniform conditions for reuse. Mandatory data sharing was later introduced in the Data Act (2023/2854) for specific data generated by connected products.

### Who benefits most from the DGA?

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and startups stand to benefit most, according to the Commission's impact assessment. They currently lack the bargaining power to negotiate data access from larger data holders or public bodies. Data intermediaries operating under the DGA offer them a trusted, non-discriminatory marketplace. The framework also benefits researchers and public interest groups — particularly in health, environment, and mobility — who can now access data through data altruism organisations.
