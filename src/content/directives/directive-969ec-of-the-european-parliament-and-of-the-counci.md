## What is it?

The Database Directive harmonised EU law on the copyright protection of databases and, more controversially, introduced a new *sui generis* database right. Under the directive, databases that represent a "substantial investment" in obtaining, verifying, or presenting their contents—even if those contents are not original in themselves—receive exclusive protection. This right lasts 15 years and can be renewed if the database is continually updated, potentially creating perpetual protection.

The directive distinguishes between two forms of protection: copyright, which applies only to databases that are "original" in the sense of the author's intellectual creation (the structure or selection), and the *sui generis* right, which protects the *contents* of the database. The *sui generis* right gives the maker the right to prevent extraction and re-utilisation of all or a substantial part of the database's contents. This was a novel, European-created monopoly on factual data—a radical departure from copyright tradition.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a specific structural tension and a moment of pan-European tech-industry anxiety. Entering force in 1996, it reflected the mid-1990s belief that databases were the "raw material of the information society", and that Europe was losing the race against the United States.

**The structural tension** was that existing copyright law, under the Berne Convention and the TRIPS Agreement, protected only *original* compilations—databases with creative selection or arrangement. A phone book, a legal database, or a stock price feed—all requiring massive investment—could be copied with impunity in Europe because the contents were facts, not creative works. The Commission argued that this created a free-rider problem: if a company spent millions assembling a comprehensive legal database, a competitor could simply copy and resell it, destroying the incentive to invest.

**The coalition dynamics** were unusual. The main push came from the European Commission's DG Internal Market, led by Commissioner Mario Monti, and from large European database publishers—most notably Reed Elsevier (owner of LexisNexis) and the Dutch legal publisher Kluwer. These companies argued that without a new right, the European database industry would be crushed by US competitors like West Publishing (later Thomson Reuters), which enjoyed strong copyright protection under US law for its compilations. The European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee, chaired by German MEP Willi Rothley, was initially sceptical but was won over after intense lobbying from database publishers and the Commission.

**The opposition** came from a coalition of academics, research institutions, and smaller publishers who warned that a new *sui generis* right would create a monopoly on facts, harming scientific research and competition. The European Association of Information Services (EUSIDIC) and the Max Planck Institute for Innovation and Competition argued that the right was unnecessary because existing unfair competition laws already protected investment. In the Council, some member states—particularly Germany and the Netherlands—had existing database protection and pushed for a strong right, while others, like the UK and Ireland, had weaker protections and were more cautious.

**The timing** was driven by a specific geopolitical fear: in 1993, the Commission published a study claiming that the European database industry held only 26% of the global market, compared to the US's 64%. The US had just passed the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) in 1994, and the WTO's TRIPS agreement was being finalised—both seen as giving US data companies an advantage. The 1994 Bangemann Report on "Europe and the Global Information Society" explicitly called for database protection as a pillar of European competitiveness. Commissioner Martin Bangemann, responsible for industrial policy, was a key architect of the directive's push.

**What changed** to make it possible was a series of Commission-backed studies and a strategic framing: the directive was presented not as a new IP right but as a *harmonisation* measure to complete the single market for data services. This allowed it to be adopted under qualified majority voting (QMV) in the Council, bypassing the national vetoes that had blocked earlier attempts. The final compromise in the 1995 common position was crucial: the right was limited to 15 years (not the 25 initially proposed), and exceptions for teaching and research were included, but the scope of "substantial investment" was left deliberately vague—a decision that would cause decades of litigation.

## Timeline

- **1992** — The Commission publishes a "Draft Directive on the Legal Protection of Databases", proposing both copyright and a new *sui generis* right, following a 1991 Green Paper on copyright.
- **1994** — The Bangemann Report explicitly calls for database protection as critical to Europe's information society strategy.
- **1995** — The Council reaches a common position on the draft directive after intense negotiations on the duration (15 years) and the definition of "substantial investment".
- **1996, March 11** — The European Parliament and Council adopt Directive 96/9/EC. It enters into force 20 days later.
- **1998, January 1** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2004** — The first major *sui generis* database court ruling: the European Court of Justice (now CJEU) issues its decision in *The British Horseracing Board v. William Hill* (C-203/02), sharply restricting the scope of the right by ruling that "investment in obtaining" covers only the *creation* of data, not its source. The database must be a "substantial investment" in the database itself, not in generating the underlying data.
- **2005** — The Commission publishes its first evaluation report on the directive (COM(2005) 8 final), concluding that the *sui generis* right had "negative" effects: the number of non-original databases in the US market grew by 50% between 1997 and 2003, while in the EU it fell by 10%.
- **2016** — In *Ryanair v. PR Aviation* (C-30/14), the CJEU clarifies that the directive does not prevent database makers from imposing contractual restrictions on use, even for databases not protected by the right.
- **2018** — The Commission's second evaluation report (SWD(2018) 146 final) acknowledges that the *sui generis* right has largely failed to achieve its objectives, noting that the global database market remains dominated by US companies and that the right has created "legal uncertainty" and increased transaction costs.

## Impact and consequences

The directive is widely considered a failure by economists and legal scholars. The Commission's own 2005 evaluation found that the number of database producers in the EU fell from over 3,000 in 1996 to just over 1,000 in 2004, while the US market grew significantly. The Commission's 2018 evaluation concluded that the *sui generis* right had "limited evidence of its positive impact on the EU database industry" and that it had created "significant costs" for users through increased licensing fees and legal uncertainty.

The litigation landscape was extensive but defensive. The CJEU's 2004 ruling in *British Horseracing Board v. William Hill* effectively gutted the right's scope: the court held that investment in generating the underlying data (like horse racing results) does not count as investment in the database itself. This meant that many large public databases—weather data, sports schedules, stock exchange data—could not claim *sui generis* protection. Smaller commercial databases like CD-ROM directories did benefit, but their market was already in decline due to the internet.

The directive's most significant unintended consequence was its chilling effect on scientific research and open data initiatives. Universities and research institutions found they could not freely share datasets due to unclear ownership claims, and the vague definition of "substantial investment" led to aggressive litigation from database owners. The 2005 Commission report noted that the directive had "not led to a significant increase in investment in databases" and had "increased transaction costs for users."

## Key questions answered

### Why did European attempts to create a new legal right for databases fail?

The right was designed to boost the European database industry against US dominance, but the CJEU's narrow interpretation in the *British Horseracing Board* case (2004) limited it to databases where the maker invests in *gathering and checking* pre-existing data—not in creating the data itself. This excluded most valuable databases like public registers, stock exchanges, and sports results. The result was legal uncertainty, costly litigation, and no measurable increase in database production in the EU, while the US market continued to grow.

### Does the EU's "database right" last forever?

Not technically, but effectively it can. The *sui generis* right lasts 15 years, but it is automatically renewed if the database is updated in a way that constitutes a "substantial new investment". Since commercial databases are constantly updated, this can create perpetual protection in practice—a feature that critics argue turns a temporary monopoly into a permanent one on facts, contrary to the fundamental principle that facts cannot be owned.

### Who actually won and who lost from the *sui generis* database right?

The main winners were large database publishers (like Reed Elsevier and Kluwer) and legal information providers, who used the right to enforce licensing fees and block competitors. The main losers were researchers, scientific institutions, and smaller database makers, who faced higher costs and legal uncertainty. The public also lost—many publicly funded databases (like those of national railways or weather services) became subject to restrictive licensing, limiting re-use. The biggest losers were the taxpayers, who funded the creation of the data but were then charged to access it through the database owners.
