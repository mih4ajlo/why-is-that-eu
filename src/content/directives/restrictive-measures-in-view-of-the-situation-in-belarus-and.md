## What is it?

Council Regulation 765/2006 is the legal backbone of the EU's sanctions against Belarus. It imposes asset freezes and travel bans on individuals and entities responsible for human rights violations and the repression of civil society, prohibits the export of equipment that could be used for internal repression, and — after successive expansions — bans trade in potash, petroleum products, tobacco, cement, iron and steel, wood and dual-use goods, restricts access to EU capital markets for Belarusian banks, and blocks Belarusian airlines from EU airspace.

The regulation operates alongside Council Decision 2012/642/CFSP, which provides the political framework, and is updated by the Council whenever new designations or sectoral measures are added. By 2024, the regime covered hundreds of individuals — including Alexander Lukashenko, his sons, judges, propagandists, KGB officers and election officials — and dozens of state-owned enterprises.

It is the second-most extensive EU sanctions regime after the one targeting Russia, and the two have been progressively aligned to close circumvention routes through Belarusian territory.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was born in 2006 after Lukashenko claimed an 83% landslide in a presidential election that the OSCE/ODIHR observation mission judged failed to meet democratic standards, and after riot police beat opposition demonstrators in Minsk's October Square. The Council, then under the Austrian presidency, agreed an asset freeze and visa ban on Lukashenko and around three dozen officials. For more than a decade the regime oscillated — sanctions were partially lifted in 2016 after Lukashenko released political prisoners, in a brief thaw that EU foreign policy chief Federica Mogherini hoped would pull Minsk away from Moscow.

That calculus collapsed on 9 August 2020. Lukashenko claimed 80% in a presidential vote against Sviatlana Tsikhanouskaya, who had stepped in after her husband Siarhei was jailed. Hundreds of thousands of Belarusians took to the streets; the Interior Ministry detained roughly 7,000 people in the first days, and the human rights group Viasna documented systematic torture in Akrestsina detention centre. The European Council, meeting on 19 August 2020, declared it did not recognise the election results. Foreign ministers approved a first new sanctions package in October 2020 — initially blocked for weeks by Cyprus, which tied its consent to parallel EU action against Turkey over Mediterranean drilling. Once Nicosia relented, 40 officials were listed; Lukashenko himself was added in November after further violence.

The decisive escalation came on 23 May 2021, when Belarusian air traffic control ordered Ryanair flight FR4978 from Athens to Vilnius to divert to Minsk, citing a fake bomb threat, and a MiG-29 escorted it down so authorities could arrest journalist Raman Pratasevich and his partner Sofia Sapega. An extraordinary European Council the next day, chaired by Charles Michel, called the diversion an act of state piracy. Within a month the EU adopted sweeping sectoral sanctions for the first time: bans on Belarusian potash (a major export sustaining Belaruskali, one of Lukashenko's main revenue sources), petroleum products, tobacco and access to EU capital markets, plus a ban on Belarusian carriers — including flag carrier Belavia — from EU airspace and airports.

A third wave followed in late 2021, when Lukashenko orchestrated a migration crisis at the Polish, Lithuanian and Latvian borders by flying in migrants from the Middle East and pushing them across — what then-Commission President Ursula von der Leyen called a hybrid attack. The Council adopted new listings and amended the regulation to target operators facilitating the smuggling. The fourth and largest expansion came after 24 February 2022, when Russian forces invaded Ukraine partly from Belarusian territory. Brussels treated Minsk as a co-belligerent and aligned the Belarus regime with the Russia sanctions: cutting selected Belarusian banks from SWIFT, banning iron and steel, wood, cement and dual-use goods exports, and restricting the central bank's reserves.

## Timeline

- **2006** — Council Regulation 765/2006 adopted after disputed March presidential election and crackdown on Minsk protests.
- **2016** — Most sanctions suspended after release of political prisoners; arms embargo and listings on four officials linked to disappearances retained.
- **August 2020** — Lukashenko claims 80% in disputed election; mass protests and police violence trigger EU response.
- **October 2020** — First post-2020 listings adopted after Cyprus drops veto.
- **November 2020** — Lukashenko added to the list.
- **23 May 2021** — Ryanair FR4978 forced to land in Minsk; Pratasevich arrested.
- **24 June 2021** — Council adopts first sectoral sanctions: potash, petroleum, tobacco, capital markets, aviation.
- **Late 2021** — New listings respond to the engineered border migration crisis.
- **2 March 2022** — Belarus sanctions expanded in parallel with Russia package after the invasion of Ukraine; SWIFT cut-off for selected banks.
- **2022–2024** — Successive packages add iron and steel, wood, cement, dual-use and advanced technology bans, and tighten circumvention rules.

## Impact and consequences

The sectoral measures hit Belarus's export economy hard. Potash, which the World Bank had identified as one of the country's main hard-currency earners through Belaruskali, lost direct access to EU markets and — after Lithuania cancelled the rail transit contract through Klaipėda port in early 2022 — its main westward export route. The Belarusian rouble depreciated sharply through 2022, and the Finance Ministry in Minsk defaulted on foreign-currency Eurobond payments in 2022, with the government insisting it had paid in roubles.

Belavia, blocked from EU airports and airspace, lost most of its Western fleet's leasing arrangements after Boeing and Airbus complied with the restrictions. EU airlines were required to reroute flights around Belarusian airspace, lengthening journeys between Western Europe and Asia.

Enforcement has been uneven. The Commission's 2023 review of sanctions implementation flagged persistent circumvention through third countries — particularly via Russia, the UAE and parts of Central Asia — and pushed member states to criminalise sanctions evasion, which led to Directive 2024/1226 making serious breaches of EU restrictive measures an EU-level crime. Lithuania, Latvia and Poland — the frontline states — have lobbied hardest for tighter measures, while landlocked traders in Hungary and Slovakia raised concerns about lost markets.

Listed individuals have challenged designations before the General Court of the EU with limited success; the Court has generally upheld the Council's broad discretion in foreign policy listings, while occasionally annulling specific designations for inadequate evidence.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe sanction Belarus over a Ryanair flight?

On 23 May 2021, Belarusian authorities used a false bomb threat and a fighter jet escort to force a Ryanair flight between two EU capitals — Athens and Vilnius — to land in Minsk, then arrested journalist Raman Pratasevich on board. EU leaders treated this as an attack on civil aviation and on the bloc itself, and within a month adopted the first economic sanctions targeting Belarusian potash, oil products and the aviation sector.

### Are sanctions on Belarus the same as sanctions on Russia?

They are separate legal regimes but have been deliberately aligned since Belarus allowed Russian troops to invade Ukraine from its territory in February 2022. The EU has progressively closed gaps between the two — for example, after banning iron and steel imports from Russia, Brussels extended the ban to Belarus to prevent rerouting. Some measures still differ: the oil price cap, for instance, applies to Russia.

### Did Belarus actually feel the impact?

Yes, particularly through the loss of potash exports and access to Western capital markets. The IMF and World Bank documented contraction in Belarusian GDP and a sharp depreciation of the rouble in 2022, though Russian financial support and rerouted trade through Russia have cushioned the blow. Belarus also defaulted on foreign-currency Eurobonds in 2022.

### Can Belarusian citizens still travel to the EU?

Ordinary Belarusians can still apply for Schengen visas, but the EU suspended its visa facilitation agreement with Belarus in 2021, meaning higher fees and longer processing for most travellers. Listed individuals — senior officials, judges, propagandists and businesspeople — are banned from entry, and Belarusian-registered cars were barred from entering the EU under 2023 measures aligned with the Russia regime.
