## What is it?

The Single Resolution Mechanism Regulation (SRMR) centralises the management of failing banks in the eurozone. It created the Single Resolution Board (SRB), an EU agency with direct authority to decide whether and how to resolve a bank — meaning wind it down, sell it, or restructure it — without a taxpayer bailout. It also established the Single Resolution Fund (SRF), built up from bank contributions, to provide temporary financial support during resolution.

The SRMR is the second pillar of the European Banking Union, following the Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM) that placed the European Central Bank in charge of supervising the largest banks. It applies to all eurozone countries and non-euro EU members that choose to join.

## Why was it introduced?

The SRMR was born from the brutal lesson of Ireland in 2010. When the Irish government guaranteed the liabilities of its six largest banks in September 2008, it did so believing the total exposure was manageable. By November 2010, the cost had reached €46 billion, according to the European Commission's 2011 impact assessment on crisis management. The Irish state had effectively become the banks — sovereign debt skyrocketed, and Ireland needed an EU-IMF bailout. This wasn't unique: according to the Commission's 2012 "Blueprint for a Banking Union", EU member states had injected €1.6 trillion of public money into banks between 2008 and 2011. The structural tension was clear: banks were supervised nationally but operated cross-border, and when they failed, the host country's taxpayers paid.

The political coalition that drove the SRMR was a Franco-German axis, but for different reasons. France wanted a eurozone-wide deposit guarantee scheme and resolution fund to stabilise the single currency; Germany insisted on strict "bail-in" rules to force creditors to pay first, as Chancellor Angela Merkel told the Bundestag in June 2012: "We cannot have a situation where the taxpayer is always the last resort." The European Commission, under President José Manuel Barroso, proposed the SRMR on 10 July 2013 as part of a package that also included the Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD). The two texts were designed together: the BRRD harmonised national resolution rules for all EU banks, while the SRMR centralised decision-making for eurozone banks.

The timing was everything. In the summer of 2012, the eurozone crisis was at its peak. Bank runs in Greece, Spain, and Italy had exposed the deadly feedback loop between weak banks and weak sovereigns. Mario Draghi, President of the European Central Bank, made his famous "whatever it takes" speech on 26 July 2012, but he also made clear that ECB support depended on a proper banking union. The June 2012 European Council summit had already committed to breaking "the vicious circle between banks and sovereigns", according to the summit conclusions. Without the existential threat of eurozone breakup, the SRMR would never have passed.

What had changed from earlier attempts? In 2010, the Commission had proposed crisis management rules that left resolution powers at national level. But the handling of Spanish bank Bankia in 2012 — where the Spanish government needed €41.3 billion in EU bailout funds, according to the European Stability Mechanism — showed that national authorities would always put their own taxpayers on the hook first. The SRMR's central innovation was the Single Resolution Board, an independent agency with binding powers over national resolution authorities. This was deeply contested: the UK, outside the eurozone, led opposition to granting an EU agency such power over national budgets; the European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Elisa Ferreira (S&D, Portugal), insisted on political accountability mechanisms. The final text required the SRB to work with the Commission and Council, but the Commission could only object to SRB decisions on procedural grounds — not overrule them.

The SRF was equally controversial. Germany and Finland resisted a mutualised fund, fearing they would pay for others' banks. A compromise was reached in December 2013: the SRF would be built up over eight years from bank contributions, reaching €55 billion by 2023, and during that period national compartments would gradually mutualise. The Commission's legal service had to confirm that the SRF could be created without a treaty change — it would be based on an intergovernmental agreement among participating states. The European Parliament's vote on 15 April 2014 was 380 in favour, 216 against, with 44 abstentions — a clear majority but reflecting frictions.

## Timeline

- **2008** — Irish government guarantees all bank liabilities; first major bank failure in the eurozone sparks sovereign debt crisis
- **2010** — Greece, Ireland receive first EU-IMF bailouts; Commission begins drafting crisis management rules
- **June 2012** — European Council declares "vicious circle between banks and sovereigns" must be broken
- **July 2013** — Commission proposes SRMR and BRRD as part of Banking Union
- **March 2014** — European Parliament reaches political agreement with Council on SRMR
- **15 April 2014** — European Parliament votes to approve SRMR (380-216-44)
- **15 May 2014** — Council of the EU formally adopts SRMR
- **1 January 2015** — SRMR enters into force; Single Resolution Board (SRB) becomes operational
- **1 January 2016** — SRB assumes full resolution powers for significant banks
- **8 June 2017** — SRB decides first resolution case: Banca Popolare di Vicenza and Veneto Banca (but Italian authorities opt for national insolvency instead)
- **2021** — SRF reaches €42 billion, ahead of target; mutualisation phase completes in 2022

## Impact and consequences

The SRMR fundamentally changed expectations around bank failure. Before 2014, the common assumption was that a large bank would be bailed out by its home country. After the SRMR, creditors and shareholders — not taxpayers — bear losses first. The SRB has published Minimum Requirement for Own Funds and Eligible Liabilities (MREL) targets for each bank, setting out how much bail-in-able debt they must hold. By 2023, according to the SRB's annual report, every eurozone bank had MREL targets published, and total SRF contributions reached €78 billion.

The practical impact has been limited because no major eurozone bank has actually been resolved under the SRMR. The most significant test came in 2023: Credit Suisse was taken over by UBS under Swiss law, not the SRMR, because Switzerland is not in the EU. Within the eurozone, regional banks like Banco Popular (2017) and Sberbank Europe (2022) were resolved, but without triggering bail-in losses for depositors. The Banco Popular sale to Santander for €1 was overseen by the SRB but involved minimal burden-sharing.

Industry response was mixed. Large banks, represented by the European Banking Federation (EBF), warned in a 2014 position paper that strict bail-in rules would raise funding costs and push investors to non-EU banks. The European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) estimated in 2015 that senior bondholders would face risks. In practice, funding costs did not spike dramatically — the SRB's 2021 MREL monitoring report showed that most banks met their targets well ahead of deadlines. However, the failure to resolve a truly systemic bank has left open questions about political credibility. During the 2023 Credit Suisse crisis, the SRB's chair Dominique Laboureix argued publicly that the SRMR would have applied had the bank been in the eurozone, but the ECB and Commission accepted that resolution under EU rules would have been too complex for a global systemically important bank.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take five years after the 2008 crisis to set up a system for handling failing banks in Europe?

The delay reflected deep sovereignty concerns. Germany's Constitutional Court had ruled in 2009 that EU institutions could not take fiscal decisions binding on the Bundestag. Creating a resolution authority required the UK and other non-euro states to opt out via enhanced cooperation. The political will only crystallised in 2012 when eurozone breakup seemed real — "whatever it takes" changed the calculus.

### How is the European bank bailout fund paid for?

Banks pay annual contributions calculated on their total liabilities minus own funds, adjusted for risk profile. The target is 1% of covered deposits in the eurozone (about €55-60 billion). According to the SRB's 2022 annual report, the fund reached €66 billion by year-end 2022, exceeding the target. During the eight-year build-up, national compartments existed; these were fully mutualised by 2022.

### Why hasn't the EU's system for shutting down failing banks ever been used for a major bank?

The design assumes a clean resolution: a bank with insufficient capital but viable underlying business. Complex global banks like Credit Suisse (Swiss, not EU) or Deutsche Bank (German, but never close to failure) fall outside this. Political pressure to protect regional depositors also led to national insolvency proceedings instead of resolution in cases like Veneto Banca (2017) and Monte dei Paschi di Siena (2017). The SRB has argued that its framework works for mid-sized banks but needs reinforcement for global systemically important banks.
