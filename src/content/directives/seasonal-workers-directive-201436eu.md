## What is it?

The Seasonal Workers Directive (2014/36/EU) establishes a single EU-wide procedure for admitting non-EU nationals to work as seasonal workers – primarily in agriculture, horticulture, and tourism – for up to nine months per year. It grants these workers a minimum set of rights: a written contract specifying pay and hours, accommodation that meets national health and safety standards, equal treatment with nationals regarding terms of employment (including minimum wage if applicable), and the right to join a trade union. The directive also requires employers to provide evidence of adequate housing and to pay for travel costs if the worker is dismissed before the contract ends.

A key innovation is the “employer change” mechanism: if a seasonal worker faces exploitation – unpaid wages, unsafe conditions, or violations – they can switch to another employer during their stay, provided they meet the new employer’s admission criteria. Member states had to transpose the directive into national law by 30 September 2016.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to a series of scandals that exposed systemic exploitation of seasonal migrant workers across Europe. In 2010, the European Union Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA) published a report documenting “serious and widespread abuse” of seasonal workers in Italy, Spain, Greece, and the Netherlands – including wage theft, dangerous housing, and physical violence. That same year, the Rosarno riots in Calabria erupted when African citrus workers protested after being shot at by local organised crime groups; Italian authorities later confirmed that the workers were housed in squalid, abandoned buildings with no running water. The European Parliament held a hearing in 2011 during which MEPs cited cases of workers forced to sleep in fields and paid as little as €2 per hour.

The political trigger came in 2011 when a fire in a makeshift dormitory in Apulia killed five Romanian berry pickers, and FRA research showed that over 70% of seasonal workers in some Italian regions had no written contract. The European Commission, led by Home Affairs Commissioner Cecilia Malmström, argued that existing rules – the 2009 Employers Sanctions Directive (2009/52/EC) – targeted employers of irregular migrants but did nothing for legal seasonal workers who were legally present yet still exploited.

The directive was part of the 2009 Stockholm Programme’s push for “fair and efficient” migration management. Earlier attempts at a single work permit directive had stalled because Northern and Eastern Member States feared losing control over their bilateral seasonal worker schemes. The European Commission split the proposal in 2010: a general “Single Permit Directive” (2011/98/EU) for most labour migrants, and a separate seasonal workers directive tailored to short-term, low-skilled employment. The European Parliament and trade unions, led by the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC), fought for stronger protections – notably the right to change employer without penalty – which national governments initially resisted.

The Council adopted the directive in February 2014 with a qualified majority. Germany, Austria, and Poland voted against, arguing that the nine-month maximum and the employer-change provision would undermine their existing bilateral agreements with Balkan countries. Hungary and the Netherlands abstained. In the final text, the employer-change mechanism was watered down: the worker must inform authorities and prove the new position meets admission criteria, a concession to member states that wanted to avoid a “free market” in seasonal labour.

## Timeline

- **2008** — European Commission’s “Policy Plan on Legal Migration” proposes a single work permit directive; member states deadlock over seasonal workers.
- **2009** — Employers Sanctions Directive adopted, penalising employers of irregular migrants but leaving legal seasonal workers unprotected.
- **2010** — The Stockholm Programme calls for sector-specific legal migration streams. FRA report on severe exploitation of seasonal workers published.
- **2011** — Rosarno riots in Italy; fire kills five Romanian workers in Apulia; European Parliament pushes for protective legislation. Commission presents separate Seasonal Workers Directive proposal (July).
- **2013** — European Parliament adopts its first reading position, supported by S&D, Greens, and ALDE groups. Council working groups narrow protections. Trilogue negotiations.
- **February 2014** — Council adopts directive (QMV; Germany, Poland, Austria against, Netherlands abstains). European Parliament approves in March.
- **May 2014** — Directive enters into force.
- **30 September 2016** — Deadline for member state transposition.
- **2018–2019** — Commission infringement proceedings against 12 member states for late or incomplete transposition (including Belgium, Italy, Spain).
- **2021** — European Commission evaluation finds “limited impact” due to low uptake; many countries continue using national schemes rather than the EU route.
- **2023** — Revision discussions begin under the Pact on Migration and Asylum; some MEPs call for lowering the nine‑month maximum and strengthening enforcement.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s practical impact has been mixed. By 2021, only 12 member states had fully implemented the EU seasonal worker route; the rest relied on national schemes with fewer protections. The European Commission’s 2021 evaluation noted that fewer than 60,000 third‑country seasonal workers were admitted under the directive in 2019, a fraction of the estimated million seasonal workers in EU agriculture (according to Eurostat 2019 labour force data). Italy, Spain, and France – the largest users of seasonal labour – each continued their own national schemes, claiming the directive’s nine‑month maximum was too short for some crops.

The employer‑change provision, though hailed as a breakthrough, has been rarely used. A 2020 report by the European Migration Network found that only a handful of workers in Finland and Sweden had exercised that right. The reason: workers feared retaliation if they reported exploitation, and many were unaware of the mechanism.

On the positive side, the directive established a baseline: season workers now legally have written contracts, minimum wage parity, and accommodation standards. In 2017, Italy introduced a similar “decreto flussi” system citing the directive, and Spain’s 2019 reform included many of its provisions. The threat of infringement action pushed several member states – including Greece and Portugal – to update their housing regulations.

The most significant effect has been on institutional norms: the directive shifted the political framing from “labour supply” to “worker protection”. EU agencies, such as the European Labour Authority (ELA) founded in 2019, now include seasonal worker exploitation in their joint enforcement actions.

## Key questions answered

### How does EU law protect seasonal farm workers from exploitation?

The Seasonal Workers Directive requires that third-country seasonal workers receive a written contract, accommodation meeting health standards, and equal pay (including the national minimum wage). Employers must prove they can provide these conditions before a worker is admitted. If a worker is exploited – for example, unpaid wages or unsafe housing – they can change employers without losing their visa, preventing them from being tied to an abusive boss. The European Labour Authority coordinates inspections across borders, though enforcement in practice remains patchy.

### Can a seasonal worker in Europe switch to a different employer?

Yes, but with conditions. The directive allows a seasonal worker to change employer if they inform the member state’s migration authorities and the new employer holds a valid admission certificate. This right was fiercely contested during negotiations; Germany and Poland argued it would create a “free‑for‑all”. In practice, very few workers have used it, often because they fear not being re‑hired the following year. The European Commission’s 2021 evaluation recommended making the process easier and better communicated.

### Why did some EU countries oppose the Seasonal Workers Directive?

Germany, Austria, and Poland voted against the directive in the Council because they had existing bilateral seasonal worker agreements with Balkan countries (for example, Germany’s arrangement with Kosovo). These countries argued that the nine‑month time limit and the employer‑change mechanism would disrupt their carefully managed systems, which often gave employers direct control over worker visas. They also worried about costs: the requirement to pay for travel if the worker is dismissed early would increase financial risk for agribusinesses. Despite their opposition, the directive passed because it only needed a qualified majority, and most member states were willing to accept a common framework to improve protections.

### Does the directive apply to all seasonal work in the EU?

No. The directive sets conditions for third‑country (non‑EU) seasonal workers only. Workers from within the EU, including those from member states (such as Romanians or Bulgarians working in Germany), are covered by free movement rules and not by this directive. Additionally, the directive only applies to jobs that are “seasonal” –dependent on the season or touristic demand – with a maximum stay of nine months. It does not cover permanent or year-round jobs, even in agriculture (e.g., greenhouse workers under year-round contracts).
