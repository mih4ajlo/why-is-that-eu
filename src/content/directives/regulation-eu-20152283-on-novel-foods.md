## What is it?

The Novel Foods Regulation (2015/2283/EU) defines a "novel food" as any food not used for human consumption to a significant degree within the European Union before 15 May 1997, when the first novel foods regime entered into force. This includes new sources of food (e.g., insects, algae), foods produced by new technologies (e.g., cell-cultured meat, nanotech-processed ingredients), and traditional foods from third countries (e.g., chia seeds, baobab).

Introduced as a recast of the earlier 1997 regulation, the 2015 overhaul shifted from a decentralised, member-state-driven authorisation process to a single, centralised procedure managed by the European Commission with scientific risk assessment by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA). It also created a simplified notification pathway for "traditional foods from third countries" with a history of safe use, and introduced a Union List of authorised novel foods for clarity and legal certainty.

## Why was it introduced?

The decisive trigger was not a scandal but a grinding frustration with the 1997 regime’s inefficiency—and an unexpected political opening created by the 2012 Horsemeat Scandal. French and German food safety authorities discovered in early 2013 that processed beef products across Europe contained undeclared horsemeat, often sourced through complex supply chains in Romania and the Netherlands. While horsemeat itself is not "novel," the scandal exposed how fragmented national authorisation processes could create loopholes: a product might be rejected as novel in one member state but marketed freely in another if no challenge was made.

The structural tension was clear: the 1997 regulation required any company seeking to market a novel food to apply to a member state, which then triggered a 90-day consultation among all other states. If one state raised objections, the process stalled indefinitely. By 2012, only about 170 novel foods had been authorised in 15 years, compared to hundreds of safety assessments in the United States over the same period. European companies complained of 5- to 8-year timelines for approvals, during which competitors in other markets—notably the US and China—launched products. The European Food and Feed Cultures Association (EFFCA) and the European Natural Soyfood Manufacturers Association (ENSA, now part of the European Plant-Based Foods Association) publicly warned in 2013 that the EU was "losing the innovation race" in plant-based proteins and cultured microorganisms.

The Commission’s then-Health and Consumer Protection Commissioner, John Dalli (2010–2012), had initially proposed a 2011 reform that was watered down after resistance from the European Parliament’s Agriculture Committee, which feared centralisation would undermine national control over traditional foods. But the Horsemeat Scandal of 2013 shifted the balance: the European Parliament’s Environment, Public Health and Food Safety (ENVI) Committee, led by rapporteur James Nicholson (ECR, UK), argued that a centralised system would improve traceability and prevent "forum shopping" by companies. The Council of the European Union, under the Irish Presidency in 2013, backed the reform after Ireland’s food export industry—a major producer of novel dairy ingredients like lactoferrin—lobbied for faster approvals.

Key opponents included the European Traditional Foods Association (ETFA), which feared small producers of insect-based or fermented products would be crushed by a high-cost, single-authorisation process. The French and Italian governments, joined by Austria and Greece, argued for a "positive list" of traditional foods exempt from the process, but lost the vote in the Agriculture Council in early 2014. The final regulation passed the Council on 11 November 2015 with 27 member states in favour; the Maltese delegation abstained, citing concerns over the impact on small-scale producers.

Timing was also shaped by the 2014–2019 Juncker Commission’s emphasis on "Better Regulation" and internal market completion. Commission Vice-President Jyrki Katainen, responsible for Jobs, Growth, Investment and Competitiveness, cited the novel foods reform as a concrete example of cutting red tape for business. The Commission’s 2013 impact assessment estimated that the old system cost EU food companies €50–100 million annually in lost market opportunities, and calculated that harmonisation could reduce approval times from 3–5 years to 18 months for standard novel foods.

## Timeline

- **15 May 1997** — Regulation (EC) No 258/97 enters into force, establishing the first EU novel foods framework and defining the 1997 cut-off date.
- **2008–2012** — Only 22 novel foods are authorised; industry frustration mounts. The Commission launches a public consultation in 2010, receiving 120 responses, most calling for reform.
- **2 July 2012** — John Dalli resigns as Commissioner after a lobbying scandal involving the tobacco industry. His replacement, Tonio Borg (2012–2014), prioritises food safety reform.
- **2013** — The Horsemeat Scandal exposes weaknesses in the traceability of food supply chains. The European Parliament’s ENVI Committee accelerates work on a recast.
- **18 December 2013** — The Commission publishes its formal proposal for a recast of the Novel Foods Regulation (COM/2013/0894 final).
- **11 November 2015** — The Council of the EU adopts the final text; 27 member states vote in favour, Malta abstains. The European Parliament had voted in favour on 28 October 2015.
- **1 January 2018** — Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 applies fully, replacing the 1997 regime. The Union List of authorised novel foods is published.
- **2019** — The first novel foods authorised under the new system include yacon syrup (a prebiotic from South America) and lycopene from *Blakeslea trispora*.
- **2021** — Two insect species—migratory locust (*Locusta migratoria*) and yellow mealworm (*Tenebrio molitor*)—receive novel food authorisation after years of delay under the old regime.
- **2023** — EFSA publishes 15 novel food safety assessments, the highest single-year volume to date.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation’s most immediate consequence was a surge in applications. By 2023, the Commission reported over 80 applications filed, compared to 170 authorisations over the entire 20-year lifespan of the 1997 regulation. Approval times for standard novel foods fell to an average of 18–24 months, though insect-based and cell-cultured products still face delays of up to 4 years due to EFSA’s evolving risk assessment methodology.

Industry response has been mixed. Large food multinationals—such as Nestlé, Unilever, and Danone—welcomed the single-authorisation pathway, with Danone reporting in 2022 that it saved an estimated €2 million in legal and administrative costs for its plant-based protein submissions. Small producers, however, complained that EFSA’s safety dossier requirements for novel foods cost €50,000–€200,000 per application, a barrier the European Food and Farming Partnerships (EFFP) called "prohibitive" for startups. The EU’s 2020 Farm to Fork Strategy acknowledged this, pledging to explore simplified procedures for "small- and medium-sized enterprises."

Consumer acceptance remains uneven. A 2021 Eurobarometer survey found 72% of EU citizens had "no objection" to eating insect-based foods if authorised, but only 18% said they would actively buy them. The first insect-based products—mealworm pasta, cricket protein bars—launched in Belgium, the Netherlands, and France in 2022, with supermarkets reporting "moderate" sales.

Regulatory spillover effects include pressure on EFSA to update its 1990s-era guidance on nanoparticulate novel foods, which the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) has criticised for not covering "novel risks" like intestinal absorption of engineered particles. The regulation also set a precedent for the EU’s 2023 proposal for cell-cultured meat guidelines, which the Commission intends to handle under the novel foods framework rather than as a separate category.

## Key questions answered

### Why is the cut-off date 15 May 1997?

The date corresponds to the entry into force of the first Novel Foods Regulation (258/97). Any food not consumed "to a significant degree" in the EU before that date is considered novel and requires pre-market authorisation. This created an arbitrary but legally clear boundary that has frustrated producers of traditional foods from outside Europe (e.g., chia seeds, baobab fruit), who must prove pre-1997 consumption levels despite centuries of safe use elsewhere.

### Why did the Horsemeat Scandal unlock reform?

The 2013 scandal exposed not only fraud but the system’s inability to enforce consistent food safety standards across borders. National objections in the old regime could halt a product in one country while it circulated freely in another—exactly what happened with Romanian-sourced horsemeat. The European Parliament used this failure to argue that a centralised system was essential for traceability, winning support from member states that had previously defended national control.

### Why are insect foods treated as novel while some fermented algae are not?

The pre-1997 consumption test applies only to *food consumption*, not to feed or non-food uses. Spirulina (a cyanobacterium) had been sold in health-food shops in several EU countries before 1997, so it is not novel. Insects, however, were not part of traditional European diets (except in a few local customs in Belgium and France) and require authorisation under the regulation unless a producer can prove significant pre-1997 consumption. This discrepancy has led to legal challenges, including a 2021 complaint by the International Platform of Insects for Food and Feed (IPIFF) to the European Ombudsman.

### Why did the French government oppose the centralised system?

France, through its Directorate-General for Food (DGAL), argued that national authorities should retain the right to approve or reject novel foods based on local dietary habits and production traditions. French producers of insects for animal feed, led by the association Insectes & Alimentation, feared that a one-size-fits-all EU authorisation would ignore regional food cultures and impose costs that small French insect farms could not bear. In the final vote, France voted in favour after securing the "traditional foods from third countries" notification pathway as a compromise.
