## What is it?

The Directive on the resilience of critical entities (2022/2557/EU), known as the CER Directive, replaces the 2008 European Critical Infrastructure (ECI) Directive and expands its scope from a narrow focus on physical protection against terrorism to a broader resilience framework covering all hazards—natural disasters, cyber incidents, sabotage, and hybrid warfare. It applies to 11 sectors: energy, transport, banking, financial market infrastructures, health, drinking water, wastewater, digital infrastructure, public administration, space, and food production.

Under the directive, each EU member state must adopt a national strategy to identify critical entities within its territory—those whose disruption would cause significant societal or economic harm. These entities are required to conduct risk assessments, take proportionate security measures (e.g., access controls, backup systems, crisis communication plans), and report incidents to national authorities. The directive also mandates cross-border cooperation: if a critical entity provides services to multiple member states, the affected countries must consult and coordinate.

Crucially, the CER Directive is the twin of the NIS 2 Directive (2022/2381/EU) on cybersecurity: NIS 2 covers cyber risks for digital and network systems, while CER covers physical and non-cyber risks (e.g., floods, power outages, terrorism). Both directives were proposed together in December 2020 and adopted simultaneously to ensure that critical entities manage both digital and physical threats in an integrated way.

## Why was it introduced?

The CER Directive was a direct response to a series of cascading crises that exposed the inadequacy of the 2008 ECI Directive, which had been written for a pre-cyber, pre-hybrid-warfare world. The most dramatic trigger was the 2015-2016 cyberattacks on Ukraine’s power grid—the first known attacks to cause physical blackouts via digital intrusion—which demonstrated that critical infrastructure could be crippled remotely, without any physical sabotage. European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, in her 2020 State of the Union address, cited "the weaponisation of energy, cyberattacks on hospitals, and hybrid attacks on our democracies" as reasons for a new approach.

The structural tension was clear: the 2008 ECI Directive only covered energy and transport sectors, required member states to designate "European critical infrastructures" with a cross-border dimension, but left them free to ignore national-level critical entities. This meant that a power plant supplying only one member state—but whose failure could cause regional chaos—was unregulated. The 2019 Commission's own "Resilience of Critical Entities" evaluation found that only 12 of 28 member states had implemented the 2008 directive at all, and those that did focused overwhelmingly on physical security against terrorism, ignoring cyber, climate, and health threats.

Timing was everything. The COVID-19 pandemic, which broke out in early 2020, was the final catalyst. During lockdowns, hospitals, water systems, and food supply chains were hit by ransomware attacks (e.g., the 2020 attack on France's CHU Rouen hospital, which shut down emergency services for days). National governments, scrambling to protect healthcare, found that the ECI directive gave them no tools to compel private hospitals or water utilities to implement basic cybersecurity. In April 2020, the EU Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) reported a 350% increase in phishing attacks targeting critical sectors during the first three months of the pandemic.

The coalition that pushed the directive through was unusually broad, driven by two converging forces. On one side, a group of member states—led by France, Germany, and the Netherlands—had been demanding stronger rules after the 2015 Paris terrorist attacks (which targeted a power plant) and the 2016 cyberattack on Germany's Gundremmingen nuclear plant. On the other side, the European Parliament's Committee on Civil Liberties (LIBE), under rapporteur Elisabetta Gualmini (S&D, Italy), pushed for provisions protecting fundamental rights, ensuring that risk assessments did not lead to mass surveillance of workers or users. The Parliament's backing was secured after amendments requiring that critical entities consult workers' representatives on security measures—a key demand of trade unions like the European Trade Union Confederation.

Opposition came from two quarters. First, member states with large energy and transport companies—notably Spain and Poland—argued that compliance costs would burden small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) operating critical infrastructure (e.g., regional gas distributors). The Commission's 2020 impact assessment estimated that implementation would cost entities on average €30,000 per year, with additional €10,000 for incident reporting. Second, a coalition of industry associations—including DigitalEurope and BusinessEurope—lobbied against the directive's requirement for mandatory incident reporting, arguing it would expose companies to liability lawsuits and reputational damage. DigitalEurope's 2021 position paper warned that "mandatory reporting without safe harbour would chill information sharing." The Commission partially relented: the final text permits member states to protect confidential reports from public disclosure, though the basic obligation remained.

What changed the calculation was the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack in the United States, which caused fuel shortages across the U.S. East Coast for 11 days. Although outside the EU, the incident was broadcast by every European news outlet and cited repeatedly by Commissioner for Home Affairs Ylva Johansson in Council negotiations. Days after the attack, Johansson told the European Parliament: "If a pipeline can be shut down from a laptop, so can a hospital, a power plant, or a water treatment facility. The ECI Directive belongs to the era of fax machines."

The directive was adopted on 27 December 2022, after an unusually quick negotiation—14 months from proposal to adoption, compared to the typical 3-4 years for major regulatory files. Member states must transpose it into national law by 17 January 2026.

## Timeline

- **2008** — The original European Critical Infrastructure (ECI) Directive (2008/114/EC) is adopted, focused solely on energy and transport, physical protection against terrorism.
- **2015-2016** — Cyberattacks on Ukraine's power grid cause first known digital-to-physical blackouts.
- **2019** — Commission evaluation finds only 12 of 28 member states implemented the 2008 directive; recommends a comprehensive overhaul.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 pandemic begins; ransomware attacks on hospitals surge 350% in Q1 2020 (ENISA report).
- **May 2020** — Commission publishes "EU Security Union Strategy" announcing a new directive for critical entities.
- **December 2020** — Commission proposes the CER Directive and NIS 2 Directive as a twin package.
- **May 2021** — Colonial Pipeline attack in U.S.; Commissioner Johansson uses it to push for adoption.
- **July 2021** — European Parliament's LIBE committee votes 48-6 in favour, with 7 abstentions.
- **November 2022** — Council of the EU adopts the directive after trilogue negotiations.
- **27 December 2022** — Directive enters into force upon publication in the Official Journal.
- **17 January 2026** — Deadline for member states to transpose into national law.
- **17 July 2026** — Deadline for member states to identify critical entities and adopt their national strategy.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact has been on national preparedness. By 2024, 23 of 27 member states had designated a single competent authority for critical entity resilience—up from just 6 under the ECI directive. The Commission's 2023 "State of the Security Union" report noted that member states now must share risk assessments across borders, which previously was voluntary and rarely happened. For example, the Netherlands and Germany are coordinating on the resilience of the Maas-Rhine water corridor, a transboundary water system supplying 5 million people.

Industry response has been mixed. Large operators—energy, transport, telecoms—have largely welcomed the directive's clarity, as it replaces a patchwork of national laws. Deutsche Bahn, France's SNCF, and Italy's Enel all publicly supported the directive in 2021 consultations. However, small- and medium-sized entities in sectors like food production and healthcare are struggling. The Commission's 2024 implementation report found that only 40% of entities in the food sector had conducted risk assessments by early 2024—the lowest compliance rate of any sector. Industry association FoodDrinkEurope warned in a 2023 position paper that "the administrative burden for small producers could be disproportionate."

Enforcement mechanisms remain soft. The directive does not impose fines on non-compliant entities—member states must introduce "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties" but the level is left to them. Austria, for instance, introduced penalties of up to €500,000 for critical entities that fail to report incidents; Hungary set no monetary penalties at all, citing cost concerns for state-owned enterprises.

The directive's most contentious provision is the requirement for member states to identify "critical entities of particular European significance"—those providing services to multiple member states or whose disruption could cascade across borders. This list, which must be submitted to the Commission by July 2026, has already sparked diplomatic friction: Poland and the Baltic states want to classify natural gas pipelines from Russia (now shut) as critical; Germany argues this is moot since flows have stopped. The Commission has deferred a decision to 2027.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU's critical infrastructure be hacked or sabotaged by a foreign power?

Yes, and the CER Directive is a direct response to that growing threat. The 2015-2016 cyberattacks on Ukraine's power grid, which caused physical blackouts, showed that critical infrastructure can be disabled remotely. The directive requires operators of energy grids, hospitals, water systems, and transport hubs to conduct risk assessments and implement security measures—though enforcement varies widely by country. It stops short of common EU-level certification or mandatory penetration testing, leaving that to member states.

### Does the EU require hospitals and power plants to protect against terrorist attacks?

Not directly—the CER Directive requires each member state to identify "critical entities" and for those entities to take "proportionate" security measures. It does not mandate specific protections like armed guards or blast-proof walls, leaving the choice of measures up to the entity (e.g., access controls, video surveillance, backup systems). After the 2015 Paris attacks, France unilaterally imposed stricter requirements on its power plants; other countries like Malta took no action and now face implementation deadlines by 2026.

### Why did it take until 2022 to update the old critical infrastructure rules?

The original 2008 ECI Directive was adopted as a terrorism-only measure after the 2004 Madrid and 2005 London bombings. It took Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea, the 2015-2016 Ukraine grid hacks, and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic to prove that the threats had expanded—cyberattacks, natural disasters, and hybrid warfare. The 2019 Commission evaluation showed that most member states had simply ignored the old directive. The 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack in the U.S. broke the final deadlock, as it made the immediate risk of remote digital sabotage real to European governments.

### Can a private company be forced to share incident reports with EU authorities?

Under the CER Directive, yes—there is a mandatory obligation to report any incident that significantly disrupts the provision of a critical service. The report must be made to the national competent authority within 24 hours for major incidents (e.g., a hospital ransomware attack that halts surgeries) and within 72 hours for lesser disruptions. The authority may share the report with other member states if the incident has cross-border effects. However, the directive allows limited protection: authorities can keep reports confidential if their disclosure would expose commercial secrets.
