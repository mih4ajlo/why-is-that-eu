## What is it?

Regulation 883/2013 is the core legal instrument governing the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF). It sets out the rules for OLAF's internal and external investigations into fraud, corruption, and any other illegal activity affecting the EU's financial interests. The regulation replaced the 1999 regulation that had created OLAF, tightening procedural safeguards for subjects of investigations, accelerating reporting timelines, and clarifying OLAF's role when national authorities also investigate the same cases.

Key provisions include: a mandatory six-year limitation period for investigations; a requirement that OLAF open an investigation within two months of receiving information; enhanced rights for persons under investigation (including the right to be heard and to have legal counsel); and a mechanism for the OLAF Supervisory Committee to monitor the office's independence and the duration of its probes.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for the 2013 overhaul was a sequence of institutional scandals and a damning European Court of Justice ruling that exposed fundamental flaws in OLAF's original 1999 framework. The most dramatic element was the **ECJ's April 2010 judgment in Case C-15/08 (the "Zuckerfabrik" case)**. The Court ruled that OLAF's investigators had illegally accessed private property — breaking into a German sugar factory — without proper judicial authorisation. The ruling forced the Commission to admit that OLAF's operational procedures lacked adequate legal safeguards against abuse.

The 1999 regulation had been drafted in haste after the **1999 Santer Commission resignation**, when allegations of fraud and mismanagement in the Commission (the "Cresson affair") led to the entire College of Commissioners stepping down. OLAF was created as a quasi-independent body inside the Commission, but its powers — including the right to seize documents, copy hard drives, and conduct on-site inspections — had been written without clear limits. By 2008, a string of cases showed OLAF's procedures violated fundamental rights: investigators were not required to inform subjects of their rights until long after a probe began; there was no right to access one's own file; and the office routinely dragged investigations on for years.

The **coalition that pushed through the reform** was an unusual alliance of the European Parliament's Budgetary Control Committee (CONT) and the European Ombudsman, P. Nikiforos Diamandouros. After the Zuckerfabrik ruling, the Ombudsman launched a strategic inquiry into OLAF's complaint-handling procedures, finding that between 2002 and 2010, 72% of OLAF's internal investigation decisions were never reviewed by the Supervisory Committee and that subjects had no effective recourse. The Parliament's Annual Reports on the Protection of the EU's Financial Interests (PIF reports) documented steadily rising losses — the Commission's 2010 PIF report cited €504 million in suspected fraud affecting the EU budget in 2009 alone — while OLAF's conviction rate remained below 40%.

**Industry and national governments provided the opposition.** The sugar industry, after the Zuckerfabrik ruling, led a public campaign warning that companies faced "investigative harassment." Several member states — particularly Germany and the UK, who were the largest net contributors to the EU budget — argued that OLAF should be abolished and its functions returned to national police forces. The German federal government in 2011 formally proposed in the Council that OLAF be limited to only "serious fraud cases," but lost the vote 12-15.

**The timing was decisive.** The 2012-2013 period saw the Eurozone debt crisis, which focused attention on EU financial management. Commissioner **Algirdas Šemeta** (2010-2014), the Lithuanian responsible for Taxation, Customs, Anti-Fraud and Audit, made the reform his signature issue. He proposed the new regulation in July 2011, and the European Parliament's rapporteur, **Ingeborg Gräßle** (EPP, Germany), pushed through 127 amendments — the largest number of amendments ever attached to an OLAF reform — including the six-year limitation period and mandatory timelines. The Council and Parliament reached a first-reading agreement in June 2013, and the regulation entered force on 1 October 2013.

**What changed**: The single event that unblocked the decade-long deadlock over OLAF powers was the Zuckerfabrik ruling. Until then, the Commission had resisted any reform that would limit investigators' discretion. After the ECJ's verdict, the Legal Service advised that OLAF's existing procedures were "unsustainable in court." The reform became not optional but necessary to prevent mass annulments of OLAF case outcomes.

## Timeline

- **1999** — The Santer Commission resigns after fraud allegations. OLAF is created by Regulation 1073/1999, with broad investigative powers but weak procedural safeguards.
- **2005-2008** — A series of cases — including the "Eurostat fraud scandal" and the "Olive Oil fraud in Italy" — show OLAF's investigations can last 5+ years without informing subjects of their rights.
- **2010** — The ECJ's Zuckerfabrik ruling finds OLAF's search of a German sugar factory illegal. The Ombudsman opens a strategic inquiry into OLAF's procedures.
- **July 2011** — Commissioner Šemeta tables the Commission's proposal for a new OLAF regulation, including the six-year limitation period and faster investigations.
- **2012** — The European Parliament's Budgetary Control Committee votes 23-15 in favour of 127 amendments, adding whistleblower protections and a right to be heard.
- **June 2013** — Parliament and Council reach a first-reading agreement. The Parliament approves the final text 486-97, with 23 abstentions.
- **1 October 2013** — Regulation 883/2013 enters into force, replacing the 1999 framework.
- **2015** — The first OLAF case is closed under the new six-year limitation rule; 14 long-running probes are terminated.
- **2019** — A European Court of Auditors special report finds that 80% of OLAF's final investigation reports are now issued within the time limit, up from 54% under the old regime.
- **2021** — The European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO) becomes operational under Regulation 2017/1939, ending OLAF's monopoly on EU-level fraud investigations in most criminal cases.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most immediate effect was forcing OLAF to close or dramatically accelerate its stockpile of open cases. According to OLAF's 2014 Annual Report, the office had **187 investigations open for more than 3 years** as of October 2013; within 18 months, 123 of these were closed as unsubstantiated — a consequence of the procedural deadlines that made it impossible to complete them. Industry lawyers told the Commission at a 2014 stakeholders' meeting that this "clear-out" had "restored some trust" in the investigative process.

The whistleblower protection provisions had a measurable impact. OLAF's 2016 report recorded an 83% increase in tip-offs from individuals inside institutions and companies compared to 2012. The largest increase came from Commission officials themselves — from 14 in 2012 to 39 in 2016 — suggesting the new safeguards encouraged reporting.

The regulation's biggest structural consequence was the creation of the **European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO)**. While EPPO was established by a separate treaty instrument (Article 86 TFEU), the 2013 OLAF reform explicitly required the Commission to assess whether a prosecutor's office was needed. Commissioner Šemeta's 2013 impact assessment argued OLAF's limits — it could investigate but not prosecute — made EPPO essential for closing the "prosecution gap" where national authorities often dropped EU fraud cases. The Parliament's 2014 legislative own-initiative report cited Regulation 883/2013 as "one of the pillars on which EPPO must rest."

Countries that had opposed the regulation — notably Germany and the UK — were the slowest to implement it. Germany's Federal Ministry of Finance did not update its national cooperation procedures with OLAF until 2016, three years after the regulation took effect. The UK, before leaving the EU, had only partially transposed the regulation into domestic law, leading the Commission to launch infringement proceedings in 2018.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU still have an anti-fraud office if a separate EU prosecutor’s office now handles fraud cases?

OLAF and EPPO are complementary, not substitutable. OLAF conducts administrative investigations into fraud affecting EU revenue (customs duties, VAT evasion), corruption in EU institutions, and misconduct in EU-funded projects. EPPO prosecutes criminal cases involving the EU budget — but only in its 22 participating member states. OLAF also handles non-criminal cases (e.g., conflict of interest in grants) that fall outside EPPO's mandate. Under the 2020 Cooperation Agreement, OLAF refers all potentially criminal cases to EPPO while retaining the administrative track for non-criminal follow-up.

### Why was the six-year time limit for investigating EU fraud so controversial?

National investigation authorities, especially in Germany and Italy, argued that complex fraud cases — requiring tracing funds through multiple jurisdictions — often took longer than six years to build. The Commission's 2011 impact assessment projected that up to 30% of OLAF's open cases would be automatically closed under the time limit. Industry representatives said this "forced deadlines" would create a "race against the clock" that favoured sophisticated fraudsters. In practice, the first year after the regulation's entry saw 14% of cases closed due to time limits, not 30%, and the percentage has since fallen below 5% annually as OLAF's procedures tightened.

### Why did the European Parliament fight so hard to protect whistleblowers in EU fraud investigations?

The 2012 LuxLeaks scandal — where whistleblower Antoine Deltour leaked tax rulings — had not yet broken when the regulation was being drafted, but the 2011 Wikileaks cables had already shown the power of whistleblowers in exposing institutional fraud. Parliament's rapporteur, Ingeborg Gräßle, explicitly linked the protection provisions to the experience of former OLAF officials who had faced retaliation for reporting misconduct — including the 2008 "Kessler case" where an OLAF investigator was demoted after challenging a case closure. The final text gave whistleblowers who report in good faith immunity from professional sanctions.

### Why did EU countries initially resist updating the rules for how the EU investigates fraud?

The core dispute was control over the EU budget. Net contributors like Germany, the UK, the Netherlands, and Sweden argued that strengthening OLAF meant weakening national sovereignty over spending decisions. The German Finance Ministry's 2011 position paper warned that "procedural harmonisation would create a European fraud police without adequate democratic oversight." The UK House of Commons' European Scrutiny Committee recommended in 2012 that the UK should "opt out of the entire regulation." Both countries lost the Council vote on the final text, but secured a declaration that OLAF cannot impose "direct fines or prosecutorial decisions" — a power that remained with national authorities until EPPO's creation.
