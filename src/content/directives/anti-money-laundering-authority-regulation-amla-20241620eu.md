## What is it?

The Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA) Regulation establishes a new EU agency with direct supervisory powers over the most risk-exposed financial institutions — those operating in multiple member states or whose activities pose particular money-laundering risks. The regulation is the centrepiece of the EU’s 2021 Anti-Money Laundering Package, a wholesale reform of the bloc’s fragmented anti-money laundering (AML) framework.

Under the AMLA regulation, the authority will directly supervise up to 40 of the highest-risk financial entities, with the power to conduct on-site inspections, request information, impose fines of up to 10% of annual turnover, and ban individuals from management functions. For other financial entities, AMLA will oversee national supervisors — issuing binding standards, conducting peer reviews, and intervening when national authorities fail to enforce rules effectively. The regulation also creates a new supervisory college system for cross-border financial groups.

The regulation was adopted alongside of the 6th Anti-Money Laundering Directive (2024/1640) and the recast Single Rulebook Regulation (2024/1639), forming a comprehensive overhaul of EU AML governance that took more than three years to negotiate.

## Why was it introduced?

The AMLA Regulation is a direct institutional response to a decade of spectacular money-laundering scandals that exposed the bankruptcy of a supervisory system dependent on national authorities policing their own banks. The trigger was a cascading series of convictions and court rulings that showed how easily dirty money flowed through European banks with little consequence.

The turning point came in 2018-2019, when revelations surfaced about massive money laundering through Scandinavian bank branches in the Baltics. The so-called "Danske Bank scandal" — in which €200 billion in suspicious transactions flowed through the bank’s Estonian branch between 2007 and 2015, much of it from Russia and former Soviet states — became the emblem of the system's collapse. In Denmark, the bank’s CEO Thomas Borgen resigned in September 2018, and subsequent investigations revealed that Estonian regulators had flagged concerns years earlier but taken no meaningful action. The European Banking Authority (EBA) later found that both Danish and Estonian authorities had breached EU law by failing to cooperate or act.

But Danske was never alone. In 2019, the "FinCEN Files" — leaked Suspicious Activity Reports published by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ) — showed that major EU banks including Deutsche Bank, HSBC, and ING had processed billions in suspicious transactions despite internal warnings. Deutsche Bank alone was implicated in moving an estimated $1.3 trillion in suspicious funds over two decades.

The structural tension at the heart of the old system was a classic home-host supervisory failure: National authorities had no incentive to police banks whose profits came from cross-border operations but whose risks could be exported. The EBA — created in 2010 — had the mandate to coordinate but lacked direct supervisory powers. After the Danske scandal, the European Commission pushed through a 2019 reform giving the EBA stronger tools, including the power to investigate breaches of EU law and ask the European Commission to issue formal opinions. But by 2020, it was clear these were insufficient. The European Parliament's own-initiative report from MEP Emil Radev in 2020 — adopted by 534 votes to 25 — explicitly called for a dedicated AML authority.

The coalition that drove AMLA was unusual. Southern European countries — particularly Italy and Spain — had long suffered from organised crime infiltration of banking systems and pushed for stronger EU-level enforcement. Nordic countries, scalded by Danske and Swedbank scandals, became converts to EU supervision. Germany, with Deutsche Bank permanently under a cloud of money-laundering investigations, was a reluctant supporter. The European Parliament, led by the Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON) under chair Irene Tinagli, was the most aggressive advocate, insisting on direct supervisory powers for the new authority rather than just coordination.

The opposition came primarily from smaller member states — Estonia, Luxembourg, Malta, and Cyprus — who feared losing their competitive advantage as financial centres with lighter oversight. The Estonian government, in particular, lobbied heavily against giving AMLA direct powers over national supervisors, arguing it would infringe on national sovereignty. In the Council, these countries held the line until summer 2022, when a pivotal negotiation session in the Economic and Financial Affairs Council (ECOFIN) broke the deadlock. The compromise: AMLA would supervise only the riskiest entities directly (a list determined by a delegated act), while most banks would remain under national supervision with AMLA oversight.

The final legislation was adopted in the European Parliament on 24 April 2024 with 479 votes in favour, 74 against, and 54 abstentions. The Council adopted it unanimously on 30 May 2024.

## Timeline

- **2008-2015** — Danske Bank’s Estonian branch processes €200 billion in suspicious transactions from former Soviet states. Internal whistleblowers are ignored.
- **2015** — The 4th Anti-Money Laundering Directive (2015/849/EU) enters force, strengthening customer due diligence and beneficial ownership registers, but leaves supervision entirely national.
- **2018** — ICIJ publishes the "Danske Papers" exposing the scale of the scandal. CEO Thomas Borgen resigns in September. The European Commission begins drafting reform proposals.
- **2019** — The EBA issues a binding decision finding that Estonian and Danish authorities breached EU law. The FinCEN Files are published in September.
- **July 2021** — The European Commission, under Commissioner Mairead McGuinness (Financial Services), proposes the AML Package including the AMLA Regulation.
- **June 2022** — ECOFIN Council reaches partial general approach after difficult negotiations. The Parliament’s ECON committee adopts its position in March 2023.
- **December 2023** — Trilogue negotiations conclude after marathon sessions. The main sticking point — which banks AMLA supervises directly — is resolved with a compromise allowing AMLA to supervise banks with activities in at least six member states.
- **24 April 2024** — European Parliament adopts the final text (479 in favour, 74 against, 54 abstentions).
- **30 May 2024** — Council formally adopts the regulation.
- **19 June 2024** — Regulation published in Official Journal (2024/1620/EU).
- **First half 2025** — Selection process for AMLA’s seat (competing bids from Frankfurt, Paris, Rome, Madrid, Vilnius, and others).
- **Mid 2026** — Expected start of direct supervisory operations.
- **2027** — AMLA begins directly supervising the first cohort of high-risk financial entities.

## Impact and consequences

The AMLA Regulation represents the most significant centralisation of financial supervision in the EU since the Banking Union was created in 2014. It directly addresses a systemic vulnerability: 25 of the 29 biggest money-laundering scandals in EU history occurred in the decade before AMLA was created, according to the 2023 Albrecht Report commissioned by the European Parliament.

Industry impact has been substantial and immediate. Even before AMLA’s operational start, major banks have begun restructuring their AML compliance operations. In 2023, Deutsche Bank announced it would increase its AML spending by 30% compared to 2020 levels; HSBC expanded its financial crime compliance team to over 9,000 staff globally. The EU’s banking sector spent an estimated €28 billion on AML compliance in 2022, according to a 2023 Deloitte survey referenced in Commission documents.

The most contested impact concerns smaller member states. Estonia’s financial sector shrank by 18% in terms of non-resident deposits between 2019 and 2023, according to the European Banking Authority, as banks voluntarily shed high-risk clients in anticipation of stricter rules. Luxembourg and Malta have seen similar contractions.

Enforcement consequences have already begun despite AMLA not yet operating. In 2022, the EBA used its post-Danske reform powers to impose a €200 million fine on Danske Bank for AML failings — the largest such penalty in EU history. The new regulation gives AMLA the power to impose fines of up to 10% of annual turnover directly on the entities it supervises, which could mean penalties in the billions for the largest cross-border banks.

Critics have warned of "supervisory arbitrage" — the risk that the most sophisticated money launderers will simply move to the roughly 60,000 smaller financial entities that remain under national supervision. The European Commission’s 2023 impact assessment acknowledged that AMLA’s direct supervision of only 40 entities leaves a "supervisory gap" for mid-tier financial institutions operating in multiple member states but below the six-member threshold.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU countries create a single money-laundering supervisor after years of national control?

Because a cascade of scandals — from Danske Bank’s €200 billion Russian money pipeline to the FinCEN Files showing Deutsche Bank moved $1.3 trillion in suspicious funds — proved national supervisors would not police their own banks. The European Parliament forced the issue after it became clear that the existing patchwork of national authorities let money flow freely across borders with no single agency responsible.

### Which financial institutions will the new EU anti-money laundering watchdog directly oversee, and how many will that cover?

AMLA will directly supervise up to 40 of the highest-risk financial entities — those operating in at least six member states or whose activities pose particular AML risk. These include the largest cross-border banks, investment firms, and crypto-asset service providers. The specific list will be determined by a delegated act in 2025-2026.

### Can the new EU anti-money laundering authority actually fine a bank for money laundering?

Yes. AMLA has the power to impose fines of up to 10% of a supervised entity’s annual turnover, conduct on-site inspections, demand documents and data, and ban individuals from management positions. This is significantly stronger than the previous system where national authorities imposed widely varying penalties — from millions in the UK to effectively nothing in some member states.

### What happens to banks that the new EU watchdog doesn't directly supervise?

They remain under national supervision, but AMLA will oversee and coordinate those national authorities. It can conduct peer reviews, issue binding standards, and intervene if a national supervisor fails to enforce rules effectively. The regulation also creates supervisory colleges for cross-border groups not directly supervised by AMLA.
