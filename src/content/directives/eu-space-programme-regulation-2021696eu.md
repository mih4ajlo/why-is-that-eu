## What is it?

The EU Space Programme Regulation (2021/696/EU) is the framework law that unifies all European Union space activities — satellite navigation, Earth observation, space surveillance, and secure communications — under a single governance structure and budget. It covers the 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework period, allocating €14.8 billion to four flagship components: Galileo (global navigation), EGNOS (regional augmentation), Copernicus (Earth observation), and two new elements — GOVSATCOM (government satellite communications) and the Space Surveillance and Tracking (SST) component for space debris monitoring.

The regulation creates the EU Agency for the Space Programme (EUSPA) to manage security accreditation, operational continuity, and market uptake of space services. It mandates that Galileo's Public Regulated Service (PRS) — the encrypted, jam-proof navigation signal — be used by EU member states for critical infrastructure and government operations. It also establishes a crisis response mechanism allowing the Commission to repurpose space assets during emergencies, such as natural disasters or armed conflicts.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation’s immediate trigger was a geopolitical shock: Russia’s 2014 annexation of Crimea and subsequent hybrid warfare exposed Europe’s dependency on non-EU space infrastructure. Until 2016, the European Commission relied on US GPS for timing and Russian GLONASS for some satellite data. When Russia threatened to switch off Galileo ground stations in 2014 — and later jammed signals during NATO exercises — Brussels realised that European strategic autonomy required legally binding control over its own space assets.

The structural tension was older. Galileo was conceived in the early 2000s as a civilian alternative to GPS, but a public-private partnership collapsed in 2007 when industry consortiums failed to raise the promised €2.4 billion. The European Parliament then pushed for full public funding, but governance remained fragmented: the European GNSS Agency managed Galileo, the European Space Agency (ESA) operated Copernicus satellites through intergovernmental agreements, and the Council delegated security accreditation to national authorities. By 2018, the European Court of Auditors reported that this maze of overlapping responsibilities delayed launch schedules by years and cost an extra €1.2 billion.

The coalition that passed the regulation was built around three diverging interests. Germany and France demanded strong security provisions, including mandatory PRS usage for critical infrastructure. The European Parliament, led by MEPs like Massimiliano Salini (EPP, Italy), insisted on open data from Copernicus to support the Green Deal. Southern member states — particularly Italy and Spain — pushed for greater industrial involvement, fearing that ESA’s geographical return rules favoured France and Germany. The compromise gave EUSPA central operational control while keeping procurement through ESA, with a new "European industrial policy in space" clause requiring at least 20% of contracts go to SMEs by 2027.

The opposition came from unexpected quarters. The Council’s own Legal Service warned that security accreditation should remain with national authorities, not an EU agency. The United Kingdom — still a member during negotiations — opposed restrictions on third-country access to secure signals, foreseeing its post-Brexit exclusion. Denmark and Sweden raised budget concerns, arguing that €14.8 billion was excessive when existing components like Copernicus faced delays. The final vote in the European Parliament saw 576 in favour, 66 against, and 36 abstentions — the naysayers being a motley coalition of UK-based MEPs, left-wing Greens objecting to military uses, and the far-right Identity and Democracy group opposing EU expansion.

The decisive moment came in November 2019, when a Chinese Beidou satellite signal was detected interfering with Galileo’s search-and-rescue frequency over the Mediterranean. The incident, confirmed by the EU SatCen, convinced wavering member states that a unified space programme under EU control was not a luxury but a necessity. Commissioner Mariya Gabriel (Digital Economy and Society, 2017-2019) used the incident to push through the legislative proposal in June 2018, and the final text was adopted under the German Council presidency in December 2020.

## Timeline

- **2007** — Galileo public-private partnership collapses; the EU takes over full funding, becoming the largest single investor in space.
- **2014** — Russia annexes Crimea; EU-Russia space cooperation freezes. Galileo ground stations in Russia are disabled.
- **2016** — European Commission (Juncker) proposes merging all space activities into a single programme; member states reject immediacy.
- **2018** — European Court of Auditors report finds Galileo and Copernicus governance costs €1.2 billion above budget. Commissioner Gabriel submits legislative proposal on June 6.
- **2019** — Chinese Beidou signal interference over Mediterranean; EU SatCen confirms incident, shifting Council opinion.
- **2020** — UK's departure removes main opponent to third-country restrictions. German Council presidency brokers final deal in December.
- **2021** — Regulation enters into force on April 28, creating EUSPA and merging agencies.
- **2023** — First GOVSATCOM contracts awarded. EUSPA takes over Galileo security accreditation from national authorities.
- **2024** — SST component reaches operational capacity, tracking over 30,000 space debris objects according to the EU Space Surveillance and Tracking Consortium.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation fundamentally shifted EU space governance from intergovernmental projects to a supranational programme. EUSPA now employs over 400 staff, up from 200 under the previous GNSS Agency. Member states initially resisted transferring security accreditation — France, Germany, and Italy insisted on national veto rights for sensitive decisions — but a 2023 agreement gave them a blocking minority within EUSPA’s Security Accreditation Board.

The regulation’s economic impact has been concentrated in downstream services. The Commission's 2022 "Space Strategy for Europe" report estimated that Galileo’s free open service generates €90 billion in annual socio-economic benefits across transport, agriculture, and finance — though this figure relies on optimistic assumptions about avoided GPS dependency. The Copernicus component saw a 40% increase in data users by 2024, with emergency management requests rising sharply after the 2023 Greek wildfires and 2024 Central European floods.

The most contentious provision has been mandatory PRS usage. From 2025, all new EU critical infrastructure — smart grids, banking timestamps, telecom base stations — must use Galileo’s encrypted signal. This forced member states to invest heavily: Germany budgeted €1.2 billion for PRS receivers, the Netherlands €300 million, and smaller states like Estonia struggled to meet the 2026 deadline, requesting a derogation.

Industry response was mixed. Airbus Defence and Space, which builds Galileo satellites with Thales Alenia Space, saw order volumes rise but complained that EUSPA’s security requirements inflated costs by 15%. SMEs benefited from the 20% contract carve-out, but ESA’s geographical return rules were only partially reformed, with France still receiving 28% of Copernicus contracts according to a 2023 Commission report.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a single space programme instead of separate projects under the EU Space Programme Regulation (2021/696/EU)?

Previous fragmentation created governance gaps exploited by third countries — Russia cut off ground station access in 2014 with no EU legal mechanism to respond. The 2018 Court of Auditors report found that separate agencies for Galileo, Copernicus, and EGNOS added €1.2 billion in duplication costs and delayed launch schedules. A single regulation gave the Commission budgetary control, enabling it to repurpose funds across components during crises — something impossible under the previous intergovernmental structure.

### Why did member states accept EUSPA taking over security accreditation from national authorities under the EU Space Programme Regulation (2021/696/EU)?

The 2019 Beidou interference incident demonstrated that national-level security oversight was insufficient for threats spanning satellite constellations. However, member states retained a veto via EUSPA’s Security Accreditation Board, where decisions require a qualified majority. France, Germany, and Italy successfully blocked the initial proposal giving EUSPA autonomy, insisting that national security authorities must sign off on any change to encrypted signal standards.

### Why are GOVSATCOM and SST new components rather than extensions of existing ones in the EU Space Programme Regulation (2021/696/EU)?

GOVSATCOM addresses a specific failure: during the 2015 migration crisis and 2017 hurricanes, EU agencies found commercial satellite capacity was saturated during emergencies, forcing reliance on non-EU providers. GOVSATCOM creates a dedicated pool of secured capacity. SST arose from the 2009 Iridium-Cosmos satellite collision that created 2,000 debris pieces — an EU legal framework was needed because no single member state had the capacity to track the now-30,000+ debris objects on its own.

### Why did the UK oppose the EU Space Programme Regulation (2021/696/EU)?

The UK was the second largest contributor to Galileo and hosted the system’s key security centre at Harwell, Oxfordshire. The regulation’s Article 47 restricts third-country access to PRS and other secure signals. Post-Brexit, the UK sought continued access — offered by the Commission in 2018 but withdrawn in 2020 when UK rejected EU oversight requirements. The UK’s departure removed the main obstruction in the Council, allowing the regulation to pass with the stronger security provisions Germany and France demanded.
