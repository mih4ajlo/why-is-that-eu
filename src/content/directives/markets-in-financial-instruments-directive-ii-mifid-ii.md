## What is it?

MiFID II is the revised version of the original Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (2004/39/EC). It came into force in 2014 and applied from January 3, 2018. Together with the Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MiFIR, regulation 600/2014/EU), it set new rules regulating nearly every aspect of European securities trading. MiFID II extended transparency to bonds, derivatives, and structured finance products — markets that had previously been opaque — and imposed strict controls on high-frequency trading (HFT), algorithmic trading, and commodity derivatives speculation. The directive also introduced robust investor protection measures, including product governance requirements for investment firms and enhanced reporting of trades to national competent authorities.

## Why was it introduced?

The origin of MiFID II lies in the structural failures exposed by the 2008 global financial crisis — specifically, the opacity of over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives markets and the collapse of trading venues that had operated outside any formal regulation. The original MiFID from 2004 had been designed for an era of fragmented but essentially transparent markets; it allowed trading to move off-exchange through "systematic internalisers" (investment firms that executed client orders against their own inventory) and through "dark pools" — private trading venues that offered no pre-trade transparency. By 2010, according to the European Commission's 2011 impact assessment, dark pool trading in EU equities had grown to over 20% of total equity turnover, and the share of OTC trading for bonds and derivatives was far higher. The crisis revealed that regulators lacked visibility into the build-up of systemic risk in these dark markets, most notably through credit default swaps (CDS) that went unseen until AIG's near-collapse in September 2008.

The political coalition for MiFID II united two powerful groups. First, the European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Markus Ferber (EPP, Germany), drove an ambitious reform agenda centered on transparency and investor protection. Ferber had been a sharp critic of the original MiFID's loopholes, warning in a 2011 committee hearing that "the shadow banking system is growing in the dark spaces of our regulation". Second, the European Commission under President José Manuel Barroso, with Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier (who took office in February 2010), championed the reform as part of a wider response to the financial crisis. Barnier, a former French finance minister, had made strengthening financial regulation a signature issue.

Opposition came from several quarters. UK-based investment banks — notably Barclays, Deutsche Bank, and Goldman Sachs — warned that mandatory trading on regulated venues and the ban on "unbundled" research fees (a key element splitting research and execution payments) would drive up costs and push trading to London's rivals in New York and Asia. The City of London Corporation, representing the UK's financial district, estimated in 2012 that MiFID II could cost the UK financial sector £1.5 billion annually in compliance costs. The German banking association (BdB) similarly lobbied against the commodity derivatives position limits, arguing they would harm agricultural producers hedging risk. The coalition held together despite these pressures, partly because the crisis narrative was still fresh and partly because the UK, then an EU member, was outnumbered by member states supporting stricter rules.

Timing was crucial. The original MiFID had been written before the crisis; post-crisis, the G20's 2009 Pittsburgh summit had committed to reforms including mandatory clearing of OTC derivatives and reporting to trade repositories. MiFID II was the EU's implementation of those G20 commitments for the securities markets. The proposal was formally introduced by the Commission on October 20, 2011, just weeks after the collapse of MF Global in the US (October 31, 2011), a brokerage that had failed partly due to hidden sovereign debt exposures — a reminder of the dangers of opaque markets. Negotiations between the Parliament and Council lasted over two years, concluding in January 2014 with a final compromise that pushed the application date to 2017 (later delayed to 2018). The directive passed with strong majority support: in the Parliament's final vote on April 15, 2014, 528 MEPs supported it, with 108 against and 14 abstentions.

What finally unblocked the earlier deadlock over commodity derivative position limits was the 2012 scandal around the London Whale trades at JPMorgan Chase, which lost $6.2 billion in the bank's Chief Investment Office through concentrated credit derivatives positions. Although the trades were executed in the US, they involved EU-based counterparties and highlighted the systemic risks of unconstrained speculation in commodity and energy derivatives markets. The scandal gave Barnier and Ferber the evidence they needed to push through hard limits on commodity derivative positions, despite industry objections.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Original MiFID (2004/39/EC) enters into force, aimed at creating a single market for investment services with limited transparency for OTC trading
- **2008** — Global financial crisis exposes systemic risks in OTC derivatives markets and dark pools; EU regulators begin discussing reform
- **2009** — G20 Pittsburgh summit commits to mandatory clearing and reporting for all OTC derivatives; EU begins work on implementation
- **October 20, 2011** — European Commission publishes formal proposal for MiFID II/MiFIR, including mandatory trading on regulated venues, transparency for bonds and derivatives, and position limits on commodity derivatives
- **2012** — JPMorgan's London Whale losses ($6.2 billion) strengthen the case for commodity derivative position limits
- **January 2014** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council after three-way negotiations (trilogues); application date set for January 2017 (later delayed)
- **May 15, 2014** — MiFID II (2014/65/EU) and MiFIR (600/2014/EU) published in the Official Journal of the EU; enter into force 20 days later
- **2016** — The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) and Commission delay application date by one year to January 3, 2018, citing the complexity of IT infrastructure upgrades required
- **January 3, 2018** — MiFID II takes effect across all 28 EU member states (plus EEA countries)
- **2019–2021** — First major enforcement actions: ESMA publishes "double volume cap" data showing which trading venues exceed share trading limits; national regulators issue first fines for failures in transaction reporting and algorithmic trading controls
- **2022** — ESMA publishes its first review report on MiFID II, recommending adjustments to the "systematic internaliser" regime and commodity derivative position limits

## Impact and consequences

MiFID II fundamentally restructured European securities trading. The most visible change was the collapse of the "dark pool" share of equity trading. According to ESMA's 2020 annual report, lit (transparent) trading venues increased their share of EU equity turnover from roughly 55% in 2017 to over 65% by 2019, while dark trading fell from 23% to around 15% of turnover. Pre-trade transparency for bonds — previously near-zero — became mandatory for liquid bonds, with ESMA publishing weekly "liquidity assessment" data that determined which bonds were subject to pre-trade transparency waivers.

The new regime also forced massive IT investment from trading venues and investment firms. The directive required firms to report all trades within one minute (later extended to five minutes for some instruments) to national competent authorities, with 65 mandatory fields per trade. The Commission's 2017 impact assessment estimated that the compliance cost for the financial industry across the EU was between €2.5 billion and €3.5 billion in the first year of implementation. Smaller trading venues, particularly regional exchanges like Athens Stock Exchange and Ljubljana Stock Exchange, struggled with the costs and some consolidated.

The "unbundling" rule — which separated research fees from execution commissions — had a dramatic effect on the sell-side research industry. According to a 2019 survey by the CFA Institute, the number of analysts covering European equities declined by an estimated 20–25% between 2017 and 2019, and the average research budget at major investment banks fell by 20–30%. Critics — including some buy-side asset managers — argued that this reduced coverage of small and mid-cap European companies, making it harder for them to raise capital.

Enforcement was initially patchy. By 2022, several national regulators had imposed fines for MiFID II violations. The UK Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), which continued to enforce MiFID II rules after Brexit through its own regime, fined Goldman Sachs International £96.6 million in 2021 for failing to produce accurate transaction reports between 2008 and 2020. In 2022, the French regulator AMF fined Crédit Agricole Corporate and Investment Bank €5 million for failing to comply with best execution rules.

Perhaps the most controversial outcome was the "consolidated tape" — a promised single EU-wide feed for pre- and post-trade data — which the directive mandated but which failed to materialize until 2023. ESMA repeatedly acknowledged that multiple competing vendors (Bloomberg, Refinitiv, Euronext) made a single tape economically unviable for most asset classes, undermining MiFID II's goal of democratizing market data.

## Key questions answered

### Why did MiFID II take so long to implement (2014 to 2018)?

The four-year gap between adoption (2014) and application (2018) was driven by the technical complexity of the new reporting and transparency infrastructure. Trading venues needed to upgrade their systems to handle real-time trade reporting and order book data for bonds and derivatives, while investment firms needed to build systems capable of generating and sending 65-field transaction reports within 60 seconds. The Commission's 2016 "omnibus" directive pushed the date back by a full year after industry — particularly Deutsche Boerse and London Stock Exchange Group — warned they could not meet the original deadline. Additionally, ESMA needed time to write detailed regulatory technical standards (RTS), including the "double volume cap" mechanism for determining which trading venues exceeded permissible dark trading limits (15% of total equity turnover).

### Why did MiFID II include a ban on "unbundling" research fees?

The ban on bundling was driven by a concern that execution commissions were inflated to subsidize research, creating conflicts of interest for investment managers. Barnier's 2011 impact assessment cited estimates that European asset managers paid between €2 billion and €3 billion annually in bundled research and execution costs, with no clear separation of value. The Parliament's rapporteur Markus Ferber argued that unbundling would reduce "soft dollars" arrangements that caused managers to choose brokers based on research quality rather than execution cost. The UK's Financial Services Authority (FSA) had already piloted a similar approach in 2012, and Ferber's staff consulted closely with the FSA. The result was Article 13 of the delegated directive (2017/593/EU), which mandates that investment firms "must make a separate charge for research" and "must agree a separate research budget with clients." The rule dramatically reduced research spending: according to a 2021 study by the Rothko Group, the aggregated research budgets of the top 15 European investment banks fell from €4.5 billion in 2014 to €2.2 billion in 2020.

### Why did the commodity derivative position limits under MiFID II survive industry legal challenge?

The inclusion of position limits on commodity derivatives (Articles 57–58 of MiFID II) was strongly opposed by the Commodity Markets Council Europe (CMCE), a trade body representing traders like Vitol, Trafigura, and Gunvor, as well as physical commodity producers. The CMCE argued that the limits would harm liquidity in agricultural and energy derivatives markets, making it harder for farmers and energy companies to hedge price risk. In January 2018, the UK Asset Management Association challenged the limits in a judicial review before the UK High Court, arguing that ESMA's methodology for setting the limits was flawed. However, the UK High Court rejected the challenge in December 2018, ruling that ESMA had acted within its discretion. The UK's withdrawal from the EU in 2020 removed the main legal avenue for further challenge, and by 2022 ESMA had set position limits for over 100 commodity derivatives (including crude oil, natural gas, wheat, and electricity contracts) across EU trading venues. The limits effectively capped any single entity's position at 5–25% of total open interest in a contract, depending on its liquidity.
