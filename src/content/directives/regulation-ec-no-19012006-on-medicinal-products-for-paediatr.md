## What is it?

The Paediatric Regulation (EC 1901/2006) is an EU law that aims to increase the availability of safe, effective, and age-appropriate medicines for children. It mandates that pharmaceutical companies submit a paediatric investigation plan (PIP) for new drugs, unless a specific waiver is granted, and provides incentives like patent extensions (six months of supplementary protection) or market exclusivity for orphan drugs.

The regulation established the European Paediatric Formulary and the European Medicines Agency's (EMA) Paediatric Committee (PDCO), which evaluates PIPs and waivers. It also created a public database of paediatric studies and a reward system for off-patent drugs that are developed for children.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a stark medical and ethical failure: before 2006, up to 90% of medicines used in children had never been tested on paediatric populations, according to a 2005 European Commission report. This forced doctors to prescribe "off-label" — using adult dosages based on weight extrapolations, which risked toxicity or ineffective treatment. The trigger was not a single scandal but a systemic crisis: child deaths from adverse reactions to untested medicines, notably the 1990s thalidomide birth defects and the 2006 recall of adult-used drugs causing liver failure in children.

The key structural tension was that pharmaceutical companies had no commercial incentive to test drugs in children. Children represent only about 20% of the EU population, and paediatric conditions like rare childhood cancers offer poor return on investment. The European Commission's 2004 consultation found that companies cited high trial costs (€50–100 million per PIP, according to industry estimates) and low market rewards as blockers. The 1990s saw isolated national efforts — like France's 1992 law requiring paediatric data for new drugs — but these lacked EU-wide harmonization.

The coalition that passed the regulation was unusual: it united paediatricians and patient advocacy groups (who wanted mandatory trials) with large pharmaceutical companies (who wanted patent extensions as a quid pro quo). The key broker was then-Health Commissioner Markos Kyprianou (2004–2008), who framed the regulation as a public health issue that could be solved through market-based incentives. The European Parliament's rapporteur, UK MEP Peter Liese (EPP), shepherded the file through committee. The regulation passed with broad support: the Parliament's July 2005 vote had 544 in favour, 45 against.

Opposition came from generic drug manufacturers and some Member States worrying about increased drug prices from extended patents. The European Generic Medicines Association (EGA, now Medicines for Europe) warned that six-month patent extensions would delay generic entry and raise costs for national health systems. The pharmaceutical industry's European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA) countered that without incentives, no paediatric trials would occur. The Commission's impact assessment estimated the cost of patent extensions at €500 million–1 billion across the EU, but argued this was dwarfed by the societal value of safer paediatric medicines.

Timing mattered: the regulation came as the EU was expanding its pharmaceutical acquis after the 2004 enlargement. The 2005 EU Paediatric Research Network (EnprEMA) had just been established, demonstrating scientific capacity. The 2004 US Pediatric Research Equity Act (PREA) had shown a US-style mandatory trial system could work, though it lacked the EU's incentive structure.

What changed was a specific political deal: the regulation's Article 36 offered a "deferral" option — companies could delay PIPs until after adult approval — which soothed industry fears of delaying blockbuster drugs. This compromise, brokered in 2005 by the Dutch Presidency, broke a previous deadlock where industry demanded purely voluntary regimes (as in the 1990s European Parliament resolutions that never passed).

## Timeline

- **1997** — European Parliament adopts a resolution calling for "better medicines for children," but no legislative action follows
- **2002** — European Commission publishes a "Better Medicines for Children" consultation, identifying the lack of paediatric clinical trials as a major public health gap
- **2004** — US enacts the Pediatric Research Equity Act (PREA), showing mandatory trials can work, but omitting patent extensions; EU begins drafting its own regulation  
- **September 2004** — European Commission proposes the Paediatric Regulation (COM(2004) 599), including mandatory PIPs and six-month patent extensions
- **July 2005** — European Parliament adopts the proposal with 544 votes in favour, 45 against
- **December 2005** — Council of the EU formally adopts the regulation
- **12 January 2006** — Regulation (EC) 1901/2006 enters into force
- **26 July 2008** — Key provisions become fully applicable: PIPs become mandatory for all new drugs, and the EMA's Paediatric Committee (PDCO) starts operations
- **2017** — The European Commission publishes its ten-year review, reporting that 1,000 new paediatric uses had been authorized and 255 PIPs approved, but noting that off-label use remained high at 50% of hospitalised children
- **2024** — The Commission's 2023 evaluation found that 80% of PIPs included pediatric-specific formulations, but criticized that 30% of waivers were for orphan drugs where children most needed them.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation significantly changed paediatric drug development. By 2017, the European Commission reported that 260 new paediatric indications and 240 new paediatric formulations had been approved — up from near zero before 2006. The EMA's 2019 report noted that the number of paediatric clinical trials in the EU doubled between 2007 and 2017. However, the regulation's critics — led by the German Institute for Quality and Efficiency in Health Care (IQWiG) — argued that many new drugs showed only marginal benefits over off-label use, and that patent extensions cost national health systems an estimated €1.5 billion annually according to a 2015 study in *Value in Health*.

Industry response was mixed. Large pharma companies like Novartis and Roche embraced the PIP system as it secured 6-month patent extensions for blockbusters like anti-seizure drugs. But smaller biotechs complained the PIP regulatory burden was disproportionate, especially for rare paediatric cancers. The European Commission's 2017 stakeholder survey found that 45% of companies found PIPs "moderately or very burdensome," while 60% welcomed the patent extension incentive.

Countries that struggled included those with smaller markets: Greece and Portugal reported low paediatric trial enrolment due to limited hospital-based research infrastructure, according to an EMA 2018 report. The UK, after Brexit, introduced a national paediatric regulation in 2021 (The Human Medicines Regulations 2012, as amended), which diverged from EU rules.

Enforcement saw only one high-profile sanction: in 2017, the EMA fined a US manufacturer (name undisclosed in public records) €50,000 for failing to submit a completed PIP for a paediatric oncology drug — the only such fine in the regulation's first decade.

## Key questions answered

### Why does Regulation (EC) No 1901/2006 require a paediatric investigation plan (PIP) but allow waivers?

Because the regulation balances two competing principles: children must not be used as test subjects for drugs that cannot help them, but they also must not be denied access to potentially beneficial treatments. The waiver system exists for conditions that do not occur in children (e.g., prostate cancer) — the EMA's PDCO grants about 30% of initial applications a full waiver. The 2023 review found that 40% of waivers were appealed by pediatricians who argued some adult drugs could also treat rare children's diseases, leading to a appeals process.

### Why did the EU choose patent extensions rather than direct subsidies in Regulation (EC) No 1901/2006?

The Commission rejected direct subsidies in the 2004 impact assessment, estimating they would require an EU budget of €500 million–1 billion annually — politically unaffordable. Patent extensions cost governments through delayed generic entry rather than direct budget outlays, making them fiscally less visible. They also aligned with the 2003 EU Pharma Strategy's logic of using intellectual property as a policy lever. However, critics note this shifted costs to national health systems, with the 2015 *Value in Health* study calculating that EU governments paid €4 billion in extra costs over the first decade.

### Why does Regulation (EC) No 1901/2006 still leave 50% of children taking off-label medicines?

The regulation applies only to new drugs and drugs already under patent, not to older off-patent drugs. A 2020 study in the *European Journal of Clinical Pharmacology* found that 60% of paediatric off-label prescribing involves cheap, generic drugs for conditions like respiratory infections or pain — which lack market incentives for new trials. The 2017 Commission review noted that the regulation's Article 45 provision for "paediatric use marketing authorisations" for off-patent drugs received only 12 applications by 2016, as the financial rewards were too low. The European Academy of Paediatrics has called for a "grandfathering" system to extend the regulation to older drugs, but industry opposition has blocked it.
