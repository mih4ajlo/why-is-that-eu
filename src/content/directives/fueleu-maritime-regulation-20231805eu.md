## What is it?

FuelEU Maritime is a regulation that mandates a gradual reduction in the greenhouse gas (GHG) intensity of the energy used by ships above 5,000 gross tonnage when operating at sea or at berth in ports under EU member state jurisdiction, including 50% of energy used on voyages starting or ending outside the EU. It covers well-to-wake emissions — the full lifecycle from fuel production to combustion — setting a baseline of 91.16 grams of CO2 equivalent per megajoule in 2020 and requiring cuts of 2% from 2025, 6% from 2030, 14.5% from 2035, 31% from 2040, 62% from 2045, and 80% from 2050.

The regulation also requires shore-side electricity use for container and passenger ships at major EU ports from 2030, and for the rest of the fleet from 2035, ending the practice of burning fuel at berth. A voluntary pooling mechanism lets operators average compliance across their fleet, while a penalty system — escalating fines from €2,400 to €4,000 per tonne of fuel oil equivalent for non-compliance — enforces the targets. Unlike the maritime carbon price under the revised EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), which covers only CO₂ from voyages, FuelEU tackles all GHG emissions and their upstream production impacts.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a structural tension: international maritime shipping, responsible for roughly 2-3% of global CO₂ emissions according to the European Environment Agency's 2021 "Transport and Environment Report", was largely excluded from EU climate law despite growing trade volumes. The International Maritime Organization (IMO), the sector's global regulator, had only adopted a voluntary initial GHG strategy in 2018 targeting a 50% cut by 2050 from 2008 levels — a pace widely criticised as insufficient by the European Parliament and environmental groups like Transport & Environment. European shipowners faced a patchwork of national fuel requirements, while ports struggled with air quality violations linked to ship exhaust, particularly sulphur and nitrogen oxides.

The political breakthrough came in December 2019 with the European Green Deal, under Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, which explicitly committed the EU to climate neutrality by 2050 and required all transport modes to contribute. Frans Timmermans, then Executive Vice-President for the European Green Deal, publicly argued that shipping could no longer be a "free rider" on climate ambition. A coalition of Nordic states — Denmark, Sweden, Finland, and the Netherlands — pushed for maritime fuel standards, backed by the European Parliament's environment committee rapporteur Jutta Paulus (Greens/EFA) who wanted 100% zero-emission ships by 2050. Opposition came from southern European shipping nations like Greece and Cyprus, alongside industry groups such as the European Community Shipowners' Associations (ECSA), which warned that unilaterally imposing EU rules would divert shipping to non-EU hubs like Tangier or Port Said. The European Parliament's transport committee initially proposed weaker targets, but the final deal — reached in trilogue negotiations on March 23, 2023 — emerged after intense pressure from the Fit for 55 package, which bundled transport decarbonisation measures together. A key unblocking event was the IMO's failure at its MEPC 77 meeting in November 2021 to agree on a carbon levy or mandatory fuel standard, convincing EU institutions that unilateral action was necessary to maintain credibility ahead of COP26 in Glasgow. The Council formally adopted the regulation on July 25, 2023.

Alternatives considered included a pure carbon tax on maritime fuels (rejected due to revenue allocation disputes), extending the EU ETS to shipping without a fuel standard (already done separately), and a technology mandate for zero-emission shore power (too costly for smaller ports according to the Commission's 2020 impact assessment). The chosen hybrid approach — GHG intensity targets plus a shore-power mandate — sought to balance certainty for fuel producers with operational flexibility for shipowners.

- **October 19, 2022** — European Parliament adopts its position, calling for 100% reduction by 2050 and mandatory shore power from 2025
- **March 23, 2023** — Trilogue agreement reached, setting final targets at 80% reduction by 2050 and shore power for container/passenger ships from 2030
- **July 25, 2023** — Council formally adopts Regulation (EU) 2023/1805
- **October 12, 2023** — Regulation published in Official Journal
- **January 1, 2025** — First compliance point: 2% GHG intensity reduction target takes effect; monitoring and reporting obligations begin
- **January 1, 2030** — Shore-side electricity requirement for container ships (over 5,000 GT) and passenger ships at TEN-T core ports
- **January 1, 2035** — Shore-side electricity mandate extends to all ships above 5,000 GT at all EU ports; 31% GHG intensity reduction target applies
- **January 1, 2050** — Final 80% GHG intensity reduction target in effect

## Impact and consequences

Shipping companies operating in EU waters faced immediate compliance responsibilities from January 1, 2025. According to the Commission's 2023 impact assessment, approximately 12,000 ships and 900 companies were directly covered by the regulation, representing roughly 90% of EU maritime emissions. The largest operators — Maersk, MSC, Mediterranean Shipping Company, CMA CGM, and COSCO were named in industry briefings as among the most exposed — began ordering dual-fuel vessels capable of running on methanol, LNG, or ammonia, with Maersk launching the first methanol-powered container ship, the "Laura Maersk", in September 2023.

The shipping industry's initial response was cautious. ECSA warned in a July 2023 press release that the regulation "could create a two-tier system" where non-EU shipping lines refuel outside EU waters to avoid compliance costs. Several major bunkering ports — notably Rotterdam, Antwerp, and Algeciras — announced investments in green fuel infrastructure, with the Port of Rotterdam targeting 2.5 million tonnes of hydrogen-derived fuels by 2030 under its "Rotterdam Hydrogen Hub" initiative. Smaller ports in the Mediterranean and Baltic risked seeing traffic diversion, as flagged by the European Court of Auditors in a 2024 special report which found that "the lack of global equivalent rules creates competitiveness risks for EU ports."

Enforcement challenges emerged early: the regulation required flag states or the European Maritime Safety Agency (EMSA) to verify compliance, but EMSA's 2024 Annual Overview noted that only 15 member states had designated verification bodies by mid-2025. Penalties for non-compliance — starting at €2,400 per tonne of fuel oil equivalent and rising to €4,000 by 2030 — were designed to be "dissuasive and proportionate" according to Article 21, but industry groups argued they were insufficient for large operators who could absorb fines as a cost of business. The first compliance reports, due in January 2026, are expected to reveal significant gaps in LNG supply chain transparency, given that methane slip — unburned methane released during engine operation — was initially excluded from penalty calculations due to measurement difficulties, a loophole identified by the NGO Transport & Environment in its March 2025 analysis.

A key unintended consequence was the "greenwash blending" risk: the regulation allows biofuels and synthetic fuels from various feedstocks, including palm oil and soy, which environmental groups like Seas At Risk argued could lead to indirect land-use change emissions worse than the fossil fuels they replace. The Commission committed to a review by December 2027 to address lifecycle accounting rules for such fuels.

## Key questions answered

### Will new EU shipping fuel rules make the cost of imported goods go up?

Yes, moderately. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated the regulation would increase maritime transport costs by 2-4% by 2030 and 10-15% by 2040 under a high-cost scenario for green fuels, translating to roughly €0.10-0.50 per container of goods shipped between Asia and Europe. However, shipping fuel costs are a small fraction of final product prices; the Commission projected an average 0.1-0.3% increase in consumer prices for imported goods by 2040. The main cost risk comes from fuel switching — green methanol and ammonia are currently 2-5 times more expensive than heavy fuel oil — but the gradual 2% reduction starting in 2025 aims to give time for scaling up production.

### Can shipping companies avoid the rules by filling up their tanks just outside EU waters?

Partially, but with diminishing returns. The regulation covers 50% of energy used on voyages that start or end outside the EU — so a ship sailing from Shanghai to Rotterdam would comply on the entire return journey too. A ship that deliberately stops in Tangier to bunker heavy fuel before entering the EU would still face compliance costs for the non-EU leg. The real risk is traffic diversion: if a shipping line moves a transshipment hub from Algeciras to Tangier to avoid EU waters entirely on certain routes, the regulation's scope shrinks. The Commission acknowledged this in its 2021 impact assessment, modelling a potential 2-5% reduction in EU port activity by 2040 under worst-case diversion scenarios. Compensation mechanisms, such as dedicating revenues from penalties to support affected ports under the Innovation Fund, were debated but not included in the final text.

### How is this different from the carbon price that ships already pay under the EU's emissions trading system?

Fundamentally. The EU ETS for shipping, which came into force January 1, 2024, puts a price on every tonne of CO₂ emitted during voyages (100% intra-EU, 50% extra-EU), starting at around €80-100 per tonne and requiring operators to buy allowances. FuelEU Maritime instead mandates the GHG intensity of the energy used — a technology and fuel standard, not a carbon price. A ship could in theory operate on dirty fuel and buy ETS allowances (paying the carbon price) but still violate FuelEU if the fuel's GHG intensity exceeds the mandated cap. Conversely, a ship using 100% green ammonia would have near-zero ETS costs but still needs to document compliance with FuelEU's intensity targets. The two instruments are designed to work together: the ETS drives short-term efficiency gains and carbon pricing revenue, while FuelEU creates a structural trajectory away from fossil fuels. Revenue from FuelEU penalties flows into a dedicated fund for maritime decarbonisation projects, similar to revenues from ETS allowance auctions.
