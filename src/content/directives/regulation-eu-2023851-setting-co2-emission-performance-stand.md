## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2023/851 amends the existing CO2 standards for cars and vans (Regulation 2019/631) to require a 100% reduction in CO2 emissions from new passenger cars and light commercial vehicles by 2035, relative to 2021 levels. This means that from 2035, any new vehicle sold in the EU that emits CO2 from its tailpipe — whether petrol, diesel, hybrid, or plug-in hybrid — cannot be registered for first sale.

The regulation includes interim targets: a 55% reduction for cars and 50% for vans by 2030. It also introduces a mechanism for the European Commission to review progress by 2026, considering the development of e-fuels and the state of charging infrastructure. The regulation was adopted as part of the "Fit for 55" package, the EU’s legislative framework to reduce net greenhouse gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030 compared to 1990 levels.

## Why was it introduced?

The decisive moment came on 14 July 2021, when the European Commission, under President Ursula von der Leyen and her Executive Vice President for the European Green Deal Frans Timmermans, proposed the "Fit for 55" package. The 2035 ban on new combustion engine cars was the single most politically charged element. The *structural tension* it addressed was clear: the EU had set a legally binding climate neutrality goal for 2050 under the European Climate Law (2021), but the transport sector — responsible for roughly a quarter of EU emissions — had seen its CO2 output rise by nearly 30% between 1990 and 2019, according to the European Environment Agency’s 2021 report. Existing CO2 standards had driven some efficiency gains, but without a terminal date for fossil-fuel vehicles, automakers had no clear investment signal to shift fully to electric.

The *coalition dynamics* were forged by a remarkable alignment. The 2035 target was championed by the Progressive Alliance of Socialists and Democrats (S&D) and the Greens/European Free Alliance, who together held 214 seats in the European Parliament after the 2019 elections. The centre-right European People's Party (EPP), the largest group with 187 seats, was deeply split: its German members, including MEPs from the CDU/CSU, opposed the ban fearing job losses in the powerful automotive sector, while its Nordic and Benelux members supported it. The crucial swing came from the liberal Renew Europe group (98 seats), which backed the target after securing a 2026 review clause. The final Parliament vote on 14 February 2023 saw 340 MEPs in favour, 279 against, and 21 abstentions — a narrower margin than the broad "Fit for 55" measures, reflecting the deep industrial and political divisions.

The *timing* was dictated by the European Green Deal, launched in December 2019. The 2021 proposal could not have succeeded earlier: the Volkswagen "Dieselgate" scandal of 2015 had shattered public trust in automakers’ claims about clean diesel, but the industry itself was in denial. By 2020, Tesla had sold 500,000 vehicles globally, and the European Court of Auditors’ 2021 special report on charging infrastructure found that while deployment had grown, it remained insufficient for mass adoption. The COVID-19 pandemic and the resulting economic stimulus packages — notably the €672.5 billion Recovery and Resilience Facility — prioritized green investments, making funding for electric vehicle (EV) charging infrastructure and battery gigafactories available at scale. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 further underscored the geopolitical cost of fossil fuel dependence, hardening political resolve.

*What changed* from earlier, failed attempts was the combination of clear climate law binding the EU to 2050 neutrality, a committed Commission president who made the Green Deal her signature initiative, and the collapse of the "clean diesel" narrative after Dieselgate. Previous proposals, such as the 2017 revision of CO2 standards that set a 37.5% reduction target for 2030, had explicitly avoided a phase-out date. The 2021 proposal broke that taboo. The German government acted as the key opponent: Chancellor Olaf Scholz’s coalition (SPD, Greens, FDP) initially agreed to the 2035 ban in March 2023, but the liberal FDP, led by Transport Minister Volker Wissing, demanded an explicit exemption for e-fuels — synthetic fuels produced using renewable energy. Just days before the final Council vote on 28 March 2023, Germany blocked the regulation, forcing last-minute negotiations. The compromise was a non-binding "recital" in the regulation stating that the Commission would assess the potential for e-fuels after 2026, allowing Germany to claim it had secured an "e-fuel loophole" while the regulation’s binding 2035 target remained unchanged. The Council adopted it on 8 March 2023 (with Germany, Italy, Poland, and Bulgaria voting against or abstaining).

## Timeline

- **2015** — Volkswagen "Dieselgate" scandal breaks, revealing systematic cheating on NOx emissions tests. EU regulators begin questioning the viability of diesel as a clean technology.
- **2019** — European Green Deal announced by Commission President von der Leyen, with a target of climate neutrality by 2050. Regulation (EU) 2019/631 sets 37.5% CO2 reduction target for cars by 2030, but no phase-out date.
- **2021** — European Climate Law (2021/1119) enters into force, legally binding the EU to 55% emissions reduction by 2030 and net-zero by 2050. On 14 July, "Fit for 55" package proposes 100% reduction by 2035.
- **2022** — European Parliament’s Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Food Safety (ENVI) votes in favour on 10 May. Plenary vote on 8 June 2022 approves text with 339-249. Russia invades Ukraine on 24 February 2022, accelerating energy independence debates.
- **2023** — Council adopts text on 8 March, but Germany blocks final approval on 21 March demanding e-fuel exemption. After last-minute negotiations, Germany lifts its block on 25 March. Regulation formally published on 25 April 2023, in force from 19 June 2023.
- **2024** — Commission begins preparatory work on the 2026 review, including a study on e-fuels feasibility, expected by end of 2025.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation sent an immediate shockwave through the European automotive industry. The European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA) warned in 2021 that the target was "hugely challenging" and that the infrastructure for 3.5 million public charging points by 2030 would cost €14 billion. Volkswagen, Europe’s largest automaker, announced in March 2022 that it would phase out internal combustion engine (ICE) sales in Europe by 2033 — two years ahead of the EU deadline. By contrast, Stellantis (owner of Peugeot, Citroën, Fiat) committed to an all-electric European fleet by 2030. BMW and Mercedes-Benz resisted a hard deadline, investing in both EVs and hydrogen fuel cells.

The regulation’s impact on consumer behaviour has been measured by the European Alternative Fuels Observatory (EAFO): by 2024, battery electric vehicles (BEVs) held 15-18% of new car registrations in the EU, up from 1.9% in 2019. However, the European Court of Auditors’ 2023 report found that charging infrastructure deployment was uneven: the Netherlands had 100 chargers per 10,000 people, while Romania and Greece had fewer than 5. The 2026 review is seen as a pressure point for member states to accelerate grid upgrades and charging rollouts.

Countries opposed to the ban — notably Italy (where Fiat’s parent Stellantis resisted), Poland (coal-dependent energy grid), and Bulgaria (low EV adoption) — have signalled they will push for a "technology-neutral" approach in the review. Germany’s e-fuel carve-out remains largely symbolic: in 2023, only 10 million litres of e-fuels were produced globally, versus 1.6 trillion litres of gasoline. The Commission’s 2026 impact assessment is expected to conclude that e-fuels cannot supply the volume needed for mass road transport by 2035 at competitive cost.

## Key questions answered

### Why was 2035 chosen as the deadline in Regulation (EU) 2023/851?

The 2035 date was derived from the EU’s 2050 climate neutrality target and the lifetime of vehicles. A new car sold in 2035 will typically remain on the road for 12-15 years. To have zero tailpipe CO2 emissions from the entire fleet by 2050, the last new fossil-fuel car must be sold around 2035. The 2030 interim target was set at 55% for cars (50% for vans) to provide a measurable trajectory.

### Does Regulation (EU) 2023/851 actually ban internal combustion engines?

No — the regulation bans tailpipe CO2 emissions, not the engine itself. A theoretical vehicle could run on an internal combustion engine using 100% CO2-neutral synthetic fuel (e-fuels) and still comply, provided the e-fuel lifecycle emissions are verified by the 2026 review. However, current e-fuels are produced at very low volumes and high cost (€3-6 per litre), making this pathway economically uncertain at scale.

### Was Regulation (EU) 2023/851 nearly blocked by a single member state?

Yes — Germany. After the Council adopted the text on 8 March 2023, the final step was formal approval by member state ambassadors. On 21 March, Germany announced it would not support the text, blocking the required qualified majority. Transport Minister Volker Wissing (FDP) demanded a clear legal exemption for e-fuels. The deadlock was broken on 25 March when the Commission issued a non-binding "statement" acknowledging the possible role of e-fuels, and Germany withdrew its objection. The regulation passed on 28 March with Germany voting in favour.

### Why did the German car industry not manage to block Regulation (EU) 2023/851?

The German automotive industry was deeply divided. Volkswagen, Europe’s largest automaker, had already committed to an all-electric European fleet by 2033, and saw the EU regulation as aligning with its strategy. BMW and Mercedes-Benz, by contrast, had invested in e-fuel and hydrogen R&D, and wanted longer lead times. The German Association of the Automotive Industry (VDA) formally opposed the 2035 ban in 2022, warning of job losses. However, the industry’s influence was diluted by two factors: the "Dieselgate" scandal had eroded political trust in automakers’ voluntary commitments; and the German government coalition included the Greens, who controlled the Economy Ministry under Robert Habeck and made the ban a coalition prerequisite. The CDU/CSU opposition in the Bundestag supported the auto sector’s position, but the governing coalition’s narrow majority held.
