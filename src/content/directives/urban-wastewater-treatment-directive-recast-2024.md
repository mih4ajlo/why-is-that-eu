## What is it?

The Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive (Recast) 2024 overhauls the 1991 version (Directive 91/271/EEC), which had become outdated as urbanization, pollution loads, and scientific understanding of micropollutants evolved. The new directive requires member states to upgrade secondary treatment for all towns above 1,000 population equivalent (down from the previous 2,000 threshold) and to introduce tertiary treatment for nitrogen and phosphorus in areas vulnerable to eutrophication. It also mandates quaternary treatment (removal of micropollutants like pharmaceuticals and microplastics) for larger plants above 100,000 population equivalent, with a review clause for lower thresholds.

For the first time, the directive establishes an Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) scheme for pharmaceutical and cosmetic companies, requiring these industries to cover at least 80% of quaternary treatment costs. It also sets an energy neutrality target: treatment plants above 10,000 population equivalent must achieve energy-neutral operations by 2045, with intermediate milestones at 2030 and 2040.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for the recast was a perfect storm of scientific evidence, legal pressure, and political will. In 2018, the European Environment Agency's "European waters — assessment of status and pressures" report revealed that only 40% of surface water bodies in the EU were in good ecological status, with nutrient pollution from untreated or insufficiently treated wastewater being a primary driver. The European Court of Justice had issued repeated rulings — including Case C-67/12 in 2014 and Case C-251/17 in 2020 — finding member states like Luxembourg, Belgium, and Greece in violation of the 1991 directive's deadlines. By 2020, the Commission reported that 13 member states were still not fully compliant, with over 200 infringement cases pending.

The pharmaceutical industry's growing imprint on water systems became impossible to ignore. The Commission's 2019 "European Union Strategic Approach to Pharmaceuticals in the Environment" noted that residues from painkillers, antibiotics, contraceptives, and antidepressants were detected in 94% of European rivers, according to a University of York study published in Environmental Toxicology & Chemistry. Industry associations like the European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA) initially opposed mandatory quaternary treatment, arguing costs would be prohibitive, but public pressure from citizen campaigns and NGOs like the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) shifted the debate.

Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius, the Lithuanian Commissioner for Environment, Oceans and Fisheries, made the recast a priority of the European Green Deal. The proposal, released on 27 October 2022, coincided with the EU's Zero Pollution Action Plan, which set a 2030 target to reduce nutrient losses from wastewater by 50%. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Danish MEP Niels Fuglsang (S&D), pushed for the EPR scheme, while the Council — led by the Swedish Presidency in early 2023 and the Spanish Presidency in late 2023 — negotiated the energy neutrality timeline, scaling it back from an earlier proposal of 2030 to 2045 after resistance from Central and Eastern European states like Poland and Hungary, which argued that their legacy infrastructure required longer transition periods.

The final text was adopted on 5 November 2024, with the European Parliament voting 584 in favor, 42 against, and 74 abstentions. The key opposition came from conservative and far-right MEPs who argued the EPR scheme would increase drug prices, as well as from some industry groups including Cosmetics Europe, which warned of €1.3 billion in annual compliance costs across the cosmetics sector. The EEA's 2023 State of Water report estimated that total investment needs across the EU would range from €45 billion to €115 billion by 2040, depending on the stringency of implementation.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Original Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive (91/271/EEC) adopted, setting treatment standards for towns above 2,000 population equivalent.
- **2014** — European Court of Justice ruling (Case C-67/12) against Luxembourg for failing to comply with 1991 directive.
- **2018** — EEA report finds only 40% of EU surface water bodies in good ecological status, wastewater identified as key pressure.
- **2020** — ECJ ruling (Case C-251/17) against Belgium; Commission publishes strategic approach on pharmaceuticals in environment.
- **27 October 2022** — Commission proposes recast directive as part of European Green Deal and Zero Pollution Action Plan.
- **2023** — Ongoing trilogue negotiations; Swedish and Spanish Presidencies broker compromises on energy neutrality timeline.
- **5 November 2024** — Directive adopted by European Parliament (584 votes in favor, 42 against, 74 abstentions).
- **8 December 2024** — Directive published in Official Journal of the European Union.
- **28 December 2024** — Entry into force; member states have 30 months (until mid-2027) to transpose into national law.
- **2027 (expected)** — First quaternary treatment obligations begin for plants above 100,000 population equivalent.
- **2030** — Intermediate milestone: 30% energy neutrality for large plants.
- **2045** — Full energy neutrality target for all plants above 10,000 population equivalent.

## Impact and consequences

The directive imposes the first-ever EU-wide producer responsibility scheme for pharmaceuticals and cosmetics. Companies like Novartis, Sanofi, Bayer, L'Oréal, and Unilever will collectively finance quaternary treatment across the EU, with cost estimates from the Commission's 2022 impact assessment ranging from €400 million to €1.4 billion annually. EFPIA warned in its 2023 position paper that this could increase drug prices by up to 5% in some markets, a claim the Commission disputed, noting that the cost per medicine would be "negligible" — less than €0.10 per pack for most products.

Smaller member states face disproportionate burdens. Malta and Cyprus, both heavily reliant on tourism and with limited freshwater resources, must invest in advanced treatment plants. The Cohesion Policy funds will cover up to 85% of capital costs in less developed regions, according to the Commission's 2024 financing framework. However, NGOs like the EEB criticized the 1,000 population equivalent threshold for secondary treatment as still too high, excluding many rural areas in Eastern Europe where septic tanks are common.

The energy neutrality target is the directive's most ambitious provision. According to Eurostat's 2023 data, treatment plants account for approximately 1% of total EU electricity consumption. Achieving energy neutrality — through on-site solar, biogas generation from sludge, and efficiency upgrades — could reduce emissions by 12 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent annually by 2045, according to the Commission's impact assessment.

Early compliance challenges are expected. The Commission's 2024 implementation report noted that Hungary, Bulgaria, and Romania have achieved less than 60% compliance for secondary treatment under the old directive, suggesting they will struggle with the new, stricter norms. The directive includes an optional derogation for small towns (1,000–2,000 population equivalent) in mountainous or sparsely populated areas, extended to 2035.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU introduce an extended producer responsibility scheme for pharmaceuticals?

The EPR scheme emerged from the failure of existing "polluter pays" mechanisms. Under the 1991 directive, the full cost of treating pharmaceutical residues — which are biologically active, persistent, and increasingly found in drinking water — fell on municipalities and water utilities, not the companies that designed and marketed the products. The Commission's 2022 impact assessment showed that quaternary treatment costs range from €0.15 to €0.50 per cubic meter of wastewater treated. By shifting 80% of these costs to producers, the EU aims to create a financial incentive for "green by design" pharmaceuticals that are more biodegradable, while also funding infrastructure upgrades that member states were unwilling or unable to finance from general taxation.

### Why did the energy neutrality target face opposition from Central and Eastern European states?

Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic, and Bulgaria argued that the 2030 target proposed by the Commission was unrealistic for countries with legacy infrastructure designed in the socialist era. According to the Polish Water Utility Association, 70% of Poland's treatment plants were built before 1995 and require complete retrofitting to integrate renewable energy systems. The compromise pushed the target to 2045, with intermediate milestones, and allowed member states to count energy recovered from sludge incineration — a common practice in Poland — toward the neutrality goal. The controversy reflected deeper east-west tensions over Green Deal timelines and the pace of decarbonization.

### What happens to small towns between 200 and 1,000 population equivalent that remain excluded?

Towns below 1,000 population equivalent — estimated by the Commission to number approximately 12,000 across the EU — are not covered by the directive's mandatory secondary treatment requirement. However, the recast introduces a new obligation for member states to collect data on these small agglomerations and report to the Commission every six years. The 2024 text includes a clause for a future review, by 2035, of whether the threshold should be lowered further. Environmental groups like the EEB have criticized this as a loophole, warning that rural sewage discharges continue to contaminate small streams and groundwater, particularly in regions like rural France, Ireland, and Eastern Poland.
