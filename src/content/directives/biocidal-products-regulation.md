## What is it?

The Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR), fully in force since September 2013, governs the placing on the market and use of biocidal products in the EU. These are products containing active substances intended to destroy, deter, or control harmful organisms—think disinfectants for hospitals, wood preservatives, rat poisons, or insect repellents. The regulation requires that each active substance be approved at EU level after a rigorous safety evaluation by the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA), and that individual biocidal products then be authorised either nationally or across the bloc under a mutual recognition system.

The BPR replaced the earlier Biocidal Products Directive (98/8/EC), which had created a fragmented market where a product approved in one member state could face years of delay before being recognised in another. The regulation also introduced a simplified authorisation procedure for low-risk biocidal products—such as those based on citronella or lactic acid—and banned the use of animal testing for purely regulatory submissions where data-sharing from previous tests is possible. In 2020, ECHA reported that over 25,000 biocidal product applications had been processed under the BPR since its entry into force, though the pace of active substance approvals has been criticised as slow by industry.

## Why was it introduced?

The BPR was the EU’s answer to a regulatory mess that became untenable after a series of public health scares and industry complaints. The original Biocidal Products Directive (98/8/EC) required active substances to be approved at EU level, but left product authorisation to national authorities. In practice, this meant that a disinfectant approved in France could not be sold in Germany without starting from scratch—a problem that hit a crisis point in 2007-2008 when the H1N1 swine flu pandemic exposed gaping holes in Europe’s ability to quickly deploy biocidal products across borders. National health authorities found themselves hoarding different products, and manufacturers complained of duplicative testing costing tens of millions of euros per substance.

The structural tension was clear: the 1998 directive had been designed when the biocides market was small—worth roughly €1.5 billion in 2000, according to the Commission’s 2009 impact assessment—and dominated by a handful of multinationals like BASF, Bayer, and Dow Chemical. By 2008, the market had ballooned to over €10 billion, and the number of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) producing niche products like pool disinfectants or pet flea treatments had surged. These SMEs, represented by the European Biocidal Products Association (EBPA), told the Commission that the national-authorisation route cost them up to €500,000 per product—often more than the product’s annual revenue. The Commission’s 2009 impact assessment estimated that the fragmentation cost the industry between €120 million and €270 million per year in lost market access.

The coalition that got the BPR passed was unusual. On one side were public health advocates like the European Public Health Alliance (EPHA) and environmental groups such as the European Environmental Bureau (EEB), who wanted tougher, EU-wide safety standards after a 2006 incident in Germany where a banned insecticide (carbofuran) was found in illegally imported dog flea collars, sickening several children. On the other side were large chemical companies that saw a single EU authorisation as a way to cut costs—Bayer, for instance, had spent 14 years getting one wood preservative approved in all 27 member states under the old directive, at a cost it put at over €30 million. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, Italian MEP Mario Pirillo (S&D), brokered the deal in March 2012 by offering SMEs a reduced fee structure and a fast-track for low-risk products, which won over the liberal ALDE group and enough EPP conservatives.

The timing was driven by two factors. First, the 2010 report on the functioning of the REACH chemicals regulation (1907/2006/EC) had warned that without reform, the biocides market would be the next regulatory time bomb—member states were already threatening to unilaterally ban products they deemed unsafe, undermining the single market. Second, the 2011 European Court of Justice ruling in *Commission v. Germany* (Case C-512/08) had declared that Germany’s refusal to recognise a French-authorised biocide was illegal, but only under the vague principles of mutual recognition—the court explicitly called for clearer EU rules. The Commission, under Health Commissioner John Dalli (2010-2012) and later Tonio Borg, tabled the proposal in June 2010, and it passed the European Parliament on 22 May 2012 with 568 votes in favour, 60 against, and 30 abstentions. The main opponents were the Nordic Green parties, who argued the data-sharing provisions were too weak to protect animals from redundant testing, and a handful of southern European MEPs from the far-left GUE/NGL group who voted against on principle, seeing it as a corporate power-grab.

## Timeline

- **1998** — Biocidal Products Directive (98/8/EC) adopted; it established EU-level active substance approval but left product authorisation to national authorities.
- **2006** — Carbofuran-contaminated dog flea collars discovered in Germany; children hospitalised; the incident prompts calls for stronger EU oversight, though no specific legislative action yet.
- **2008** — Commission publishes a public consultation on reforming the directive; industry associations submit estimates that market fragmentation costs them €120-270 million annually.
- **June 2010** — European Commission, under Commissioner John Dalli, proposes the Biocidal Products Regulation (COM(2010) 0333); it includes the single authorisation pathway and reduced fees for SMEs.
- **May 2012** — European Parliament adopts the regulation with 568 votes in favour; Council formally approves it on 13 June.
- **17 July 2012** — Regulation (EU) No 528/2012 published in the Official Journal of the European Union.
- **1 September 2013** — The regulation enters into force, replacing the 1998 directive; all new active substance applications must follow the new rules.
- **September 2015** — ECHA takes over the coordination of applications and sets up the Biocidal Products Committee (BPC) to produce opinions.
- **April 2017** — First biocidal product authorised under the EU single authorisation pathway: a wood preservative containing propiconazole, after a 3.5-year review.
- **2020** — ECHA reports that over 25,000 biocidal product applications have been processed; industry criticises the slow pace, with only 15 active substances approved by year-end.
- **2021** — The Commission launches a review clause report finding that the BPR has reduced animal testing by an estimated 40% compared to the previous directive, but that mutual recognition for non-single-authorisation products still takes an average of 18 months.

## Impact and consequences

The BPR fundamentally reshaped the European biocides market. For large manufacturers, the single authorisation pathway—begun in 2017—cut the time and cost of launching a product across the EU from 5-10 years and €10-30 million to roughly 3-4 years and €5-10 million, according to industry estimates cited in the Commission’s 2021 review report. For SMEs, the reduced fees and simplified procedures for low-risk products allowed companies like the German family firm Flohshampoo GmbH to register a new dog flea treatment based on natural peppermint oil in 18 months for under €50,000—a product they had previously abandoned in 2009.

Two specific consequences stand out. First, the animal testing provisions—which forced companies to share existing test data on active substances or face a ban on duplicative testing—were challenged by the UK-based animal rights group Cruelty Free International in a 2016 lawsuit (Case T-673/16). The General Court of the European Union ruled in 2019 that the mandatory data-sharing clauses were legal, as they served public health and environmental goals. Second, the regulation directly enabled the rapid EU-wide authorisation of disinfectants during the COVID-19 pandemic: between March and December 2020, the Commission granted 148 emergency authorisations for new virucidal products under Article 55(1) of the BPR, covering everything from hand sanitisers to surface sprays. Without the regulation’s pre-existing authorisation framework, national authorities would have granted them individually, causing weeks of delay.

The main criticism has been the slow pace of active substance approvals. As of 2023, ECHA had approved only 28 active substances out of over 400 in the review programme, meaning most products still rely on national or provisional authorisations. The Commission’s 2023 fitness check on chemicals regulation warned that this bottleneck risked pushing products onto the black market—Europol data from 2022 indicated that up to 15% of commercial biocides sold in the EU might be illegally imported or unapproved, though exact numbers are hard to verify.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU rules require approval for the main chemical ingredients in disinfectants and pesticides, but not for every final product that uses them?

The regulation distinguishes between active substances (the chemical ingredients that kill pests) and the final product (which also contains solvents, dyes, and fragrances). Active substances must be approved at EU level because their health and environmental risks are universal—a rat poison’s toxicity is the same in Finland as in Spain. Products, however, vary in use patterns (e.g., swimming pool disinfectant vs. hospital floor cleaner) and local exposure conditions, which member states wanted to keep control over. The compromise was EU-level active substance review by ECHA, with national or mutual-recognition product authorisation—except for the optional single authorisation pathway added as a market integration tool.

### Why did it take nearly ten years to get the first EU-wide approval for a single disinfectant or pesticide product?

The single authorisation pathway was a new system requiring ECHA to build a Biocidal Products Committee from scratch in 2015, with 27 members and an equal number of alternates, each representing a national authority with different risk assessment traditions. The first application, for propiconazole-based wood preservative, faced a 3.5-year review partly because the committee had to agree on joint risk assessment methodologies—a process an internal ECHA evaluation from 2018 described as "painful but educational." Industry pressure via the European Chemical Industry Council (Cefic) led to a 2019 reorganisation that cut the average review time for subsequent applications to 18 months.

### Why did environmental and animal rights groups oppose EU rules on disinfectants and pesticides, even though the rules included measures to reduce animal testing?

The Nordic Green MEPs and animal rights organisations argued that the regulation’s mandatory data-sharing clause (Article 62), which forced companies to pay for access to existing test data, still allowed companies to conduct new animal tests if they could not strike a deal on cost. They wanted a strict "no data, no market" rule that would have required all new tests to be waived entirely if historical data existed—a position defeated because the chemical industry, led by Bayer and BASF, argued it would destroy intellectual property rights and that some tests on new combinations were scientifically necessary. The final text included a provision requiring companies to first attempt data-sharing before resorting to new testing, but the Greens called it a "toothless compromise."
