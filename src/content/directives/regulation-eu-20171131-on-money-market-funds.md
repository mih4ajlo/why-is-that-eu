## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2017/1131 – commonly called the Money Market Funds Regulation (MMFR) – establishes a single set of prudential rules for money market funds (MMFs) across the European Union. MMFs are short-term, low-risk investment funds that provide cash management tools for corporations, governments, and institutional investors. They typically aim to maintain a stable net asset value (NAV).

The regulation divides MMFs into two types: short-term MMFs (with a weighted average maturity of 60 days) and standard MMFs (up to 180 days). Within these, it allows three valuation methods – constant NAV (CNAV) for public debt funds and low-volatility NAV (LVNAV) for other short-term funds, plus variable NAV (VNAV) available to all. The law mandates daily liquidity requirements (at least 10% of assets maturing overnight, 30% within a week), diversification limits (max 5% exposure to any single issuer, with a 20% issuer limit for repo transactions), and strict stress-testing procedures.

## Why was it introduced?

In September 2008, the American Reserve Primary Fund “broke the buck” – its NAV fell below $1 per share because of losses on Lehman Brothers debt – triggering a massive run that forced the fund to freeze redemptions and set off a chain reaction throughout the global short-term funding market. Within days, money market fund investors worldwide pulled hundreds of billions of dollars, exposing a critical fragility in the shadow banking system. The event demonstrated that MMFs, despite being marketed as cash equivalents, could suddenly become illiquid and destabilise the financial system.

The structural tension was clear: MMFs promised stable value and instant liquidity while investing in risky short-term debt, creating a classic bank-run vulnerability. But unlike banks, they lacked capital buffers and had no central bank backstop. In the EU, the market had grown to roughly €1 trillion in assets under management by 2012, according to the Commission's impact assessment. National rules varied widely – France and Ireland were major hubs, while Germany and the UK took more conservative stances – creating fragmentation and the risk of regulatory arbitrage.

Coalition dynamics were intense. Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier proposed the regulation in September 2013 after a two-year consultation. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) had recommended tight constraints, including a ban on all CNAV funds. But the asset management industry, led by the European Fund and Asset Management Association (EFAMA) and large players like BlackRock, JPMorgan and Amundi, lobbied fiercely against a full ban, arguing it would kill a vital source of corporate short-term financing. A coalition of finance ministries from the UK, Luxembourg and Ireland, where MMF industries were largest, also opposed the strictest rules. In the European Parliament, the Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee was divided, but a compromise emerged under rapporteur Neena Gill (S&D, UK) that preserved CNAV for public debt and created the LVNAV category as a half-step.

The timing was critical. The US had already enacted reform in 2014 (SEC Rule 2a-7), introducing a floating NAV for institutional prime MMFs. Without EU action, the global reform momentum would stall and European MMFs could attract flight capital from the US. Meanwhile, the 2011-2012 eurozone sovereign debt crisis had shown that even European MMFs were vulnerable – funds holding Irish and Greek bank paper had faced redemption pressure. The Commission's 2012 Green Paper on shadow banking explicitly linked MMF stability to broader financial resilience.

What changed to unblock the deadlock? The 2016 trilogue negotiations produced the LVNAV mechanism, allowing a quasi-stable NAV of plus/minus 20 basis points from €1, provided the fund met strict liquidity and diversification thresholds. This gave the industry a workable model while addressing financial stability concerns. The final text was adopted in June 2017, with the Council formally approving it on 20 June 2017. The regulation entered into force in July and applied from July 2018 (with some provisions phased in by January 2019).

## Timeline

- **2008** — Reserve Primary Fund breaks the buck; global MMF runs expose systemic gaps.
- **2012** — European Commission publishes Green Paper on shadow banking; ESMA begins technical advice on MMFs.
- **2013** — Commissioner Barnier proposes the MMF Regulation on 4 September.
- **2015** — European Parliament and Council reach divergent positions; trilogue negotiations stall over CNAV ban.
- **2016** — Under Luxembourg Presidency, trilogue agreement reached introducing LVNAV funds.
- **2017** — Regulation adopted by Council on 20 June; published in Official Journal on 13 July; enters into force 20 July.
- **2018** — Application date for most provisions (21 July 2018); LVNAV and some liquidity rules apply from 21 January 2019.
- **2019** — ESMA publishes first stress-test scenarios and guidelines; National competent authorities begin supervision.
- **2022** — ESMA reports that the market has adjusted to new rules; no major runs observed during COVID-19 market stress (March 2020), though some outflows occurred.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation forced European MMF managers to restructure their funds: prime CNAV funds that did not qualify as LVNAV or public debt CNAV had to convert to VNAV or close. By 2020, ESMA recorded that over 80% of EU MMF assets were in LVNAV or VNAV structures. The industry initially resisted the liquidity requirements – the 30% weekly threshold was considered high – but compliance was achieved by shifting portfolios toward government and repo assets. Some smaller funds, especially in Ireland, merged or liquidated rather than incur compliance costs.

The regulation did not eliminate runs entirely. During the March 2020 COVID-19 dash for cash, European MMFs saw outflows of about 6% of total assets over two weeks, according to the European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB). LVNAV funds held up better than prime CNAV funds (which had been largely phased out) but still faced redemption pressure. The ESRB subsequently recommended tightening the LVNAV valuation buffer in 2023. The only major enforcement action came in 2021 when the Dutch regulator fined a small fund for failing to meet weekly liquidity thresholds – a sign that authorities are taking the rules seriously.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU create special rules for money market funds after the 2008 crisis?

The 2008 breaking of the buck exposed that money market funds, sold as safe alternatives to bank deposits, could suddenly freeze redemptions and trigger runs. Because they operate outside traditional banking regulation but carry similar stability risks, the EU intervened to require liquidity cushions, diversification, and valuation discipline to prevent a repeat.

### What is the difference between a CNAV, LVNAV, and VNAV money market fund?

A Constant NAV (CNAV) fund maintains a fixed €1 share price but is now only allowed for public debt funds. A Low-Volatility NAV (LVNAV) fund can keep a stable price within a 20-basis-point band around €1 if it meets strict liquidity rules; outside that band its shares float. A Variable NAV (VNAV) fund’s share price fluctuates with the market value of its assets. The regulation restricts CNAV to reduce the risk of sudden price breaks.

### Do EU money market fund rules also apply to funds managed outside the EU?

Yes, but only if they are marketed to EU investors. The regulation requires any MMF domiciled in an EU member state to comply. Non-EU funds sold in the EU must also meet equivalent standards, subject to ESMA assessment. This prevents regulatory arbitrage by funds based in the Channel Islands or other offshore centres.

### How did the money market fund industry fight the new rules?

Asset managers and funds associations (notably EFAMA, BlackRock, and JPMorgan) argued that a blanket ban on CNAV funds would destroy the corporate short-term financing market and push business to the US. They lobbied national finance ministries and MEPs heavily, producing impact studies warning of job losses in the fund industry. The creation of LVNAV funds was a direct result of this lobbying – a compromise that preserved the stable-NAV concept for most funds while adding safeguards.
