## What is it?

The Floods Directive (2007/60/EC) is EU legislation that obliges member states to take a structured, three-step approach to flood risk management: first, produce a preliminary flood risk assessment; second, create flood hazard maps and flood risk maps for areas with significant potential risk; and third, develop flood risk management plans focused on prevention, protection, and preparedness. The directive explicitly requires coordination on shared river basins and coastal areas, meaning that countries must exchange information and align plans where floods cross borders.

The directive does not set specific engineering standards or prescribe how much to spend. Instead, it establishes a common framework so that national authorities plan consistently, involve the public, and update their assessments every six years. It sits alongside the Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC) and the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive, but was created to fill a gap: the earlier laws dealt with water quality and project-level impacts, not the specific geographic and transboundary nature of flood risk.

## Why was it introduced?

In August 2002, catastrophic floods swept across central Europe, hitting the Elbe and Danube river basins hardest. The flooding killed over 100 people, forced the evacuation of tens of thousands, and caused damage estimated by the European Commission at roughly €15 billion across Austria, the Czech Republic, Germany, Hungary, Poland, Slovakia, and Russia. Germany alone saw the Elbe rise to levels not recorded in centuries, inundating the historic city of Dresden. The disaster was not just a national crisis—it was a transboundary one. Floodwaters that overwhelmed the Czech Republic hours later submerged parts of Saxony, revealing how little coordination existed between upstream and downstream authorities.

The immediate political response was fragmented. The EU had a Solidarity Fund (created after the 1997 Oder floods) to disburse emergency aid, but it offered no preventive structure. The European Environment Agency had warned in 2001 that climate change would increase the frequency and intensity of extreme floods, yet no EU-wide framework required countries to map flood-prone areas or plan jointly. In the aftermath of 2002, member states began pushing for action. The German federal government, under Chancellor Gerhard Schröder, and the Czech Republic both called for better early-warning systems and joint river-basin management. The European Parliament's environment committee, chaired at the time by Swedish MEP Karl-Heinz Florenz, took up the issue.

The European Commission, then led by President Romano Prodi, issued a communication in 2004 titled "Flood risk management – flood prevention, protection and mitigation" that laid out the case for a directive. Commissioner for Environment Stavros Dimas, who took office in 2004, made the proposal a priority. The formal legislative proposal (COM(2006) 15 final) was tabled in January 2006. Negotiations in the Council and Parliament moved relatively quickly—a sign of the political consensus forged by the 2002 disaster. The Council adopted its common position in April 2007, and the Parliament gave its approval in September. The directive was signed into law on 23 October 2007.

Opposition came mainly from a few member states that feared additional administrative burden and from industry groups concerned that mandatory mapping could affect property values or insurance markets. The Confederation of European Business (BusinessEurope) argued that the directive overlapped with existing national laws. However, the widespread public shock from 2002 and the clear transboundary failures made it hard to resist. No serious alternative—such as a purely voluntary framework or better-funded national programmes—gained traction because the 2002 floods had proven that unilateral efforts could not manage shared river systems.

What changed to unblock earlier hesitations was the sheer scale of the 2002 event. Previous attempts to address flood risk had stalled because floods were seen as national problems. The 2002 floods killed people in multiple countries simultaneously and demonstrated that upstream land use decisions (e.g., drainage, dam releases) could cause downstream damage across borders. The directive explicitly recognised this by requiring that flood risk management plans "take account of the potential impact of floods on human health, the environment, cultural heritage and economic activity" and coordinate with neighbouring states in the same river basin district.

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced every EU member state to systematically map flood risk—not just for rivers but also for coastal areas and flash floods. Countries that had not previously produced such maps (e.g., some Mediterranean states) had to build new administrative capacity. Transboundary coordination improved significantly: for the Rhine, the Elbe, the Danube, and the Meuse, joint flood risk management plans now exist, coordinated through international river basin commissions. The Commission's 2019 implementation report noted that "almost all member states have established flood hazard and risk maps" and that "cross-border cooperation has been strengthened," though it warned that many plans remained too vague on concrete measures.

Private insurers and real estate developers were directly affected: the maps became a public reference for flood insurance premiums and land-use planning. In Germany, for instance, the maps led to stricter building restrictions in designated flood zones. Smaller municipalities sometimes struggled with the mapping costs, but the EU's Solidarity Fund and Cohesion Policy funds helped finance the work. The 2021 floods in Germany and Belgium—which killed over 200—tested the directive's effectiveness. While early-warning systems had improved, critics argued that land-use planning had not kept pace, and the Commission later initiated an evaluation of the directive's implementation.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU make countries manage flood risk across borders?

The directive requires countries sharing a river basin or coastal area to coordinate their flood risk assessments, maps, and management plans. They must exchange data on flood hazards (e.g., where water is likely to spread) and agree on measures such as retention areas or dyke alignments. International river commissions—like the International Commission for the Protection of the Danube River—provide a forum for this cooperation. If a country fails to coordinate, the European Commission can launch an infringement procedure.

### What major disaster prompted the EU's flood legislation?

The catastrophic floods of August 2002 in central Europe, which killed over 100 people and caused damage estimated at €15 billion by the European Commission. The floods crossed national borders along the Elbe and Danube rivers, exposing the absence of any EU‑wide framework for joint flood risk management. Before 2002, flood policy was almost entirely national; the disaster created the political will to act at EU level.

### Do EU countries have to publish flood risk maps, and who can see them?

Yes. Member states must produce flood hazard maps (showing the extent of flooding for low, medium, and high probability events) and flood risk maps (showing potential damage to people, property, and the environment). These maps must be made available to the public, typically through national water agencies' websites. The directive also requires that the public be consulted when the flood risk management plans are being drawn up.
