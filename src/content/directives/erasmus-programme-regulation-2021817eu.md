## What is it?

Erasmus+ is the European Union's 25-year-old programme that funds student exchanges, vocational training, youth projects, and sport cooperation across Europe and beyond. The 2021 regulation (2021/817/EU) sets the legal framework for the programme's 2021–2027 cycle, with a total budget of €26.2 billion (current prices). It expands access to include people with fewer opportunities, integrates a green travel incentive, and adds a European Youth Together strand to support youth-led networks.

The regulation replaced the previous Erasmus+ Regulation (1288/2013/EU) and merged several earlier programmes—Lifelong Learning Programme, Youth in Action, and others—under a single umbrella. It is managed by the European Commission's Directorate-General for Education, Youth, Sport and Culture (DG EAC), with national agencies in each member state handling local applications and disbursements.

## Why was it introduced?

The Erasmus+ programme began in 1987 as an intergovernmental agreement pushed by European Commission President Jacques Delors, who saw student mobility as essential to forging a "people's Europe." That year, 3,244 students took part. By 2018, the number had grown to over 600,000 participants annually, according to the Commission's 2020 mid-term evaluation. Yet the pre-2021 regulation had structural problems: it was too complex, underfunded relative to demand, and did not reach disadvantaged groups. The Commission's impact assessment for the 2021–2027 proposal noted that only 4% of participants came from low socio-economic backgrounds, and the programme was often inaccessible to people with disabilities, rural youth, and early school-leavers.

The political coalition for expansion was broad. The European Parliament's Culture and Education Committee, led by rapporteurs such as Milan Zver (EPP) and Petra Kammerevert (S&D), consistently demanded a tripling of the budget. Member states in the Council were more cautious. Germany and France pushed for a strong programme, while the so-called "Frugal Four" (Austria, Denmark, the Netherlands, Sweden) resisted large increases. The breakthrough came during the negotiation of the Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) for 2021–2027 in 2020. The COVID-19 crisis gave new urgency to investing in digital education and youth recovery. Commission President Ursula von der Leyen promised a doubled budget, which was accepted as part of the MFF compromise.

The timing also reflected demographic and geostrategic pressures. With the United Kingdom leaving the EU, the programme risked losing one of its top destination countries. The UK had hosted 27,000 incoming Erasmus students per year in 2018–19, according to the UK's Universities UK International. The regulation was designed to deepen ties with non-EU partner countries through a new "International Dimension," including a dedicated budget for cooperation with the Western Balkans, Eastern Partnership countries, and the Mediterranean region.

Opposition came from budget hawks and from voices that questioned the programme's efficiency. The European Court of Auditors' 2012 special report had criticised Erasmus for low completion rates and weak monitoring of learning outcomes. The Commission responded by simplifying grant rules, introducing a single simplified cost model, and requiring national agencies to publish project results.

## Timeline

- **1976** — The EU (then EEC) adopts a resolution on a first action programme for higher education cooperation.
- **1985** — Commission President Jacques Delors proposes a dedicated exchange programme; the Erasmus pilot starts with 11 universities.
- **1987** — The first Erasmus regulation (87/327/EEC) is adopted; 3,244 students participate that year.
- **2007–2013** — The Lifelong Learning Programme bundles Erasmus with other mobility schemes; 2.5 million students participate.
- **2013** — Regulation (1288/2013/EU) merges all education, youth, and sport programmes under the single Erasmus+ brand, with a budget of €14.7 billion for 2014–2020.
- **2018** — The European Commission proposes a new Erasmus+ regulation with a budget of €30 billion for 2021–2027.
- **2020** — EU leaders agree the MFF; Erasmus+ gets €26.2 billion, a doubling of the 2014–2020 envelope. The regulation is adopted by the European Parliament and Council in May 2021.
- **2021** — The new regulation (2021/817/EU) enters into force on January 1; calls for proposals open in March.
- **2023** — The Commission launches the first "European Degree" pilot under the new framework.

## Impact and consequences

The 2021 regulation more than doubled the programme's budget, making Erasmus+ the largest EU-funded education programme in the world. By 2023, annual participation had rebounded to over 600,000 after a COVID dip, according to the Commission's 2023 Erasmus+ Dashboard. The inclusion priorities led to a 22% increase in participants from under-represented groups in the first two years, though the Commission's 2024 interim report noted that reaching the most marginalised remains challenging.

The green travel incentive—supporting train and bus travel over flights—was used by 38% of mobility participants in 2022, according to the European Commission's 2023 report on the programme's environmental impact. The new "Virtual Exchange" component expanded cooperation with non-EU countries, with 30,000 youth participating in online dialogues in 2022–23.

Countries with the highest uptake include France, Germany, Spain, and Italy, each sending over 50,000 students per year. The UK, after leaving the Erasmus programme in 2020, launched its own "Turing Scheme," which the Commission's impact assessment estimated would reduce UK–EU mobility by 30–40%. The regulation's non-EU partnership budget (€1.4 billion) helped rebalance that loss.

The regulation also introduced a "European University" networks initiative, which had funded 50 cross-border alliances by 2024, covering over 500 higher education institutions. Critics argue that the budget remains insufficient to meet the demand: the Commission's 2022 evaluation of the first year of implementation found that only one in four eligible applicants received a grant.

## Key questions answered

### Can any European student study abroad with full funding?

Yes and no. Any student enrolled in a higher education institution in an Erasmus+ programme country can apply for a grant covering travel, living costs, and tuition (up to the full cost). However, funding levels vary by country and duration—monthly grants range from €250 to €600 depending on the host country's cost of living. In practice, demand often exceeds supply: national agencies allocate places based on academic merit and motivation, and only about one in four applicants receives a grant, according to the Commission.

### Is Erasmus only for university students?

No. The 2021 regulation expanded the programme to cover not just higher education students but also vocational education and training (VET) learners, school pupils (from age 13), adult learners, and youth workers. The "Apprenticeships and VET" strand has grown rapidly; in 2022, 120,000 VET participants went abroad, up from 80,000 in 2018, according to the Erasmus+ Annual Report 2022.

### Why did the budget double?

The doubling of the budget to €26.2 billion was driven by three factors: the European Parliament's persistent demands, the post-COVID recovery need to invest in youth skills, and the political compromise on the 2021–2027 Multiannual Financial Framework. The UK's exit also freed up a share of the EU budget that was reallocated. Commission President Ursula von der Leyen personally championed the increase, calling the programme "a symbol of European integration."

### What happens after the programme ends in 2027?

The Commission has already begun consulting on the post-2027 Erasmus+ programme. The 2024 mid-term review recommended maintaining the budget level and further simplifying rules. The European Parliament has called for a tripling of the budget and the introduction of a "European Student Card" to reduce administrative barriers. Negotiations will be part of the next MFF round, starting in 2025.
