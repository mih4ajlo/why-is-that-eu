## What is it?

Directive 2019/944/EU is the core legal framework for the EU's internal electricity market, replacing the 2009 Third Energy Package. It sets binding rules on market liberalisation, consumer empowerment, and network regulation. The directive requires all member states to ensure suppliers can compete freely, households can switch providers within 24 hours at no cost, and every household must be given a smart meter unless a cost-benefit analysis justifies a delay.

Beyond consumer rights, the directive strengthens unbundling – separating generation, transmission, and supply – and creates a framework for dynamic electricity pricing, aggregators, and demand response. It is part of the "Clean Energy for All Europeans" package, and directly linked to the 2019 Electricity Regulation (which governs cross-border trade) and the Renewable Energy Directive.

## Why was it introduced?

For two decades after the first liberalisation directives of the 1990s, Europe's electricity markets remained stubbornly national and opaque. By 2014, the European Commission's impact assessment for the new directive found that fewer than 6% of households had switched supplier in the previous year in countries like France, Slovakia, and Poland. Consumers faced switching fees, waiting periods of up to six weeks, and contracts buried in fine print. Incumbent companies – EDF in France, Enel in Italy, RWE in Germany – controlled grid data and resisted sharing it with rivals.

The trigger came in 2015 when the Juncker Commission launched the "Energy Union" strategy. Energy Commissioner Miguel Arias Cañete – a Spanish centre-right politician – made consumer empowerment a flagship goal. The formal proposal landed in November 2016 as part of the "Winter Package". Rapporteur MEP Zdzisław Krasnodębski (European Conservatives) worked with centre-left MEPs to push for stronger consumer rights than the Commission originally proposed.

The political coalition that carried the directive was broad but fragile. The European Parliament adopted its position in December 2017 with 514 votes in favour, 127 against. The main opposition came from some member states (notably Germany and France) that feared losing control over regulated tariffs and network charges. The final compromise allowed a transition period for regulated prices but banned them for most consumers from 2023.

A critical unblocking factor was the Paris Agreement of 2015, which reframed market liberalisation as a climate tool: only competitive markets could integrate renewable energy efficiently. Major renewable lobby groups (WindEurope, SolarPower Europe) pushed hard for faster switching and smart meters.

The directive also solved a structural tension from the 2009 package: safe-harbour rules for national monopolies had created windfall profits for incumbents while cross-subsidising their own generation. In 2013, the Commission's energy advisory group ACER documented that incumbents in several countries had gross margins of 30-40% while blocking new entrants.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Third Energy Package (Directive 2009/72/EC) brings unbundling and a right to switch, but implementation is weak; switching times remain long.
- **2015** — Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker's Energy Union strategy singles out consumer rights as a priority.
- **November 2016** — Commission publishes the "Clean Energy for All Europeans" package, including a new Electricity Market Directive.
- **December 2017** — European Parliament adopts its negotiating position (514-127). Rapporteur Krasnodębski celebrates "a new deal for consumers".
- **May 2019** — Directive (EU) 2019/944 enters into force.
- **31 December 2020** — Transposition deadline for member states.
- **1 January 2021** — Application date for most provisions. Several states (including Poland, Germany, Czechia) miss deadlines; Commission opens infringement cases.
- **2021–2023** — Smart meter rollouts accelerate. ACER's 2022 Market Monitoring Report shows 17% of EU households switched supplier in 2021, up from 6% in 2014.
- **2024** — Target date for 80% smart meter coverage in member states with positive cost-benefit analyses (most except Germany and some others).

## Impact and consequences

The most tangible effect is the consumer right to switch supplier within 24 hours – with no fee. The European Commission's 2023 Consumer Markets Scoreboard found that household switching rates had more than doubled since the directive's adoption. In the Netherlands, 38% of households switched in 2022; in the UK (which left the EU but implemented similar rules), comparable numbers.

Smart meter deployment, however, remains uneven. According to the Commission's 2020 Smart Metering Deployment Report, only 13 member states had completed cost-benefit analyses as of 2019, and only three (Spain, Sweden, Italy) were close to full rollout. The directive forced the rest to accelerate: by 2024, the EU average penetration stands at roughly 60%, still short of the 80% target.

The directive faced its first major enforcement test during the 2021–2022 energy price crisis when several countries – including France and Spain – reintroduced regulated tariffs for vulnerable consumers, contrary to the directive's spirit. The Commission launched infringement procedures against France in 2023 for maintaining excessive price caps. The case is ongoing.

Industry resistance was strongest in Germany, where incumbent utilities (E.ON, RWE) and municipal utilities (Stadtwerke) challenged the smart meter mandate in court. Germany only transposed the directive in August 2021, after a referral to the European Court of Justice.

## Key questions answered

### Why can I easily switch electricity providers in Europe now?

The 2019 directive forced all member states to make switching possible within 24 hours at no cost, and banned any penalties for changing supplier. Previously, companies could impose waiting up to six weeks, charge switching fees of €50 or more, or demand complex forms. The change came after a decade of complaints from consumer groups and under pressure from the European Parliament.

### Are smart meters mandatory in every EU home?

Not exactly. The directive requires member states to deploy smart meters for 80% of households by 2024, but only if a cost-benefit analysis shows it makes economic sense. Countries like Germany, where upfront costs were estimated at €5–8 billion, delayed and opted for a slower rollout. If the analysis is positive – as in France, Sweden, or Italy – the rollout is compulsory; households cannot refuse.

### How does EU law stop energy companies from overcharging?

The directive bans regulated prices for most consumers as of 2023, except for vulnerable groups during emergencies. Instead, it requires that all bills include a "disaggregated" breakdown of the energy cost, network charge, taxes, and margin. Suppliers must also make it easy to compare offers online. Breaches can trigger national fines and EU infringement cases.
