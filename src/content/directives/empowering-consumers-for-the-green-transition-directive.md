## What is it?

The Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition Directive (EU 2024/825) is an amendment to the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC) and the Consumer Rights Directive (2011/83/EU). It bans several types of misleading sustainability claims and introduces new mandatory information requirements about product durability and repairability.

Specifically, the Directive outlaws generic environmental claims like "eco-friendly" or "green" unless backed by recognised certification schemes or third-party verification. It also prohibits claims that a product has a "neutral," "reduced," or "positive" environmental impact based solely on carbon offsetting schemes. Sellers must now inform consumers about a product's existence of a commercial guarantee of durability and its duration, and provide repair scores or other relevant repair information where Member States have established such systems.

## Why was it introduced?

The Directive was driven by a confluence of evidence demonstrating widespread consumer deception and a structural failure in the single market for green products. The European Commission's 2020 "Sweep" of online websites—coordinated by the Consumer Protection Cooperation (CPC) Network—found that in over half of cases (53.3%), traders failed to provide sufficient information to substantiate "green" claims. A more alarming 42% of claims examined were deemed potentially false or deceptive. This wasn't just a minor oversight; it was a systemic market failure where companies could gain a competitive advantage through unsubstantiated eco-labeling, creating a "race to the bottom" for genuine sustainability.

The political coalition driving this was a classic "green deal" alliance of the European Parliament's centre-right EPP, the Socialists & Democrats (S&D), and the Greens/EFA, supported by the von der Leyen Commission as a flagship deliverable of the European Green Deal timeline. The directive built on the earlier 2019 "New Deal for Consumers" (Directive 2019/2161/EU), which had already strengthened consumer enforcement powers, but stopped short of directly regulating environmental claims. Industry opposition was sharpest from sectors with weak or commoditised eco-labels. The European Chemical Industry Council (Cefic) warned that banning "climate neutral" claims based on offsetting would undermine voluntary industry efforts, while BusinessEurope argued the rules would impose disproportionate compliance costs on SMEs. However, the coalition held because consumer trust in "green" labels had collapsed—the Commission’s 2020 "Consumer Market Study on Environmental Claims" found that only 33% of EU consumers trusted businesses' environmental claims.

The specific event that unblocked final agreement in the Council was the escalating public and regulatory backlash against greenwashing in the fast fashion and aviation sectors. In October 2023, the Dutch Authority for Consumers and Markets (ACM) fined KLM €450,000 for misleading "Fly CO2 Zero" claims based on carbon offsets, ruling the airline failed to prove its sustainability claims. The ruling created a political imperative for uniform EU rules, as multiple Member States (Netherlands, France, Germany, and Denmark) had begun developing their own national enforcement approaches—the Commission’s 2023 impact assessment noted this fragmentation risked raising compliance costs for cross-border traders by up to 20%. The timing of the Commission's proposal (March 2022) was also strategic: it was published just weeks after the UNEP "Climate Plan" report showed that corporate net-zero pledges based primarily on offsets were not reducing emissions, directly undermining the credibility of offset-based claims.

Alternatives considered during the legislative process included a mandatory EU-wide Green Claims Directive (which later became a separate, more detailed proposal in March 2023), and a voluntary "Green Mark" system. The Commission's impact assessment concluded that a voluntary system would fail to address the problem because consumers could not distinguish between certified and non-certified claims without mandatory substantiation rules.

## Timeline

- **2015** — European Commission begins "Fitness Check" of EU consumer law, identifying greenwashing as a growing issue.
- **2020** — Commission's "Sweep" of 344 websites finds 42% of green claims potentially false or deceptive.
- **March 2022** — Commission proposes the Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition Directive as part of the Circular Economy Action Plan.
- **September 2022** — European Parliament's IMCO Committee adopts its position with 48 votes in favour, 2 against, and 1 abstention.
- **May 2023** — Council of the EU adopts its general approach.
- **September 2023** — EU institutions reach trilogue agreement on the final text.
- **January 2024** — European Parliament adopts the final text by 593 votes to 21, with 14 abstentions.
- **February 2024** — Council formally adopts the Directive.
- **March 26, 2024** — Directive enters into force.
- **September 27, 2026** — Member States must transpose the Directive into national law.
- **September 27, 2027** — Application date for the new rules.

## Impact and consequences

The Directive directly affects millions of consumer products sold in the EU, from fast fashion to electronics to household cleaning products. The Commission’s 2022 impact assessment estimated the rules would reduce compliance costs for businesses by up to €12 billion over ten years compared to a scenario of fragmented national laws. However, BusinessEurope’s 2023 position paper warned that compliance costs for SMEs could still range from €1,500 to €10,000 per product line for substantiation and label redesign.

The most immediate consequence was felt by industries heavily reliant on carbon offset claims. Airlines (Ryanair, Lufthansa, Air France-KLM) and fuels companies (BP, Shell) had to rapidly adjust marketing strategies. In September 2024, Ryanair faced a preliminary finding by Italy’s antitrust authority AGCM for "CO2 neutral" flight claims, as the new rules made such claims presumptively illegal unless backed by third-party certification. The fast fashion sector, where H&M and Zara had heavily marketed "conscious" and "sustainable" collections, faced the most significant compliance challenge. The Commission’s 2023 "EU Ecolabel" report noted that less than 1% of textile products sold in the EU carried the EU Ecolabel, exposing the gap between marketing claims and verifiable standards.

The durability information requirements are having a more gradual but structural impact. France’s repairability index, introduced for electronics in 2021, is serving as a model for the new mandatory information. The European Environmental Bureau (EEB) estimates the rules could extend the average lifespan of smartphones by two to three years, potentially reducing e-waste by 5 million tonnes annually by 2030.

Member States are expected to enforce the rules through their consumer protection authorities. The CPC Regulation (2017/2394) allows for coordinated enforcement actions. The first major test case will likely involve a cross-border sweep of online marketplaces scheduled for 2027.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU regulate greenwashing through consumer law rather than environmental law?

The Commission chose the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive as the legal basis because greenwashing is primarily a problem of *deception* rather than *environmental damage*. Under consumer law, regulators can act faster by seeking injunctions and fines for misleading claims under a lower evidentiary threshold than proving actual environmental harm would require under environmental law. This enabled the EU to bypass the longer and more contested co-decision process for environmental legislation and directly leverage existing national consumer enforcement authorities.

### Why does the Directive ban "climate neutral" claims based on carbon offsets?

The ban is a direct response to systematic evidence that offset-based claims mislead consumers. The Commission’s 2020 study found that 73% of consumers interpreted "climate neutral" labels as meaning the product itself had a neutral footprint, not that the company had purchased offsets. The UNEP "Net-Zero Asset Owner Alliance" report (2023) showed that many offset credits did not represent real, additional, or permanent emission reductions. The Directive requires that any residual carbon impact claims must be based on actual reductions within the company's own value chain, not on offsetting.

### How does this Directive differ from the separate Green Claims Directive proposed in 2023?

This Directive sets minimum standards by banning the *most egregious* forms of greenwashing (generic claims, offset-based claims, planned obsolescence). The separate Green Claims Directive (2023/0085/COD), still under negotiation, establishes a more detailed verification framework requiring all *substantiated* environmental claims to be verified by an accredited third party before publication. The Empowering Consumers Directive is thus the simpler, faster "floor" while the Green Claims Directive will be the more stringent "ceiling."
