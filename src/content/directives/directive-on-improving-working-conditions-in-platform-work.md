## What is it?

The Platform Work Directive (PWD) establishes a legal framework for determining the employment status of people working through digital labour platforms like Uber, Deliveroo, and TaskRabbit. It introduces a rebuttable presumption of an employment relationship: if a platform controls certain conditions of work — such as setting pay, supervising performance, or restricting freedom to choose hours — the worker is presumed to be an employee, shifting the burden of proof to the platform to argue otherwise.

The directive also sets the EU's first rules on algorithmic management, requiring platforms to inform workers about automated monitoring and decision-making systems, and to ensure human oversight of decisions like account deactivation or pay changes. It mandates transparency on how algorithms assign tasks, set prices, and evaluate performance.

Member states have until 2 December 2026 to transpose the directive into national law, with application from that date. It represents a major intervention in the gig economy, which the European Commission estimated in 2021 employed over 28 million people in the EU, with that number projected to reach 43 million by 2025.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origins lie in a cascade of court rulings and labour disputes across Europe that exposed a fundamental mismatch between existing employment law and the reality of platform work. The most dramatic trigger came from Spain, where in 2020 the Supreme Court ruled that delivery riders for Glovo were employees, not freelancers — a decision that catalysed the so-called "Riders Law" (Ley Rider) passed in 2021, the first national legislation creating a presumption of employment for platform workers. This Spanish law became a template for EU action.

The structural tension was clear: existing employment classification rules, designed for mid-20th century industrial relations, treated self-employment as a binary choice. Platforms exploited this by classifying workers as independent contractors, thereby avoiding minimum wage, sick pay, holiday entitlement, social security contributions, and collective bargaining rights. A 2019 study by the European Foundation for the Improvement of Living and Working Conditions (Eurofound) found that 54% of platform workers were classified as self-employed even when their work was closely controlled by algorithms — a contradiction the Commission's 2021 impact assessment described as creating a "regulatory race to the bottom" in labour standards.

The coalition that drove the legislation was led by the European Parliament's Employment Committee, with rapporteur Elisabetta Gualmini (S&D, Italy) and shadows from the Greens and Left. The centre-right European People's Party (EPP) was split: while some MEPs backed stronger worker protections, others — particularly German and Polish members — warned of destroying "flexibility" in the sector. The business lobby mounted a fierce campaign. Uber commissioned a study by Copenhagen Economics in 2021 claiming that reclassification would cost the platform economy €5.8 billion annually and eliminate up to 500,000 jobs across Europe — figures the Commission's impact assessment disputed as "worst-case scenarios not supported by empirical evidence." Deliveroo, Just Eat Takeaway, and Wolt all lobbied national governments directly, with Deliveroo threatening in 2022 to withdraw from multiple EU markets if the directive passed in its original, stricter form.

The timing was shaped by three factors. First, the COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the centrality of platform workers to essential services like food delivery and grocery shopping, while also exposing their lack of protections — a contradiction that became politically unsustainable. Second, the 2021 Spanish Riders Law demonstrated that national action was possible and created pressure for an EU-wide floor to prevent a patchwork of rules. Third, the von der Leyen Commission had made "fair working conditions" a pillar of its 2020 European Pillar of Social Rights Action Plan, giving political cover for legislative action.

What changed to unblock earlier deadlock was the failure of the "co-regulation" approach favoured by the previous Juncker Commission. Between 2017 and 2019, the Commission had instead pushed a non-binding "quality framework" for platform work, encouraging voluntary agreements between platforms and worker representatives. This produced nothing: no major platform signed onto any meaningful commitment. The 2020 ECJ ruling in *B v Yodel Delivery Network* (Case C-692/19), which held that a delivery driver could be genuinely self-employed if they organised their own substitutes and hours, further muddied the waters. But it was the Spanish law that broke the dam, providing a proof-of-concept for a presumption of employment.

A key alternative considered by the Commission was the "UK model" of a third category — "dependent contractor" status — similar to the UK's worker/employee distinction. This was rejected as legally complex and potentially creating a two-tier workforce. Instead, the Commission opted for a rebuttable presumption, designed to be compatible with existing national employment definitions while shifting the burden of proof.

## Timeline

- **2021** — Spain passes its "Riders Law" (Ley 12/2021), creating a presumption of employment for delivery platform workers; the European Commission publishes a public consultation on platform work, receiving over 1,500 responses
- **9 December 2021** — Commission President Ursula von der Leyen and Social Affairs Commissioner Nicolas Schmit present the proposed Platform Work Directive
- **2022** — European Parliament and Council begin negotiations; Deliveroo exits Spain, citing the Riders Law, though analysts attribute the exit primarily to market saturation
- **1 February 2023** — European Parliament adopts its negotiating position (376 votes in favour, 212 against, 44 abstentions), including a stronger presumption of employment
- **June 2023** — Council adopts its general approach after months of deadlock; Germany, Greece, and Estonia block a stronger presumption, insisting on more flexibility for platforms to rebut it
- **November 2023** — Trialogues begin; the main sticking point is the scope of the presumption and the criteria that trigger it
- **March 2024** — A provisional political agreement is reached after 24-hour negotiations; the final text includes a list of five criteria — any two of which trigger the presumption — but allows member states to add or modify criteria
- **23 April 2024** — European Parliament approves the final text (505 votes in favour, 73 against, 21 abstentions)
- **14 October 2024** — Council formally adopts the directive
- **11 November 2024** — Directive enters into force (20 days after publication in the Official Journal)
- **2 December 2026** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law
- **2 December 2028** — Commission to report on implementation, including an assessment of the automatic reclassification of platform workers

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate consequence was a massive lobbying war that reshaped the text from the Commission's original ambition. The original 2021 proposal included a "default presumption" of employment triggered by any one of five criteria — meaning platforms had to prove workers were genuinely self-employed. By the final text, two criteria were needed, and member states could weaken them further. Germany and Estonia successfully argued for watering down the criteria on "effective control" of working time, a concession that labour unions called a "betrayal" of the original intent.

The directive has already influenced markets. Deliveroo, which had been a high-profile opponent, announced in 2023 that it was prepared to reclassify some riders in EU markets, though it simultaneously restructured its operations in Belgium and the Netherlands. Uber shifted its business model in several countries, moving from a "worker-for-hire" model to a "marketplace" where drivers can set their own prices — a structural change designed to sidestep the presumption of employment. As of 2024, Uber had reclassified over 150,000 drivers in France and Germany following court rulings, but the directive now generalises this requirement across the EU.

The hardest-hit member state is Spain, where the Riders Law already caused major disruption — Glovo was forced to reclassify 20,000 riders in 2022, and its parent company Delivery Hero took a €150 million provision. But enforcement has been uneven: a 2023 report by Spanish labour inspectorates found that 40% of platforms were still misclassifying workers two years after the law passed. The directive promises to create a level playing field across member states, but the flexibility to set criteria may lead to regulatory divergence — much like the GDPR's implementation issues.

Algorithmic management rules represent the first EU-wide regulation of how algorithms govern workers. Platforms must now inform workers of automated monitoring (like rating systems, GPS tracking, and screen recording) and decisions (like account deactivation or pay changes). Workers have the right to human review of automated decisions — a provision directly responding to Deliveroo's "Frank" algorithm, which was found by a 2021 Italian court to have unfairly penalised riders through a black-box rating system.

## Key questions answered

### Why was the rule that gig workers should be assumed employees weakened during negotiations?

Germany and Estonia led a bloc of member states — including Greece and Cyprus — that feared a strong presumption would trigger mass reclassification in their relatively small platform economies, causing job losses. They argued for "flexibility" to allow platforms to prove self-employment through contracts and actual working conditions. The final text's two-out-of-five criteria test was a compromise: it preserved the presumption's core but gave member states significant discretion to define the criteria, meaning a rider could be considered an employee in Spain but self-employed in Germany.

### How do the new EU rules control how apps and platforms use algorithms to manage workers?

The directive requires platforms to provide workers with clear information about all automated monitoring and decision-making systems that affect their working conditions. Workers must be told how algorithms set prices, assign tasks, evaluate performance, and make decisions on account suspension or pay adjustments. They have the right to human review of automated decisions, and platforms must conduct risk assessments on how algorithmic management affects workers' health, safety, and privacy. This directly responds to cases like the 2020 UK High Court action against Uber's algorithm that automatically terminated drivers' accounts based on passenger ratings without human oversight.

### Who fought against the new gig worker protections and what were their reasons?

The main opposition came from business associations like Digital Europe and the US Chamber of Commerce, along with individual platforms such as Uber, Deliveroo, and Wolt. Their argument was that reclassification would destroy the "flexibility" of gig work — a position echoed by the EPP's more conservative wing in the Parliament. In Council, the opposition was led by Germany (where the trade union IG Metall was divided, with some fearing job losses) and several Eastern European member states where platform work provided crucial income in low-wage economies. The closest they came to blocking it was in June 2023, when Germany, Estonia, and Greece withheld their support in the Council working group, requiring the Swedish presidency to broker a last-minute compromise.

### Will the new rules force platforms to reclassify millions of gig workers as employees?

Probably not immediately, but it will create strong legal pressure. Eurofound estimated in 2023 that 5.5 million EU platform workers would be reclassified under the original, stronger criteria. Under the final text, that number is likely lower — perhaps 3-4 million — largely because platforms can restructure their business models to avoid triggering the criteria. Uber's shift to a "marketplace" model in some countries is designed precisely to avoid the presumption. The directive's impact will depend on how member states set their criteria and how active national labour inspectorates are in enforcement. The first Commission report in 2028 will be the key test of whether the directive is working.
