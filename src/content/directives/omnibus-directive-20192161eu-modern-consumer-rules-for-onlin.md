## What is it?

The Omnibus Directive — formally Directive (EU) 2019/2161 — is a package of amendments to four existing EU consumer protection laws: the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC), the Consumer Rights Directive (2011/83/EU), the Unfair Contract Terms Directive (93/13/EEC), and the Price Indication Directive (98/6/EC). Rather than creating new legislation from scratch, the omnibus approach bundled updates across multiple directives into a single legislative vehicle, covering gaps exposed by the rise of e-commerce and digital platforms.

Its key provisions include: mandatory pre-contractual information requirements for digital services (e.g., specifying compatibility with free services that monetise user data), transparency rules for online marketplaces (requiring clear identification of who the seller is), rules against fake discounts (the price before a reduction must be the lowest price in the previous 30 days), and doubled maximum penalties for EU-wide infringements (4% of annual turnover, harmonised across member states).

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to a string of consumer enforcement scandals in online retail that exposed a structural gap: EU consumer law, written in the early 2000s for physical shops and classified ads, simply did not apply to the economics of modern e-commerce. The trigger was not a single crisis but a pattern of failures that the Commission's 2017 "Consumer Conditions Scoreboard" quantified: 74% of consumers encountered problems with online marketplaces, and 40% of online price reductions were found to be deceptive — a figure the Commission cited in its 2018 impact assessment.

The political coalition that pushed the directive through was unusual. The European Parliament's Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO), led by German MEP Eva Maydell (EPP), formed a working majority with the Socialists & Democrats and Renew Europe. They were backed by a coalition of national consumer agencies (notably the UK's Competition and Markets Authority, which had begun its own enforcement against fake discounts), and the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC), which published a 2017 study showing that 60% of "sales" on major EU marketplaces were not genuine price reductions.

Industry opposition came from two fronts. Traditional retailers, represented by EuroCommerce, supported the price-history transparency provisions because they had long complained that online-only competitors could claim artificial discounts. The real battle was with large digital platforms — Amazon, eBay, and Booking Holdings — which argued through the tech lobby group EDiMA that mandatory seller identification would create regulatory burdens and that the 30-day price rule was unworkable for dynamic pricing algorithms. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment noted that 28 companies across the retail and platform sectors submitted formal objections.

The timing was critical. The Juncker Commission, in its final legislative push, needed a clear consumer win after the controversial copyright reform (the DSM Directive) had split its majority. Commissioner for Justice, Consumers and Gender Equality Věra Jourová made the Omnibus Directive her signature file, announcing in March 2018 that "consumers should not be misled by fake discounts — the rules should be the same online as offline." The European Parliament passed it by a wide margin on 17 April 2019 (591 votes in favour, 59 against, 39 abstentions), and the Council adopted it in November 2019.

What changed the legislative dynamic was the 2018 Cambridge Analytica scandal and the subsequent "free services" debate. Until then, the main resistance had been to classifying free digital services (where users pay with data) as "consumer transactions." The scandal — and the resulting political pressure on Facebook and Google — broke the deadlock. The final text explicitly states that the Consumer Rights Directive applies "where the consumer provides personal data, even where no monetary payment is made."

- **11 April 2018** — Commission under Juncker proposes the New Deal for Consumers, including the Omnibus Directive; Věra Jourová leads presentation

- **November 2019** — Council of the European Union adopts the Directive
- **27 November 2019** — Directive published in the Official Journal, with a transposition deadline of 27 November 2021
- **28 May 2022** — Application date: member states must have implemented the rules
- **May 2023** — First year of enforcement reports begin; several consumer authorities cite the new penalty powers against platforms including Wish, Amazon, and Temu

## Impact and consequences

The directive has reshaped online retail pricing practices across the EU. Price comparison websites, which had routinely shown "original prices" that never existed, had to redesign their interfaces — for instance, Germany's Check24 and Verivox removed non-compliant discount claims by late 2022. Online marketplaces — including Amazon, eBay, and bol.com — have added "lowest price in the past 30 days" labels, which studies from national consumer agencies suggest have reduced discount claims by 25-40%.

The most significant enforcement action came in 2022, when Germany's Federal Network Agency (Bundesnetzagentur) and the Italian Competition Authority (AGCM) coordinated to fine the mobile brand Wiko €500,000 for advertising a "50% discount" that was based on a price never actually charged. This was the first major case using the new penalty ceiling (4% of turnover) as a deterrent.

Consumer enforcement cooperation has improved: the Consumer Protection Cooperation (CPC) Network now publishes annual action plans under the Omnibus rules. In 2023, the CPC Network coordinated a "sweep" of 900 online stores across 20 member states, finding that 45% initially failed to identify the seller as required. By mid-2024, compliance had risen to 78%.

Smaller platforms — particularly marketplace startups and second-hand goods apps — have faced disproportionate compliance costs, with the Commission's 2023 evaluation noting that 12% of smaller platforms reported the seller-identification rules as "a significant barrier to scaling." At the same time, traditional retailers have benefited: the EuroCommerce trade association reported in 2023 that 68% of its members saw an improvement in price-transparency fairness.

## Key questions answered

### Why can't online stores in Europe just put huge fake discounts on products anymore?

EU law now requires that any price reduction show the lowest price the item sold for in the previous 30 days. This means a store cannot invent a fake "original price" to make a discount look bigger. The rule applies to all online sales within the EU and was passed because consumer agencies found around 40% of online discounts were deceptive.

### What must online marketplaces like Amazon or eBay tell buyers about the seller?

The Omnibus Directive forces online platforms to clearly disclose who the actual seller is — the platform itself or a third-party merchant. If it is a third party, the platform must state the seller's name, address, and legal status. This was introduced because consumers had no way to know whether they were buying from Amazon or a random reseller, making returns and complaints nearly impossible.

### Does the EU have special rules for "free" apps and services that collect user data?

Yes. The 2019 Omnibus Directive extended consumer rights to cover free digital services where users pay with their personal data instead of money. This means that free apps, social media platforms, and email providers must give users the same pre-contractual information (about features, termination rights) as paid services, following the Cambridge Analytica scandal which showed users had no contractual protections.

### What changed about penalties for companies breaking EU consumer rules across multiple countries?

The maximum fine for widespread EU-wide consumer violations was increased to 4% of the company's annual turnover in the affected member state(s). Before the Omnibus Directive, penalties varied wildly by country — from a few thousand euros in smaller states to €50,000 in Germany — making it cheaper to break rules than comply. The new ceiling was designed specifically to deter large digital platforms from ignoring consumer law.
