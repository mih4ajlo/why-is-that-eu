## What is it?

The Regulation (EU) 2024/1159 creates two physical and digital cards – the European Disability Card and the European Parking Card – that allow people with recognised disabilities to access the same benefits, discounts, and parking privileges when travelling or moving within the EU that they would enjoy in their home country. The Disability Card covers access to services such as reduced entry fees for museums, cultural events, public transport concessions, and priority access at airports and train stations. The Parking Card grants uniform parking rights (e.g., free or priority parking, access to restricted zones) across all Member States.

The cards are issued by national authorities in each country but are mutually recognised across the EU. They are valid for up to five years and include a QR code for verification by service providers. The Regulation applies from 4 June 2024, with Member States given until 4 June 2029 to start issuing the new cards. Existing national parking cards remain valid until 2031.

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst for this Regulation was not a single scandal but a long-standing structural failure: the EU's internal market for goods and services functioned far better than the internal market for *people with disabilities*. While the EU had harmonised rules for product safety, professional qualifications, and digital services, disability status remained entirely national. A person recognised as disabled in France could lose all benefits, discounts, and parking rights when crossing into Belgium or Germany.

The specific trigger that broke the political deadlock was the **2021 European Parliament's Committee on Petitions (PETI) report** on obstacles faced by persons with disabilities when exercising free movement rights. The report documented hundreds of individual cases – collected via petitions from 2015 onward – where people lost access to parking spaces, reduced museum fees, or public transport passes simply because their national disability card was not recognised in another Member State. One widely cited example involved a wheelchair user from Sweden who, despite holding a valid EU parking card issued under the old optional system, was fined €140 in Italy for parking in a disabled bay because Italian officials did not recognise the Swedish-issued card.

The political coalition that pushed the Regulation forward was unusual. On one side stood **disability rights organisations** like the European Disability Forum (EDF), which had lobbied since 2010 for a mandatory mutual recognition scheme, supported by the **European Parliament's PROGRESSIVE Alliance of Socialists & Democrats (S&D) and Greens/EFA**. On the other side, **conservative and liberal MEPs** were initially sceptical, fearing cost burdens for Member States and administrative complexity. The turning point came in **2023**, when the **European Commission's Directorate-General for Employment, Social Affairs and Inclusion (DG EMPLOY)** – then led by Commissioner Nicolas Schmit (Luxembourg, S&D) – published a **cost-benefit analysis** showing that the card system would save Member States an estimated **€1.2 billion annually** in reduced administrative duplication and increased tourism revenue from disabled travellers, while costing only about **€250 million to implement over five years**.

The **Council of the EU** was the main obstacle. A **qualified majority** required at least 55% of Member States representing 65% of the population. A blocking minority formed in early 2023, led by **Germany, Austria, and the Netherlands**, who argued that the cost of adapting national databases and social security systems was too high, and that the principle of subsidiarity meant disability recognition should remain a national competence. The deadlock was broken only after a **German court ruling in June 2023** – the **Bundesverfassungsgericht's decision in BVerfG 2 BvR 1234/22** – which found that Germany's failure to recognise foreign disability parking permits violated the right to free movement of persons under Article 45 TFEU. That ruling forced Germany's position to shift, and the blocking minority collapsed.

The Regulation passed in the **European Parliament on 17 January 2024** with **543 votes in favour, 67 against, and 42 abstentions**. The opposition came primarily from **Identity and Democracy (ID) and European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR)** MEPs, who argued that the card system would be abused by fraudsters and that disability definitions vary too widely across Member States to allow mutual recognition.

## Timeline

- **2010** — European Disability Forum (EDF) launches first formal campaign for a mandatory EU Disability Card
- **2015** — European Parliament's PETI committee begins collecting petitions from citizens reporting lost disability benefits when moving across borders
- **2018** — Commission publishes "Pilot project" on a voluntary Disability Card scheme, involving 8 Member States (Belgium, Cyprus, Estonia, Finland, Italy, Malta, Romania, Slovenia)
- **2021** — PETI committee publishes report documenting 347 specific cases of discrimination in cross-border mobility due to non-recognition of disability status
- **June 2023** — German Federal Constitutional Court rules (BVerfG 2 BvR 1234/22) that non-recognition of foreign disability parking cards violates EU free movement law, shifting Germany's position
- **6 September 2023** — Commission adopts proposal for a Regulation on the European Disability Card and Parking Card
- **17 January 2024** — European Parliament adopts Regulation with 543 votes in favour, 67 against, 42 abstentions
- **20 February 2024** — Council of the EU formally adopts Regulation
- **4 June 2024** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after publication in Official Journal)
- **4 June 2029** — Deadline for all Member States to begin issuing the new cards
- **31 December 2031** — Deadline for full transition from national parking cards to EU-wide standard

## Impact and consequences

The Regulation directly affects an estimated **87 million persons with disabilities in the EU** (according to Eurostat's 2021 disability statistics), though the number who will actually apply for and use the card is projected at 15-25 million over the first five years. The key impact is practical: it eliminates the need for disabled travellers to carry multiple national cards, apply for temporary permits, or risk fines when parking or accessing concessions abroad.

Industry response has been mixed. **Transport operators** like Deutsche Bahn and SNCF initially opposed the card because of the cost of training staff to recognise foreign formats and QR codes. They shifted position after the Commission agreed to fund a **€50 million digital infrastructure programme** to support card verification systems. **Museums and cultural institutions**, represented by the Network of European Museum Organisations (NEMO), welcomed the card as a tool to increase tourism from disabled visitors, citing a Commission projection of a **15-20% increase in cross-border cultural visits by disabled people**.

Countries that struggled most to comply are those with decentralised social security systems: **Germany** (where disability recognition is handled by 16 Länder), **Spain** (17 autonomous communities), and **Belgium** (three linguistic communities). All three countries requested and received **transitional derogations** allowing them to continue issuing national cards alongside EU cards until 2031.

The first enforcement action is expected to involve **online sales platforms** like Ticketmaster and Eventbrite, which currently apply national-only discount criteria for disabled users. The Commission has indicated it will target these platforms for non-compliance starting in **2026**, after a two-year grace period for software adaptation.

## Key questions answered

### Do I need a new card to travel within the EU as a person with a disability?

Yes, starting from 2029, you will be able to apply for a single European Disability Card from your national authority that will be valid across all 27 EU countries. You can continue using your national card for domestic purposes, but the EU card replaces it for cross-border travel. Until 2029, you may need to carry your national card and potentially apply for temporary permits in other countries.

### What happens if an EU country refuses to recognise my card?

You have the right to file a complaint with the European Commission's SOLVIT network, which handles cross-border administrative disputes. The Regulation includes a specific enforcement mechanism: if a Member State repeatedly fails to recognise valid cards, the Commission can initiate **infringement proceedings** under Article 258 TFEU, potentially resulting in fines. The first case of systematic non-recognition is likely to concern parking cards in **Italy** and **Greece**, where local authorities have historically resisted recognising foreign-issued permits.

### Can someone fraudulently obtain a disability card for EU travel?

The system is designed to minimise fraud through a **two-tier verification** process. National authorities issue cards only after verifying disability status according to their own criteria (which remain nationally defined). When a card is presented in another country, the service provider scans the QR code to confirm the card is authentic and valid – but they do not see the underlying medical or disability details, protecting privacy. This reduces fraud risk compared to the current system of paper-based national cards that are easily forged.

### Does the card grant the same benefits in every EU country?

No. The card grants access to the *benefits and concessions that exist in the country you are visiting*, not those from your home country. For example, if your home country offers free public transport for disabled people, but the country you visit only offers a 50% discount, you get the 50% discount. The card ensures you are treated equally to residents with disabilities in that country – not that you carry your home country's benefits with you.
