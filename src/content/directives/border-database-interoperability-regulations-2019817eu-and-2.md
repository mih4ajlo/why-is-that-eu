## What is it?

The Interoperability Regulations adopt a technical and legal framework that forces the European Union's major large-scale IT systems for borders, visas, asylum, and criminal records to communicate with each other. The systems involved are the Schengen Information System (SIS), the Visa Information System (VIS), Eurodac (asylum seekers’ fingerprints), the Entry/Exit System (EES), the European Travel Information and Authorisation System (ETIAS), and the European Criminal Records Information System for Third-Country Nationals (ECRIS-TCN).

The regulations establish four central components to make this possible: a **European Search Portal** (ESP) that allows a single query against all databases; a **Shared Biometric Matching Service** (sBMS) that recognises fingerprints and facial images across systems; a **Common Identity Repository** (CIR) storing biographical and biometric data from multiple systems; and a **Multiple-Identity Detector** (MID) that flags when the same person uses different identities. These components are managed by eu-LISA, the EU agency for the operational management of large-scale IT systems.

## Why was it introduced?

The driving force was the 2015–2016 migration crisis, which exposed a dangerous blind spot: a person could hold a visa in one system, be wanted for a crime in another, and have applied for asylum under a third identity — and no border guard could connect the dots. In 2015 alone, according to Eurostat, EU member states received over 1.3 million first-time asylum applications. Several high-profile security incidents—including the November 2015 Paris attacks—were later linked to individuals who had crossed EU borders using multiple identities or documents that should have been flagged.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had built its databases piecemeal over 20 years, each mandated by a separate legal instrument and designed for a single purpose. SIS was for policing, VIS for visa processing, Eurodac for asylum. They were legal and technical silos. As the European Commission noted in its 2016 impact assessment, "a person can have different identities in different systems, and the current legal and technical framework does not allow systematically to detect these connections."

The political coalition for interoperability spanned the European People’s Party, the Socialists & Democrats, and most member states, particularly Germany and France, which pushed for faster, more comprehensive data sharing after the Paris and Brussels attacks. The European Parliament’s civil liberties committee (LIBE) held lengthy debates on privacy safeguards. Opposition came from civil society groups—Privacy International, EDRi—and from a minority of MEPs with Green and left-wing backgrounds, who warned that merging databases would create massive surveillance infrastructure without proven security benefits. The European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) issued a critical opinion in 2017, calling the proportionality of the measures "not sufficiently demonstrated."

The timing was decisive. By early 2016, the European Council had repeatedly called for "interoperability of databases" in its conclusions. The Juncker Commission’s Security Union package, driven by Commissioner for Migration, Home Affairs and Citizenship Dimitris Avramopoulos, turned that political will into a legislative proposal in April 2016. The parallel push for the Entry/Exit System (adopted 2017) and ETIAS (adopted 2018) created a natural sequence: those new systems would also be brought under the interoperability umbrella. The regulations were finally adopted in May 2019, just before the European Parliament elections, and published in the Official Journal in June 2019.

An alternative scenario—a single giant database merging all data into one repository—was considered and rejected. It would have required tearing up the legal bases of existing systems and raised even greater privacy concerns. Interoperability, with separate legal gateways but a technical backbone, was the compromise that could win a majority.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Migration crisis peaks; security weaknesses in identity checks become politically untenable.
- **April 2016** — European Commission publishes proposal for interoperability regulations, as part of the Security Union agenda.
- **July 2017** — European Parliament publishes its position on the first regulation (borders/visa); Council follows with its general approach.
- **May 2018** — Co-legislators reach a political agreement after trilogues.
- **May 2019** — Regulations adopted by the Council and the European Parliament.
- **June 2019** — Published in the Official Journal; enter into force 20 days later.
- **2020–2023** — eu-LISA develops the technical components; member states adapt national systems.
- **2024 onwards** — Phased rollout: European Search Portal and Shared Biometric Matching Service become operational, followed by the Multiple-Identity Detector and Common Identity Repository.

## Impact and consequences

The regulations fundamentally change how EU border and security agencies handle identity data. For the first time, a border guard can query SIS, VIS, Eurodac, EES, ETIAS, and ECRIS-TCN in a single search—without knowing which system holds the data. This reduces the likelihood of missed alerts or duplicate entries. The Multiple-Identity Detector is designed to warn officers when a person using different names appears across databases, enabling them to investigate identity fraud.

Implementation has been slow. eu-LISA reports that several member states struggled to meet the 2023 deadlines for connecting to the interoperability components, and some components—particularly the full CIR and MID—have required additional development. The EDPS continues to monitor data protection compliance, and as of 2024 no major fines have been issued, but the agency has stressed that purpose limitation controls remain essential.

Industry impact has been significant. eu-LISA awarded contracts for the Shared Biometric Matching Service and Common Identity Repository to consortia led by companies such as Sopra Steria and Thales, which supply biometric and border management systems. Smaller vendors have faced barriers in meeting the complex security and interoperability standards.

Citizens and third-country nationals are affected in different ways. EU citizens are not stored in most of the databases (except SIS alerts and ECRIS-TCN, but the latter only covers non-EU nationals). Third-country nationals entering the Schengen area will have their biometrics stored in the Common Identity Repository and can be linked across visa, entry/exit, and asylum records. Privacy advocates argue that the sheer scale of biometric data aggregation creates new risks of function creep—data originally collected for one purpose being used for another, such as law enforcement.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU want all its border databases to talk to each other?

Before the regulations, a person could be wanted on a European arrest warrant in SIS, be registered under a different name in Eurodac as an asylum seeker, and have a visa in VIS — and no border guard would see all three. The goal is to close these identity gaps so that security-relevant information appears in a single search, reducing the chance that someone slips through unnoticed.

### Does this mean border guards can see all my data from different systems?

Only if you are a third-country national interacting with the EU's border, visa, or asylum systems. EU citizens are not stored in most of the databases. For third-country nationals, the guard sees a single query result combining hits from all relevant systems — but only if the search matches their biometrics or personal data. The regulations include purpose limitation rules: data retrieved from one system can only be used for the purpose of the specific check.

### When will the new system be fully operational?

The regulations entered into force in 2019, but the technical components developed by eu-LISA have been rolled out in phases. The European Search Portal and Shared Biometric Matching Service became operational first, followed by limited use of the Multiple-Identity Detector. Full operational capability of all four components, including the Common Identity Repository, was originally planned for 2024 but has faced delays; eu-LISA now expects completion by 2025–2026.
