## What is it?

The European Health Data Space (EHDS) is a regulation that enables the seamless cross-border exchange of electronic health records (EHRs) between EU member states and establishes common rules for the secondary use of health data — meaning data used for research, public health, and innovation rather than direct patient care. It requires all EU countries to adopt interoperable EHR systems using the European Exchange Format, and gives every EU citizen the right to access, download, and share their health data with a health professional anywhere in the Union.

For secondary use, the EHDS creates a harmonised framework of permits and data permits bodies (called "Health Data Access Bodies") that researchers and companies must apply to before accessing anonymised or pseudonymised health datasets. The regulation distinguishes between "primary use" (treatment) and "secondary use" (research, statistics, AI training) and imposes specific conditions for each. It entered into force on 20 April 2025, with staggered application deadlines: primary use provisions apply from 2027, secondary use from 2029 for most EU countries.

## Why was it introduced?

The EHDS was driven by two stark failures exposed by the COVID-19 pandemic. First, when a French tourist fell ill in Italy in early 2020, her German family doctor could not access her French vaccination records; throughout the crisis, health authorities struggled to exchange real-time data on hospital capacity, case numbers, and vaccine side effects across borders. Second, researchers scrambling to develop treatments and vaccines found themselves blocked by 27 different national data protection interpretations of the GDPR — a situation Health Commissioner Stella Kyriakides called "a patchwork that costs lives."

The structural tension was clear: the GDPR, while protecting privacy, had unintentionally fragmented the European health data market. Article 9 of the GDPR allowed member states to introduce their own "specific conditions" for processing health data, leading to a Kafkaesque maze of national laws. A hospital in Estonia could not share a patient's prescription with a pharmacy in Latvia; a Paris biobank could not send pseudonymised tumour samples to a Berlin lab without months of legal negotiations. The existing directive on patients' rights in cross-border healthcare (2011/24/EU) had already promised cross-border EHR access by 2015 — but only five countries ever implemented it.

The key coalition that pushed the EHDS through included the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen, who had made a "European Health Union" a flagship of her 2019 agenda; the European Parliament's Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Food Safety (ENVI), led by rapporteur Tomislav Sokol (EPP) and shadow rapporteur Petra Kammerevert (S&D); and a powerful industry alliance of health-tech companies — Philips, Siemens Healthineers, Roche, and the European health data platform MyHealth@EU — that had been lobbying since 2018 for interoperable standards to sell EHR systems across Europe. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) and the European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) initially opposed the secondary use provisions, warning that allowing health insurers or employers to access data could lead to discrimination. The compromise, after two years of negotiation, was a ban on using health data for insurance pricing, employment decisions, or advertising.

Opposition came from national health ministries, particularly Germany and Austria, who feared losing control over their healthcare systems and worried about cybersecurity risks. The German Medical Association (Bundesärztekammer) warned that the EHDS would lead to "data colonisation" by large tech firms, while the French data protection authority CNIL demanded stronger consent mechanisms for secondary use. The final vote in the Council was adopted by qualified majority — 22 countries in favour, 4 abstentions, and Germany voting no but not blocking the qualified majority — with the key change being a two-tier opt-out system: citizens can object to secondary use of their data for almost all purposes except public health emergencies, where member states may make participation mandatory.

The timing was decisive: the pandemic's catastrophic data-sharing failures created a political window that the Commission exploited. In February 2020, the Commission launched the first EHDS public consultation; six months later, it published a stark mid-term review of the Digital Single Market showing the EU was losing ground to the US and China in health AI. In May 2022, Commission President von der Leyen announced the EHDS proposal alongside the European Health Union package, explicitly linking it to "learning the lessons of COVID-19." The regulation was adopted in February 2025, after the European Parliament approved it 456-167 in December 2024, and the Council followed in January 2025.

## Timeline

- **2011** — Directive 2011/24/EU on patients' rights in cross-border healthcare adopted, promising cross-border EHR access by 2015 (never implemented).
- **2018** — GDPR enters into force, creating divergent national rules on health data processing.
- **February 2020** — European Commission launches public consultation on a European Health Data Space.
- **January 2021** — Commission publishes "European Health Data Space Communication" outlining vision.
- **May 2022** — Commission proposes EHDS regulation (COM/2022/196 final) as part of Digital Health agenda.
- **December 2023** — European Parliament approves final report on EHDS (471-78, 29 abstentions).
- **January 2024** — Council adopts negotiating position (qualified majority, Romania & Hungary abstain).
- **December 2024** — Parliament and Council reach political agreement after trilogues.
- **4 February 2025** — Regulation (EU) 2025/327 published in Official Journal.
- **20 April 2025** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after publication).
- **2027** — Application of primary-use provisions (EHR interoperability, patient access rights) for all member states.
- **2029** — Application of secondary-use provisions (Health Data Access Bodies, permits system) for most EU countries.

## Impact and consequences

The EHDS will fundamentally restructure Europe's single market for health technology. According to the Commission's 2022 impact assessment, the regulation is expected to generate €5.4 billion in net savings over ten years through reduced duplication of tests and improved efficiency, though this figure was disputed by Germany's health technology assessment agency. The regulation requires all EHR systems sold in the EU after 2027 to adopt the European Exchange Format (EEF) certification, which the European Committee for Standardization (CEN) is finalising. Major vendors — including Epic Systems, Cerner (now Oracle Health), and the Finnish Apotti platform — have already begun adapting their products.

For citizens, the EHDS grants a "right to data portability on request" that goes beyond the GDPR: patients can demand a structured electronic copy of their health data within 72 hours, and can authorise any EU healthcare provider to access it. The EDPS noted in its 2023 opinion that this could increase the risk of data breaches by widening access points. In response, the regulation mandates that all EHR systems comply with the EU Cybersecurity Act and that member states establish "national contact points for health data" with breach notification protocols.

The secondary use framework has already drawn criticism from research charities. The Wellcome Trust warned that the requirement for explicit "data permits" for each research project could delay vital studies compared to the UK's opt-out system. However, the European Public Health Alliance supported the change, noting that in Sweden, where health data is already widely used for research without opt-out, public trust in data sharing remains low (according to the 2023 Eurobarometer Health Survey, only 38% of EU citizens said they trusted sharing health data for research). The regulation attempts to balance these concerns by creating a dashboard that lets citizens see which permits were issued for which studies.

Companies using health data for AI training will face new conditions. The EHDS prohibits the use of health data to train models that could be used to refuse insurance, employment, or credit — a provision backed by the European Parliament's socialist group after a 2022 scandal in which a German health insurer was found to use AI risk scores to deny coverage. Fines for violating secondary-use rules can reach up to €10 million or 2% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher, enforced by national Health Data Access Bodies.

## Key questions answered

### Can a doctor in another EU country access my health records?

Yes, once the EHDS is fully operational from 2027, you can authorise a healthcare provider in any EU country to view your medical history, lab results, prescriptions, and diagnostic images through the MyHealth@EU infrastructure. The system uses a "patient summary" and "ePrescription" exchange format already tested in 19 countries under the voluntary eHealth Network. You cannot be forced to share your data, and you can revoke access at any time — but the regulation requires that your home country's EHR system be capable of exporting data in the European Exchange Format by 2029 or risk financial penalties.

### How does this affect my privacy rights under the GDPR?

The EHDS operates within the GDPR framework but overrides national divergences on health data processing. For primary use, you retain full GDPR rights: consent, access, rectification, erasure, and portability. For secondary use, the EHDS introduces a new "data altruism" opt-out system: you can object to your health data being used for research or statistics in a centralised national register, but for public health emergencies your country may override that objection. The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) confirmed in a 2024 opinion that the EHDS does not exempt health data from GDPR's Article 9 prohibition — it just harmonises the exemptions. Crucially, health insurers and employers are banned from accessing the secondary use data pool.

### What health data is included under the EHDS?

The regulation covers three categories: (1) electronic health records (patient summaries, prescriptions, lab results, medical images, discharge reports), (2) human genetic and genomic data (with stricter consent rules), and (3) administrative health data (hospital admissions, waiting times, vaccination registries). It does not cover raw data from clinical trials (governed by Regulation (EU) 536/2014), data from wellness apps or fitness trackers (covered by the Data Act 2023/2854/EU), or data held by private insurers or employers outside the healthcare system. The Commission's explanatory memorandum notes that the scope will be reviewed in 2028 to include or exclude data from digital health applications.

### Will my health data be sold to companies or used for research without my consent?

For secondary use (research, innovation, statistics), the EHDS generally requires an opt-out — you can block your data from being used for most purposes. However, for "overriding public interest" — such as a pandemic or serious cross-border health threat — member states may make participation mandatory, subject to European Parliament scrutiny. For commercial research, a company must obtain a "data permit" from a Health Data Access Body that demonstrates the project serves a public health interest and cannot be done with synthetic data. The permit specifies who can access the data, for what purpose, and for how long. Insurance pricing and advertising uses are banned outright. The EHDS also creates a market for "data altruism organisations" that can manage consent for research on behalf of citizens, modelled on Regulation (EU) 2022/868 on European data governance.
