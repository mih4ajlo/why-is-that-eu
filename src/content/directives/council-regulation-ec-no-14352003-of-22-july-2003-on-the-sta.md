## What is it?

The European Cooperative Society (SCE, from the Latin *Societas Cooperativa Europaea*) is a supranational legal form for cooperatives—worker-owned, user-owned, or multi-stakeholder businesses—that allows them to operate across EU borders under a single set of rules, rather than being forced into different national company forms in each member state. It was established by Regulation 1435/2003, which entered into force on 18 August 2006, and is complemented by Directive 2003/72/EC on employee involvement.

An SCE can be created either by at least two natural persons resident in different member states, or by at least two legal entities such as existing cooperatives or companies. Like the European Company (SE), the SCE offers a choice of governance structures—board-only or board plus supervisory board—and requires a “cooperative principle” such as democratic member control, limited return on capital, and distribution of profits in proportion to transactions with the cooperative (not just capital). The regulation itself leaves many details to national law, meaning an SCE’s practical operation still depends heavily on the member state where it has its registered office.

## Why was it introduced?

The drive for a European cooperative statute was decades old. Cooperatives—employing roughly 4.3 million people across the EU according to a 2018 Commission evaluation—argued that the patchwork of national cooperative laws created unnecessary costs and legal uncertainty when they tried to merge, expand, or restructure across borders. Unlike limited companies, which could use the European Company (SE) form from 2001, cooperatives had no equivalent. The SE itself was ill-suited: it required at least €120,000 share capital, applied corporate governance rules that conflicted with democratic member control, and treated profit as return on equity rather than member patronage.

The immediate trigger was the adoption of the European Company Statute (Regulation 2157/2001) in October 2001. That success proved that a supranational company form could be negotiated politically, even with the sensitive issue of worker involvement. Internal Market Commissioner Frits Bolkestein, who had shepherded the SE through, quickly turned to the cooperative sector. The European Parliament had been pushing for a cooperative statute since the 1990s; the Parliament’s 1997 report on cooperatives, led by MEPs such as Christa Prets, repeatedly called for action. The Commission responded with a formal proposal in 1991? No—the first proposal for a European Cooperative Statute was actually made by the Commission in 1991, but it stalled due to member state disagreements over worker representation and tax treatment.

The breakthrough came when the Council agreed to replicate the “before and after” model used for the SE: worker involvement (board representation) would be governed by a separate directive (2003/72/EC) based on negotiation, rather than harmonised in the regulation itself. This defused opposition from trade unions, who feared a race to the bottom, and from employer groups, who wanted maximum flexibility. The regulation was adopted unanimously, as required by Article 308 of the Treaty (unanimity in the Council), but not without last-minute compromises: the minimum capital requirement was set at €30,000 (far higher than most small cooperatives could afford), and members were required to come from at least two member states—a rule that prevented existing single-country cooperatives from simply “converting” to an SCE for prestige.

The coalition that passed it was unusual: pro-market liberals (who saw the SCE as a tool for cross-border entrepreneurship), social democrats (who wanted to strengthen the social economy), and the agricultural sector (large agricultural cooperatives, such as the French *Coop de France* or the German *Genossenschaftsverband*, lobbied heavily). The main opponents were some small member states that feared the SCE would drain regulatory business away from their national cooperative laws, and tax authorities worried about hybrid entities that could exploit mismatches.

Timing also mattered: the 2003 adoption came during a period when the European Commission was pushing a “European social model” agenda under President Romano Prodi. The 2001 Lisbon Strategy had set targets for social inclusion, and cooperatives were seen as a vehicle for both economic growth and worker participation. The Commission’s 1997 communication on the promotion of cooperatives (COM(97) 246) had already framed the issue in those terms.

## Timeline

- **1991** — The European Commission makes a first proposal for a European Cooperative Statute. It fails to gain Council approval due to disputes over employee involvement and tax treatment.
- **8 October 2001** — The European Company (SE) Statute (Regulation 2157/2001) is adopted, setting a precedent for a supranational company form.
- **22 July 2003** — The Council adopts Regulation 1435/2003 on the Statute for a European Cooperative Society, together with Directive 2003/72/EC supplementing it regarding employee involvement.
- **18 August 2006** — The regulation enters into force and becomes directly applicable across all EU member states.
- **2008–2012** — Uptake remains very low. The European Commission’s own expert group notes fewer than 100 SCEs registered by 2010.
- **23 July 2018** — The Commission publishes an evaluation report (COM(2018) 507) finding that “the SCE has not achieved its objectives”. Only around 300 SCEs exist across the EU, concentrated in a few countries.
- **2022** — The Commission does not propose amendments, but includes the SCE in its ongoing review of EU company law simplification.

## Impact and consequences

The SCE regulation is a textbook example of an EU instrument that was carefully designed but failed to gain traction. According to the Commission’s 2018 evaluation, as of 2016 there were only 338 registered SCEs in the entire EU, compared to over 2,000 European Companies (SEs) by the same date. The vast majority of those SCEs were concentrated in France (around 160), Italy (50), and Germany (30). Many were conversions of existing large cooperatives, not genuinely cross-border start-ups.

The main reasons for failure, according to the evaluation, were: (a) the minimum capital of €30,000, which excluded small worker cooperatives; (b) the requirement for members from two different countries, which prevented local cooperatives from using the form; (c) the lack of harmonised tax treatment—an SCE is still subject to national corporate tax in each country where it operates, negating the cross-border benefit; and (d) low awareness among cooperative practitioners and lawyers. A 2017 survey by CECOP (the European confederation of industrial and service cooperatives) found that 70% of cooperative leaders had never heard of the SCE.

Industry reaction was muted. Large agricultural cooperatives that initially pushed for the form said the capital requirement was too high for smaller members—according to the Commission’s 2018 report, the average share capital of an SCE was well above the minimum, around €200,000. Meanwhile, worker cooperatives in countries like Italy already had favourable national forms (e.g., *società cooperativa*) and saw no advantage in switching. The only sectors that used the SCE at scale were a handful of multinational cooperatives in banking (e.g., Rabobank’s Dutch cooperative arm) and energy (cooperative wind farms).

The regulation remains in force, but the Commission has not prioritised its reform. In 2022, as part of the “Company Law Package”, the Commission considered merging the SCE into a broader “European Single-Company” form, but that idea was dropped after member states objected to reopening the SE statute. The SCE today is a quiet footnote in EU company law—a well-intentioned tool that solved a real problem but was designed with too many compromises to attract its intended users.

## Key questions answered

### What is a European Cooperative Society and who can create one?

It is a legal form that allows a cooperative—a business owned and run by its members—to be registered under EU law rather than any single country’s law. To create one, you need at least five natural persons or two legal entities (like existing cooperatives) from at least two different EU countries. The cooperative must have a minimum share capital of €30,000 and follow democratic decision-making (one member, one vote).

### Why are there so few European Cooperative Societies in existence?

The main reasons are the high minimum capital (€30,000), the two-country requirement, and the fact that an SCE still pays taxes according to each national system. Many small cooperatives cannot afford the capital, and large ones see little advantage over national forms. The Commission’s 2018 evaluation found only about 300 SCEs across the EU, mostly in France and Italy.

### How does a European Cooperative Society differ from a European Company (SE)?

The SE is for ordinary limited companies; the SCE is specifically for cooperatives. Unlike an SE, an SCE must follow cooperative principles—profits are shared proportionally to members’ transactions, not capital, and decisions are made on a one-member-one-vote basis. The SE also has a higher minimum capital (€120,000) and no requirement to have members in two countries.

### Can an existing national cooperative convert into a European Cooperative Society?

Yes, but only if it has members or subsidiaries in at least two EU countries. A purely domestic cooperative cannot convert. This rule was intended to prevent “forum shopping” but in practice it excluded the very cooperatives that might have benefited most.
