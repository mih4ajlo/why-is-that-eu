## What is it?

Directive 2009/48/EC lays out the legally binding safety rules that any toy sold in the European Union must meet. It covers physical, mechanical, flammability, electrical, hygiene, and – for the first time comprehensively – chemical hazards. The directive requires manufacturers to carry out a safety assessment, affix the CE mark, and provide clear age warnings and instructions. Toys that cannot be put in the mouth by children under 36 months face less stringent chemical limits than those that can be mouthed, reflecting the greater risk from ingestion.

The directive also tightens the obligations of importers and distributors, making them co-responsible with the manufacturer for placing only compliant toys on the market.

## Why was it introduced?

In the summer of 2007, a wave of toy recalls – most prominently Mattel’s recall of 19 million Chinese-made toys – smashed public confidence in toy safety. The recalls were driven by excessive levels of lead paint and loose magnets that could be swallowed. The EU’s Rapid Alert System (RAPEX) reported a surge in notifications for chemical risks in toys, especially phthalates in soft plastic toys and heavy metals in painted surfaces.

The existing Toy Safety Directive (88/378/EEC) had not been updated since 1988, and its chemical safety provisions were weak: they set no binding limits for most heavy metals and left phthalates unregulated until a temporary emergency measure in 1999 (later extended). The REACH regulation (adopted in 2006) provided a framework for controlling chemicals, but toys still fell short. The recalls exposed a structural tension between the global toy supply chain (80% of toys sold in the EU were manufactured in China) and a directive that treated safety as a matter of manufacturer self‑declaration.

Pressure came from consumer organisations (notably BEUC), MEPs in the Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee, and national market surveillance authorities. The European Commission, under Commissioner for Enterprise and Industry Günter Verheugen, proposed a revision in January 2008. The proposal introduced binding migration limits for 19 heavy metals (aluminium, antimony, arsenic, barium, boron, cadmium, chromium, cobalt, copper, lead, manganese, mercury, nickel, selenium, strontium, tin, organic tin, zinc, and lead again separately) and kept the existing emergency ban on three phthalates (DEHP, DBP, BBP) while extending restrictions on three others (DINP, DIDP, DNOP) for toys that can be mouthable.

The Council reached political agreement in December 2008. The European Parliament adopted the text on 18 December 2008 with 591 votes in favour, 102 against, and 16 abstentions. Opposition came from some industry groups and free‑trade MEPs who argued the new chemical limits would drive up costs and reduce consumer choice. Germany, which had a strong toy industry and already applied stricter national chemical limits (the 1997 German Toys Ordinance), pushed for ambitious EU‑wide limits. France and the UK, more reliant on imported toys, were cautious about implementation costs.

The timing was decisive: the 2007 recalls had created a window that neither the Commission nor the Parliament was willing to waste. Earlier attempts to update the 1988 directive had stalled over exactly these chemical limits; the Mattel scandal broke the logjam.

## Timeline

- **1988** — Original Toy Safety Directive (88/378/EEC) enters into force, focusing on physical and mechanical hazards but with weak chemical rules.
- **1999** — EU emergency ban on six phthalates (DEHP, DBP, BBP, DINP, DIDP, DNOP) in toys intended for children under three years. Made permanent in 2005.
- **Summer 2007** — Major recalls by Mattel, Fisher‑Price, and others of toys with excessive lead paint and magnets. RAPEX notifications spike to over 2,000 per year, 40% involving toys.
- **January 2008** — European Commission proposes a new Toy Safety Directive, introducing binding migration limits for 19 heavy metals and codifying the phthalates ban.
- **18 December 2008** — European Parliament votes 591–102 in favour.
- **18 June 2009** — Council formally adopts Directive 2009/48/EC.
- **20 July 2009** — Directive enters into force.
- **20 July 2011** — Most provisions become applicable (physical, mechanical, electrical, flammability, labelling).
- **20 July 2013** — Chemical safety requirements (heavy metal migration limits) become applicable. A temporary derogation is allowed for materials such as glass, wood, and textiles to allow industry to adapt test methods.
- **2015** — Commission evaluation (REFIT) finds the directive effective but notes challenges in enforcement and test reproducibility for some chemical limits.

## Impact and consequences

The directive harmonised toy safety across the EU, replacing a patchwork of national rules that had complicated trade. For manufacturers, the biggest change was the burden of proving compliance: the CE mark now required a documented safety assessment, and for toys containing chemicals that are not covered by the migration limits (such as fragrances and certain preservatives), additional EU‑level restrictions apply.

According to the Commission’s 2015 REFIT evaluation, the number of serious toy safety incidents reported via RAPEX fell significantly after 2013, though the evaluation cautioned that improved surveillance systems might account for some of the change. The cost of compliance fell disproportionately on small and medium‑sized enterprises (SMEs), particularly those importing toys from outside the EU. The Commission estimated additional compliance costs of €118–146 million for industry in the first two years, but the cost of non‑compliance – fines, recalls, reputational damage – was far higher for firms caught selling unsafe toys.

Market surveillance authorities gained stronger powers to demand information, withdraw products, and penalise non‑compliance. Between 2009 and 2018, the EU funded joint enforcement actions (like “Toy Safety, One “Health”) that coordinated border checks across member states. The share of non‑compliant toys detected at EU external borders remained around 20% of tested samples as late as 2017, according to a 2018 Commission report.

## Key questions answered

### Are there different safety rules for toys in different EU countries?

No. The Toy Safety Directive is a “New Approach” directive that sets fully harmonised requirements. Once a toy carries the CE mark, it must be accepted for sale in all EU member states without additional national testing or certification. Before the 2009 directive, countries like Germany and France had their own stricter chemical limits, forcing manufacturers to produce multiple versions. Now the whole EU applies the same limits.

### Why are some toys banned for babies but allowed for older children?

The directive sets much stricter chemical migration limits for toys intended for children under 36 months – and for any toy that can be put in the mouth – because younger children have a higher exposure risk from mouthing and swallowing. For example, lead migration is capped at 13.5 mg/kg for scraping toys (not mouthable) but at 2.0 mg/kg for mouthable toys. This age‑based differentiation reflects the scientific evidence that small children absorb more heavy metals relative to body weight.

### Does the CE mark guarantee that a toy is safe?

No. The CE mark is a manufacturer’s self‑declaration that the toy meets all EU requirements. For most toys, no independent third‑party testing is required before putting the product on the market. The mark is not a quality or safety certification; it is a legal statement of compliance. Market surveillance authorities can test toys at any time, and if a toy is found unsafe, the manufacturer or importer is liable. The directive does require third‑party inspection for some categories, such as toys with electrical components or those intended for activities like chemistry sets.

### What were the main chemical risks that drove the new limits?

The most prominent were lead (from paint and metal components), phthalates (used as plasticisers in soft PVC toys, linked to endocrine disruption), and cadmium (in plastics and paints). The 2007 Mattel recalls involved lead paint, but EU authorities had flagged phthalate risks since the late 1990s. The directive introduced binding migration limits for 19 heavy metals, transferred the existing phthalates ban from an emergency measure into permanent law, and banned the use of certain allergenic fragrances (like eugenol and coumarin) in toys.
