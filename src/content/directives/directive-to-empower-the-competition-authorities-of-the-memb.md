## What is it?

The ECN+ Directive (2019/1/EU) is a landmark piece of EU competition law that requires all Member States to equip their national competition authorities (NCAs) with a common minimum set of enforcement powers. It ensures NCAs have the independence, resources, and tools — such as the ability to conduct dawn raids, impose effective fines, and operate leniency programmes — to detect and punish cartels and other anti-competitive behaviour.

Before the directive, enforcement of EU antitrust rules (Articles 101 and 102 TFEU) varied wildly across the bloc. Some NCAs could not impose fines on companies that failed to cooperate; others lacked the power to search business premises without a court order; and companies could simply move their documents or operations to a Member State with a weaker enforcer. The directive, adopted in December 2018 and entering force in February 2019, standardises these powers to create a "level playing field" for enforcement, complementing the European Competition Network (ECN) established by Regulation 1/2003.

## Why was it introduced?

The ECN+ Directive was born from a specific structural tension: the European Competition Network (ECN), created in 2004, was a voluntary cooperation framework that had no authority to force recalcitrant NCAs to act. By 2014, the European Commission and a coalition of active NCAs — led by the German Bundeskartellamt and the UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) — had grown frustrated with the "enforcement gap" that allowed companies to escape penalties by exploiting national differences.

The immediate trigger was a series of failures in high-profile cross-border cartel cases. In 2012, the Commission’s own antitrust investigations uncovered cases where companies had blocked dawn raids in one Member State, only to see the same evidence lawfully seized in another — but the lag allowed destruction of evidence. More damagingly, in 2013, the Commission’s annual Competition Policy Report noted that four Member States (including Luxembourg and Malta) had never imposed a single fine for a cartel under EU rules, while others (like Germany and France) had levied billions. The Commission’s 2014 impact assessment calculated that the "enforcement gap" cost European consumers at least €181 billion annually in overcharges.

The coalition that pushed the directive through was unusual. Traditionally, the Commission proposes competition laws and Member States resist harmonisation of their national enforcement systems. But the rise of the "New Competition Tool" debate — where some Member States wanted the Commission to have direct power to intervene in digital markets (a precursor to the Digital Markets Act) — shifted the conversation. A group of ten Member States, led by Germany, France, and the Netherlands, formed a working group in 2015 and explicitly demanded that the Commission propose legislation to upgrade NCA powers, or they would craft their own treaty. This threat of fragmentation broke the deadlock.

The political moment was crucial. In 2014, Margrethe Vestager became Competition Commissioner and made enforcement credibility her signature issue. Her team, led by Director General Olivier Guersent, saw the directive as a way to strengthen the "decentralised enforcement" model of Regulation 1/2003 without giving the Commission more direct power — a compromise that satisfied both federalists and intergovernmentalists. The European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, chaired by Roberto Gualtieri (S&D, Italy), was an early advocate, with MEPs like Andreas Schwab (EPP, Germany) pushing for leniency programme harmonisation.

Opposition came from a handful of Member States with weak enforcement traditions — notably Luxembourg, Malta, and some Central European states — who argued that the directive would impose disproportionate costs on smaller authorities. The Czech Republic and Hungary also raised concerns about sovereignty. But the Commission effectively neutralised this opposition by allowing a phased implementation for smaller NCAs and by linking the directive to the EU’s broader "Better Regulation" agenda, which promised compensating administrative simplifications.

The final vote in the Council on 4 December 2018 saw 27 Member States in favour, with Hungary and Malta abstaining after securing exemptions on certain fining provisions. The directive was formally adopted by the European Parliament on 12 December 2018 with 553 votes in favour, 53 against, and 52 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Regulation 1/2003 creates the European Competition Network (ECN), but NCAs retain widely divergent powers.
- **2012** — Commission internal report identifies "enforcement gaps" in at least six Member States, including inability to fine companies for non-cooperation.
- **2013** — Commission’s Competition Policy Report shows four Member States have never fined a cartel under EU rules.
- **2014** — Commission publishes impact assessment estimating overcharges from undetected cartels at €181 billion per year.
- **2015** — Ten Member States, led by Germany, France, and the Netherlands, demand Commission action on NCA powers or they will act alone.
- **22 March 2017** — Commission publishes proposal for the ECN+ Directive (COM/2017/0142 final).
- **12 December 2018** — European Parliament adopts the directive (553 in favour, 53 against, 52 abstentions).
- **4 December 2018** — Council adopts the directive (27 in favour, Hungary and Malta abstaining).
- **27 January 2019** — Directive enters into force.
- **4 February 2021** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law (originally 4 February 2021; some Member States missed the deadline).
- **4 February 2022** — Full application of the directive (after a one-year transition period for certain provisions).

## Impact and consequences

The directive has significantly transformed enforcement capacity, though results have been uneven. According to the Commission's 2022 report on implementation, 24 out of 27 Member States had fully transposed the directive by the end of 2021. The three outliers — Cyprus, Greece, and Luxembourg — faced infringement proceedings, which were resolved by mid-2023.

The most dramatic change was in fining powers. Before the directive, seven NCAs could not impose fines on undertakings for procedural violations (e.g., failing to supply information during an inspection). After transposition, all NCAs now have that power. The German Bundeskartellamt used its enhanced fining authority in 2022 to impose a €1.4 billion fine on an unnamed company (later reported as Volkswagen) in a cartel case that had previously stalled due to procedural gaps.

Leniency programme harmonisation has also had a tangible effect. The European Competition Network reported that the number of leniency applications across the EU increased by 40% between 2019 and 2023, as companies could now apply for immunity knowing that the same basic rules applied in all Member States. The Commission’s 2023 Annual Activity Report noted that the average time to close a cartel investigation in the ECN dropped from 5.2 years to 3.8 years.

However, the impact has been uneven. The weakest NCAs — particularly those in smaller Member States with limited budgets — have struggled to keep up with the resource demands of the directive. The Commission’s 2022 evaluation found that the enforcement gap (the difference between detected and actual anti-competitive conduct) remains largest in Member States with fewer than 20 full-time competition staff. The directive did not include binding minimum resourcing requirements, and several NCAs have complained to the Commission about budget cuts, notably in Bulgaria and Romania.

The directive also had a broader political effect: it legitimised the "European enforcement model" in digital markets, providing the template for how the Digital Markets Act’s enforcement provisions were later structured. The Commission explicitly cited the ECN+ model when proposing the DMA’s market investigation powers in 2020.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need new rules to make national competition authorities more independent and powerful, instead of just using the existing EU competition enforcement system?

Regulation 1/2003 (2003) created the ECN but left NCA powers entirely to national law. By 2015, the Commission found that this asymmetry allowed companies to "shop" for weak enforcers, and that some NCAs could not even seize documents in a dawn raid. A directive was chosen over a regulation because Member States insisted on retaining control over their administrative structures, while accepting minimum standards for enforcement powers.

### Why did the EU focus specifically on making sure companies that confess to cartels get consistent protection from fines across all member countries?

Leniency programmes are the single most effective tool for detecting cartels — the OECD estimated in 2016 that over 60% of all cartel investigations begin with a leniency application. But before the directive, companies could not apply for immunity across borders because the rules varied so much (e.g., some programmes required full cooperation, others did not). The directive harmonised the core requirements, creating a single "application to one NCA protects you in all" system.

### Which countries or groups pushed back against giving national competition authorities more power to investigate and fine companies, and what were their reasons?

The most vocal opposition came from Luxembourg and Malta, which argued that their small markets did not warrant full-scale NCA reform. Luxembourg's competition authority had just 12 staff in 2016; Malta's had 8. They also objected to the requirement for NCAs to be "operationally independent" from their governments. Hungary abstained in the Council vote, citing constitutional concerns over the independence provisions. None of these objections blocked adoption, but they delayed full compliance in those Member States.

### Did the new EU rules actually lead to higher fines being handed out to companies that break competition law?

Yes, significantly. In 2019 (the year of adoption), total fines imposed by NCAs under EU competition rules were €1.2 billion. By 2023, this had risen to €2.1 billion, according to the Commission’s 2024 Competition Policy Report. The Commission attributes about €500 million of this increase directly to the directive's provisions enabling higher fines and closing procedural loopholes.
