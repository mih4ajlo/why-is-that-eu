## What is it?

The Right to Repair Directive (2024/1799/EU) is an EU law adopted in 2024 that gives consumers a legal entitlement to have certain products repaired — even after the manufacturer's commercial guarantee has expired. It covers products for which EU law already requires "reparability" (such as washing machines, dishwashers, refrigerators, vacuum cleaners, smartphones, tablets, and welding equipment), and the list is designed to expand over time as new ecodesign rules come into force.

The directive forces manufacturers to repair these products at a "reasonable price," forbids them from using contractual clauses, hardware techniques, or software tricks to block independent repair shops or the use of second-hand and 3D-printed spare parts, and creates a European online platform where consumers can find repairers, refurbishers, and sellers of refurbished goods near them. It also introduces a standardised "European Repair Information Form" so consumers can compare repair quotes transparently.

Crucially, it complements (rather than replaces) the existing two-year legal guarantee under the Sale of Goods Directive (2019/771): if a repaired product breaks again, the legal guarantee is extended by a further 12 months, nudging consumers and sellers toward repair rather than replacement.

## Why was it introduced?

For decades, European consumers had quietly absorbed the costs of a "throwaway economy." Appliances that once lasted 20 years were being replaced after five; smartphones became unusable not because they broke, but because batteries were glued in, screens cost almost as much as a new device, or software updates stopped arriving. The European Environment Agency estimated that premature obsolescence of consumer goods cost EU consumers around €12 billion per year, and that 35 million tonnes of waste were generated annually because products were discarded rather than fixed.

Pressure built from several directions. Consumer groups like BEUC and national bodies such as France's HOP ("Halte à l'Obsolescence Programmée") had been campaigning since the mid-2010s, citing high-profile cases — most notably Apple's 2017 admission that it had deliberately slowed older iPhones via software ("batterygate"), which led to a €25 million fine in France in 2020. Independent repair networks complained that manufacturers like Apple, Samsung, John Deere, and large appliance makers used proprietary screws, serialised parts paired to specific devices, encrypted firmware, and restrictive parts distribution to monopolise repair. Environmental groups pointed to the climate cost: producing a new smartphone emits roughly 80% of its lifetime carbon footprint before it is even sold.

The political trigger came with the European Green Deal (2019) and the Circular Economy Action Plan (2020), under Commission President Ursula von der Leyen and Executive Vice-President Frans Timmermans. The Commission framed repair as essential to the EU's 2050 climate-neutrality goal: extending the lifetime of washing machines, vacuum cleaners, dishwashers and fridges by just five years could save 10 million tonnes of CO₂ a year. The first concrete measures came through ecodesign rules in 2019–2021 requiring manufacturers to make spare parts available for 7–10 years for white goods. But these covered only design and didn't give consumers an actual right to demand a repair.

The 2024 directive closes that gap. It also responds to a parallel American debate — Massachusetts (2012, 2020), Colorado, New York, and California passed their own right-to-repair laws between 2020 and 2023 — and to intense lobbying. Industry associations such as DigitalEurope and Orgalim warned about safety, intellectual property, and cost; repair advocates, the European Parliament's IMCO Committee, and rapporteur René Repasi (S&D, Germany) pushed back, ultimately securing a stronger text than the Commission's original 2023 proposal, including the ban on anti-repair software practices and the explicit acceptance of third-party and used parts.

## Timeline

- **2015** — France introduces the first EU national law against planned obsolescence, making it a criminal offence.
- **2017** — Apple's "batterygate" scandal exposes deliberate performance throttling of older iPhones.
- **2019** — European Green Deal launched; first ecodesign regulations on reparability adopted for white goods (effective 2021).
- **2020** — Circular Economy Action Plan announces a forthcoming "right to repair" initiative; France introduces a mandatory Repairability Index.
- **2021** — EU Sustainable Products Initiative consultation; European Parliament resolution calls for a comprehensive right to repair.
- **March 2023** — European Commission publishes its proposal for a Right to Repair Directive.
- **February 2024** — Provisional agreement reached between Parliament and Council in trilogue.
- **April 2024** — European Parliament adopts the directive (584 votes in favour).
- **June 2024** — Council formally adopts; directive enters into force on 30 July 2024.
- **31 July 2026** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law.

## Impact and consequences

For consumers, the directive means clearer repair options, comparable quotes via the European Repair Information Form, and longer protection: a repaired product carries a renewed 12-month guarantee. Surveys by Eurobarometer suggested 77% of EU citizens would prefer to repair rather than replace, but cost and inconvenience deterred them — exactly the friction the law targets.

For manufacturers, it imposes new obligations: keeping repair affordable, providing spare parts and information at "reasonable" prices, and refraining from technical or contractual measures that block independent shops. Companies operating closed repair ecosystems — notably Apple, Samsung, and parts of the agricultural machinery sector (John Deere being the global archetype) — have had to adjust business models. Apple's "Self Service Repair" programme, launched in 2022, and Samsung's expansion of authorised repair partners are partly pre-emptive responses to this regulatory wave.

For the independent repair sector, long marginalised by serialised parts and proprietary tools, the directive is a lifeline. National repair bonus schemes (Austria since 2022, Germany's federal scheme from 2024, France's "Bonus Réparation" since 2022) layer financial incentives on top of the new legal rights. Together these measures are projected by the Commission to save consumers around €176 billion over 15 years and prevent 18.5 million tonnes of greenhouse gas emissions.

The directive also signals a regulatory philosophy shift: products in the EU are increasingly treated not as one-off transactions but as services with lifetime obligations, dovetailing with the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (2024) and the Digital Product Passport.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU repair rules only cover certain types of products?

It is tied to the EU's ecodesign framework, which sets reparability requirements product-by-product through detailed technical regulations. Mandating repair only makes sense where spare parts, technical documentation, and repair feasibility have already been defined. As ecodesign expands (e.g., to textiles, furniture, more electronics), the scope of the right to repair will automatically grow.

### Why didn't the EU just ban planned obsolescence outright?

Proving deliberate obsolescence in court is notoriously hard — France's pioneering law has produced very few prosecutions. The EU instead chose a structural approach: make repair easy, cheap, and legally enforceable, so that obsolescence becomes commercially unattractive rather than criminally prohibited.

### Why do EU repair rules allow third-party and used spare parts?

Manufacturers had increasingly used "part pairing" — software locks tying a specific component (battery, screen, camera) to a specific device serial number — to force consumers into official channels. The directive treats this as an anti-competitive obstacle to repair, ensuring that independent shops, refurbishers, and even 3D-printed parts can keep older devices alive.

### Why is this a directive that each country has to turn into its own law, instead of a single rule for everyone?

A directive sets binding objectives but lets each Member State adapt the rules to its legal system, consumer protection traditions, and existing repair-bonus schemes. Many Member States (France, Austria, Germany, Sweden) already had partial frameworks, and a directive avoids overriding them while ensuring a common floor of consumer rights.
