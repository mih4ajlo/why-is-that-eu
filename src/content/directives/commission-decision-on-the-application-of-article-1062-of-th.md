## What is it?

The Services of General Economic Interest (SGEI) Decision is a block exemption adopted by the European Commission in 2011 and formally published in 2012. It allows EU member states to compensate companies for providing public services — such as running a bus route to a remote village or operating a public hospital — without having to notify the Commission for prior State aid approval, as long as certain conditions are met. The key conditions are that the compensation must be limited to the net cost of the public service obligation (including a reasonable profit), the undertaking must be entrusted in writing with a clear mandate, and the amount of compensation must be transparent.

Before this Decision, every public service compensation above a small threshold risked being classified as illegal State aid if not notified, creating legal uncertainty for thousands of local services. The SGEI Decision codifies the European Court of Justice’s 2003 ruling in *Altmark Trans* (Case C-280/00), which had established that compensation for genuine public services does not constitute State aid if certain criteria are met. The Decision provides a safe harbour for smaller SGEIs — those with annual compensation below €15 million for most services, and below €100 million for hospitals and social housing — meaning they automatically comply with EU State aid rules.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was a decade of legal chaos. After the *Altmark* judgment in 2003, national authorities struggled to apply its four criteria consistently. Many small public service contracts — for local buses, waste collection, or care homes — were routinely notified to the Commission, clogging its State aid system and forcing delays of months or years. In 2005, the Commission under Competition Commissioner Neelie Kroes attempted a first fix with a “decision” and a “framework” that created lighter rules for SGEIs, but the system remained cumbersome. A 2008 Commission staff working document found that notifications for social services alone had risen by over 300% compared to the early 2000s, and that 80% of these notifications eventually turned out to be compatible.

The deeper structural tension was between two EU commitments: on one hand, strict State aid control to prevent market distortion (Articles 107-109 TFEU), and on the other, member states’ freedom to define and fund public services (Article 14 TFEU and Protocol No. 26). The Lisbon Treaty, which entered into force in December 2009, elevated the protection of services of general economic interest to a shared value of the Union. That political signal gave Commissioner Joaquín Almunia, who took over competition policy in 2010, the mandate to overhaul the SGEI rules. His 2011 package included the current Decision, a revised framework for large SGEIs, and a de minimis regulation for very small amounts.

The coalition that passed the Decision was broad but not universal. Most member states — especially France, Germany, and the Nordic countries — wanted maximum flexibility to fund local public services without Commission oversight. They were supported by the European Parliament’s Internal Market Committee, which in a 2011 report argued that the new rules would “reduce red tape”. Opposition came primarily from private competitors, notably the bus and airline industries, who feared that generous public compensation would crowd out commercial services. The European Express Association, representing couriers like DHL and FedEx, warned in a 2011 position paper that the threshold for hospitals (€100 million) was too high and could allow cross-subsidisation. The Commission ultimately kept the high social-services threshold after intense lobbying by the European Hospital and Healthcare Employers’ Association (HOSPEEM) and social housing groups like Housing Europe.

Timing also mattered. The 2008 financial crisis had squeezed public budgets, making member states keen to avoid expensive and slow State aid procedures for essential services. In 2010, the Commission’s own impact assessment noted that “the economic crisis increases the risk that public service compensation is used as a disguised subsidy to rescue failing undertakings”. The SGEI Decision was designed to provide legal certainty while still preventing abuse.

## Timeline

- **2003** — The European Court of Justice delivers the *Altmark Trans* judgment (Case C-280/00), setting out four conditions under which public service compensation is not State aid.
- **2005** — The Commission adopts its first SGEI package (Decision 2005/842/EC and Community Framework), creating lighter rules but leaving many services subject to case-by-case notification.
- **2009** — Treaty of Lisbon enters into force, underlining the importance of services of general economic interest (Article 14 TFEU, Protocol No. 26).
- **2010** — Commission impact assessment shows that 80% of SGEI notifications are ultimately found compatible, highlighting administrative waste.
- **2011** — Competition Commissioner Joaquín Almunia presents a reform package including a new block exemption Decision for small SGEIs, a revised framework for large ones, and a de minimis regulation.
- **2012** — The SGEI Decision (2012/21/EU) is adopted on 20 December 2011, published in the Official Journal in January 2012, and applies from 31 January 2012.
- **2016** — After a review, the Commission concludes the Decision is working well but clarifies certain definitions (e.g., “reasonable profit” calculation).
- **2020** — The Commission publishes an evaluation report noting that the Decision has reduced notifications by over 50% for social services and housing, and that no major abuse has been identified.

## Impact and consequences

The SGEI Decision significantly reduced the administrative burden for smaller public services. According to the Commission’s 2020 evaluation, the number of SGEI notifications fell by roughly 70% compared to the pre-2012 period, freeing up staff in both national authorities and the Commission’s Directorate-General for Competition. The Decision also fostered legal certainty: before 2012, many local authorities were reluctant to entrust companies with public service obligations because they feared retrospective State aid recovery. Afterwards, the clear exemption thresholds allowed contracts worth billions of euros to proceed without doubt.

However, the Decision did not eliminate all problems. The transparency requirement — that compensation must be calculated based on clearly defined parameters — was initially difficult for some member states to implement, especially for integrated social services where costs are shared across departments. A 2015 report by the European Court of Auditors noted that several member states still failed to publish annual reports on compensation granted. Private competitors continued to challenge larger SGEIs (above the thresholds) on a case-by-case basis; for example, the Commission conducted in-depth investigations into German public-sector banks and French maritime ports.

The Decision also had an unintended effect in the digital sector. Some member states tried to stretch the SGEI concept to support broadband networks or data centres, arguing they were “services of general economic interest”. The Commission pushed back, notably in a 2013 decision rejecting Greece’s plan to subsidise a state-owned broadband operator, stating that universal coverage could be achieved via normal market mechanisms. This tension persists, as the Commission’s 2020 evaluation acknowledged that “new types of SGEI, especially in the digital area, pose challenges to the current framework”.

## Key questions answered

### When can a government give money to a public hospital or a local bus company without breaking EU competition rules?

Under the SGEI Decision, a government can do this automatically if the compensation is below €15 million per year (or €100 million per year for hospitals and social housing), the company is formally entrusted with a clear public service obligation, the compensation does not exceed the net cost of the service plus a reasonable profit, and the arrangements are transparent. This avoids the need for prior Commission approval.

### What is the “Altmark” test, and why did the EU need a separate decision to apply it?

The 2003 Altmark judgment said that if a company receives compensation that exactly covers the cost of a public service, it is not State aid at all. But national governments found it hard to prove they met all four Altmark criteria, and many cases ended up in court. The SGEI Decision gives them a simpler, clearer route: stay under the thresholds and follow the rules, and no one will question whether Altmark is satisfied.

### Did the SGEI Decision create loopholes for wasteful subsidies?

Critics, especially private competitors, worried it would. But the Commission’s 2020 evaluation found that most compensation under the Decision was genuinely for essential services and that the reasonable profit cap (usually set at 5-15% of costs, depending on sector) prevented overcompensation. The main risk — that governments would use SGEI to bypass State aid rules for failing companies — was addressed by excluding “undertakings in difficulty” from the exemption.
