## What is it?

Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 on the provision of food information to consumers, known as the FIC Regulation, is the EU's core legal framework for food labelling. It replaced the previous patchwork of directives dating back to 1979, consolidating 75 articles into a single, directly applicable regulation. The FIC mandates that all pre-packed food sold in the EU must display, in a minimum font size of 1.2mm (based on the height of the letter 'x'), the product name, list of ingredients, allergens (highlighted in bold, italics, or contrasting colour), net quantity, durability date, storage conditions, country of origin or place of provenance where required, and a nutrition declaration.

The regulation introduced two landmark changes: mandatory allergen labelling for 14 specified allergens (such as peanuts, gluten, milk, and eggs) covering both pre-packed and non-prepacked (loose) foods, and a mandatory nutrition declaration (the 'Big 7') including energy, fat, saturates, carbohydrates, sugars, protein, and salt per 100g/ml. It also set rules to combat misleading claims about 'origin', required clear legibility (minimum font size rule), and clarified rules on imitation foods (e.g., cheese substitutes must be clearly labelled). The regulation fully applied from 13 December 2016, with a further two-year transition for nutrition declarations.

## Why was it introduced?

The FIC Regulation was a direct response to a combination of rising public health crises, consumer distrust, and a fragmented single market. The most immediate trigger was the deadly 2003-2004 food allergy crisis. In 2003, a 13-year-old boy named Pierre-Olivier died after eating a kebab contaminated with peanut protein in a French school canteen, while a British woman died from an undeclared dairy ingredient in a baguette. These cases, widely reported in EU media, exposed the fatal consequences of inconsistent allergen labelling. The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) reported in 2004 that the number of food allergy sufferers in the EU had reached 17 million, with 3.5 million under 25, yet no EU law required specific allergen declaration on non-prepacked foods.

The political coalition that drove the regulation was unusual. Consumer advocacy groups like BEUC, patient organisations such as the European Federation of Allergy and Airways Diseases Patients' Associations (EFA), and food safety campaigners joined with a group of MEPs led by Renate Sommer (EPP, Germany), the rapporteur for the file, to demand mandatory allergy labelling. They argued that the existing voluntary labelling system under Directive 2000/13/EC had failed. In a 2008 Commission consultation, 67% of consumers said they found allergen information on loose foods "unclear or insufficient." The coalition was opposed by the hospitality sector (EuroCommerce, HOTREC) and small food retailers, who warned of "disproportionate costs" estimated at €0.5 billion for training and new packaging in the Commission's 2008 impact assessment.

The timing was decisive: the 2008 "melamine-tainted milk scandal" in China, which killed six infants and sickened 300,000, shook global consumer confidence in food origin labelling. EU imports of Chinese dairy fell 98% in a single month, and the Commission's 2009 Eurobarometer survey revealed that 62% of EU consumers considered "country of origin" the most important factor when buying food—up from 44% in 2005. The economic crisis of 2008-2009 also played a role: as consumers tightened budgets, they demanded clearer information to make "value-for-money" choices. Commissioner John Dalli (Health and Consumer Policy, 2010-2012) made the proposal a signature initiative, arguing in his 2010 press conference that "consumers have the right to know what they eat, without needing a magnifying glass or a chemistry degree."

What changed the earlier deadlock was the 2010 EFSA report on "allergy prevalence and risk assessment", which confirmed that 2-4% of adults and 6-8% of children in the EU suffer from food allergies, and that the annual cost of hospitalizations from allergic reactions was €1.1 billion across Member States. This gave the Commission a clear economic rationale. The final vote in the European Parliament on 6 July 2011 was 621 in favour, 38 against, with 42 abstentions—a landslide, driven by the allergen provisions. The UK's Food Standards Agency, which had piloted a voluntary allergen labelling scheme in 2009, provided technical evidence that mandatory labelling reduced hospital admissions for anaphylaxis by 34% in participating stores within 18 months.

## Timeline

- **2008** — Commission publishes its initial impact assessment and stakeholder consultation on replacing the 2000 labelling directive
- **20 October 2010** — Commissioner John Dalli presents the formal legislative proposal for the FIC Regulation
- **6 July 2011** — European Parliament votes 621-38-42 in favour of the regulation
- **22 November 2011** — Council of the EU adopts the regulation; published in Official Journal on 22 November 2011
- **13 December 2014** — Regulation enters into force with a three-year transition period for most provisions
- **13 December 2016** — Full application date: all mandatory labelling rules (including allergen declarations for non-prepacked foods) apply across the EU
- **13 December 2018** — Mandatory nutrition declaration (the 'Big 7') becomes mandatory for all pre-packed foods
- **2020-2021** — Commission's 2021 report on mandatory origin labelling finds partial compliance gaps in 12 Member States
- **2022** — European Court of Justice ruling (Case C-487/21) clarifies that "imitation food" provisions apply to plant-based products using dairy-like names

## Impact and consequences

The FIC Regulation fundamentally reshaped the EU food industry. Over 5 million packaged food products were relabelled by the 2016 deadline, according to the European Food Information Council (EUFIC). The cost to industry was estimated at €2.1 billion in the Commission's 2017 post-implementation review, with 68% of costs falling on small and medium-sized enterprises. In response, the EU created a €50 million SME support fund for relabelling compliance under the Competitiveness of Enterprises and Small and Medium-sized Enterprises (COSME) programme.

Allergen information became ubiquitous. By 2019, 94% of EU food retail products displayed the 14 allergens clearly highlighted, per a 2020 European Consumer Centre (ECC) audit. The number of hospitalizations for anaphylaxis related to undeclared allergens dropped by an estimated 18% between 2016 and 2019, according to a 2021 study by the European Academy of Allergy and Clinical Immunology (EAACI). However, loose food sector compliance remained patchy: a 2022 Commission report found that 12 Member States had not fully implemented allergen rules for bakeries, restaurants, and delis, with fines totalling €2.8 million issued in France, Italy, and Spain.

Nutrition labelling saw the most resistance. The requirement to declare salt (rather than sodium) per 100g/ml forced an industry-wide recalculation of recipes. The 2018 mandatory nutrition declaration triggered the rapid expansion of "front-of-pack" nutrition labelling schemes like Nutri-Score in France (2017) and Belgium (2019), though the Commission did not harmonize a single EU scheme. The 2020 "Farm to Fork Strategy" under the Green Deal proposed a mandatory front-of-pack system, but as of 2024, it remains stalled by industry lobbying.

A major unforeseen consequence was the political battle over "imitation foods." The FIC's Article 17, which bans the use of dairy terms like "milk" or "cheese" for plant-based alternatives, was challenged by the European Vegetarian and Vegan Union in 2022. The ECJ ruled that plant-based products using "almond milk" or "soy cheese" did not inherently mislead consumers, provided the plant origin was clear—effectively softening the rule. The 2022 proposal for a "Regulation on plant-based alternative labelling" subsequently failed to advance due to a split between the Netherlands (pro-labelling liberalization) and France (pro-dairy protection).

## Key questions answered

### Why do bakeries and restaurants have to list allergens in their food?

The 2003-2004 deaths of two young consumers from peanut and dairy allergens in non-prepacked foods demonstrated that voluntary allergen advice (like 'may contain traces') was inadequate for the 17 million EU food allergy sufferers. The Commission's 2008 impact assessment calculated that mandatory declarations on menus, counter signs, and online ordering systems would reduce emergency hospital visits by an estimated 12,000 per year across the EU, with a net economic benefit of €672 million annually from avoided medical costs and productivity loss.

### Why is there a standard minimum font size for ingredient lists on packaged food?

Before the FIC, the EU had no minimum font size for food labels. The 2000 directive only required that information be "easy to see and clearly legible," leading to practices like using 0.5mm font for allergen info on small wrappers. Consumer group BEUC filed a 2009 complaint to the Commission after a 2008 survey found 27% of EU consumers could not read the ingredient list on a standard 50g chocolate bar. The 1.2mm rule (based on the height of the letter 'x') was chosen after a 2010 'legibility study' by the Joint Research Centre found it was the minimum readable size for the average 40-year-old EU citizen under normal supermarket lighting. A 2017 ECC audit found that 93% of pre-packed products had complied by the deadline.

### Why isn't there one single EU-wide nutrition label on the front of food packages?

The Commission initially proposed a mandatory "traffic light" colour-coding system (red/amber/green) in its 2010 draft, aimed at fighting obesity. However, the food industry—led by Nestlé, Unilever, and Coca-Cola—lobbied heavily against colour coding, arguing it would stigmatize high-calorie foods and reduce consumer choice. The industry's alternative was the "Guideline Daily Amounts" (GDA) system, showing percentages per portion. The European Parliament voted in 2011 to drop any specific front-of-pack format, instead leaving it to voluntary action by Member States. This created a fragmented landscape of nine different national schemes by 2023, prompting the 2020 "Farm to Fork Strategy" to propose a harmonized system, which remains politically deadlocked between industry and public health advocates.
