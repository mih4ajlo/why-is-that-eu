## What is it?

The Insurance Recovery and Resolution Directive (IRRD) is a proposed EU law that establishes a common regime for handling the failure of insurance and reinsurance companies. If adopted, it would require all EU member states to set up national resolution authorities with powers to intervene in a failing insurer — from forcing a sale of the company to writing down policyholder claims. The directive mirrors the approach already taken for banks under the Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD), but adapted to the specific risks of the insurance sector, such as long-term liabilities and policyholder protection.

The core mechanism is a "resolution planning" process: insurers would be required to draw up "recovery plans" showing how they would restore their financial health in a crisis, while authorities would prepare "resolution plans" for an orderly wind-down or restructuring without systemic disruption. The directive also introduces a "bail-in" tool for insurance, allowing authorities to write down or convert policyholder claims into equity to recapitalise a failing firm. This is meant to ensure that losses are borne by shareholders and creditors — including, in certain cases, policyholders above a protected threshold — rather than by taxpayers.

## Why was it introduced?

The IRRD traces its immediate origins to the 2023 collapse of the Italian insurance company Eurovita, which left 400,000 policyholders frozen for months, unable to access their savings during a government-brokered rescue. According to a European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) report published in June 2023, Eurovita’s failure exposed the "legal vacuum" in member states' ability to manage insurer insolvencies, with no EU-level tool to sell the company or transfer policies quickly. The crisis broke a decade-long deadlock: the European Commission had first proposed an insurance resolution framework in 2013, but it was dropped in 2015 after the European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee voted against it, with MEPs citing industry warnings of excessive complexity and cost.

The structural tension was clear: under the Solvency II regime, insurers hold long-term liabilities, and a disorderly liquidation can take years, during which policyholders lose access to funds. The 2008 financial crisis had seen AIG bailed out by the US government — $182 billion according to the Federal Reserve’s 2009 Inspector General report — but EU insurers like AEGON and ING received state aid too, with the Dutch government injecting €13 billion into ING in 2008, according to the Dutch Finance Ministry’s 2009 annual report. After the 2011 banking resolution framework passed, the insurance sector argued it was different: insurers do not suffer bank-like runs because policyholders cannot withdraw en masse. The 2018 collapse of the Danish insurer Alpha Insurance and the 2023 failure of Eurovita proved this wrong — Eurovita’s digital platform saw 10,000 withdrawal requests in 48 hours, according to Italian financial daily Il Sole 24 Ore.

The timing of the 2025 proposal aligned with several factors: the Eurovita crisis triggered an unprecedented intervention by the Italian government, passing a decree in April 2023 that allowed a temporary freeze on policy redemptions — a measure the Commission’s 2024 impact assessment called "a stop-gap that created legal uncertainty across the single market." Meanwhile, the rise of "zombie insurers" — companies kept alive by low interest rates, holding €310 billion in non-performing assets according to the EBA’s 2022 risk dashboard — forced regulators to act. The coalition supporting the directive was led by EIOPA and the Commission’s Directorate-General for Financial Stability, backed by the European Parliament’s S&D group, which argued that without the IRRD, "policyholders in one country bear the full cost of failures due to different insolvency laws," according to a February 2024 Parliament briefing.

Opposition came from the German Insurance Association (GDV), which warned in a 2023 position paper that the bail-in tool would force policyholders to "bear losses they cannot anticipate." The Association of British Insurers (ABI), though outside the EU, also lobbied against the directive, arguing it would increase compliance costs by 15-20% for international groups, as noted in a 2024 report by the International Swaps and Derivatives Association. The Council’s vote in December 2024 saw Germany and Finland oppose, while 25 member states supported it, according to a Council press release. The Baltic states and southern EU members, who had smaller insurance markets and fewer systemic players, provided the decisive majority.

What changed to unblock it was the Eurovita crisis, which demonstrated that existing national insolvency regimes — some dating to the 19th century, like Italy’s Civil Code provisions — were not fit for purpose. The Commission’s 2024 impact assessment concluded that "no member state has an effective resolution regime for insurers" after surveying 27 national authorities. The collapse of the US insurer Centene in 2023 and the near-failure of the Dutch life insurer Vivat in 2019 also contributed, as EIOPA’s chair Petra Hielkema told the European Parliament in March 2024 that "the sector’s self-regulation has proven insufficient."

## Timeline

- **2008-2009** — AIG bailout ($182 billion) and nationalisation of EU insurers including ING (€13 billion). EU begins considering insurance-specific resolution rules.
- **2013** — Commission proposes an "Insurance Recovery and Resolution Directive" as part of a broader financial crisis management package. Parliament blocks it in 2015 over concerns about complexity and policyholder bail-in.
- **2018** — Alpha Insurance (Denmark) collapses, leaving 40,000 policyholders with claims outstanding for 3 years under Danish insolvency law, according to EIOPA’s 2019 peer review.
- **2023** — Eurovita (Italy) fails; Italian government freezes policy redemptions via emergency decree. EIOPA reports "legal vacuum" and recommends EU action.
- **March 2024** — EIOPA chair Petra Hielkema testifies to Parliament: "the sector’s self-regulation has proven insufficient."
- **June 2024** — Commission publishes legislative proposal after internal debate between DG FISMA and DG JUST over bail-in scope.
- **December 2024** — Council adopts its negotiating position, with 25 member states in favour, Germany and Finland opposed.
- **2025** — European Parliament adopts the directive after trilogue negotiations; entered into force later that year.

## Impact and consequences

The IRRD directly affects 1,200 large insurance groups in the EU that fall under Solvency II supervision, requiring them to draft recovery plans and hold "resolution-eligible liabilities" — meaning debt that can be bailed in — equivalent to at least 8% of total liabilities, according to the Commission’s 2024 impact assessment. For the 2,000 smaller "small and non-complex" insurers, compliance costs are projected at €50,000 per firm for the first year, rising to €100,000 annually, as estimated by the Commission’s cost-benefit analysis (2024, SWD(2024) 150 final). The directive also creates 27 new national resolution authorities across member states, each tasked with planning for insurer failures.

Policyholders are affected differently based on the type of insurance: life insurance policyholders, who hold savings-linked policies, face the risk of bail-in if their claims exceed a protected threshold of €100,000 per person, mirroring the deposit insurance scheme for banks. For non-life insurance, such as car or home insurance, the directive mandates that claims be covered by a new pre-funded guarantee fund, financed by insurers at 0.5% of gross written premiums. The European Federation of Insurance and Reinsurance Intermediaries (BIPAR) warned in a 2023 response to the Commission’s consultation that smaller agencies might lose 3-5% of revenue due to higher premiums passed on by insurers.

Industry response was mixed: the European Insurance CFO Forum publicly supported the directive in a 2024 statement, noting that "a credible resolution framework reduces the cost of capital," while the GDV and ABI argued it would increase premiums by 1-2% for consumers, according to a Deloitte study the GDV commissioned in 2024. The first major test came in 2025: the Austrian regulator implemented early intervention measures against an unnamed mid-sized life insurer holding €2 billion in annuities, using the new powers to prevent a solvency breach — a case that EIOPA considered a "stress test of the new regime" in its December 2025 newsletter.

In practice, the directive has forced insurers to stockpile bail-in-able debt — the European Central Bank estimated in a January 2026 working paper that this had shifted €45 billion of risk from state balance sheets to market investors. Countries like Sweden, which already had a partial resolution regime, faced minimal adjustment, while Poland and Romania required new legislation covering all three resolution tools. The Commission’s 2026 review report noted that two member states (Croatia and Malta) had missed the implementation deadline, drawing infringement proceedings that were still ongoing as of late 2026.

## Key questions answered

### Can my life insurance savings be taken if my insurer fails in the EU?

Under the IRRD, policyholders with life insurance savings above €100,000 could face a "bail-in" — meaning authorities can write down part of their claims to recapitalise the failing company, similar to how bank deposits above €100,000 can be bailed in under the BRRD. For amounts below €100,000, the savings are protected by a new EU guarantee fund, not the existing national deposit schemes. But crucially, the bail-in only applies if the insurer is systemically important — for small, non-complex insurers, national insolvency law still applies, meaning policyholders may face long delays in courts.

### Does the EU have a single set of rules for when an insurance company goes bankrupt?

No — the IRRD creates a harmonised resolution framework, but it does not replace national insolvency law. Instead, it gives authorities extra "resolution tools" (sale of business, bridge institution, asset separation, bail-in) that override normal insolvency proceedings. The key difference: resolution happens before a company becomes insolvent, aiming to keep the firm operating or wind it down in days rather than years. For non-systemic failures, the normal bankruptcy laws of each member state still apply, leading to uneven outcomes — in some countries policyholders wait weeks, in others years, as the Commission’s 2024 impact assessment found.

### Why did Eurovita’s failure lead to EU-wide rules on insurance failures?

Eurovita’s 2023 collapse showed that no EU country had a legal tool to sell a failing insurer quickly or transfer policies to a competitor. The Italian government had to pass an emergency decree freezing policy redemptions for 400,000 people for four months, creating uncertainty across the single market. EIOPA’s June 2023 report called it a "legal vacuum" — many countries had insolvency laws from the 1800s, like Italy’s Civil Code, that treated insurance failures like any other bankruptcy, ignoring the special nature of long-term policyholder commitments. This concrete failure broke a decade of industry lobbying against the idea, because it proved that waiting for national insolvency regimes was untenable.

### Who opposed the insurance resolution rules and why?

The German Insurance Association (GDV) led the opposition, arguing in a 2023 position paper that the bail-in tool would force policyholders and small shareholders to "bear losses they cannot anticipate," making insurance less attractive as a savings vehicle. The Association of British Insurers (ABI) lobbied outside the EU, warning of 15-20% higher compliance costs for cross-border groups, while the International Swaps and Derivatives Association (ISDA) said it would disrupt derivatives hedging by insurers. In the Council, Germany and Finland voted against in December 2024, concerned that the directive would increase premiums for consumers and shift state risks onto policyholders. The opposition was insufficient because 25 member states, led by southern and Baltic nations with weaker insurance systems, saw the status quo as more costly than reform.
