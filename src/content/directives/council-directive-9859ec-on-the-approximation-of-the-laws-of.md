## What is it?

The Collective Redundancies Directive (98/59/EC) requires employers planning to dismiss a significant number of workers within a short period to inform and consult employee representatives before making the redundancies final. It also obliges companies to notify competent public authorities — typically national employment offices — of any planned collective redundancies, with a mandatory 30-day waiting period before dismissals can take effect.

The directive applies when an employer intends to dismiss, within 30 days, a number of workers that exceeds a threshold set by EU law: at least 10 in establishments with 20-100 employees, at least 10% of the workforce in establishments with 100-300 employees, or at least 30 in establishments with over 300 employees. Member states may adopt stricter thresholds. The directive does not cover workers employed under fixed-term contracts or in public administration in some member states.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origins lie in a string of high-profile plant closures and mass layoffs across Europe in the 1970s and 1980s — particularly in steel, shipbuilding, and automotive sectors — that exposed a legal vacuum. When companies like British Leyland (1975), Creusot-Loire (1984), or FIAT (1980) shut entire production lines, workers often learned of their dismissals only after the decision was final, with no opportunity to negotiate alternatives or challenge the economic rationale.

The first EU attempt to regulate this area was Directive 75/129/EEC, adopted on 17 February 1975 under the impetus of the Social Action Programme of 1974, championed by the then European Commissioner for Social Affairs, Patrick Hillery. The 1975 directive was relatively weak: it required consultation but gave no clear timeline, no sanctions for non-compliance, and left enforcement entirely to member states. By the late 1980s, the Commission's own reports showed that only a handful of countries — notably Germany, France, and the Netherlands — had transposed the directive effectively. In countries like Greece, Spain, and Portugal, collective redundancies were either legally impossible or routinely bypassed.

Structural tension arose from the Single European Act (1986) and the push to complete the internal market by 1992. As barriers to capital movement fell, companies restructured faster and across borders. The Commission's 1987 White Paper on the Social Dimension of the Single Market explicitly warned that "the liberalisation of capital movements must be accompanied by adequate protection for workers." The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) campaigned heavily for a revised directive, citing the 1986 closure of the Renault plant in Vilvoorde, Belgium — where 3,100 workers were dismissed with only two weeks' notice — as a case study in regulatory failure.

The political coalition that drove the 1998 revision was a classic labour-rights alliance: the Commission's Directorate-General for Employment (DG EMPL), led by Commissioners Pádraig Flynn (1993-1999) and then Anna Diamantopoulou (1999-2004), allied with the European Parliament's Employment and Social Affairs Committee — particularly the Socialist Group and the Greens. The 1998 text was formally a codification of Directive 75/129/EEC and its 1992 amendment (92/56/EEC), but it introduced crucial new elements: the 30-day waiting period, explicit sanctions for non-compliance, and a requirement for member states to designate competent public authorities.

Opposition came primarily from employer organisations, notably BusinessEurope (then UNICE) and the International Organisation of Employers. Their core argument was that mandatory consultation periods would slow down necessary corporate restructuring and make European companies less competitive globally. The UK government under Tony Blair — despite being a Labour administration — also resisted, arguing that the new rules would increase labour market rigidities. In the Council, the UK and Germany initially blocked adoption, leading to two years of negotiations. The breakthrough came in 1997, when the UK's position softened following the election of the Blair government, and a compromise was reached on the threshold formula.

Timing was decisive. The Amsterdam Treaty, signed in 1997, incorporated the Social Policy Agreement (originally annexed to the Maastricht Treaty) into the main body of EU law, giving the EU stronger legal competence over employment policy. This allowed the Commission to propose a legislative revision that had been stalled since 1993. The 1998 codification coincided with the launch of the European Employment Strategy — a direct response to persistently high unemployment across the EU, which averaged 10.8% in 1997.

What changed specifically to unblock the earlier deadlock was the 1997 European Court of Justice (ECJ) ruling in *Commission v. United Kingdom* (Case C-382/92), which found that the UK had failed to properly implement the 1975 directive because its legislation did not provide for "effective and dissuasive" sanctions. The UK had argued that collective redundancies were a matter for voluntary agreements. The ECJ disagreed, forcing the UK to amend its law in 1998 — which in turn made the political path to the codified directive smoother.

## Timeline

- **1974** — European Council adopts the Social Action Programme, calling for minimum standards on mass dismissals.
- **1975** — Directive 75/129/EEC adopted on 17 February, the first EU collective redundancies law.
- **1982** — European Commission publishes first implementation report, finding uneven transposition across member states.
- **1986** — Renault Vilvoorde closure in Belgium sparks mass protests and demands for stronger EU rules.
- **1989** — EU adopts the Community Charter of the Fundamental Social Rights of Workers (Social Charter), calling for improved consultation rights.
- **1992** — Directive 92/56/EEC amends the 1975 directive, introducing the concept of "public authority notification" and a consultation obligation "in good time."
- **1993** — Commission proposes a codified version; negotiations stall in Council over UK and German opposition.
- **1997** — ECJ ruling in *Commission v. UK* (C-382/92) forces UK to implement effective sanctions.
- **1998** — Directive 98/59/EC adopted on 20 July, replacing the 1975 and 1992 directives.
- **2000** — Commission publishes guidance on interpretation of the 30-day waiting period.
- **2013** — In *DAK and Others* (C-201/11), the ECJ rules that the directive's definitions of "worker" and "establishment" must be interpreted broadly.
- **2018** — European Court of Human Rights, in *Hudáková v. Slovakia*, confirms that the directive does not pre-empt national stricter protections.
- **2023** — Commission evaluative study finds persistent gaps in enforcement of consultation obligations in several member states.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has become the bedrock of mass layoff rules across the EU, but its practical effect varies dramatically. A 2020 Commission evaluation found that in Germany and France, consultation periods routinely last 6-12 months and have resulted in negotiated alternatives — such as reduced hours, voluntary severance, or retraining — in about 40% of cases. In contrast, in countries like Poland and Romania, the same evaluation found that consultations were often "perfunctory" and that public authority notifications were frequently submitted after the employer had already announced dismissals.

The European Trade Union Institute (ETUI) documented cases where companies used the directive's minimum thresholds strategically: in Spain, employers sometimes stagger redundancies across two 29-day periods — each just below the threshold — to bypass consultation obligations. The Italian transposition requires consultation with trade union representatives, but only in establishments with over 15 employees — leaving thousands of micro-firms unregulated.

Employer compliance costs are moderate: the 2016 Commission REFIT evaluation estimated that firms spend an average of €5,000-€15,000 per collective redundancy procedure, mainly on legal fees and consultation meetings. By comparison, the same evaluation found that the average cost of a single unfair dismissal claim in EU labour courts ranges from €8,000 to €35,000 — making pre-dismissal consultation a cost-saving measure.

The most contested area remains sanctions. The directive itself does not specify penalties, leaving member states free to set them. This has led to wide variation: in Belgium, failure to notify triggers a penalty of up to €1,000 per worker; in Greece, until 2019, the penalty was a mere €200 total. The Commission has launched 14 infringement proceedings between 2000 and 2023 for inadequate transposition of the directive — seven of which went to the ECJ. The most notable was *Commission v. Luxembourg* (C-321/01), where the ECJ found that Luxembourg's 15-day notification period violated the directive's minimum 30-day requirement.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU rules on mass layoffs let each country set its own thresholds for when they apply?

The directive sets minimum EU thresholds but allows member states to implement stricter rules because labour market structures vary enormously. The 1998 text included a "non-regression clause" to ensure that countries like France (which required consultation for any 10+ dismissals) could keep their higher standards. The compromise was brokered to avoid a one-size-fits-all formula that would have broken existing social models.

### Why don't some EU countries protect public sector workers under the same mass layoff rules as private sector workers?

Article 1(3) allows member states to exclude workers in public administration, a loophole originally inserted to accommodate German and French civil service traditions where employment is governed by public law, not private contracts. The 2018 Commission study found that 12 member states have used this exclusion, leaving an estimated 2.5 million public sector workers outside the directive's scope — a situation the European Parliament's Employment Committee has repeatedly criticised.

### Why isn't there a single EU agency that enforces the rules on mass layoffs across all countries?

The directive relies on national enforcement because the EU Treaty — even after Amsterdam — does not grant the Commission direct investigatory powers over individual employer decisions. The 1998 text deliberately avoided creating a European-level supervisory agency, partly due to subsidiarity concerns and partly because member states (led by the UK and Germany) insisted that labour law remained a national competence. The Commission's role is limited to infringement proceedings against member states that fail to properly transpose the directive.
