## What is it?

The Equal Treatment in Employment Directive (2000/78/EC) prohibits discrimination in employment and occupation on four specific grounds: religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation. It covers access to employment, vocational training, employment conditions, and membership in employer or employee organisations. The directive requires member states to establish legal remedies, shift the burden of proof to employers in certain cases, and create equality bodies to support victims.

The directive is a framework — it sets minimum standards and allows member states to maintain or introduce more protective measures. It permits some exceptions, such as genuine occupational requirements for religious organisations or age-based distinctions that are objectively justified. Its adoption in 2000 marked the first time EU law addressed discrimination on grounds beyond sex, race, and ethnic origin.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origins lie in a 1996 European Court of Justice ruling, *P. v. S. and Cornwall County Council* (Case C-13/94), which held that discrimination against a trans woman for gender reassignment was sex discrimination under the Equal Treatment Directive of 1976. The ruling exposed a gap: the EU's anti-discrimination framework covered sex and nationality, but left other forms of workplace bias unaddressed. In 1996, a Eurobarometer survey found that 64% of EU citizens wanted stronger EU action against discrimination, but national laws varied wildly — some member states (like the Netherlands and Denmark) had comprehensive protections, while others (like Greece and Portugal) had none for sexual orientation or disability.

The political breakthrough came in 1997 with the Treaty of Amsterdam, which gave the EU new legal authority to combat discrimination on the grounds listed in Article 13 — including sexual orientation and age. Social Affairs Commissioner Pádraig Flynn (Ireland, 1993-1999) pushed for legislation, but faced resistance from the UK (still under John Major) and some southern member states who feared the costs of adapting employment laws. The 1997 election of Tony Blair's Labour government shifted UK policy: the UK became a proponent, wanting to show EU leadership on social issues.

The legislative process accelerated under the Finnish Presidency in late 1999. Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (Greece, 1999-2004) took over the file and made it a priority. The proposal was submitted in November 1999. In the European Parliament, the rapporteur was MEP Evelyne Gebhardt (PES, Germany), who secured cross-party support from the Socialists, Liberals, and Greens — but the centre-right EPP sought exemptions for churches and religious organisations. A closed-door compromise in early 2000 allowed religious employers to require loyalty to their ethos for certain jobs, a concession that secured the directive's passage. The Council adopted it unanimously on November 27, 2000 — the first time the EU used Article 13 of the Amsterdam Treaty.

## Timeline

- **1996** — *P. v. S. and Cornwall County Council* ruling; Eurobarometer shows 64% public support for EU action against discrimination
- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam adds Article 13, empowering EU to combat discrimination on multiple grounds
- **1999** — Commission President Romano Prodi takes office; Diamantopoulou tasked with anti-discrimination legislation
- **November 1999** — Commission proposal for a Directive establishing a general framework for equal treatment in employment
- **October 2000** — European Parliament adopts report with 367 votes in favour, 126 against, 47 abstentions
- **November 27, 2000** — Council adopts Directive 2000/78/EC unanimously
- **2003** — Member states required to transpose into national law (with possible 3-year extension for disability and age provisions)
- **2004** — First infringement proceedings against Luxembourg and Austria for late transposition
- **2008** — Commission publishes first implementation report, finding gaps in 12 member states

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced sweeping legal changes across the EU. In 2004, Austria amended its *Behinderteneinstellungsgesetz* (Disabled Employment Act) to include prohibition of disability discrimination; in 2006, Germany passed the *Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz* (General Equal Treatment Act), which came into force on August 18, 2006, after years of delay. The UK already had some protections but had to extend them to cover age and sexual orientation in employment through the Employment Equality (Sexual Orientation) Regulations 2003.

The CJEU has issued several landmark rulings under the directive: *Mangold v. Helm* (Case C-144/04, 2005) established that the principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age is a general principle of EU law; *Coleman v. Attridge Law* (Case C-303/06, 2008) extended protection against disability discrimination to employees caring for a disabled person; *Hay v. Crédit Agricole Mutuel de Charente-Maritime et des Deux-Sèvres* (Case C-267/12, 2013) held that clauses in collective agreements distinguishing between married employees and those in civil partnerships (for same-sex couples) could constitute direct discrimination on grounds of sexual orientation.

The Commission's 2008 implementation report noted that 12 member states had incomplete or incorrect transposition, leading to infringement actions. By 2010, all member states had adopted implementing legislation, though the quality varied. The directive did not cover discrimination in access to goods and services — that gap was addressed by a separate 2008 proposal that stalled in the Council and has never been adopted.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the Equal Treatment in Employment Directive exclude religion-based discrimination in employment from its broader equal treatment scope?

The directive permits religious organisations to require employees to respect their ethos, provided the requirement is genuine, legitimate, and proportionate. This was a political compromise to secure the directive's passage in 2000, as several member states (Ireland, Poland, Italy, and Germany) had strong church interests demanding that faith-based employers (schools, hospitals, charities) retain the ability to hire co-religionists. Without this exemption, the directive would have faced a blocking minority in the Council.

### Why was the Equal Treatment in Employment Directive not extended to cover goods and services?

A proposed "horizontal" directive covering access to goods and services (COM(2008) 426) was presented by Commissioner Vladimír Špidla in July 2008, but it stalled in the Council due to disagreements about the scope for services (healthcare, education, housing) and the treatment of multiple discrimination. As of 2025, the proposal remains blocked in the Council — 12 member states, led by Germany and Poland, have opposed it, arguing that it infringes national competences over social services and religious freedom.

### Why did the Equal Treatment in Employment Directive take so long to enforce?

The directive allowed a three-year extension for the disability and age provisions, meaning that for some member states (Ireland, Luxembourg, Austria, and the UK requested and received extensions), national implementation deadlines fell in 2006. This gave member states with weaker social protection systems time to adjust. The Commission also faced a high-volume of complaints — by 2010, the Commission had received over 1,200 complaints related to age discrimination alone, according to its 2014 report on the application of the directive.
