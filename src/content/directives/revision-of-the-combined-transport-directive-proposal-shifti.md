## What is it?

The proposal to revise the Combined Transport Directive (originally 92/106/EEC), introduced by the European Commission in November 2023, rewrites the rules that govern how freight moves across Europe using multiple modes — typically a long-distance rail or water leg bookended by road transport. The core idea is simple: if a truck drives to a rail terminal, its container is loaded onto a train for the long haul, and another truck completes the final delivery, the entire journey qualifies as "combined transport" and receives preferential treatment — exemptions from driving bans, reduced road taxes, and simplified paperwork.

The 1992 directive created these benefits but defined "combined transport" too narrowly: the initial or final road leg had to be under 150 km (later 200 km), the paperwork burden was heavy, and the incentives were rarely enforced consistently across member states. The revision proposes abolishing the distance limit, allowing multiple trailers or containers, forcing member states to grant tax relief, and streamlining administrative procedures. The Commission's impact assessment estimated that without revision, the share of combined transport would stagnate at around 5% of total EU tonne-kilometres, while road freight would continue growing.

## Why was it introduced?

The revision was driven by a stark structural failure: the 1992 Combined Transport Directive had been overtaken by a booming trucking industry — European road freight grew by 25% between 2000 and 2019, according to Eurostat — while rail freight's share of inland freight declined from 18% in 2000 to 11% in 2020. The original directive, negotiated when the EU had 12 member states and containerised shipping was still expanding, assumed that cheap rail access and simple administrative benefits would attract shippers. It did not.

The political moment arrived with the European Green Deal, announced by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019. Her Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy, published in December 2020, set a specific target: shift 30% of road freight over 300 km to rail or water by 2030, and 50% by 2050. Transport Commissioner Adina Vălean, a Romanian liberal, repeatedly warned that "the directive is simply not fit for purpose" and that "the barriers have multiplied while the incentives have eroded." In a 2021 speech to the European Railway Summit, she cited European Environment Agency data showing that transport emissions had risen by 33% since 1990, making it the only sector where emissions were still increasing — and road freight was a major contributor.

The coalition that pushed the revision included the European Parliament's TRAN committee, particularly rapporteur Caroline Nagtegaal (Renew, Netherlands), and a broad alliance of rail operators, environmental NGOs like Transport & Environment, and logistics companies like DB Schenker and Hupac. The European Intermodal Association, representing combined transport operators, lobbied heavily for the 150 km limit to be dropped, arguing it excluded the most efficient long-distance corridors — Rotterdam to Milan, for example, often required road legs exceeding that limit for last-mile delivery.

Opposition came from the road transport industry — notably the International Road Transport Union (IRU), which argued that any shift away from trucks would increase costs for final consumers. Germany's BGL and French road hauliers warned of "modal shift at any cost" in a joint 2022 position paper. Some member states — particularly Poland, the Czech Republic, and the Baltic states — also resisted, partly because their rail infrastructure is still largely single-track and electrified only in part, making modal shift physically impossible on congested corridors. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment acknowledged that 40% of the EU's core rail network had bottleneck capacity, according to the European Court of Auditors' 2022 special report.

Timing was critical: the revision succeeded because of the confluence of three factors. First, the 2019 European Green Deal gave legal and political cover for aggressive modal-shift targets. Second, the COVID-19 pandemic exposed the fragility of road-only supply chains — when border closures hit truck drivers in spring 2020, rail freight moved largely uninterrupted, as the Commission noted in its 2021 Freight Transport Action Plan. Third, the energy crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 made energy efficiency a national security issue: rail and inland waterway transport use roughly 1/4 the energy of road per tonne-kilometre, according to the European Commission's Joint Research Centre.

What changed to break the deadlock after decades of stagnation? The 2019 European Court of Justice ruling in *Asociación de la Carne de la Comunidad de Madrid* (Case C-123/18) clarified that member states could not impose additional restrictions on combined transport beyond those in the directive — but it also showed that the directive's vagueness had allowed 15 member states to apply it inconsistently, according to the Commission's 2020 evaluation. That evaluation, published in October 2020, concluded that the directive had "failed to achieve its objectives" and that a "fundamental revision" was needed — not just amendments.

## Timeline

- **1975** — First EU Directive on combined transport (75/130/EEC) introduced, offering limited tax breaks for road-rail journeys.
- **1992** — Consolidated Combined Transport Directive adopted (92/106/EEC), expanding definitions and adding exemptions from driving bans.
- **2000** — Rail freight's share of EU inland freight stands at 18%; road freight is expanding.
- **2020** — Commission publishes evaluation of 92/106/EEC, concluding it has "failed to achieve its objectives." Rail freight share falls to 11%.
- **December 2020** — Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy sets modal-shift targets: 30% of road freight over 300 km to shift by 2030.
- **2021** — Commission's Freight Transport Action Plan announces intention to revise the Combined Transport Directive.
- **February 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; energy prices surge, boosting investment case for rail and water.
- **2022** — European Court of Auditors special report finds that 40% of core rail network bottlenecks have not been resolved.
- **7 November 2023** — Commission officially proposes the revision of Directive 92/106/EEC, alongside a proposal to increase permissible truck weights for zero-emission vehicles.
- **2024** — European Parliament TRAN committee adopts its report, proposing 130 amendments; negotiations with Council ongoing.

## Impact and consequences

If adopted as proposed, the revision would force all 27 member states to grant tax relief on combined transport — currently only 8 do so consistently, according to the Commission. It would eliminate the 150 km road-leg limit, opening up corridors like Hamburg-Munich and Rotterdam-Lyon to combined transport. The Commission estimated the revision could shift 10-15 million tonnes of freight annually from road to rail and water by 2035.

The road transport industry pushed back hard: the IRU argued in a December 2023 briefing that removing the 150 km limit would "destroy the level playing field" because combined transport would qualify for tax breaks even when the road leg dominated the journey. The European Rail Freight Association, representing operators like Lineas and Rail Cargo Group, countered that the current limit forced drivers to take empty trucks back to terminals beyond 150 km, wasting fuel.

Several countries — notably Poland, Romania, and Lithuania — warned that their rail networks lacked the capacity to absorb additional freight without massive investment. The Commission proposed a new "National Combined Transport Action Plan" requirement for member states with low modal shares. Germany, which already runs the world's largest logistics firm (DHL) and has the highest rail freight density, supported the revision but demanded tougher enforcement of driving-ban exemptions.

The real-world impact will depend heavily on whether member states actually enforce the new rules. The 1992 directive contained mandatory exemptions from road traffic bans, but many cities simply ignored them — the Commission found that in Vienna, combined transport was still not exempt from the night-time driving ban in 2022. The revision mandates automatic recognition and a single electronic document (the Intermodal Transport Document), modelled on a system used in the Alpex corridor between France and Spain.

## Key questions answered

### Why does so little freight go by rail in Europe?

Rail freight carries about 11% of inland freight in the EU, down from 18% in 2000. The reasons are: outdated infrastructure — much of the network was built for passenger or coal trains, not modern intermodal containers; high costs — the average freight train in the EU runs at 55% capacity, according to the Commission; and fragmented regulations — each member state still runs its own railway system, with different signalling, safety rules, and tax regimes. Combined transport, which should be easier, is buried in paperwork.

### Does the EU want to ban long-distance trucking?

No. The revision does not ban any truck journey — it creates incentives for combined transport. Companies can still drive a truck from Lisbon to Warsaw if they wish. But the 2030 target (shift 30% of road freight over 300 km) signals that the cost advantage of long-distance trucking — currently about 15% cheaper than rail on many corridors, according to the Commission — will be eroded through tax breaks and lower tolls for combined transport. Shippers that ignore the shift may face higher costs as road tolls rise.

### What currently stops a company from using combined transport?

Three main barriers: the 150 km road-leg limit means that a shipment from an industrial park 180 km from the nearest rail terminal cannot qualify for benefits; the administrative burden — a single combined transport shipment can require up to 10 different documents, including the CMR consignment note, intermodal bill of lading, and customs declarations; and inconsistent enforcement — a truck moving combined transport goods can be fined for violating a driving ban in Milan even though the directive exempts it. The revision eliminates the distance limit, standardises documentation (one electronic document), and forces cities to recognise exemptions.

### Could this actually work, given the state of EU rail infrastructure?

It will depend on investment. The Commission's 2023 TEN-T revision now requires all core rail corridors to support 740-metre trains (needed for efficient combined transport). But the European Court of Auditors found in 2022 that only 60% of the core network meets that standard. The revision also includes a "no backsliding" clause: no member state can worsen its combined transport incentives. The European Investment Bank has committed €30 billion for rail infrastructure in the 2021-2027 period, part of which is earmarked for intermodal terminals. The key question is not whether the directive is perfect — it is not — but whether it is better than the 1992 version, which had simply stopped working.
