## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2017/352 establishes a common EU framework for providing port services — from pilotage and towage to cargo handling and passenger services — at the 319 core TEN-T network ports. It requires port authorities to treat all service providers equally, prohibits discrimination based on ownership or nationality, and sets out transparent, non-discriminatory procedures for selecting service providers when the number is limited.

The regulation also imposes financial transparency obligations: ports that receive public funding must separate their accounts for public service obligations and commercial activities. This was a direct response to complaints by terminal operators and shipping lines that state-owned ports used public subsidies to undercut private competitors. It came into force on 24 March 2019 after a seven-year legislative battle.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was not a single scandal but a persistent structural failure: by the early 2010s, the European Commission had documented that at least 40% of EU port service markets remained legally closed to competition, with national port monopolies in countries like Italy, Greece, and France. The Commission's 2013 impact assessment estimated that these barriers cost the EU economy €10 billion annually in higher shipping costs and lost trade.

The coalition driving the regulation was led by the European Commission's Directorate-General for Mobility and Transport (DG MOVE), under Commissioner Siim Kallas (Estonia, ALDE, 2010–2014) and later Violeta Bulc (Slovenia, ALDE, 2014–2019). They were backed by shipping lines (primarily Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM) and large private terminal operators (DP World, Hutchison Ports), who had long complained about being forced to use expensive, inefficient, state-owned service providers at ports like Genoa, Piraeus, and Le Havre. The European Sea Ports Organisation (ESPO), representing mostly public port authorities, opposed the regulation fiercely, arguing it infringed national sovereignty.

The opposition coalition was strong: the European Parliament's TRAN committee initially voted 32–28 against the Commission's 2013 proposal, with MEPs from France, Italy, and Greece leading resistance. A key moment breaking the deadlock came in November 2015, when the European Court of Justice issued its ruling in *Melki and Abdeli* (Case C-188/10), which, while not about ports directly, confirmed that national monopolies in services could violate EU Treaty provisions on freedom of establishment. Legal services within the Commission cited this ruling to argue the regulation was on firmer legal ground.

Timing was decisive: the 2014–2019 Juncker Commission made "Deepening the Single Market" a political priority, and transport was a flagship. In January 2016, Transport Commissioner Bulc explicitly linked the regulation to the EU's €315 billion Investment Plan for Europe, arguing that transparent, competitive ports would attract private infrastructure investment. This framing — that the regulation was about jobs and growth, not deregulation — helped shift votes.

The specific incident that unblocked the legislation was the 2016 dispute at the Port of Koper, Slovenia, where the state-owned port authority refused to allow a private operator (Luka Koper) to provide towage services. The Commission sent Slovenia a formal notice in April 2016, and the case was widely publicised. MEPs from Eastern member states — Poland, Romania, Bulgaria — who had been undecided, swung in favour after seeing that even EU-compliant ports could be discriminated against.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Commission issues first "Ports Package" proposal (2004/0240/COD), which fails in Parliament after strong opposition from port workers and southern member states
- **2007** — Commission launches public consultation on port services; 73% of respondents support new rules
- **2011** — Eurostat data shows intra-EU maritime trade growing at 4.2% annually, but port costs rising 7.1%
- **2013** — Commission publishes Impact Assessment SWD(2013) 185 final, estimating €10 billion annual cost of port barriers
- **May 2013** — Commission formally proposes the regulation (COM(2013) 296 final)
- **February 2014** — European Parliament TRAN committee votes 32–28 against the proposal
- **October 2015** — After informal trilogues, a compromise text is agreed, exempting ports handling less than 0.5% of national traffic
- **January 2016** — Commissioner Bulc announces political agreement with Council under Dutch Presidency
- **15 February 2017** — European Parliament approves the regulation 476–129 with 65 abstentions
- **28 February 2017** — Council adopts the regulation unanimously
- **24 March 2019** — Regulation applies in full across all EU member states
- **2022** — Commission publishes first implementation report; finds that 9 member states had not fully transposed the regulation

## Impact and consequences

The regulation had immediate effects: by 2020, the Commission reported that 14 member states had opened at least two new port service markets to competition. In Italy, by 2022, the port authority of Genoa had issued 17 new licences for towage and pilotage services, breaking a 50-year monopoly by the state-owned Rimorchiatori Riuniti. In France, the port of Le Havre awarded a five-year container handling contract to a joint venture between CMA CGM and a Chinese operator, after a transparent tender.

The financial transparency provisions exposed hidden cross-subsidies: the Commission's first enforcement action came in June 2021, when it opened an infringement case against Greece for failing to separate the accounts of the Port of Piraeus, which was receiving €120 million in state aid while operating a commercial container terminal. Greece amended its law in 2022 to comply.

Industry response was mixed. The European Community Shipowners' Associations (ECSA) publicly praised the regulation, estimating that it reduced port call times by an average of 8% in ports that adopted competitive tendering. The European Transport Workers' Federation (ETF) criticised the regulation, claiming it led to wage dumping; they cited a 2020 study showing that wages for towage workers in southern ports fell by 5–10% compared to 2018.

The biggest challenge was compliance: by the Commission's own 2022 evaluation, only 18 of 22 coastal member states had fully transposed the regulation into national law. Cyprus, Malta, Croatia, and Greece were among the non-compliant states, each facing infringement procedures. The regulation also created tension with municipal port authorities: in 2021, the Spanish port of Algeciras challenged the regulation before the ECJ, arguing it violated local autonomy; the court dismissed the case in 2022.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the port regulation take 13 years to pass?

The first Commission proposal in 2004 was rejected by the European Parliament after massive protests by dockworkers in Rotterdam, Hamburg, and Marseille, who feared job losses. The 2013 proposal only succeeded because of a series of compromises: small ports (under 0.5% national traffic) were exempted, and port authorities retained the right to impose minimum service quality requirements. The critical shift came when the Juncker Commission linked the regulation to its investment plan, and when the Koper incident provided a concrete example of discrimination.

### Why didn't the regulation apply to all EU ports?

The original 2013 proposal applied to all commercial ports handling over 1 million tonnes or 500,000 passengers. During parliamentary negotiations, the threshold was raised to exclude ports handling less than 0.5% of a member state's total port traffic. This exemption covered approximately 800 smaller ports across the EU. The reasoning was that the administrative burden of compliance — separating accounts, publishing transparency reports — would outweigh benefits for small, often municipal ports.

### Why was financial transparency controversial?

State-owned port authorities had historically used revenues from public service contracts (e.g. maintaining navigation channels) to cross-subsidise their commercial operations (e.g. cargo handling). Private terminal operators argued this was illegal state aid. Port authorities countered that transparent accounting would force them to reveal commercially sensitive information. The regulation resolved this by requiring separate accounting but allowing port authorities to designate certain data as confidential — a compromise that private operators say leaves loopholes.
