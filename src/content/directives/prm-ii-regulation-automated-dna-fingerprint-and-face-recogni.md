## What is it?

The Prüm II Regulation (2023/1729/EU) is an EU law that mandates police forces across member states to automatically search each other's national databases for DNA profiles, fingerprint data, and — for the first time at EU level — facial images of suspected criminals. It also requires automated exchange of certain categories of police records. The regulation replaces and expands the earlier Prüm Decisions from 2008.

Under the system, a police officer investigating a crime in, say, France can submit a digital request that automatically queries databases in all other member states. If a match is found (a "hit"), the requesting country receives basic confirmation and then can request full personal data through a separate mutual legal assistance channel. The core principle remains "hit/no-hit" — personal data is not centrally stored in a single EU database but remains in national systems.

The regulation establishes a **Centralised Routing System** operated by eu-LISA (the EU agency for IT systems in justice and home affairs) to manage the traffic of queries, replacing the previous decentralised network of bilateral connections. It also sets common technical standards for data quality and exchange, and adds obligations for member states to participate within specific timeframes.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a botched police chase in 2015 that became a political scandal known across Europe as the "Paris train attack". On 21 August 2015, a heavily armed man — Ayoub El Khazzani — opened fire on a Thalys train travelling from Amsterdam to Paris, injuring several passengers before being overpowered. French and Belgian authorities later discovered that El Khazzani had been flagged in Spanish intelligence databases as a known jihadist sympathiser, but this information had not been shared in real time with French police or border control. The attack exposed a gaping hole in cross-border police information sharing — the existing Schengen Information System (SIS) was supposed to catch such individuals, but it relied on national police forces proactively entering data, which they often failed to do.

The European Commission, under First Vice-President Frans Timmermans and Commissioner for Migration and Home Affairs Dimitris Avramopoulos, responded with the **European Security Agenda** in April 2015, which identified fragmented law enforcement data systems as a key vulnerability. But the immediate political impetus came from the November 2015 Paris attacks, when coordinated suicide bombings and shootings killed 130 people. Investigators later found that several of the attackers had travelled freely between Belgium and France, and that French intelligence had not been able to query Belgian police databases automatically for fingerprints or DNA found at crime scenes. The hit/no-hit system under the original Prüm Decisions (2008/615/JHA and 2008/616/JHA) existed on paper but was technically underused: as of 2017, the Commission's evaluation found that only 13 member states had fully operational bilateral connections for DNA exchange, and only 8 for fingerprints.

The structural tension was clear: the original Prüm framework, designed for a small club of seven countries (the 2005 Prüm Convention was signed by Belgium, Germany, Spain, France, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, and Austria as an intergovernmental treaty), relied on voluntary bilateral agreements for each new connection. This created a two-speed Europe where well-connected countries exchanged data efficiently while newer or smaller member states remained outside the network. Meanwhile, the 2008 EU Decisions had no central oversight — no agency was responsible for monitoring whether countries actually connected.

The coalition that pushed for Prüm II formed around the **German Presidency of the Council in the second half of 2020**. Germany, which had been a champion of the original Prüm Convention, made police data interoperability a priority. The German Federal Ministry of the Interior, under Horst Seehofer, argued that the fragmented system was no longer tolerable after the 2015-2016 terrorist attacks in Paris, Brussels, Berlin, and Nice. The **Dutch** joined as strong supporters, having conducted a pilot project in 2019 that demonstrated how automated exchange could connect police, immigration, and EU databases.

Opposition came from civil liberties groups — notably **EDRi** (European Digital Rights) and **Politico** reported strong pushback from the **European Parliament's civil liberties committee (LIBE)** during negotiations in 2021-2022. The core objections were: (1) the expansion to facial images and police records opened the door to mass surveillance, since facial recognition could be used to identify bystanders or protesters, not just suspects; (2) the Centralised Routing System created a de facto central EU database of biometric searches, even if the data remained in national systems; (3) data quality standards were weak — a biometric "hit" on a poor-quality image or partial fingerprint could lead to false arrests. The **German and French governments**, however, insisted that without facial images the system would become obsolete, as criminals increasingly used encrypted messaging and cashless transactions, leaving fewer physical traces.

The timing was decisive: the **2020-2021 period** saw the **European Commission adopt the Security Union Strategy** (July 2020) and the **Schengen Strategy** (June 2021), both of which explicitly called for mandatory automated data exchange. The Commission under Ursula von der Leyen, with Commissioner for Home Affairs Ylva Johansson, made this a legislative priority. The **COVID-19 pandemic** also shifted the political centre of gravity — governments were under pressure to show they could protect citizens, and digital cross-border policing seemed like a concrete, technical fix that avoided more controversial measures like strengthening Europol or expanding SIS.

What changed the legislative dynamic was a specific event: the **February 2021 publication of the Commission's impact assessment** for the "Prüm II" proposal. The assessment contained a modelling exercise showing that automated facial image exchange, covering only convicted offenders and wanted persons, could have identified the **2016 Berlin Christmas market attacker (Anis Amri)** three days earlier, and the **2020 Vienna attacker** (Kujtim Fejzullai) several hours earlier. Commission officials briefed these figures intensively to member state ministers during the Portuguese Presidency (first half of 2021). By the time the formal proposal was published on 8 December 2021, the political resistance had weakened enough that the Parliament and Council reached a preliminary agreement in June 2023, with final adoption on 13 July 2023.

The voting in the Council was not unanimous: **Austria and Slovenia** abstained, citing concerns about data protection and technical implementation burdens for smaller countries. In the Parliament, the final plenary vote was 469 in favour, 137 against, with 39 abstentions — a comfortable majority that masked deep divisions in the LIBE committee.

## Timeline

- **2005** — Seven EU member states sign the Prüm Convention, establishing automated DNA and fingerprint exchange outside the EU framework.
- **2008** — Prüm Decisions (2008/615/JHA and 2008/616/JHA) incorporate the Convention into EU law, mandating all member states to eventually connect.
- **August 2015** — Thalys train attack; French and Belgian authorities discover critical information was not shared across borders.
- **November 2015** — Paris attacks (130 killed); investigation reveals automated data exchange failures between France and Belgium.
- **April 2015** — Commission publishes European Security Agenda, calling for interoperability of police databases.
- **December 2016** — Berlin Christmas market attack (12 killed); attacker Anis Amri had been in German police databases but cross-border checks failed.
- **2017** — Commission evaluation finds only 13 member states have operational DNA connections; 8 for fingerprints.
- **July 2020** — Commission adopts Security Union Strategy, promising legislative proposal for mandatory automated data exchange.
- **December 2020** — German Presidency of the Council prioritises Prüm reform; Ministerial conference on police data interoperability.
- **2019** — Dutch pilot project (connected police, immigration, and Europol databases) demonstrates feasibility of centralised routing.
- **June 2021** — Commission publishes Schengen Strategy, reiterating commitment to Prüm expansion.
- **8 December 2021** — Commission publishes formal proposal for Prüm II Regulation, including facial images.
- **June 2023** — European Parliament and Council reach provisional political agreement.
- **13 July 2023** — Regulation adopted (2023/1729/EU), published in Official Journal.
- **30 July 2023** — Entry into force (20 days after publication).
- **3 January 2028** — Deadline for member states to implement connection for DNA and fingerprints.
- **3 January 2029** — Deadline for implementation of facial image exchange.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation affects all 27 EU member states plus **Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Switzerland** (associated Schengen states). Approximately **3,000 police forces** across the EU will need to upgrade their national databases to comply with the new data quality standards and connect to the Centralised Routing System operated by eu-LISA.

The most immediate practical consequence is that **face recognition** will now be used routinely across borders for criminal investigations. Europol, in its 2022 "European Union Serious and Organised Crime Threat Assessment (SOCTA)", estimated that over 70% of crime networks in the EU operate in multiple member states, making automated biometric exchange a logical — if controversial — tool. The regulation explicitly **prohibits the use of biometric data for racial profiling or indiscriminate mass surveillance**, but civil liberties organisations like EDRi argue the safeguards are insufficient because the data quality thresholds for facial images (set out in Article 9) are low enough to allow erroneous matches, and individuals are not informed that their biometric data is being searched.

Member states must now appoint a **national contact point** responsible for ensuring data quality compliance. The estimated **one-off implementation cost** per member state, according to the Commission's 2021 impact assessment, ranges from €2-8 million depending on the size of the country and the state of its existing connections. Smaller states like **Malta and Luxembourg** are expected to face disproportionate costs relative to population.

A key early test case will be **France**, which has been rolling out its own national facial recognition system (TAJ: Traitement des Antécédents Judiciaires). French police already use facial image searches on a database of 18 million images of convicted offenders and suspects. Under Prüm II, this data must become automatically searchable by all other member states — a step that French privacy regulator CNIL has warned could lead to "mission creep" if the scope is later expanded to include surveillance footage from public spaces.

The Centralised Routing System will be built and managed by **eu-LISA** at its headquarters in Tallinn, Estonia, with a satellite site in Strasbourg. eu-LISA already operates similar infrastructure for the Schengen Information System (SIS) and Eurodac (asylum fingerprint database). The agency's 2024 budget allocated €14 million for the Prüm II routing system development.

## Key questions answered

### Can a German police officer automatically search French face recognition databases?

Yes, if the German officer is investigating a specific criminal offence. Under the Prüm II Regulation, a police officer in one member state can submit a digital request that automatically queries facial image databases in all other participating states. The system returns a "hit/no-hit" result — if a match is found, the requesting country can then request full personal data through a separate mutual legal assistance procedure. The search is not for mass surveillance — it must be linked to a specific crime investigation.

### What happens if a facial recognition match is wrong?

A false match (a "false hit") can lead to the wrong person being questioned, detained, or placed under surveillance. The regulation requires each member state to designate a **national contact point** that must verify the match before sharing personal data. The verification involves a manual check by a trained officer — the system alone cannot authorise an arrest. If a false match leads to wrongful detention, the individual can seek compensation under national law, but there is no automatic EU-level redress mechanism.

### Why does this regulation include police records, not just biometrics?

Police records — criminal records, arrest records, and case files — are included because a biometric match is often useless without context. A DNA match at a crime scene might belong to a legitimate visitor to the building, not a suspect. The regulation mandates exchange of **police records in categories linked to the offence being investigated** (Article 13), so that a hit on a fingerprint or facial image can be evaluated alongside the known circumstances: was this person a victim, a witness, on the scene for an unrelated reason, or the suspect? Without this context, automated biometric exchange risks wasting police time and violating innocent people's privacy.

### Does the regulation apply to migrants or asylum seekers?

No — the Prüm II Regulation explicitly excludes the use of facial images and fingerprints from migration and asylum databases for criminal investigations unless a judge authorises it on a case-by-case basis (Article 14). This was a key demand of the European Parliament during negotiations, to prevent "mission creep" where asylum seekers' biometric data (collected under Eurodac) would be routinely searched for criminal matches. The separation of criminal and migration databases remains a politically sensitive boundary in EU data protection law.
