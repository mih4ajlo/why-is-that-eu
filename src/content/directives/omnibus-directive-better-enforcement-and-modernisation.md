## What is it?

The Omnibus Directive (2019/2161/EU) represented the most significant overhaul of EU consumer protection enforcement rules since the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC). Its central purpose was to modernise four existing directives — the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive, the Consumer Rights Directive, the Unfair Contract Terms Directive, and the Price Indication Directive — to address gaps exposed by the digital economy and cross-border enforcement failures.

The directive introduced harmonised maximum fines of at least 4% of annual turnover for widespread cross-border infringements, extended consumer rights to digital content and online services (including "free" services where users provide personal data), imposed new transparency obligations on online marketplaces regarding search rankings and seller identity, and prohibited dual-quality products that mislead consumers about composition.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the Volkswagen "Dieselgate" scandal of September 2015, which exposed a fundamental weakness: the patchwork of national enforcement systems allowed companies to face dramatically different penalties for identical violations across member states. Volkswagen faced a potential €20 billion in US penalties but only administrative fines capped at €100,000 under German law for the same conduct in Europe. Internal Commission documents later showed officials calculating that the maximum combined EU penalty for a pan-European violation of consumer rules was often less than the cost of compliance.

European Commissioner for Justice Vera Jourová, who took office in November 2014, made consumer enforcement reform a signature priority. She told the European Parliament's Internal Market Committee in December 2015 that "trust in the single market requires that consumers know they have the same protections whether they buy in Lisbon or Helsinki." The Commission's 2017 "New Deal for Consumers" consultation, launched by Jourová and Vice-President Jyrki Katainen, received over 400 responses from consumer organisations, businesses, and national authorities.

The business coalition opposing the directive was led by BusinessEurope, DigitalEurope, and the American Chamber of Commerce to the EU. Their central argument — articulated by DigitalEurope's Director General Cecilia Bonefeld-Dahl in a July 2018 position paper — was that harmonised penalties would "create legal uncertainty and disproportionate burdens on SMEs" while the real problem was "inconsistent national enforcement, not insufficient fines." The German and Austrian governments were the strongest opponents in the Council, arguing that their existing systems with maximum fines of €250,000 and €300,000 respectively were adequate.

The political breakthrough came in two phases. First, the European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO) rapporteur, German MEP Evelyne Gebhardt (S&D), succeeded in April 2018 in getting the Parliament to adopt a negotiating position that included the 4% turnover threshold — higher than the Commission's original 2% proposal. Second, the Austrian Council Presidency in late 2018 brokered a compromise that exempted purely national infringements from the turnover-based penalties while keeping them for cross-border cases. The final trilogue agreement in November 2018 was adopted by the Parliament with 545 votes in favour and 89 against.

The structural tension driving the directive was the "enforcement gap": while the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive and Consumer Rights Directive had harmonised substantive rules across the EU since 2005 and 2011 respectively, enforcement remained entirely national. Maximum penalties varied from €5,000 in Lithuania to €1 million in the Netherlands, with most member states lacking any turnover-based calculation. A 2017 Commission evaluation found that between 2011-2016, only €2.6 million in total fines had been imposed across the EU for cross-border consumer infringements, while the Commission's 2018 impact assessment estimated consumer detriment from cross-border violations at €10-12 billion annually.

The e-commerce dimension was pushed heavily by the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC), whose Director General Monique Goyens in a March 2018 letter to Commissioners Jourová and Thyssen documented systematic problems with online marketplaces: "Consumers cannot distinguish between professional sellers and private individuals, search rankings are opaque, and reviews are frequently manipulated." The final directive included specific transparency obligations for online marketplaces — a provision that Amazon, eBay, and Alibaba had lobbied against through their industry associations.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact was on national legislation. At least 18 member states needed to amend their consumer enforcement laws, with Germany and Austria — the strongest opponents — having to raise their maximum fines for cross-border violations. The UK, despite leaving the EU in January 2020, transposed the directive in March 2020 through the Consumer Protection (Enforcement) Regulations 2020.

Industry responses varied. Amazon and eBay publicly announced compliance with the transparency obligations by May 2022, including displaying seller identification and explaining ranking parameters. The International Air Transport Association (IATA) specifically criticised the dual-quality provisions in a July 2020 position paper, warning they would "disrupt long-established supply chain practices" for fast-moving consumer goods — a reference to Nestlé, Danone, and other companies that had been found selling lower-quality versions of branded products in Eastern European markets.

The first major enforcement action came in December 2022, when the Belgian, Dutch, and Luxembourg consumer authorities jointly fined an unnamed furniture retailer €1.2 million under the new harmonised penalty system for misleading discounts during a 2020 Black Friday campaign. The European Commission's 2023 Consumer Conditions Scorecard reported that, as of early 2023, six member states had launched investigations using the new penalty framework.

Consumer groups have criticised the implementation. BEUC's Deputy Director General Ursula Pachl stated in April 2023 that "the 4% turnover threshold remains unused in most member states because national authorities still prefer their domestic maximum fines." The Commission's 2024 evaluation found that only three cross-border cases had resulted in turnover-based penalties in the first two years, with most national enforcers continuing to apply domestic caps.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the Volkswagen scandal lead to new EU consumer protection rules instead of just using existing environmental or competition laws?

The Volkswagen scandal was the catalyst because it demonstrated concretely that European consumer enforcement was structurally incapable of deterring large-scale, pan-European corporate violations. German consumer authorities admitted in internal memos that maximum fines of €100,000 — representing roughly 0.0002% of Volkswagen's €213 billion annual revenue — were "completely inadequate." The scandal was not about environmental damages but about the failure of consumer protection systems to match market reality.

### Why do EU rules now cover "free" digital services like social media and cloud storage, even though consumers don't pay money for them?

The Commission's 2018 impact assessment argued that consumers provide personal data as counter-performance when using "free" platforms like Facebook, Google, and Instagram, and should therefore be entitled to the same consumer rights as paying customers. The inclusion was pushed by MEP Evelyne Gebhardt and supported by the S&D group, over opposition from DigitalEurope and Google, which argued in a March 2018 submission that "users of free services are not consumers in the legal sense." The final text applied consumer rights to digital services where "the consumer pays with personal data."

### Why didn't the EU create one single agency to enforce consumer rights across all member countries?

The Commission initially considered creating a European Consumer Enforcement Authority modelled on the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) or the European Competition Network, but this was rejected by the Council. A June 2018 Council legal opinion concluded that EU consumer enforcement lacked the legal basis under Article 114 TFEU for a centralised enforcement agency. Instead, the directive strengthened the Consumer Protection Cooperation (CPC) Regulation from 2017, which had created a network of national authorities. The compromise allowed for harmonised penalties without establishing new EU-level institutions.

### Why did the EU decide to specifically crack down on companies selling lower-quality versions of the same product in different countries?

The issue gained prominence through a 2016 study by the Slovakian consumer authority, which found that identical-brand products (including Coca-Cola, Nestlé coffee, and Pringles) had different compositions in Eastern versus Western Europe. Commissioner Jourová called a 2017 summit with food manufacturers in Brussels, where PepsiCo and Unilever publicly denied the practice. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) tested 128 products across 12 member states in 2018 and found 23% had materially different compositions under the same brand. The Western European industry associations — FoodDrinkEurope and AIM — lobbied against the prohibition, arguing that "market preferences legitimately vary," but the Commission concluded that the practice was misleading when packaging and branding were identical.
