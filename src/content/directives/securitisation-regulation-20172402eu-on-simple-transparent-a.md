## What is it?

Securitisation is the financial process of pooling loans — mortgages, car loans, credit card debt — and selling them to investors as bonds. Before 2008, this market was huge and largely unregulated. The 2017 Securitisation Regulation, part of the EU's Capital Markets Union agenda, created a single rulebook for all securitisation in the European Union, replacing a patchwork of national approaches.

The regulation introduces a new label: "simple, transparent and standardised" (STS) securitisation. To qualify, a securitisation must meet strict criteria on the underlying assets (they must be homogeneous, not re-securitised), the structure (clear waterfall of payments, no complex triggers), and the disclosure to investors (loan-level data must be available). The STS designation is meant to signal quality, allowing investors to conduct less intensive due diligence and allowing banks to hold less capital against STS positions.

Beyond STS, the regulation also imposes uniform rules on risk retention — the requirement that originators keep 5% of the credit risk of any securitisation they create — and on due diligence by institutional investors. It also bans re-securitisation (packaging securitised bonds into new securitisations) except for limited, justified cases like managing non-performing exposures.

## Why was it introduced?

The entire rationale for the Securitisation Regulation is a direct response to the 2007–2008 global financial crisis. Before the crisis, securitisation was the engine of the so-called "originate-to-distribute" model: banks made loans, packaged them into bonds, and sold them to investors. The system collapsed because securitisations were often opaque, contained toxic subprime mortgages, and the originators had no incentive to ensure loan quality since they offloaded all risk.

In the EU, the crisis exposed a specific structural failure: securitisation markets froze completely. Between 2008 and 2012, issuance of residential mortgage-backed securities (RMBS) in Europe dropped by roughly 75%, according to the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA). The market never fully recovered. The European Central Bank (ECB) repeatedly warned that the lack of a functioning securitisation market was choking bank lending to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which rely on bank loans more than in the US.

The political coalition that drove the regulation was an unusual alliance. On one side stood the European Commission under President Jean-Claude Juncker, whose 2015 Capital Markets Union action plan explicitly called for "reviving high-quality securitisation" to free up bank balance sheets. Then-Commissioner Jonathan Hill (Financial Stability, Financial Services and Capital Markets Union) pushed it as a supply-side reform to increase private sector lending without taxpayer risk. The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Paul Tang (S&D, Netherlands), supported the STS framework but insisted on strong investor protections and a ban on re-securitisation.

Opposition came from two fronts. First, some Member States — particularly Germany and France — were wary of any relaxation that might reignite US-style "subprime" practices. Their finance ministries pushed for strict criteria that would ensure only truly safe products got the STS label. Second, consumer groups and left-leaning MEPs argued the whole exercise was a gift to the banking industry. Finance Watch, the Brussels-based NGO, warned in a 2015 report that "the problem of the last crisis was not a lack of simple securitisation, but a conviction that complex securitisation was simple."

The timing was driven by two factors. First, the 2014–2015 period saw a political window: the acute phase of the eurozone crisis was over, but bank lending was still weak. The ECB's quantitative easing, launched in early 2015, included purchases of asset-backed securities, giving the central bank a direct interest in market quality. Second, international momentum mattered. In 2014, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) and the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO) published their own STS criteria, and the EU wanted to align while going further.

What changed to unblock earlier deadlock? The key breakthrough was the 2015 agreement on the Capital Markets Union. Before that, securitisation reform was stuck in a cycle of national fragmentation and post-crisis distrust. The CMU gave the Commission a compelling narrative: market-based financing needed to complement bank lending, and securitisation was the missing link. The 2016 "Brexit" vote also inadvertently accelerated the regulation, as London — Europe's securitisation hub — was now bound for exit, making a unified EU rulebook even more urgent.

## Timeline

- **2008–2012** — EU securitisation market collapses to roughly 25% of pre-crisis levels (ESMA estimate); regulators focus on capital requirements and risk retention.
- **2014** — BCBS-IOSCO publish international STS criteria; EU begins internal work on regulation.
- **30 September 2015** — European Commission publishes the Capital Markets Union action plan, including a legislative proposal for a Securitisation Regulation.
- **30 September 2015** — Commission proposes the regulation; rapporteur Paul Tang (S&D) leads EP negotiations.
- **December 2016** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement; key compromises include a ban on re-securitisation and strict STS criteria.
- **17 January 2017** — Regulation (EU) 2017/2402 published in the Official Journal.
- **1 January 2019** — Regulation becomes fully applicable; STS notifications begin with ESMA.
- **2021** — ESMA reports that the STS market has not grown as fast as hoped; only around 60 STS securitisations registered by mid-2021.
- **2022** — European Commission proposes amendments to integrate securitisation more closely with the EU's sustainable finance framework.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation achieved its core goal of standardising the EU securitisation framework. National rules — previously in place in 11 Member States — were replaced by a single regime. But the market revival has been modest. According to data from the Association for Financial Markets in Europe (AFME), EU securitisation issuance in 2020 was €235 billion, still far below the 2008 peak of over €1 trillion. The STS segment accounted for roughly 10–20% of that, below initial expectations.

Industry response was mixed. Large banks — particularly BNP Paribas, Deutsche Bank, and Santander — actively sought STS designation, as it allowed them to reduce capital requirements. But smaller originators, such as regional banks and non-bank lenders, found the compliance costs high. The European Banking Authority (EBA) in 2020 noted that "the costs of achieving STS status may outweigh the benefits for smaller originators."

The most significant unintended consequence has been the continued dominance of the UK securitisation market post-Brexit. London remains the largest European securitisation hub, and EU issuers now face higher costs when dealing with UK-based counterparties. The regulation did not — and perhaps could not — revive the cross-border market to the degree hoped.

On enforcement, ESMA has been cautious. As of 2023, no major fines had been issued under the regulation, though the authority has issued several public statements reminding originators of disclosure requirements. The risk retention rule — the 5% skin-in-the-game — has been widely complied with, but a 2021 ESMA peer review found that supervisory practices varied significantly across Member States.

## Key questions answered

### Why does Europe need a special label for financial products that are "simple"?

The 2008 financial crisis showed that investors could not distinguish between safe and toxic securitisations. The STS label is meant to solve an information problem: by setting objective, verifiable criteria, it allows investors to trust that an STS product meets minimum quality standards without having to conduct full due diligence. Banks also get lower capital charges for holding STS securitisations, creating an incentive to use the label.

### Did EU rules revive the market for bundling loans into investment products after the financial crisis?

Only partly. The regulation unified the rules and created the STS framework, but total EU securitisation issuance by 2020 was still about 75% below pre-crisis levels (AFME data). The market has shifted: post-crisis, most EU securitisation is now retained by banks for use as collateral with the ECB, rather than sold to investors. The STS label helped but did not spark the renaissance the Commission hoped for.

### Who opposed stricter rules on bundling loans into investment products, and why?

Large European banks opposed the strictest versions of the regulation, arguing that overly tight criteria would make securitisation uneconomical. The European Banking Federation (EBF) lobbied for simpler capital treatment and warned that EU rules should not be stricter than global Basel standards. Consumer groups and some MEPs argued the opposite: that the regulation was too industry-friendly and would allow banks to originate risky loans again.

### What happens if a bundled loan investment product doesn't meet the "simple and transparent" criteria?

It can still exist and be traded, but it does not get the STS label. Investors must conduct full due diligence, and banks must hold more capital against it. The regulation applies equally to non-STS securitisations in terms of risk retention and disclosure requirements. The only difference is the public designation and the associated capital relief and due diligence simplification.
