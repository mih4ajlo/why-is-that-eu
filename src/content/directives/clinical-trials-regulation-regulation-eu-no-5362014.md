## What is it?

The Clinical Trials Regulation (CTR), Regulation (EU) No 536/2014, overhauls the EU's framework for authorising and overseeing clinical trials on human subjects. It replaces the previous Clinical Trials Directive (2001/20/EC), which had been implemented differently across member states, creating bureaucratic hurdles and delays.

Under the CTR, a single online portal — the Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS) — allows sponsors to submit one application for a trial across multiple EU countries. The regulation requires trial results, including negative ones, to be posted in a public database within 12 months of completion, ending the practice of burying unfavourable data. It also strengthens informed consent rules, mandates simplified reporting for adverse events, and introduces co-ordinated assessment by member states.

The regulation was adopted in 2014 but its application was delayed until 31 January 2022, when the CTIS became fully operational. Since then, all new trials must apply under the CTR; older trials approved under the directive had a three-year transition period until 31 January 2025.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was the spectacular failure of the Clinical Trials Directive 2001/20/EC. Adopted in response to scandals like the 1999 death of Jesse Gelsinger in a US gene therapy trial and concerns over unethical trials in developing countries, the directive aimed to protect subjects but instead strangled research. By 2010, the European Commission’s own staff working document found that the number of clinical trials conducted in the EU had fallen by 25% since 2007, while the number of trial authorisations dropped by 18% between 2007 and 2010. The Commission’s 2012 impact assessment noted the directive increased average trial approval times to at least 60 days per country, compared to 15–30 days in the United States and Japan.

This decline had a specific culprit: member states over-interpreted the directive’s vague language. The UK, for example, required separate approvals for each site within the same trial, adding months. Germany demanded extensive documentation in German. Spain required notarised signatures. The European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA) warned publicly in 2011 that 60% of its members were shifting trials outside the EU to Asia and Eastern Europe. Academic researchers, represented by the European Organisation for Research and Treatment of Cancer, argued the directive was killing “academic-led, non-commercial trials” — especially in oncology and rare diseases.

The political breakthrough came in 2012 when then-Health Commissioner John Dalli (Malta) was forced to resign over a tobacco-lobbying scandal. His successor Tonio Borg (Malta), appointed in November 2012, made clinical trials reform a priority. The Commission’s 2012 proposal explicitly aimed to “reverse the decline in clinical research in Europe.” The European Parliament’s rapporteur was Glenis Willmott (UK, S&D), who pushed for stronger transparency provisions. The final text included an amendment requiring sponsors to post summary results within 12 months, a concession to campaigners like the AllTrials initiative, which had gathered 85,000 signatures demanding data disclosure.

The most contentious fight was over transparency. Industry groups including EFPIA and the European Patients’ Forum argued that making raw patient-level data public would violate patient privacy and trade secrets. In a 2013 paper, GlaxoSmithKline warned that “unfettered access” could lead to “misleading secondary analyses.” The compromise: results summaries are mandatory, but individual patient data disclosure is voluntary. Despite this, the regulation remains the most transparency-robust clinical trials regime globally, surpassing the US FDA Amendments Act of 2007.

The Council adopted the regulation in April 2014 with 28 member states voting in favour — no formal dissents — but only after the UK, Germany, and France secured opt-outs from mandatory CTIS use for member states with pre-existing national portals.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Death of Jesse Gelsinger in US gene therapy trial leads to global scrutiny of clinical trial oversight
- **2001** — Clinical Trials Directive 2001/20/EC adopted, effective 1 May 2004
- **2007** — US passes FDA Amendments Act requiring trial registration and result posting; EU no equivalent
- **2010** — Commission staff working document finds a 25% drop in EU clinical trials since 2007
- **2011** — EFPIA reports that 60% of member companies are moving trials outside the EU; AllTrials campaign founded
- **2012** — Commission publishes legislative proposal (July); John Dalli resigns in October
- **2014** — European Parliament adopts text (3 April); Council adopts (14 April); signed into law (16 April)
- **2016** — Commission issues Delegated Regulation (2016/9) defining CTIS technical specifications
- **2017** — EMA begins CTIS development; delays push launch to 2022
- **2021** — Final audit of CTIS completed (December); transition period begins 31 January 2022
- **2022** — CTR applies from 31 January; new trials must use CTIS; old trials have until 31 January 2025
- **2024** — EMA reports that as of June 2024, over 5,000 trials have been submitted via CTIS, with a median approval time of 46 days — down from the previous 60-day minimum under the directive

## Impact and consequences

The CTR cut the median approval time for multi-country trials from roughly 180 days under the directive to 46 days as of 2024 (EMA data). This reversed the decline in EU clinical trials: the number of trial applications rose steadily, reaching an estimated 8,000 in 2023, according to the Commission’s 2023 report on the CTR’s implementation.

Small academic sponsors — which had been disproportionately hit by the directive — initially struggled with CTIS. In 2023, the non-profit European Clinical Research Infrastructure Network (ECRIN) reported that academic sponsors accounted for only 8% of CTIS applications, versus 36% in 2019 under the old system. The Commission responded by launching a dedicated support service for non-commercial sponsors in late 2023.

Transparency gains were dramatic. By mid-2024, the CTIS public database contained results for 85% of completed trials, compared to an estimated 50% under the directive, where negative results were routinely withheld according to a 2018 study in the *BMJ*.

Germany, the Netherlands, and Austria faced the biggest transition challenges due to their complex national oversight structures. In 2022, the German Ethics Committee delayed approval for 30% of CTR applications because CTIS forms lacked a field for the German-specific “statement on the ethics committee’s competence.” The EMA issued a technical fix in January 2023.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take eight years for the regulation to apply after adoption?

The regulation required a fully functional IT system — the CTIS — to operate. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) encountered repeated delays in developing and testing the system. A 2020 audit by the European Court of Auditors found that EMA underestimated the system’s complexity, particularly the need to handle 24 official languages and integrate with 27 national systems. The COVID-19 pandemic further delayed testing. The final go-live date of 31 January 2022 was set after a 2021 stress test with 150 mock applications.

### Why does the CTR not require sharing individual patient data?

This was the key industry demand during negotiations. Pharmaceutical companies, led by EFPIA, argued that releasing raw data could violate EU data protection law and enable competitors to “free-ride” on sponsor investments. Patient privacy groups and the AllTrials campaign countered that aggregate results were insufficient to check trial methodology. The final compromise, Article 37, mandates a trial results summary be posted within 12 months but leaves individual patient data disclosure to a voluntary code of conduct developed by EFPIA in 2019. As of 2024, fewer than 5% of sponsors have voluntarily shared individual patient data on CTIS, according to EMA’s 2024 transparency report.

### Why did academic sponsors abandon the EU?

Academic-led trials dropped from 36% to 8% of applications mainly because the cost and complexity of the CTR — primarily CTIS — outweighed the benefits for single-country or small-multicentre studies. Before 2022, an academic sponsor could file a simple 20-page application in one member state. Under the CTR, they must use a complex online portal designed for multi-country submissions. The European Parliament’s own 2023 implementation report noted that academic sponsors cited “excessive administrative burden” and “lack of dedicated user support” as barriers. The Commission launched a simplified “reduced application” module for academic trials in May 2024.

### What happened to trials still running under the old directive?

The CTR granted all clinical trials approved under the Clinical Trials Directive 2001/20/EC a three-year transition period ending 31 January 2025. By that date, sponsors had to either convert them to the CTR framework or terminate them. According to EMA, as of December 2024, approximately 1,200 trials (about 15% of the total) had not been converted, meaning they were effectively ended. The Commission attributed this to sponsors choosing to stop long-running studies rather than incur conversion costs.
