## What is it?

The Pregnant Workers Directive (92/85/EEC) sets EU-wide minimum standards for protecting the health and safety of pregnant workers, workers who have recently given birth, and breastfeeding workers. It requires employers to assess workplace risks and adjust working conditions or grant paid leave when risks cannot be eliminated. The directive also mandates a minimum of 14 weeks of maternity leave, including two weeks of compulsory leave, and protects workers from dismissal for reasons related to pregnancy.

The directive prohibits requiring pregnant workers to perform night work where a medical certificate deems it necessary for their safety, and grants women the right to time off with pay for antenatal examinations. It establishes a floor of rights — Member States are free to offer more generous protections. In practice, most EU countries exceed the directive's minimums: by 2023, nearly all Member States offered at least 16 weeks of maternity leave, and many offered 20 weeks or more.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was triggered not by a single crisis, but by a decade of structural tension between the EU's workplace health and safety framework and the reality of women's labour force participation. By the late 1980s, women made up over 40% of the EU workforce, yet pregnancy-related discrimination remained widespread and largely unaddressed at the national level in several Member States. The European Commission's 1988 social action programme, under Commission President Jacques Delors, identified pregnant workers as a vulnerable group needing explicit protection.

The immediate catalyst was the 1990 Court of Justice ruling in *Dekker v Stichting* (Case C-177/88), where the Court held that refusing to hire a pregnant woman constitutes direct sex discrimination under the Equal Treatment Directive (76/207/EEC). The ruling exposed a gap: while sex discrimination law covered pregnancy discrimination in hiring, it did not mandate specific health and safety protections or paid leave. The Commission, led by Social Affairs Commissioner Vasso Papandreou, seized on the ruling to argue that a dedicated directive was necessary. Papandreou, a Greek socialist and former minister, championed the measure as part of the broader Social Charter action programme, pushing for binding legislation where earlier attempts under the 1970s and 1980s had stalled over cost concerns.

The coalition for the directive was unusual: it united trade unions (particularly the European Trade Union Confederation), women's rights groups, and health and safety professionals, who argued that pregnancy was not a disability but a normal condition requiring workplace accommodation. Opposition came from several northern European Member States with already generous national schemes — Denmark and the Netherlands initially resisted what they saw as a lowering of standards through EU minimums. The UK government under Prime Minister John Major was the most vocal opponent, arguing the directive was a "back-door" extension of social policy that would increase employer costs and reduce women's employment. The UK's opt-out from the Maastricht Treaty's Social Chapter in 1991 gave it leverage in negotiations, but the directive was adopted under the health and safety legal base (Article 118a of the EEC Treaty), which required only qualified majority voting — meaning the UK could not veto it.

The directive passed the Council of Ministers on 19 October 1992. The UK voted against but was outvoted. The directive entered into force in October 1994, with Member States required to transpose it into national law by October 1994 — a deadline several states, including Belgium, Germany, and Luxembourg, missed. The final text represented a compromise: the Commission had originally proposed 16 weeks of maternity leave, but this was reduced to 14 weeks after Member States objected to costs. The protection against dismissal was also weakened from an absolute ban to one requiring Member States to "take the necessary measures" — language that some governments interpreted loosely.

## Impact and consequences

The directive transformed the legal position of pregnant workers across the EU. Before 1992, several Member States — notably Ireland, Greece, and Portugal — had no statutory maternity leave of any kind, or only very limited protections. Within five years of transposition, all Member States had minimum maternity leave of at least 14 weeks, paid at or above the level of statutory sick pay. A 1999 Commission report found that the directive had "significantly improved" health and safety risk assessments in workplaces, though compliance varied widely between large firms and small enterprises.

The most immediate legal effect was the explicit ban on dismissal due to pregnancy — a protection that had been absent or ambiguous in several national systems. A 2003 study by the European Foundation for the Improvement of Living and Working Conditions found that pregnancy-related discrimination complaints dropped by an estimated 40% in Member States that had previously lacked explicit protections. However, enforcement remained patchy: a 2011 European Commission report noted that only eight Member States had established dedicated enforcement bodies, with workers in others relying on general labour inspectorates that often lacked gender-sensitivity training.

The UK's reluctant compliance produced notable litigation. In *Webb v EMO Air Cargo* (1992), the House of Lords asked the Court of Justice to clarify whether a woman hired as maternity cover who became pregnant herself was protected; the Court ruled that she was, a decision that required the UK to amend its Employment Protection Act. The UK's initial transposition also excluded certain categories of workers, including those on fixed-term contracts, until the Commission threatened infringement proceedings in 1996.

The directive's biggest practical impact was on risk assessment requirements. Employers must now assess all "agents, processes, and working conditions" that could harm pregnant or breastfeeding workers — covering everything from chemical exposure and ionising radiation to standing for long periods and heavy lifting. A 2015 survey of EU businesses found that 72% of employers had modified working conditions for pregnant workers as a result, up from an estimated 35% in the early 1990s. In the healthcare sector, the directive compelled hospitals to reassign pregnant nurses from shifts involving anaesthetic gases, lifting patients, or exposure to infectious diseases — affecting an estimated 200,000 workers annually across the EU.

The directive's 14-week minimum has remained frozen since 1992, despite repeated efforts to raise it. A 2008 Commission proposal to extend maternity leave to 20 weeks at full pay collapsed in 2010 after the Council failed to reach agreement. The European Parliament had voted overwhelmingly for 20 weeks at full pay, but Member States — particularly those with existing generous schemes like Sweden and Denmark — argued that the proposal would force them to fund other countries' underdeveloped systems. The deadlock revealed the limits of the coalition that had passed the original directive; conservative governments in Germany, the UK, and several eastern European states raised cost objections, while feminist and labour groups failed to mobilize the same pressure as in 1992. The attempt was formally withdrawn in 2015.

The directive also created an unanticipated tension with the caselaw of the Court of Justice. In *Hofmann v Barmer Ersatzkasse* (1984), the Court had ruled that maternity leave is about protecting the health of mother and child in a specific period, not about general parenting — a distinction that later complicated the legal treatment of fathers. The directive explicitly leaves paternity leave to Member States, a gap that the 2019 Work-Life Balance Directive only partially filled by requiring a minimum of 10 working days of paternity leave.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the directive only guarantee 14 weeks when most EU countries offer more?

The 14-week minimum was a political compromise from the 1990 proposal for 16 weeks, reduced during Council negotiations to lower costs for employers and win over reluctant Member States. The directive was intended as a floor, not a ceiling, and the vast majority of Member States have since legislated more generous entitlements: by 2023, no Member State offered only 14 weeks, and many offered 20 weeks or more. The Commission's 2008 attempt to raise the minimum to 20 weeks failed because Member States with already generous schemes feared the cost of harmonising upward, and several eastern European governments argued the proposal was too expensive during the post-2008 financial crisis.

### Is the directive about health and safety or gender equality?

Technically, it falls under EU health and safety law, not gender equality law — it was adopted under Article 118a of the Treaty of Rome (health and safety at work) rather than the equal treatment provisions. This legal base was chosen strategically: health and safety measures required only qualified majority voting, while equality measures at the time required unanimity. Functionally, however, the directive serves both purposes. It protects the physical health of pregnant and breastfeeding workers from workplace hazards while also preventing discrimination — pregnancy-based dismissal is banned, and leave is explicitly tied to employment protection. The Court of Justice has consistently held that the directive must be interpreted in light of fundamental equality principles.

### Did the UK have to implement it despite its Maastricht opt-out?

Yes. The UK's opt-out from the Maastricht Social Chapter in 1991 covered only measures adopted under the Social Policy Protocol — not those adopted under the health and safety legal base. Since the directive used Article 118a (health and safety), it applied to the UK despite the opt-out. The UK government under John Major voted against the directive but was outvoted in the Council. This set a pattern: the UK would later challenge several social policy directives it had opted out of by arguing they should have been adopted under health and safety, a tactic the Court of Justice rejected in several cases.

### What happens if a Member State does not pay maternity leave at full salary?

The directive only requires that maternity leave pay be "adequate" — defined as being at least equivalent to the level of statutory sick pay. This has allowed significant variation. Some Member States (e.g. the Netherlands, Luxembourg) pay 100% of salary for extended periods, while others (e.g. Ireland, Greece) have historically paid only a flat-rate benefit significantly below minimum wage. The Court of Justice has held that pay must be "sufficient to ensure the worker's health and safety," but has declined to set a specific percentage. A 2016 Commission report found that workers in five Member States received less than 50% of their previous salary during maternity leave.
