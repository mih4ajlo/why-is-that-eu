## What is it?

The European Accessibility Act (EAA) is an EU directive that sets common accessibility requirements for a core set of digital products and services sold or used within the European Union. It covers items like smartphones, computers, e-readers, ATMs, ticketing machines, banking services, e-commerce websites, and e-books. The goal is to ensure these are perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust for persons with disabilities — for example, screen readers on phones, audible signals on ticket machines, or easy-to-read text on websites.

The directive applies from June 28, 2025. It does not cover all digital products — only those listed in its annexes, such as consumer electronics used for digital services, telecommunications equipment, and audio-visual media services. It also exempts micro-enterprises (fewer than 10 employees and turnover under €2 million) from some obligations but not from the principle of accessibility.

## Why was it introduced?

The European Accessibility Act was born from a fundamental structural tension: by 2015, the EU had 80 million persons with disabilities (according to the Commission's 2015 impact assessment) — roughly 16% of its population — and a rapidly aging demographic where one in four Europeans over 65 faced some form of impairment. Yet the market for accessible digital products was fractured across 28 national laws, voluntary standards, and court rulings. A screen reader that worked in France might fail in Germany; a bank's accessible app built for Sweden could not be sold in Poland without costly re-engineering. Industry complained that compliance costs were 20-30% higher because they had to navigate different national rules, according to the European Disability Forum (EDF) and business groups.

The key trigger was not a single scandal but a cumulative failure: the EU's disability strategy (2010-2020) had set voluntary guidelines, but national transposition was slow and uneven. By 2012, only 14 Member States had adopted some form of digital accessibility rules, and those diverged wildly — from the UK's rigorous Equality Act to Greece's minimal provisions. In 2015, the European Parliament's Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO) commissioned a study showing that the fragmented market cost businesses €110 billion annually in lost opportunities, per a 2011 Copenhagen Economics report cited by the Commission.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. The push came from an alliance of disability rights organisations (the European Disability Forum, the European Blind Union), progressive MEPs, and — critically — the technology industry itself. Tech giants like Apple, Google, and Microsoft lobbied in favour of harmonisation, seeing a single EU-wide rulebook as cheaper than 28 national ones. Apple's Director of Accessibility, Sarah Herrlinger, publicly stated in 2018 that accessibility was "a core value" and that uniform rules would accelerate innovation. The counter-coalition came mostly from SMEs, who feared costs, and from some Member States (notably Germany's Bundesrat and Polish officials) who argued that the directive encroached on national social policy competence.

The timing mattered. The Juncker Commission (2014-2019) had made "Digital Single Market" a flagship priority, and EU Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society, Günther Oettinger, appointed in 2014, saw accessibility as part of that puzzle. The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD), ratified by the EU in 2010, created a legal obligation that Member States had failed to meet. But the real break came in 2017, when the European Court of Justice ruled in *Case C-329/16* (the "Syndicat des avocats de France" case) that EU law could harmonise accessibility without violating national competences, removing a key legal barrier.

What changed was a practical failure: the 2016 Commission proposal for a "European Accessibility Act" had stalled in the Council for two years due to resistance from France and Germany over scope. Then, in November 2017, the European Parliament's lead rapporteur, the Croatian MEP Biljana Borzan (S&D), crafted a compromise with the Council that narrowed the scope to only digital products and services — dropping initial plans to cover physical environments like buildings — which broke the deadlock. The final vote in the European Parliament on March 13, 2019 was overwhelmingly in favour: 547 votes for, 47 against, with 58 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **2009** — The EU signs the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, committing to accessibility.
- **2010** — The European Disability Strategy 2010-2020 is launched, but voluntary measures fail to harmonise national laws.
- **2015** — The European Commission publishes an impact assessment estimating €110 billion in market fragmentation costs.
- **2016** — Commissioner Günther Oettinger proposes the European Accessibility Act as part of the Digital Single Market strategy.
- **2017** — The ECJ ruling in *C-329/16* confirms EU competence. The European Parliament's IMCO committee adopts a compromise narrowing scope.
- **2018** — The Council reaches a common position in July. Final trilogue negotiations conclude in November.
- **2019** — The European Parliament votes in favour (547-47) on March 13. The directive publishes in the Official Journal on June 28.
- **2021** — The European Commission adopts an implementing decision (2021/1348) setting technical standards.
- **2022** — The first transposition deadline for Member States passes (June 28). Only 14 Member States meet the deadline, according to the Commission's 2023 report.
- **2025** — The directive applies on June 28 to all covered products and services placed on the market.

## Impact and consequences

The EAA affects an estimated 80 million persons with disabilities in the EU, plus another 25 million people aged 65+ who face age-related impairments. The European Commission's 2015 impact assessment projected that the directive would generate net benefits of €35-60 billion over 10 years through reduced compliance costs and increased market access. However, transposition has been slow: by June 2023, only 14 of 27 Member States had fully transposed the directive into national law, with the European Commission opening infringement proceedings against six countries (including Germany and Poland) in May 2024.

Industry response was mixed. Large tech firms like Apple and Microsoft had already built accessible products for markets like the US (under the Americans with Disabilities Act) and welcomed the harmonisation. But in 2023, the German digital association Bitkom reported that 68% of small tech companies surveyed had not yet prepared for the 2025 deadline, citing cost uncertainty. In 2024, a new risk emerged: the European Commission's 2023 report noted that some Member States (including France and Italy) were "gold-plating" the directive by adding extra requirements beyond the EU minimum, threatening the harmonisation goal.

The first enforcement actions are expected in 2025-2026. Market surveillance authorities in Member States can impose fines — for example, Belgium's new law, enacted in 2023, allows penalties up to €100,000 for non-compliance. The European Disability Forum has warned that without strong enforcement, the EAA risks becoming "a paper tiger."

## Key questions answered

### Which digital products must be accessible in the EU?

The law covers consumer computers, smartphones, tablets, e-readers, game consoles, smart TVs, and public-use devices like ATMs, ticket machines, and self-service check-in kiosks. It also includes banking services, e-commerce websites, e-books, and telecom services (voice calls, SMS). But it does not cover everything: for example, private websites, elevator buttons, or physical building spaces are excluded.

### Can an EU country set stricter accessibility rules than the EU law?

Yes — but only if the product is fully produced and sold within that country. For cross-border trade, the EAA sets a maximum harmonisation ceiling: Member States cannot impose extra requirements on products legally sold across the EU. This is to prevent the old fragmentation. A few countries (like France and Italy) have tried, risking Commission infringement action.

### When do companies have to comply with the European Accessibility Act?

The directive applies from June 28, 2025, for new products and services. There is a longer transition for services that were already in use before that date — they must comply by June 28, 2030. Micro-enterprises (fewer than 10 employees, turnover under €2 million) are exempt from some documentation obligations but still must make their products and services accessible.

### Is there a risk of non-compliance being ignored?

Yes — enforcement is up to each Member State, and the Commission's 2023 report found that only 14 of 27 countries had fully transposed the law by then. The European Commission has opened infringement cases against six countries, but the real test will come in 2025-2026 when market surveillance authorities begin inspections. The European Disability Forum has warned that penalties vary widely — from €5,000 in some countries to €100,000 in Belgium — which could create a patchwork of enforcement.
