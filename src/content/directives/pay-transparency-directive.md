## What is it?

The Pay Transparency Directive, formally Directive 2023/970/EU, strengthens the principle of equal pay for equal work and work of equal value between men and women. It introduces binding transparency measures: companies with 100+ employees must report gender pay gaps, workers have the right to request individual and anonymised pay data from their employer, and pay secrecy clauses are banned. The directive shifts the burden of proof in pay discrimination cases from the worker to the employer once a *prima facie* case is established.

The directive entered into force on 6 June 2023. EU member states have until 7 June 2026 to transpose it into national law. Companies with 150+ employees must begin reporting by June 2027; those with 100–149 employees by June 2031.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the European Court of Justice’s 2019 ruling in *Schuch-Gennadiev* (Case C-664/17), which found Austria’s mandatory pay transparency law did not violate EU law, but also highlighted that member states had systematically failed to enforce existing equal pay legislation. The gap between legal principle and reality had become untenable.

The structural problem was decades old. The 1957 Treaty of Rome established the principle of equal pay for equal work, yet the gender pay gap in the EU stubbornly remained around 13-16% throughout the 2010s, according to Eurostat. The 2006 Recast Equal Treatment Directive (2006/54/EC) had proven toothless: the European Commission’s 2020 evaluation found fewer than 5% of equal pay complaints across the EU resulted in a favourable ruling for the worker, largely because employees could not access the pay data needed to prove discrimination. Victims bore the entire burden of proof.

The coalition that pushed the directive through was unusual. The European Trade Union Confederation and the European Women’s Lobby had campaigned for years. But the decisive shift came from corporate actors. In 2019, Iceland’s equal pay certification law—requiring companies to prove they pay equally, not just react to complaints—drew attention. In 2020, large firms like Salesforce and Unilever publicly supported transparency measures, arguing that pay secrecy hurt talent retention and that gender-pay reporting was already standard in their internal HR processes. The Business & Sustainable Development Commission estimated closing the gender pay gap could add €1.5 trillion to EU GDP by 2030.

Timing was critical. The directive was proposed by the von der Leyen Commission in March 2021, following the COVID-19 pandemic that had disproportionately pushed women out of the workforce. Commission President Ursula von der Leyen made “a Union of Equality” a flagship priority; Vice-President for Values and Transparency Věra Jourová led the file. The European Parliament’s Women’s Rights Committee (FEMM) pushed for stronger provisions than the Commission had proposed, particularly on extending reporting obligations to companies with fewer than 250 employees and expanding the definition of “work of equal value.”

Opposition came primarily from employer organisations like BusinessEurope, which argued the administrative burden would be excessive. The German employers’ association BDA warned it would “destroy trust-based pay systems.” Some member states—particularly Hungary, Poland, and the Czech Republic—resisted during Council negotiations. Hungary’s government argued the directive infringed on national labour market policies. But a qualified majority held: 22 member states voted in favour in the Council, with five abstaining (Hungary, Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Lithuania). No state voted against.

The Parliament adopted the final text on 30 March 2023 with 427 votes in favour, 79 against, and 76 abstentions. The Council formally approved it on 24 April 2023.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s most immediate consequence was the outright ban on pay secrecy clauses, affecting tens of thousands of employment contracts across the EU. A 2022 Eurobarometer survey had found that 65% of workers in the EU did not know what colleagues in comparable roles earned.

The reporting obligation will cover approximately 60,000 companies with 250+ employees by 2027, and an additional 40,000 companies with 100–149 employees by 2031, according to the Commission’s impact assessment. Companies with a gender pay gap exceeding 5% that cannot be justified on objective, gender-neutral grounds must conduct a joint pay assessment with workers’ representatives.

Industry response has been mixed. Multinationals with existing diversity, equity, and inclusion programs—like Microsoft, SAP, and Deutsche Telekom—publicly supported the directive, noting they already conducted internal pay audits. Smaller companies, particularly in Eastern Europe, face significant compliance costs. The Commission estimated one-off implementation costs of €300–€500 per company, but employer groups called this “significantly underestimated” in consultations.

Early enforcement actions are already visible. In January 2024, Portugal became the first member state to transpose the directive into national law, going beyond the minimum by requiring any company with 50+ employees to file gender-segregated pay data annually. Other states—including Belgium, the Netherlands, and Sweden—began legislative processes in 2024, but at least six member states (including Bulgaria, Romania, and Hungary) had not yet begun formal transposition by mid-2024, according to the European Equality Law Network.

The directive also created a new right to class-action-style pay discrimination claims: workers’ representatives, NGOs, and equality bodies can now initiate proceedings on behalf of multiple employees.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU act now when equal pay has been a principle since 1957?

The ECJ’s 2019 ruling exposed the systemic failure of existing enforcement mechanisms. Combined with the pandemic’s gendered economic impact and the 2020 #MeToo movement’s focus on workplace equity, political will converged. The von der Leyen Commission’s gender equality agenda provided the necessary institutional push.

### Why is pay secrecy banned in the directive?

Pay secrecy clauses were the single biggest obstacle to detecting pay discrimination, as workers could not gather the evidence needed to file a claim. The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment found that in member states with pay secrecy laws (such as Germany, where pay secrecy was legal), the gender pay gap was on average 4 percentage points higher than in states without such restrictions.

### Why does the directive shift the burden of proof to employers?

Under the 2006 Recast Directive, the worker had to prove discrimination *beyond* showing a pay difference—a nearly impossible task without access to colleagues’ pay data. The new directive requires the employer to provide objective, non-gender-based justifications for pay differences once a *prima facie* case is established. This mirrors the approach taken in race and disability discrimination directives.

### Which countries opposed it and why?

Hungary, Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Lithuania abstained in the Council. Hungary’s government argued it infringed on national sovereignty over labour markets; employer organisations in Poland and the Czech Republic warned of administrative burdens. No country voted against—the qualified majority was sufficiently broad to pass without their support.
