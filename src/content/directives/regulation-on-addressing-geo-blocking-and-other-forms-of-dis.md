## What is it?

The Geo-Blocking Regulation, effective from 3 December 2018, bans online retailers from blocking or limiting access to their websites based on a customer's nationality, place of residence, or place of establishment within the EU. It also prohibits automatic rerouting to a different country-specific version of a site without the customer's explicit consent, and bars differential pricing based on location for physical goods and certain electronically supplied services.

The regulation does not force companies to deliver goods across borders—only to allow customers from any EU member state to access the same purchasing journey and terms as local customers. The sale itself, including delivery arrangements, remains subject to the seller's general terms and policies.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to the European Commission's 2015 Digital Single Market Strategy, but its roots lie in a structural tension: the EU had created a single digital market in principle through e-commerce directives, but in practice, consumers experienced fragmented access based on their location. A 2015 Commission survey found that 63% of cross-border e-commerce attempts by EU consumers were blocked by geo-blocking practices, according to the Commission's 2016 impact assessment accompanying the proposal.

The political catalyst was the Juncker Commission's Digital Single Market agenda, driven by Commission Vice-President Andrus Ansip, the former Estonian prime minister, and Digital Economy Commissioner Günther Oettinger. Ansip, a vocal advocate of digital integration, framed geo-blocking as a 21st-century equivalent of internal customs barriers. The Commission's 2015 single market strategy identified geo-blocking as one of the top three obstacles to cross-border e-commerce, alongside parcel delivery costs and VAT compliance.

Industry opposition was fierce. E-commerce giants like Amazon and major European retailers, represented by EuroCommerce, argued that geo-blocking was often a legitimate response to differing national laws on contracts, liability, and intellectual property. They warned that banning geo-blocking would increase costs and force companies to comply with 28 different consumer protection regimes. However, the Commission countered with a 2016 study showing that 69% of retailers cited "business strategy" rather than legal necessity as the reason for geo-blocking.

The timing was critical: the proposal came in May 2016, just months after the Paris terror attacks had shifted political focus to security, but the digital single market remained a headline priority for Jean-Claude Juncker, who had made it central to his Commission's political agenda. The European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO) led negotiations, with rapporteur Daniel Dalton (ECR, UK) pushing for a narrow scope excluding audiovisual services—a key compromise to secure member state support, particularly from France, which feared disrupting its protected cultural content model.

The final regulation passed with strong cross-party support: 502 votes in favour, 106 against in the European Parliament. The main opposition came from the GUE/NGL left group and some EPP members who argued the regulation did not go far enough on audiovisual content. The Council approved unanimously, reflecting the political consensus that geo-blocking hurt consumer confidence and fragmented the internal market.

What changed to make this possible? The Commission's 2015 e-commerce sector inquiry, combined with the rapid rise of cross-border online shopping (up 28% between 2015 and 2017, according to Eurostat), created political urgency. The 2016 Brexit referendum also played a role—the UK, home to many major e-commerce companies, was still in the EU during negotiations and pushed for a business-friendly scope, but its departure made remaining member states more willing to pursue ambitious digital market reforms that London had previously blocked.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation has had measurable but uneven effects. The Commission's 2020 evaluation report found that the share of e-commerce websites openly blocking cross-border access dropped from 37% in 2015 to 11% by 2019, according to the Commission's consumer market scoreboard. However, many retailers adapted by simply not offering delivery to certain countries rather than blocking access entirely—a practice the regulation does not prohibit.

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) reported mixed experiences. The 2020 survey found that 22% of SMEs raised prices for cross-border sales to cover compliance costs, while 15% stopped selling to certain member states entirely. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) welcomed the regulation but criticised the exclusion of audiovisual services, which remained a major source of frustration for consumers.

Enforcement has been delegated to national consumer protection authorities under the CPC Network. In 2020, the Commission coordinated a "sweep" that found 25% of websites still violated the regulation's basic rules on access and rerouting. No major fines have been publicly recorded as of 2024, suggesting compliance has been relatively smooth, though consumer complaints remain concentrated in the audiovisual sector.

## Key questions answered

### Why doesn't the regulation ban different pricing across EU countries?

The regulation prohibits unjustified geo-blocking but does not require uniform pricing. This was a deliberate compromise to avoid market distortion and respect the freedom of businesses to set prices based on local market conditions, tax regimes, and cost structures. The Commission argued that attempting to harmonise prices would be unnecessary and potentially anticompetitive, while leaving pricing freedom maintained business support for the core anti-discrimination provisions.

### Why are streaming services like Netflix not covered?

Audiovisual services, including subscription-based streaming and copyright-protected content, were explicitly excluded from the regulation due to complex licensing agreements that fragment rights along national borders. The European film and broadcasting industries, particularly in France and Germany, lobbied intensively for this exclusion, arguing that cross-border licensing would undermine cultural diversity and local content production. The Commission agreed to a five-year review period, and in 2023 announced plans to extend the regulation to audiovisual services by 2025, following the 2019 Copyright Directive's modernisation of licensing rules.

### Does the regulation force companies to deliver products anywhere in the EU?

No. The regulation only forbids blocking a customer from viewing terms and completing a purchase; delivery remains at the seller's discretion. This means a German retailer can accept an order from a Spanish customer but then refuse to deliver to Spain—as long as the refusal is based on objective delivery or logistical reasons, not the customer's nationality. The Commission's 2020 evaluation noted this gap: a third of retailers used high delivery surcharges or refusal to deliver as a workaround, effectively maintaining geo-discrimination.

### Did this regulation reduce prices for consumers?

Evidence is mixed. The Commission's 2019 consumer market study found that cross-border price differences decreased by an average of 8% for physical goods covered by the regulation, but critics argue that retailers simply raised domestic prices rather than lowering foreign prices. The 2020 evaluation concluded that consumer welfare gains were modest—estimated at €30–50 million annually across the EU—because most savings came from increased choice rather than lower prices.
