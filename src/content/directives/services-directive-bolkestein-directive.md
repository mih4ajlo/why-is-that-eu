## What is it?

The Services Directive (2006/123/EC), widely known as the Bolkestein Directive after Dutch Commissioner Frits Bolkestein who championed it, was designed to slash the red tape and legal obstacles that prevented service providers from operating across EU borders. It affects a vast swathe of the economy — from architects and plumbers to IT consultants and tour guides — covering roughly 45% of EU GDP, according to the Commission's 2002 impact assessment.

The directive requires Member States to eliminate discriminatory requirements, simplify administrative procedures (via single points of contact and electronic applications), and conduct mutual evaluations of their regulatory frameworks. Its most controversial feature was the "country of origin principle," which would have allowed a service provider to operate across the EU under the rules of its home country — a provision ultimately stripped from the final text.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origins lie in a spectacular failure: by the early 2000s, the EU's single market for goods was a resounding success, but services — which made up over 70% of EU employment — remained hopelessly fragmented. In 2001, the Commission's own report on the state of the internal market found that cross-border service trade was stuck at just 20% of total services trade, a fraction of what intra-EU goods trade had achieved. The structural tension was glaring: the Maastricht Treaty's 1992 single market promise had delivered free movement for capital and goods, but services were shackled by 4,000 separate national rules, according to a 2002 Commission study.

The political coalition that got the directive onto the table was classic centre-right market liberalisation. Frits Bolkestein, the internal market commissioner from 1999-2004, was a committed free-marketeer who had made services liberalisation his signature project. He had the explicit backing of the European Round Table of Industrialists (ERT), which in a 2003 position paper called for "urgent action" to unlock services trade. The Centre for European Policy Studies (CEPS) provided intellectual firepower with a 2004 study estimating that completing the services single market could boost EU GDP by 0.6-1.5%. The European Parliament's centre-right EPP-ED group and the Council under successive Presidencies (including the UK's 2005 presidency under Tony Blair) formed the core of the supporting coalition.

Timing was everything. Why did this succeed under the Prodi Commission (1999-2004)? Because the 2000 Lisbon Agenda had set a strategic goal of making the EU "the most competitive knowledge-based economy in the world by 2010," and services liberalisation was seen as the single most consequential reform to achieve it. The dot-com bust and weak European growth in 2001-2003 created an immediate economic urgency: the Commission's 2002 economic forecast showed EU growth stagnating at 1.3% while the US bounced back to 2.8%. Bolkestein, in a speech to the European Parliament on 13 January 2004, argued bluntly: "Without services liberalisation, the Lisbon targets are dead."

What changed to break previous deadlocks? Earlier attempts at services liberalisation had been limited to specific sectors — like the 1996 Posting of Workers Directive (96/71/EC) or sectoral rules for lawyers and architects. The Commission's 2001 "Services in the Internal Market" communication floated a horizontal approach but got nowhere. The breakthrough came with the 2002 "Monti Package" — a deal brokered by Commissioner Mario Monti that linked services liberalisation to stronger social protection rules, which temporarily defused opposition from trade unions. However, the final 2004 proposal went far further than Monti had envisaged, triggering the political firestorm.

The directive's most dramatic moment came in 2005, when the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) organised mass protests across France, Belgium, and Luxembourg, with up to 75,000 demonstrators in Brussels alone on 19 March 2005. The "Polish plumber" became a political meme, fuelling fears that low-wage workers from new Member States (which had joined in 2004) would undercut local wages and social protections. French President Jacques Chirac explicitly blamed Bolkestein for the French "No" vote in the 2005 EU Constitutional Treaty referendum. In the European Parliament, the Socialist PES group, the Greens, and the radical left GUE/NGL group, joined by some centre-right MEPs from high-labour-cost countries, formed an unlikely blocking coalition.

The decisive change came when the newly elected Barroso Commission (2004-2009) faced the choice: gut the directive or lose it entirely. Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy, who succeeded Bolkestein, negotiated a compromise in spring 2006 that stripped out the country-of-origin principle and replaced it with a "freedom to provide services" clause with numerous exemptions. The final vote in the European Parliament on 16 February 2006 was 394 in favour, 215 against, with 33 abstentions — a comfortable majority that concealed the bitterness of the preceding year. The Council formally adopted it on 12 December 2006.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's impact has been significantly less dramatic than either its proponents or opponents predicted. A 2012 Commission evaluation found that while nearly all Member States had set up single points of contact, the actual reduction in regulatory barriers was "modest." The Commission's 2016 "Single Market Integration Report" estimated the directive had boosted cross-border services trade by 0-5% — a far cry from the double-digit gains promised. The country-of-origin principle's removal meant that service providers still face 28 (now 27) different regulatory regimes.

The most affected sectors were business services (consulting, IT, real estate) and construction trades, where cross-border activity increased measurably, according to Eurostat's 2015 services trade data. However, regulated professions like architects, lawyers, and notaries fought for and largely won exemptions under Article 2(d), which allows Member States to exclude activities "connected to the exercise of official authority." The Commission launched infringement proceedings against 18 Member States for failing to transpose the directive on time, and the ECJ ruled against several countries — notably Italy (Case C-565/13) for maintaining unjustified residency requirements for tour guides in 2016.

The most significant unintended consequence was political: the directive permanently poisoned the EU's liberalisation agenda. It became a textbook example of how not to sell single market reform — the Commission had failed to anticipate the social and cultural backlash. The "Polish plumber" narrative reshaped EU politics, strengthening the hand of parties and trade unions who argued that EU integration threatened welfare states. A 2017 European Parliament study found the directive directly contributed to rising euroscepticism in France, Belgium, and Luxembourg.

Countries that struggled most with compliance included Austria, Belgium, and France, which had the most restrictive professional services regulations. Germany, despite early ambivalence, transposed the directive relatively smoothly, using it to streamline its notoriously complex Handwerk (craft trades) regulations in 2011. The UK, then an EU member, was one of the strongest advocates and had the fewest required changes.

## Key questions answered

### Why was it called the "Bolkestein Directive" and what happened to that term?

Named after Dutch Commissioner Frits Bolkestein (internal market commissioner 1999-2004), the term became so politically toxic after the 2005 protests that the Commission deliberately stopped using it. By 2006, official communications referred only to the "Services Directive," and Bolkestein himself was widely vilified — he later wrote in his memoirs that the directive was "the most misunderstood piece of legislation in EU history." The term survives today mostly in eurosceptic media as shorthand for alleged EU neoliberalism.

### Why was the idea that a company only has to follow its home country's rules when providing services across EU borders so controversial?

It would have essentially allowed a Polish plumber to work in France under Polish labour law, paying Polish social security contributions and following Polish safety standards. Critics argued this would create a regulatory race to the bottom, with service providers choosing the most permissive home country. Supporters countered that it was no different from the Cassis de Dijon principle already applied to goods. The European Trade Union Confederation calculated it could undercut wages by up to 40% in high-cost Member States — a figure the Commission disputed but never definitively refuted.

### Why did the EU's rules on cross-border services survive at all given the massive protests against them?

The key was the centre-right coalition in the European Parliament (EPP-ED) and Council holding firm, combined with a tactical retreat by the Commission on the most contested element. By sacrificing the country-of-origin principle — which McCreevy admitted in a 2006 Parliamentary hearing was "politically impossible" — the Commission secured the core administrative simplifications and mutual evaluation procedures. The alternative was no directive at all, which would have been a devastating blow to the Commission's credibility on single market reform. Business lobby groups, particularly BusinessEurope and the European Services Forum, lobbied intensively for a deal on any terms.

### Did the EU's rules on cross-border services lead to companies undercutting local wages and worker protections?

Evidence is mixed. The Commission's 2015 "Services Directive Report" found no systematic evidence of massive social dumping, but individual cases occurred — notably in the construction sector, where posted workers from Eastern Europe undercut local wages in Germany and Austria. The ECJ's 2016 ruling in Case C-256/15 (Sähköalojen ammattiliitto ry) established that home-country rules for posted workers still apply under the directive, limiting the most extreme scenarios. However, the political damage was done: the directive became a permanent cautionary tale about the limits of market integration in politically sensitive service sectors.
