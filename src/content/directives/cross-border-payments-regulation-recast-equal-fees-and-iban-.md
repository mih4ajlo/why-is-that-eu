## What is it?

The Cross-Border Payments Regulation (2021/1230/EU) eliminated the price gap between domestic and cross-border euro transactions within the European Economic Area. Before it, a Portuguese consumer sending €50 to a German supplier might pay €5 in fees, while a German sending €50 within Germany paid nothing. The regulation forced payment service providers to charge identical fees for all euro transfers, direct debits, and card payments regardless of whether they crossed a border.

It also tackled "IBAN discrimination" — the practice where businesses refused to accept bank account numbers from other EU countries, defeating the purpose of a single payments market. A French utility company could no longer refuse payment from a client with a Belgian IBAN, for example. The European Commission's 2019 impact assessment found that cross-border euro payment fees remained 3-5 times higher than domestic ones in non-euro-area countries, despite a 2009 regulation that had capped them for credit transfers only.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a dramatic scandal but a slow-burning contradiction in the single market. By 2018, the original 2009 Cross-Border Payments Regulation (924/2009/EC) had equalised fees for euro credit transfers but left an enormous gap: direct debits and, crucially, card payments were exempt. The result was absurd — a Lithuanian tourist paying by card in a Spanish shop faced higher interchange fees than a Spanish resident using the same card, even though both were paying in euros. The single market had a payments border inside its currency union.

The political breakthrough came from an unexpected coalition. On one side stood the European Commission's Directorate-General for Financial Stability (DG FISMA), led by Valdis Dombrovskis, who saw the fee disparity as a fundamental market failure. On the other side were Nordic and Eastern European consumer protection agencies, notably from Sweden, Denmark, and Poland, whose citizens complained loudly about paying extra to use their cards abroad. A 2018 European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) survey found that 71% of consumers in non-euro-area member states found cross-border euro payments too expensive. The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, under rapporteur Eva Kaili (S&D, Greece), pushed for the strongest possible extension.

Opposition came from the payment card industry, particularly Visa and Mastercard, whose interchange fee structures relied on national differentiation. The banking sector, through the European Banking Federation, warned that forced equality would squeeze profit margins. But the opposition collapsed when the Commission's 2019 impact assessment quantified the cost: cross-border card payments in non-euro countries were 2.4 times more expensive than domestic ones, and the total consumer burden reached an estimated €1.1 billion annually. The numbers made the status quo untenable.

Timing sealed the deal. The regulation was proposed in March 2018, just as the Payment Services Directive 2 (PSD2) was entering application in most member states, having opened the market to new fintech players. The European Banking Authority (EBA) was also preparing its first IBAN discrimination guidelines in 2019. The incoming von der Leyen Commission, taking office in December 2019, had made digital finance a priority. The IBAN non-discrimination clause, originally a secondary concern, became central after the EBA's 2019 opinion found that 20% of businesses surveyed still refused foreign IBANs, despite the 2014 SEPA Regulation having required technical interoperability.

The regulation passed through Parliament in February 2021 with 641 votes in favour, 15 against, and 22 abstentions. The formal recast entered into force on December 15, 2021, and applied from April 19, 2022.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Original Cross-Border Payments Regulation (924/2009/EC) equalises fees for euro credit transfers only, exempting direct debits and card payments.
- **2014** — SEPA Regulation (260/2012/EU) makes IBAN and BIC mandatory for euro payments, enabling technical cross-border interoperability.
- **March 2018** — European Commission proposes recast regulation extending equal-fee rule to all transaction types and banning IBAN discrimination.
- **December 2019** — European Banking Authority issues opinion finding that 20% of businesses refuse foreign IBANs.
- **February 2021** — European Parliament adopts regulation with 641 votes in favour, 15 against, 22 abstentions.
- **April 19, 2022** — Regulation applies in all EU/EEA member states; fee equality takes effect.
- **2023** — European Commission's report on first year of application notes that most member states reported no systemic IBAN discrimination complaints.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate effect was on consumers in non-euro-area EU countries. A Danish consumer paying €100 by card in a German shop no longer paid higher fees than if the transaction were domestic. The European Commission's 2023 review found that cross-border euro card fees in non-euro countries fell by an average of 60-80% in the first year. The Polish Consumer Protection Office (UOKiK) reported that complaints about cross-border payment fees dropped by 90% in 2022 compared to 2020.

Industry adaptation was uneven. Large banks with pan-European operations absorbed the revenue loss easily; smaller, national-focused banks struggled. The European Banking Authority noted in its 2022 annual report that 14 member states had to issue corrective action notices to payment service providers that initially continued charging differential fees. Compliance costs for updating payment systems averaged €1.5-4 million per major bank, according to a 2021 survey by the European Payments Council.

The IBAN discrimination ban had more mixed results. While major e-commerce platforms like Amazon and Booking.com quickly adjusted, smaller businesses often remained unaware of the requirement. A 2023 European Commission Eurobarometer survey found that only 37% of EU businesses knew it was illegal to refuse a foreign IBAN. The European Consumer Centres Network recorded 412 formal complaints about IBAN discrimination in 2023, up from 89 in 2021 — suggesting that consumer awareness, not compliance, was now the binding constraint.

Enforcement has been delegated to national competent authorities under the Payment Services Directive framework. The first fines came from the French Prudential Supervisory Authority (ACPR) in late 2022, fining Crédit Mutuel €30,000 for requiring customers to open a French account to receive salary payments. The amounts were symbolic, but the signal was clear: IBAN discrimination was now a regulatory risk.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europeans have to pay more to send money across borders in their own currency?

Until 2022, EU law only required banks to charge the same fees for credit transfers between countries as within the same country, but left direct debits and card payments exempt. This meant a German using her card in Spain paid higher interchange fees than a Spanish resident. The 2021 regulation closed this loophole, forcing identical fees for all euro payment types across the entire EU/EEA.

### Can a company refuse my payment because my bank account is from a different EU country?

No. Since April 2022, EU law makes it illegal for any business accepting electronic payments to refuse or charge extra based on the IBAN's country of origin. If a French utility or a German online shop rejects your Belgian IBAN, you can file a complaint with your national consumer protection authority. The European Banking Authority has confirmed this applies to all payment types, not just SEPA transfers.

### How did the European Commission enforce these rules?

Enforcement is handled by national authorities — usually central banks or financial supervisors — under the Payment Services Directive framework. The Commission reviews compliance every three years and can launch infringement proceedings against member states that fail to enforce the rules. In the first year, 14 member states had to issue corrective orders to payment providers. First fines were small but significant, like the €30,000 penalty against Crédit Mutuel in France for IBAN discrimination.

### Did the regulation affect all EU countries equally?

Not exactly. Euro-area countries (€19) already had near-zero cross-border fees for most transactions before the regulation, so the main change was the IBAN discrimination ban. The biggest impact was in non-euro countries like Denmark, Sweden, Poland, Czechia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, and Croatia, where cross-border euro card fees had been 2-5 times higher than domestic ones. For these countries, the regulation eliminated a hidden consumer tax on using a European currency abroad.
