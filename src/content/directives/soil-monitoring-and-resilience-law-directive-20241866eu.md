## What is it?

The Soil Monitoring and Resilience Law (Directive 2024/1866/EU) is the EU’s first comprehensive legal framework dedicated specifically to soil health. It requires all 27 member states to establish national soil monitoring systems to assess the condition of all soil types—agricultural, forest, industrial, and urban—using a common set of indicators (e.g., organic matter content, erosion rates, contamination levels). By 2030, member states must adopt national targets for improving soil resilience and submit action plans to the European Commission.

The law shifts from voluntary guidelines, which existed for over a decade with little effect, to binding obligations. It mandates member states to identify contaminated sites, register them in public databases, and remediate those posing significant health or environmental risks. The directive also introduces a principle of “no net land take” by 2050, aiming to halt soil sealing (e.g., covering soil with concrete) in urban expansion.

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst was a 2020 European Court of Auditors (ECA) report titled “Soil protection: A growing concern for EU policymakers,” which found that 70% of EU soils were estimated to be in an unhealthy state—degraded by erosion, compaction, salinization, and contamination—and that existing EU law was “fragmented and ineffective.” The report cited Commission data showing annual soil degradation costs the EU economy at least €50 billion in lost agricultural productivity, water purification services, and carbon storage.

A deeper structural tension lay in the failure of the voluntary approach under the EU’s 2006 “Thematic Strategy for Soil Protection.” That strategy proposed a directive in 2006, but it was blocked for eight years in the Council of the EU by a blocking minority led by Germany, the UK, Austria, and the Netherlands—farming-heavy states that feared mandatory soil rules would add costs for landowners and reduce agricultural competitiveness. The proposal was withdrawn in 2014 by Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker’s team, who prioritised deregulation and “better regulation” agendas.

The timing changed with the European Green Deal, launched by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019. The deal’s “Zero Pollution Action Plan” (2021) and “Biodiversity Strategy for 2030” both identified soil health as critical for carbon sequestration and climate neutrality. Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius (Environment, Oceans and Fisheries), a Lithuanian Green, made soil monitoring a personal priority, building a coalition of environment ministers from Denmark, Luxembourg, and France.

What broke the deadlock was the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, which exposed the EU’s dependence on imported fertilisers and the vulnerability of soils to conflict-related pollution. The invasion triggered a food price crisis that compelled even agricultural ministers to accept that soil degrading was a strategic risk: the Commission’s 2022 impact assessment noted that degraded soils reduce crop yields by 10-20% in parts of southern Europe. The Council’s blocking minority collapsed; Germany dropped its opposition under pressure from its own Green coalition partner.

The European Parliament passed the proposal on April 27, 2024, with 320 votes in favour, 210 against, and 70 abstentions. The main opposition came from the European People’s Party (EPP) and the far-right Identity and Democracy (ID) group, who argued the “no net land take” target would cripple housing construction and infrastructure projects. The EPP’s amendment to exempt agricultural soils from mandatory monitoring was rejected by just 15 votes—a key moment in the final trilogue.

## Timeline

- **2002** — The Commission’s “Towards a Thematic Strategy for Soil Protection” communication acknowledged soil degradation as a major EU-wide issue
- **2006** — Commission proposes the Soil Framework Directive; blocked by Germany, UK, Netherlands, Austria in Council
- **2014** — Commission President Juncker withdraws the stalled directive, stating “no added value”
- **2020** — European Court of Auditors report finds EU soil protection “fragmented and ineffective”; ECA Special Report No 33/2020
- **2021** — Commission’s “Zero Pollution Action Plan” sets 2030 target for reducing soil pollution
- **2022** — Russian invasion of Ukraine shifts food security calculus; Commission announces legislative proposal in “Soil Health and Food Security” package
- **November 28, 2023** — Commission publishes official proposal for Soil Monitoring Law
- **April 27, 2024** — European Parliament adopts its position with 320 votes in favour
- **June 11, 2024** — Council adopts the directive by qualified majority (no member state voted against, but Germany and Poland abstained)
- **July 17, 2024** — Publication of Directive 2024/1866/EU in the Official Journal
- **August 7, 2024** — Entry into force (20 days after publication)
- **August 7, 2027** — Deadline for member states to transpose into national law

## Impact and consequences

The directive directly affects all land managers in the EU: approximately 10 million farmers, forestry owners, urban planners, and industrial site operators. The Commission’s 2023 impact assessment estimated compliance costs at €1.2 billion over 10 years for monitoring programs, but claimed this would be offset by €8 billion in annual avoided degradation costs.

Industry response was mixed. CropLife Europe, the pesticide association, warned that mandatory monitoring of pesticide residues in soils would “create unrealistic expectations” and potentially trigger liability claims. The European Land Registry Association praised the “no net land take” target but noted that property values could decline in areas with publicly listed contaminated sites.

Several member states face major challenges. Poland has no national soil monitoring system; its environment ministry said in July 2024 that building one from scratch would take at least five years—missing the 2027 transposition deadline. Italy, with an estimated 2,300 contaminated “brownfield” sites from its industrial north, must create the largest public database in the EU.

## Key questions answered

### What is the EU doing about degraded farmland in Europe?

The directive requires each member state to monitor and report on soil health indicators—like organic matter levels and erosion—across all agricultural lands. By 2030, countries must set national targets to improve soil degradation and submit plans to the European Commission. It’s a binding framework after 18 years of voluntary guidelines that failed to halt degradation affecting an estimated 70% of EU soils, according to the European Court of Auditors.

### Will new rules stop cities from building on undeveloped green land?

The directive introduces a principle of “no net land take” by 2050, meaning urban expansion on undeveloped soil must be compensated by restoring an equivalent area elsewhere (e.g., demolishing old industrial sites). It does not ban construction, but requires local authorities to prioritise brownfield development. The provision was the most contested part of the law, with the European People’s Party nearly blocking it in Parliament.

### Who has to pay for cleaning up polluted old factory sites?

The law makes the polluter liable for remediation of sites listed in mandatory national databases. If the polluter cannot be identified or has gone bankrupt, member states must fund cleanup from public budgets—potentially billions of euros in countries like Germany (estimated 200,000 suspected contaminated sites) and Belgium (13,000 sites from steel and coal history).

### Why did it take so long for Europe to get rules protecting soil health?

A 2006 Commission proposal was blocked in the Council for eight years by a coalition of northern European farming states—Germany, the UK, Netherlands, Austria—who feared higher costs for landowners. The legislation was withdrawn in 2014. It took the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, which exposed food system vulnerabilities, and the political weight of the European Green Deal under Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius, to break the deadlock and secure a qualified majority in 2024.
