## What is it?

The Industrial Emissions Directive recast (IED 2.0) is the EU's updated framework regulating pollution from roughly 75,000 industrial installations, including power plants, refineries, chemical factories, waste incinerators, and — for the first time — large pig and poultry farms. It sets binding emission limit values based on "best available techniques" (BAT), requires operators to obtain integrated permits covering air, water, soil, and waste, and introduces a new obligation to digitise permitting processes across member states.

The recast lowers the threshold for livestock farms from 40,000 to 16,000 pigs and from 2,000 to 700 broiler chickens (or 350 laying hens), bringing an estimated 185,000 additional farms under EU oversight. It also mandates public access to the European Pollutant Release and Transfer Register (E-PRTR) in real time, and requires companies to submit transition plans for reaching zero pollution by 2050, with intermediate targets for 2030 and 2040.

## Why was it introduced?

The most specific trigger was a 2018 European Court of Auditors special report that found the original 2010 IED had failed to reduce industrial emissions at the pace required for the EU's 2030 climate and 2050 zero-pollution goals. The auditors documented that 18 of 27 member states had missed deadlines for transposing the 2010 directive, and that national permitting authorities routinely issued permits with emission limits 30–40% above what BAT conclusions recommended. But the deeper structural tension was the directive's farm exemption: agriculture accounted for 94% of EU ammonia emissions — which form PM2.5 particles linked to 400,000 premature deaths annually, per the European Environment Agency's 2020 report — yet large-scale livestock operations were entirely outside the permit system. This created a regulatory loophole that left rural communities in the Netherlands, Flanders, and northern Italy exposed to nitrogen pollution while industrial plants faced tightening caps.

The coalition that secured passage was unusual. The European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen proposed the recast in April 2022 as part of the Zero Pollution Action Plan, backed by the Green Deal's political momentum. The European Parliament's ENVI committee, led by rapporteur Radan Kanev (EPP, Bulgaria), pushed for extending the IED to mining and battery recycling — sectors that had lobbied hard against inclusion. The final compromise, reached in trilogues on 28 November 2023, gave member states more flexibility on farm thresholds (allowing exemptions for farms with less than 5 years of operation) and delayed the digital permitting requirement to 2027 for smaller operations. Opposition came from the European People's Party's agricultural wing, which argued the farm extension would devastate family farming, and from the European Livestock Association (ELA), which claimed compliance costs would reach €5,000 per farm annually. The Council vote on 12 March 2024 passed with 24 member states in favour — Belgium, Finland, and the Netherlands abstained over concerns about national nitrogen plans conflicting with the new rules.

Timing was critical. The recast succeeded in 2024 because of two converging crises: the European Court of Justice's 2023 ruling in Case C-364/20 (the "Dutch Nitrogen case") that had forced the Netherlands to halve nitrogen emissions by 2030, effectively making agricultural pollution a legal emergency; and the 2022 energy crisis, which had shifted public attention to industrial emissions from fossil fuel plants running at higher capacity. Earlier attempts to reform the IED — a 2017 Commission evaluation and a 2020 "fitness check" — had stalled because the farm lobby successfully framed the issue as a threat to food security. The CJEU ruling broke that deadlock by imposing judicially enforceable emission cuts that only an expanded IED could operationalise.

What changed technologically was the enabling capacity of digital permits. The 2010 directive relied on paper-based permitting that took three to five years per installation to renew. The recast mandates electronic permit applications, real-time emissions reporting via the E-PRTR portal, and automatic cross-checks against BAT conclusions updated every four years. This was driven by the success of the Dutch "Netbeheer" digital permitting pilot, which reduced average permit processing time from 18 months to 10 weeks between 2019 and 2022.

## Timeline

- **2010** — Original Industrial Emissions Directive (2010/75/EU) enters into force, consolidating seven previous directives
- **2017** — European Commission publishes ex-post evaluation of the IED, finding "limited progress" on emission reductions and "persistent implementation gaps"
- **2018** — European Court of Auditors special report criticises the IED for permitting delays and non-compliance with BAT conclusions
- **2020** — Zero Pollution Action Plan published by the Commission, promising to "address the regulatory gap for agriculture"
- **2023** — CJEU ruling in Dutch Nitrogen case (Case C-364/20) mandates enforceable national nitrogen emission caps
- **28 November 2023** — Trilogue agreement on IED recast text
- **12 March 2024** — Council votes 24-3 to adopt the recast (Belgium, Finland, Netherlands abstain)
- **15 July 2024** — Official Journal publication: Regulation 2024/1785/EU
- **20 days after publication** — Entry into force
- **2030** — First intermediate zero-pollution targets due; digital permitting required for all new installations
- **2040** — Second intermediate targets
- **2050** — Zero pollution target for industrial emissions

## Impact and consequences

The recast directly impacts an estimated 75,000 industrial installations already covered by the 2010 IED, plus 185,000 livestock farms newly brought under the permit system. The Commission's 2022 impact assessment estimated compliance costs for agriculture at €1.8 billion annually, offset by €4.3 billion in annual health benefits from reduced air pollution. The pig farming sector in Spain and Poland immediately challenged the rules, with the Spanish Pig Farming Association (ASPRIC) filing a legal complaint on 20 September 2024 claiming the thresholds would force consolidation of small farms into agribusiness giants.

In the industrial sector, the battery manufacturing industry — represented by the European Battery Association (EUBA) — successfully lobbied for a 2027 transition period for critical raw materials extraction, arguing that the IED's emission limits could impede the EU's strategic autonomy goals. The mining chamber of Sweden, which hosts the EU's largest rare earth deposits, warned of "regulatory strangulation" at a 2024 hearing.

Member states face the most visible consequence: the obligation to digitise permitting infrastructure. By 2030, all permit applications must be submitted and processed electronically. This is proving hardest for countries with decentralised permitting systems — Germany (where 16 Länder manage permits independently), Belgium (three regions), and Italy (20 regions) — each of which must harmonise databases and create single digital gateways.

The first enforcement actions are expected from the European Commission in 2026 against member states that fail to transpose the recast on time. The Commission's DG Environment has already flagged Austria, Poland, and Greece as "at risk" for transposition delays.

## Key questions answered

### Does EU law now regulate factory farming for the first time?

Yes, but only for the largest operations. The recast brings farms with at least 16,000 pigs, 3,000 pigs for breeding, 700 broiler chickens or 350 laying hens under EU pollution permit rules for the first time. Smaller farms and cattle operations remain exempt. The threshold is a compromise: the European Parliament called for 8,000 pigs and 300 broiler chickens, while the Council wanted no farm extension at all. The resulting rules apply to about 16% of EU pig farms and 12% of poultry farms — the ones responsible for the vast majority of ammonia emissions.

### Why is the EU making companies submit "transition plans" for pollution?

The recast requires each industrial installation to produce a plan showing how it will reach zero pollution by 2050, with intermediate targets for 2030 and 2040. This mirrors the corporate sustainability reporting directive (CSRD) requirements for climate transition plans. The idea is to force operators to think long-term rather than just meeting next year's permit limits. If an installation fails to submit a credible plan, the permitting authority can deny or revoke its permit. Environmental groups like ClientEarth and the European Environmental Bureau successfully lobbied for this provision, arguing that traditional BAT-based permits leave companies with no incentive to innovate beyond current best practice.

### How will digital permits work in practice?

By 2030, every EU member state must operate a digital system where industrial operators can apply for pollution permits, submit emissions data, and receive automated compliance checks against the most current BAT conclusions. This breaks with the paper-based system in which permits legally required physical signatures and took 3-5 years per installation to renew. The digital shift allows cross-border data sharing through the E-PRTR portal, meaning a company with installations in three countries can manage permits from a single dashboard. The fly in the ointment is interoperability: 14 member states currently use completely different databases for permit tracking, and the Commission has not yet funded the standardisation connectors required.
