## What is it?

The Data Act, formally adopted in November 2023, sets the legal framework for access to and use of data generated by Internet of Things (IoT) devices — from smart tractors to industrial sensors to connected cars. It grants users (the owners or lessees of a device) the right to access the data their device generates, and to share that data with third-party service providers. This breaks the current default where manufacturers capture and control virtually all device data.

The regulation also mandates rules for business-to-government data sharing in exceptional circumstances (such as public emergencies), introduces obligations for cloud service providers to enable smooth switching between providers, and establishes requirements for smart contracts on blockchain and other distributed ledger technologies. It applies primarily to non-personal data, complementing the GDPR's regime for personal data. Fines for violations can reach up to 20 million euros or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher.

## Why was it introduced?

The Data Act's origins lie in a structural tension at the heart of the EU's data economy: connected devices generated an estimated 80% of all industrial data according to the Commission's 2020 European Data Strategy, but less than 20% of it was actually used. The rest remained locked in proprietary silos controlled by device manufacturers — a situation the Commission's impact assessment called "a market failure."

The immediate political trigger was the failure of voluntary industry initiatives. Between 2016 and 2019, the Commission had encouraged several sectors to develop self-regulatory data-sharing codes of conduct. None produced operational results. In agriculture, for example, tractor manufacturer John Deere faced sustained farmer protests across Europe — including a notable mass protest in Poland in 2021 — because farmers who owned their tractors could not access diagnostic data to choose independent repair shops. The Right to Repair movement, which gained strong traction in the European Parliament from 2018 onward, turned this into a legislative demand.

The coalition that passed the Data Act was a broad cross-party alliance led by the European People's Party and the Socialists and Democrats, with crucial support from the Greens. Rapporteur Miapetra Kumpula-Natri (S&D, Finland) described it as "a new chapter in the single market for data." The key opposition came from technology manufacturers, represented by DigitalEurope, which warned in a 2022 position paper that the rules would "undermine trade secrets and innovation incentives." The German automotive industry — represented by VDA — mounted particularly strong resistance, arguing that vehicle data of the weight of a car could be considered personal data, a claim the European Data Protection Board rejected in a formal opinion.

The timing reflected the end of a political deadlock. Earlier efforts to mandate data access had stalled in the first von der Leyen Commission because of internal divisions between DG CONNECT (which favoured strong access rights) and DG GROW (which worried about harming European manufacturing competitiveness). The 2020 publication of the European Strategy for Data broke this impasse by framing data access as essential to the EU's digital sovereignty. The legislative momentum was accelerated by the 2022 energy crisis: Member States like France and Spain pushed for provisions allowing public authorities to access private-sector energy data for grid management, which became the business-to-government data sharing chapter.

What changed specifically was the 2021 political agreement on the Data Governance Act (2022/868/EU), which established the principle of data intermediation and created the legal architecture for voluntary sharing. The Data Act built on this by making sharing mandatory where the Data Governance Act had only enabled it. The Commission's own 2023 impact assessment estimated that the combined package would boost EU GDP by 4% and create 9 million additional jobs by 2030, though these figures were disputed by industry groups.

## Timeline

- **2018** — The European Data Protection Board issues formal opinion that vehicle data may constitute personal data, complicating industry arguments for exclusive manufacturer control
- **2020** — European Commission publishes European Strategy for Data, estimates 80% of industrial data goes unused
- **2021** — Political agreement on Data Governance Act, establishing framework for voluntary data sharing
- **23 February 2022** — Commission adopts Data Act proposal, presented by Commissioner for Internal Market Thierry Breton
- **2022** — European Parliament receives 2,701 proposed amendments from MEPs; intense lobbying from DigitalEurope and VDA
- **March 2023** — European Parliament plenary adopts its position with 500 votes in favour <small>(source: European Parliament press release, March 2023)</small>
- **May 2023** — Council of the EU adopts its position; trilogue negotiations conclude
- **9 November 2023** — European Parliament gives final approval with 481 votes in favour, 31 against, 71 abstentions
- **27 December 2023** — Regulation published in Official Journal
- **11 January 2024** — Regulation enters into force
- **11 September 2025** — Full application date (most provisions), with cloud switching provisions applying from 12 January 2027

## Impact and consequences

The Data Act primarily affects manufacturers of connected devices — estimated by the Commission to cover more than 100 million new devices sold in the EU annually. The automotive sector was the most vocal opponent, with European automobile manufacturers' association ACEA stating in 2023 that the rules would "create legal uncertainty for all players involved in the data value chain."

For consumers and businesses, the immediate practical effect will appear in sectors like agriculture — farmers controlling their tractor data — and connected cars, where drivers could authorise independent garages to access vehicle diagnostics. The public sector provision was tested conceptually during the COVID-19 pandemic, when health authorities could not access aggregated mobility data from ride-sharing platforms; the Data Act mandates a formal mechanism for such cases.

Cloud service providers face new obligations to eliminate switching barriers. The Commission estimated that removing these barriers could save EU businesses €6.6 billion annually <small>(source: Commission impact assessment, 2022)</small>. Major providers including Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud opposed the switching provisions, warning of costs to maintain interoperability.

Implementation challenges are significant. The regulation requires Member States to designate competent authorities by September 2024, but several countries — including Germany and France — had not completed this process by late 2024. The Commission was preparing implementing acts on trade secrets protection and contractual fairness as of early 2024.

First enforcement actions are expected from 2026 onward. The regulation does not establish a single EU-level enforcer; instead, each Member State's designated authority — typically the data protection authority or competition authority — handles complaints. This fragmentation creates enforcement risks similar to those seen in early GDPR cases.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the Data Act exclude personal data?

The Data Act is explicitly designed to complement the GDPR, not replace it. Personal data already has a regulatory framework under GDPR, which grants data subjects rights of access and portability. The Data Act focuses on the gap — non-personal data from IoT devices, such as machine performance metrics, soil composition readings, or industrial sensor logs — which had no clear legal framework for access and reuse. Where data is mixed (personal and non-personal), both regulations apply simultaneously, a complexity the Commission promised to address in guidance.

### Why did John Deere oppose the Data Act?

John Deere's opposition reflected the broader agricultural equipment industry position. The company argued that revealing diagnostic data and maintenance protocols would expose proprietary algorithms and trade secrets — a claim the Commission's impact assessment acknowledged, but countered by noting that the Data Act's trade secrets provisions (Article 4) require data holders to protect trade secrets "by means of appropriate technical and organisational measures" before sharing. The practical effect: a manufacturer can refuse to share data if doing so would "likely cause serious damage" to trade secrets, but the burden of proof is on the manufacturer, not the data requester.

### Why are cloud switching provisions of the Data Act delayed until 2027?

The delayed application (12 January 2027) for cloud switching provisions reflects intense industry lobbying. Cloud providers argued that implementing full interoperability and data portability for enterprise workloads required significant infrastructure changes — particularly for "vertical" cloud services like software-as-a-service platforms. The Commission accepted a phased approach, with smaller providers receiving additional time. The European Cloud Federation, representing providers like OVHcloud and Deutsche Telekom, supported the provisions but warned that overly aggressive timelines could harm Europe's still-nascent cloud competitors.

### Why does the Data Act also regulate smart contracts?

The inclusion of smart contract provisions was a late addition during trilogue negotiations, driven by concerns about the reliability of automated data-sharing agreements. The regulation requires smart contracts used for data sharing to include a "kill switch" mechanism and to be auditable. This addresses cases like the 2022 collapse of algorithmic stablecoin TerraUSD, which raised broader questions about automated code without human oversight. The provisions apply only to smart contracts executing automated data-sharing agreements, not to blockchain applications generally.
