## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2019/1896 reconstituted the European Border and Coast Guard Agency (Frontex) as something qualitatively different from the small Warsaw-based coordination body it had been since 2004. The regulation gave the agency its own standing corps — a permanent uniformed force that ramps up to 10,000 border guards by 2027, drawn from a mix of Frontex staff (Category 1), long-term seconded national officers (Category 2), and short-term deployments (Category 3). For the first time, EU staff carry service weapons and exercise executive powers — checking documents, stamping passports, refusing entry — on Member State territory.

The regulation also handed Frontex its own budget for buying ships, planes, vehicles and surveillance equipment rather than depending on Member State contributions in kind. It expanded the agency's mandate to cover returns (deporting rejected asylum-seekers), cooperation with non-EU countries through status agreements, and a broad role in screening at the EU's external borders. The Executive Director — at the time of adoption, Fabrice Leggeri — gained substantially expanded authority, including the power to deploy the standing corps when a Member State fails to act.

It superseded the 2016 European Border and Coast Guard Regulation (2016/1624), itself only three years old, which had already been a major upgrade from the 2004 Frontex founding regulation.

## Why was it introduced?

The political trigger was the 2015–2016 Schengen crisis. More than one million people crossed the Mediterranean to Europe in 2015, the vast majority via the Aegean from Turkey to the Greek islands, according to UNHCR arrival data. Greece's coast guard and the small Frontex of the time — fewer than 400 staff and entirely dependent on Member States volunteering boats and officers — were overwhelmed. Germany suspended Dublin returns for Syrians in August 2015; Hungary built a fence on its Serbian border; Austria, Slovenia, Sweden and Denmark reintroduced internal Schengen border checks. The free-movement area that the Commission has long called Schengen's crown jewel came closer to collapse than at any point since its creation.

Jean-Claude Juncker's Commission responded first with the 2016 regulation, which renamed Frontex as the European Border and Coast Guard and created a "rapid reaction pool" of 1,500 officers nationally pre-committed. But the 2016 design still leaned on Member State goodwill, and Commissioner Dimitris Avramopoulos acknowledged repeatedly that pledges to Frontex operations were chronically under-fulfilled — the agency itself reported persistent gaps between requested and delivered personnel and equipment in its annual reports.

The decisive political moment came in September 2018, when Juncker used his State of the Union speech to call for a 10,000-strong standing corps by 2020 — three years ahead of where negotiators eventually landed. The proposal was deliberately maximalist: the Commission wanted to lock in the post-2015 political consensus before Schengen fatigue set in. The file moved unusually fast. The Council and Parliament reached political agreement in April 2019, and the regulation entered into force in December 2019.

The coalition that pushed it through stitched together centre-right governments demanding "more Europe on borders" (notably Austria, then holding the Council presidency in late 2018, under Sebastian Kurz), southern Member States that wanted operational support rather than just monitoring (Italy, Greece, Spain, Malta), and a Commission keen to demonstrate that the EU could deliver on migration after the Dublin reform deadlocked. Opposition came from two directions: Member States jealous of border sovereignty — Hungary, Poland and at moments France — pushed back on the agency's executive powers, while the Greens, the GUE/NGL group and human-rights NGOs including Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch warned that a beefed-up Frontex without serious fundamental-rights safeguards risked institutionalising pushbacks. The compromise reduced Commission ambition on supranational command — the host Member State retains overall authority — but kept the headline 10,000 figure intact, shifting the full deployment target to 2027.

## Impact and consequences

The standing corps was deployed for the first time on 1 January 2021, with officers visible at the Bulgarian, Greek and Croatian land borders in distinctive blue uniforms with the EU emblem — a deliberately symbolic moment. Frontex's budget grew sharply through the 2021–2027 Multiannual Financial Framework, making it one of the largest EU agencies by spending, according to Commission budget documents.

The expansion collided almost immediately with a fundamental-rights crisis. The European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF) opened an investigation into Frontex's role in Aegean pushbacks; its 2022 report, leaked to Der Spiegel, Le Monde and other outlets, was scathing about management's handling of incidents involving Greek authorities. Executive Director Fabrice Leggeri resigned in April 2022. The European Parliament refused to grant Frontex discharge for the 2020 budget — an unusual sanction — citing fundamental-rights concerns. The European Ombudsman, Emily O'Reilly, opened multiple inquiries into the agency's complaints mechanism and fundamental-rights officer arrangements.

Operationally, Frontex has become the visible EU presence at hotspots from Lampedusa to Lithuania's border with Belarus during the 2021 Lukashenko-engineered crisis. Its return operations have expanded sharply, with the agency now organising or co-financing the majority of EU-coordinated deportation flights according to its own annual activity reports. Status agreements signed with Albania, Serbia, Montenegro, North Macedonia and Moldova allow Frontex officers to operate on non-EU territory — a power previously unthinkable for an EU agency.

## Key questions answered

### Can EU border guards carry weapons and arrest people?

Yes — since January 2021, members of the European Border and Coast Guard standing corps wear EU uniforms and carry side arms while deployed in Member States or third countries with status agreements. They can check documents, stamp passports and refuse entry under the authority of the host state. This made Frontex the EU's first armed uniformed service, a constitutional novelty for what had been a coordination body.

### Why did Europe build a 10,000-strong border force?

The 2015 Schengen crisis exposed that Europe's external border — particularly the Greek islands facing Turkey — depended on whichever Member State happened to be there, with only ad hoc reinforcement. When Greece was overwhelmed, internal Schengen borders began to reappear and the free-travel area nearly collapsed. The standing corps was designed so that the EU would never again be dependent on Member State volunteers showing up; the Commission could deploy its own permanent force.

### Has Frontex been involved in illegal pushbacks?

The OLAF anti-fraud investigation, completed in 2022, examined Frontex management's handling of incidents in the Aegean involving Greek coast guard pushbacks of asylum-seekers. The leaked findings led directly to Executive Director Fabrice Leggeri's resignation in April 2022 and to the European Parliament refusing budget discharge. Multiple inquiries by the European Ombudsman have examined the agency's fundamental-rights complaint mechanisms; reform is ongoing under successor Hans Leijtens.

### Can Frontex operate outside the EU?

Yes — the 2019 regulation introduced "status agreements" allowing Frontex officers with full executive powers to operate on the territory of non-EU countries. Agreements are in force with Western Balkan states including Albania, Serbia, Montenegro and North Macedonia, and with Moldova. This was politically sensitive because it embeds armed EU officers in third-country sovereign operations, but the Commission argued it was essential to manage migration routes upstream of the EU border.
