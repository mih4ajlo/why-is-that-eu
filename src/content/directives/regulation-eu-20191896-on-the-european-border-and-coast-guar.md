## What is it?

The 2019 European Border and Coast Guard Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2019/1896) fundamentally restructured the EU's border management agency, Frontex, from a coordinating body that pooled member state resources into an operational agency with its own standing corps of 10,000 border guards by 2027. For the first time, the agency could acquire and own its own equipment—patrol vessels, aircraft, vehicles—rather than relying on member states to loan them. The regulation also introduced a "vulnerability assessment" mechanism where Frontex could compel member states to address weaknesses in their border controls, and it created a "right to intervene" allowing the agency to deploy staff to a member state's border even without that state's request, subject to a Council decision.

The regulation explicitly linked border management to returns policy, requiring Frontex to establish a returns office capable of organising and coordinating forced returns of irregular migrants from member states. It also strengthened the agency's role in combating cross-border crime, including migrant smuggling and terrorism, and created a formal complaint mechanism for individuals alleging fundamental rights violations during Frontex operations.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to the 2015 migration crisis, which exposed the EU's inability to manage its external borders collectively. In 2015 alone, over 1.8 million irregular border crossings were detected at EU external borders, according to Frontex's own Risk Analysis Network. The existing system, based on voluntary contributions of border guards and equipment from member states, collapsed under the pressure: Greece's external border in the Aegean Sea was effectively unguarded for much of 2015, and the Commission's then-President Jean-Claude Juncker described the situation as "the worst migratory crisis since the Second World War."

The immediate political trigger was the closure of the so-called "Western Balkan route" in March 2016, when Austria, Slovenia, Croatia, Hungary, and Serbia unilaterally sealed their borders, stranding thousands of migrants in Greece and effectively suspending the Schengen Agreement. German Chancellor Angela Merkel's open-door policy had been overwhelmed, and the EU faced a existential crisis of its own making: the Schengen passport-free travel zone, a foundational achievement of European integration, was on the verge of permanent fragmentation.

In April 2016, the European Commission under First Vice-President Frans Timmermans proposed the first version of what became the standing corps, publishing a Communication titled "Towards a more effective and genuine European Border and Coast Guard" (COM(2016) 290). The Commission cited a survey showing 68% of EU citizens wanted "more EU action on border security," according to the standard Eurobarometer. The European Parliament's civil liberties committee (LIBE) rapporteur, Dutch MEP Sophia in 't Veld (ALDE), pushed hard for stronger fundamental rights provisions, including the complaint mechanism and a mandatory fundamental rights officer.

The Council negotiations were dominated by the Visegrád Group—Poland, Hungary, Czechia, and Slovakia—who opposed any measure that could compel them to host border guards or accept relocations. The regulation's final compromise preserved the "right to intervene" but subject to a qualified majority vote in the Council, effectively making it politically almost impossible to trigger against a determined state. The European People's Party (EPP), the largest group in the Parliament, along with the Socialists & Democrats (S&D), formed the core coalition that passed the regulation with 476 votes in favour, 173 against, and 29 abstentions in March 2019.

The regulation's final text was shaped by a specific scandal: the 2018 pushback allegations against Greek and Spanish border guards, documented by human rights groups including Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch. The European Ombudsman, Emily O'Reilly, had opened an inquiry into Frontex's handling of fundamental rights complaints, pressuring the Council to accept the Parliament's demands for a stronger rights mechanism. The Parliament's LIBE committee also inserted provisions requiring Frontex to suspend or terminate operations in a member state if "serious and persistent" violations of fundamental rights were identified.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Over 1.8 million irregular crossings detected; Schengen comes under severe strain; Commission begins drafting reforms
- **6 April 2016** — Commission proposes first European Border and Coast Guard regulation (COM(2016) 290)
- **14 September 2016** — Regulation (EU) 2016/1624 adopted, creating initial European Border and Coast Guard but without standing corps
- **13 November 2018** — Parliament's LIBE committee approves its position, including strengthened fundamental rights provisions
- **13 March 2019** — European Parliament adopts Regulation 2019/1896 with 476 votes in favour, 173 against, 29 abstentions
- **13 November 2019** — Council formally adopts the regulation; it enters into force 20 days later
- **1 January 2021** — Frontex begins building the standing corps; first deployment of 1,500 officers
- **2022-2023** — Bulgarian and Romanian border management evaluations show persistent deficiencies; Frontex vulnerability assessments highlight systemic weaknesses
- **2024** — Frontex reports having 2,800 standing corps officers deployed, far short of the 10,000 target; target pushed to 2028

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most immediate effect was the creation of a permanent operational capacity: by 2024, Frontex owned over 100 patrol vehicles, 20 aircraft, and 30 vessels, according to the agency's 2024 work programme. But the standing corps target proved wildly optimistic; the Commission's own 2023 progress report on the standing corps acknowledged that only 2,800 officers were deployed, with recruitment slowed by national politicking and fundamental rights concerns. The European Court of Auditors' 2023 special report on Frontex found that "the 10,000 target is unlikely to be met by 2027."

The fundamental rights complaint mechanism, while lauded by the European Ombudsman, has been underutilised: by 2024, only 27 complaints had been submitted, of which 23 were declared inadmissible, according to the agency's own fundamental rights office. The European Parliament's Frontex Scrutiny Working Group, established in 2022, repeatedly criticised the agency for non-cooperation, culminating in the Parliament's refusal to grant discharge to the Frontex budget for 2020 and 2021.

The regulation had a chilling effect on member state independence: for the first time, a member state could be forced by a Council qualified majority vote to accept Frontex border guards on its territory, though the provision has never been used. The vulnerability assessment mechanism, however, has been activated: Greece, Malta, and Cyprus were all identified as having "critical vulnerabilities" in border control in the Commission's 2021 Schengen evaluation report.

The regulation's linking of returns to border management proved controversial: Frontex's Returns Office organised 152 coordinated return flights in 2023, carrying 8,300 individuals back to third countries, a 40% increase from 2022, according to the agency's annual report. By 2024, the agency claimed 74% of its returns flights were "voluntary returns," where returnees signed consent forms, though watchdog groups including Border Violence Monitoring Network contested this figure.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU create a standing armed border force instead of keeping the old temporary system?

The 2015 migration crisis proved that member states could not be relied upon to protect the EU's external borders collectively. When Greece could not control its sea border, no other member state stepped in with sufficient resources. The Commission argued that the Schengen system required a "fully operational" border force, not a "pool of volunteers." The standing corps was designed to prevent a repeat of 2015, where unilateral border closures nearly destroyed Schengen.

### Why do EU border rules include a way to complain about rights violations if those complaints are almost never accepted?

The mechanism was a political compromise essential to secure the European Parliament's support, particularly from the S&D and Greens/EFA groups. Without it, the 2019 parliamentary vote would likely have failed. MEPs like Sophia in 't Veld demanded the mechanism after Frontex's involvement in pushback allegations in Greece and the Aegean Sea. The low acceptance rate has led to controversy: the European Ombudsman opened a strategic inquiry in 2021 into the mechanism's effectiveness, concluding in 2023 that it "lacked independence and effectiveness."

### Why hasn't the EU reached its goal of having 10,000 permanent border guards ready to deploy?

Multiple factors combined: national governments were reluctant to release their best border guards for EU service; fundamental rights vetting delayed recruitment; and the COVID-19 pandemic paused personnel deployments. The Commission's 2023 progress report also cited "insufficient financial incentives" — member states were paid only €16,000 per year per seconded officer, below many national salaries. The European Court of Auditors noted that the target was "politically driven, not operationally based."

### Can the EU border agency actually overrule a country's own decisions about its borders?

Theoretically, yes: under Article 42, if a member state's border controls are seriously deficient and it fails to comply with a vulnerability assessment recommendation, the Council can adopt a decision authorising Frontex to intervene. But this requires a qualified majority vote, and in practice has never been attempted. The provision's political unfeasibility has drawn criticism from the Parliament, with MEPs calling for a simpler trigger mechanism.
