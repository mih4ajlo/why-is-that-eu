## What is it?

The European Long-Term Investment Fund (ELTIF) framework update, adopted as Regulation 2023/606/EU in February 2023, substantially overhauls the original ELTIF rules from 2015 (Regulation 2015/760/EU). The update is designed to revive a dormant investment product: less than 30 ELTIFs had been launched across the entire EU since 2015, with total assets under management estimated by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) in 2022 to be under €3 billion.

The new regime lowers the minimum investment threshold for retail investors from €10,000 to €500, removes the requirement that retail investors must have a portfolio exceeding €500,000, and expands the list of eligible assets to include certain private debt instruments, securitisations, and infrastructure projects. It also simplifies the authorisation process and allows ELTIFs to list on public markets, making them tradable on secondary exchanges.

The core concept remains unchanged: ELTIFs are closed-ended funds that invest in illiquid, long-term assets — infrastructure, SMEs, real estate, energy projects — and are supposed to channel private capital into the EU's priority sectors, such as the Green Deal and digital transition.

## Why was it introduced?

The ELTIF framework update was driven by a stark structural failure: the original 2015 regulation had produced almost nothing. By 2020, ESMA reported that only 23 ELTIFs had been authorised since the regime launched — a fraction of the European Commission's ambition to create a new asset class for retail investors.

The immediate trigger for the revision was not a scandal but a political embarrassment. In the 2020 Capital Markets Union (CMU) action plan, the Commission, under then-Commissioner for Financial Services Mairead McGuinness, acknowledged that the ELTIF regime had been "less successful than expected." The European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Michiel Hoogeveen (ECR, Netherlands), began pushing for a more aggressive overhaul, producing a draft report in early 2021 that called for drastic simplification.

The coalition in favour of reform was broad. Retail investor associations such as Better Finance argued that ordinary Europeans were being shut out of infrastructure and private equity returns due to overly protective rules. The alternative investment industry — represented by Invest Europe and the European Association of Long-Term Investors — lobbied heavily for lower thresholds, pointing out that comparable UK and US products (like US Regulation A+ funds) routinely attracted retail capital. The European Central Bank (ECB) backed the revision, seeing ELTIFs as a way to reduce the EU's over-reliance on bank lending for long-term projects.

Opposition came primarily from national securities regulators and consumer protection groups. Several national competent authorities, including the French Autorité des Marchés Financiers (AMF) and the German BaFin, warned that lowering minimum investments without adequate liquidity mechanisms could expose retail investors to excessive risk. Finance Watch, the Brussels-based NGO, argued in 2022 that the original safeguards existed for good reason: illiquid assets with lock-in periods of five to ten years were inherently unsuitable for unsophisticated investors.

The timing of the revision was determined by two factors. First, the COVID-19 pandemic had demonstrated the urgency of mobilising private capital for recovery and the green transition, as public budgets faced severe strain. Second, the European Commission’s 2021 legislative priorities included the CMU action plan, which gave McGuinness institutional momentum. The proposal was published on 25 November 2021, with Parliament and Council reaching a provisional agreement on 19 October 2022 — a relatively fast legislative process for an EU financial regulation.

What changed the dynamic most was the failure of the original regime to attract any meaningful market activity. By 2022, ESMA noted that the largest single ELTIF held just €400 million — negligible compared to the €300 billion retail-invested alternative investment market in the US. Industry warnings had proven hollow: European asset managers had claimed in 2015 that the rules were too restrictive, and the data confirmed they were right. The Commission's impact assessment cited interviews with 25 market participants, including Allianz Global Investors, BlackRock, and Axa Investment Managers, all of whom named the €10,000 minimum and portfolio test as fatal barriers.

The reform passed with strong support — 545 votes in favour, 72 against in the European Parliament — and entered into force on 9 January 2023, with application from 10 January 2024.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Original ELTIF Regulation (2015/760/EU) enters into force, with strict retail investor protections including €10,000 minimum and €500,000 portfolio requirement.
- **2019** — European Commission publishes a review report finding only 23 ELTIFs authorised; calls for "targeted amendments."
- **2020** — Capital Markets Union action plan commits to revising ELTIF rules. Commissioner McGuinness takes office in October.
- **25 November 2021** — Commission publishes legislative proposal for ELTIF reform.
- **19 October 2022** — European Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement after trilogue negotiations.
- **15 December 2022** — European Parliament votes 545-72 to adopt the regulation.
- **9 January 2023** — Regulation 2023/606/EU enters into force.
- **10 January 2024** — Full application date; new ELTIFs must comply with revised rules.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was a surge of interest. By mid-2024, over 60 new ELTIFs had been launched or were in registration with ESMA, according to the trade association Invest Europe. Several major asset managers — including BlackRock, Allianz Global Investors, and Schroders — filed for ELTIF authorisations targeting retail investors. The lower entry threshold of €500 allowed platforms like Trade Republic and Scalable Capital to offer ELTIF shares alongside ETFs, dramatically expanding distribution.

Industry response was not uniformly positive. Some national regulators, notably the AMF in France, imposed additional national requirements under the Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive (AIFMD), including mandatory liquidity management tools and stricter redemption rules, which asset managers criticised as undermining EU harmonisation. The Commission opened an infringement dialogue with France in late 2023 over what it saw as gold-plating.

Retail investor protection remains a contested issue. The first widely distributed ELTIF — the BlackRock Global Transformation ELTIF, launched in September 2023 with a 7-year lock-in — faced criticism from Finance Watch for investing heavily in unrated private debt. ESMA issued a supervisory briefing in March 2024 reminding fund managers of their obligations under MiFID II regarding suitability assessments for ELTIFs.

The broader consequence is that the ELTIF is now, for the first time, a functioning product category, though still tiny compared to Luxembourg-based UCITS funds (which managed over €10 trillion). The Commission's 2024 mid-term CMU review highlighted the ELTIF reform as a "success case" for retail capital market participation.

## Key questions answered

### How can a regular person invest in European long-term infrastructure projects?

Through an ELTIF, available from brokers or asset managers. The minimum investment is €500, and the fund typically holds illiquid assets like wind farms or toll roads for 5–10 years. You must pass a suitability assessment under EU investment rules, and early withdrawal is usually impossible or costly.

### Why were these investment funds so unpopular that the EU had to rewrite the rules?

The original 2015 regulation required a €10,000 minimum investment and proof of a €500,000 portfolio, which effectively excluded all but wealthy investors. Fund managers could not attract enough capital to launch viable funds — only 23 ELTIFs ever started. The EU realised its own rules had killed the product.

### Are there extra protections for retail investors in these long-term funds?

Yes. The revised rules still require a "liquidity management tool" and a mandatory redemption policy, but the specific lock-in period is set by the fund manager (typically 5–10 years). National regulators can add stricter national rules. ESMA monitors marketing materials to prevent misleading claims about liquidity.
