## What is it?

Brussels IIb (Regulation 2019/1111) is the EU’s core framework for resolving cross-border family disputes involving children. It determines which member state’s court has jurisdiction in divorce and parental responsibility cases and sets rules for recognizing and enforcing those decisions across national borders. It also implements the 1980 Hague Convention on child abduction within the EU’s legal order.

The regulation is a recast of its predecessor, Brussels IIa (2201/2003/EC), and was designed to fix structural weaknesses that had emerged over two decades of application. It entered into force in July 2019 but its substantive provisions – including the new deadlines for child abduction proceedings – apply only from 1 August 2022, giving member states time to adapt their judicial systems.

The most significant changes include a mandatory requirement for courts to hear the child in proceedings involving parental responsibility (unless manifestly against the child’s best interests), the abolition of the *exequatur* procedure for all parental responsibility judgments (making enforcement automatic), and a strict six-week deadline for first-instance decisions in child abduction cases.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform was driven by a series of concrete failures exposed by national courts, the European Court of Justice, and European Parliament inquiries. A pivotal event was the 2014 report of the European Commission on the application of Regulation 2201/2003, which documented that child abduction return procedures under Brussels IIa routinely exceeded six months, and in some cases stretched to two years or more – a direct breach of the Hague Convention’s goal of “prompt return.”

The structural tension lay in the original regulation’s compromise: member states had insisted on retaining a limited *exequatur* (declaration of enforceability) for custody and access judgments, creating a second layer of litigation that could delay enforcement for months. At the same time, the Hague Convention’s rapid-return mechanism was blunted by a lack of coordination between EU courts, with parallel proceedings in different countries producing conflicting orders.

The coalition that pushed for the recast was led by the European Parliament, which in a 2015 own-initiative resolution called for automatic recognition of all family-law decisions and for children’s views to be heard as a default. The Commission under First Vice-President Frans Timmermans and Justice Commissioner Věra Jourová seized the mandate in June 2016, publishing a proposal that drew on a 2012 public consultation that had received more than 1,200 responses, predominantly from parents and family-law professionals.

Member states were split. Northern and Western European countries – notably Germany, the Netherlands, and Sweden – advocated for stronger child-hearing requirements and abolition of *exequatur*. Several Central and Eastern European states, wary of ceding too much judicial sovereignty in family matters, argued for flexibility. The compromise was brokered during the Bulgarian Presidency in the first half of 2018, with key concessions: the six-week deadline was introduced but with a possibility to extend in exceptional cases, and the child’s right to be heard included an opt-out when a hearing would be “manifestly contrary to the child’s best interests.”

Timing was crucial: the proposal coincided with a growing political focus on children’s rights following the 2014 UN Convention on the Rights of the Child review, and a series of high-profile abduction cases that had been covered widely in national media. The 2016 *Povse v. Austria* judgment of the European Court of Justice had also exposed gaps in mutual trust, ruling that a child’s return could not be refused solely because the requesting court had already issued a custody order – a finding that irritated many national judges.

What ultimately unblocked the recast was not a single crisis but a cumulative loss of confidence. The 2014 report showed that the number of abductions within the EU had risen steadily – according to the Hague Conference, around 1,200 intra-EU applications were filed annually by the early 2010s – and that the existing system was failing children. The Commission’s impact assessment estimated that abolishing *exequatur* alone could save €260 million per year in legal costs and reduce average enforcement time from nine months to three.

## Timeline

- **2003** – Regulation 2201/2003 (Brussels IIa) enters into force, replacing earlier conventions.
- **2010–2012** – Commission consultations and a European Parliament study highlight delays in abduction cases and inconsistent hearing of children.
- **2014** – Commission publishes its report on the application of Brussels IIa, identifying lengthy proceedings and lack of child participation as core problems.
- **2015** – European Parliament adopts resolution urging a recast with mandatory child-hearing and abolition of *exequatur*.
- **30 June 2016** – Commission proposes a recast regulation (COM/2016/0411).
- **2017–2018** – European Parliament and Council negotiate; Parliament’s rapporteur is Jean-Marie Cavada (ALDE, France).
- **June 2018** – Political agreement reached under Bulgarian Council Presidency.
- **2 July 2019** – Regulation 2019/1111 published in the Official Journal.
- **22 July 2019** – Entry into force.
- **1 August 2022** – Date of application (substantive provisions become binding on member states).
- **2023** – The first preliminary references to the European Court of Justice under the new regulation begin.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation’s most immediate effect is on national court systems. Every member state was required to designate a single specialised court or chamber for child abduction cases, centralising expertise to meet the six-week deadline. By mid-2023, the vast majority of states had complied, though qualitative reports from the European Judicial Network suggested that some smaller states struggled with staffing levels.

Children’s right to be heard is now the default in all cross-border parental responsibility proceedings. Early data from the Commission’s 2023 EU Justice Scoreboard (based on national governance indicators) indicated that compliance varied: countries with established child-psychology services, such as Finland and Germany, adapted quickly, while others relied on written submissions only, prompting infringement concerns.

The abolition of *exequatur* for custody and access orders has made enforcement virtually automatic, reducing litigation for parents. However, the regulation also introduced a “ceasing effect” provision – a stronger public-policy defence that a court can use to refuse recognition if the original judgment violated a child’s fundamental rights – which some legal experts argue opens a new avenue for appeal.

No fines have been issued yet under the regulation, but the Commission monitors compliance through its annual EU Scoreboard and can launch infringement proceedings. The first challenge will likely concern the six-week deadline: in 2022, the Hague Conference noted that while many member states had reduced average return times to 40–50 days, a few still averaged over 10 weeks.

## Key questions answered

### What happens if a parent takes a child to another EU country without permission?

If a child is wrongfully removed or retained in another EU member state, the left-behind parent can apply for a return order under the 1980 Hague Convention, but with EU-specific enhancements: a specialised court must handle the case, and it must issue a first-instance decision within six weeks. The child’s views must be heard unless it would harm them. If the court orders return, the decision is automatically enforceable across all member states without any extra procedure – no *exequatur* needed.

### How does EU law ensure children are heard in custody disputes across borders?

The Brussels IIb regulation makes a child’s hearing mandatory in any proceeding concerning parental responsibility (including return cases), unless the court decides that a hearing would be “manifestly contrary” to the child’s best interests. The judge must give reasons if they deny a hearing. The regulation also encourages the use of “hearing the child via video” or through trained social workers to avoid physically transporting the child across borders.

### Why did the EU update its child abduction rules?

The previous rules (Brussels IIa, from 2003) caused persistent delays: some abduction cases took over a year to resolve, harming the child’s stability. The European Commission’s 2014 evaluation showed that many judges were not hearing children directly, and that parents had to go through two court procedures (first to get a return order, then to get it enforced in the other country). The recast collapsed these steps into one, introduced a strict six-week deadline, and made child-hearing the default – all to align with the child’s right to a prompt and fair process.

### Can a court order about a child be enforced automatically in another EU country?

Yes – for all parental responsibility decisions (custody, visitation, return orders), the regulation abolished *exequatur*. As of 1 August 2022, a judgment from one member state is directly enforceable in another without a separate declaration of enforceability. The parent simply presents a certified copy of the order, and the court in the enforcing state can only refuse if the order violates a fundamental principle of justice (such as a serious breach of the child’s right to be heard).
