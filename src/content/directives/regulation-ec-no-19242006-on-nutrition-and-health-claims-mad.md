## What is it?

The Nutrition and Health Claims Regulation (NHCR) harmonises rules across the EU for claims made on food packaging and advertising. A nutrition claim asserts a beneficial nutritional property (e.g., "low fat," "high in fibre"), while a health claim links a food or constituent to health (e.g., "calcium helps maintain normal bones"). Under the NHCR, all claims must be based on generally accepted scientific evidence, pre‑approved by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), and listed in a public EU Register. The regulation also introduced a nutrient‑profiling system to determine which foods can carry claims, designed to prevent products high in fat, sugar, or salt from being marketed as healthy.

The regulation applies to all foods placed on the EU market, including imported products. It bans claims that encourage excessive consumption, refer to weight loss rates, or imply that a balanced diet cannot provide adequate nutrients. A transitional period allowed existing claims until January 2010 for health claims. Since 2012, unauthorised claims are prohibited, and EFSA has evaluated over 2,700 submissions.

## Why was it introduced?

The NHCR was a direct response to the marketing wars of the 1990s and early 2000s, when food companies flooded supermarket shelves with "low fat" and "functional" products — often with dubious scientific backing. The term "low fat" itself became a battlefield. As consumers embraced low‑fat diets after the US‑led anti‑fat wave, manufacturers stripped fat but added sugar, salt, or starch to maintain taste. By the early 2000s, consumer groups such as the European Consumers' Organisation (BEUC) had documented dozens of products where "low fat" claims masked high sugar content, undermining public health messages.

The structural tension was clear: national rules varied wildly. The UK had voluntary guidelines, France had a 1999 decree, and Germany relied on the 1993 German Food Labelling Ordinance. This fragmentation created barriers to cross‑border trade — a product approved in one member state could be blocked in another for using the same claim. As the internal market for food grew (worth €836 billion in retail sales by 2004, according to Eurostat), the European Commission under President Romano Prodi and Health Commissioner David Byrne (1999–2004) saw a Single Market logic to harmonise.

The coalition for the regulation was forged by a surprising alliance: consumer NGOs, public health organisations (such as the European Heart Network), and the European Parliament's centre‑left and green MEPs. Austrian Social‑Democrat Karin Scheele served as rapporteur and pushed strongly for a nutrient‑profiling system, arguing that "low fat" claims should not shield unhealthy products. The lead Commission official was Robert Madelin, then Director‑General for Health and Consumer Protection, who argued that self‑regulation had failed after the CIAA (the European food industry association, now FoodDrinkEurope) failed to agree on a voluntary code in 2002.

Opposition came from the food processing industry, led by companies such as Danone, Kellogg's, Unilever, and Nestlé. They warned that nutrient profiling would "demonise" products like breakfast cereals and yogurt, and that EFSA’s scientific assessment would be slow and kill innovation. Industry lobbyists at the 2005 Brussels conference "Health Claims and the Consumer" predicted job losses and a competitive disadvantage against non‑EU imports. The European Parliament’s final vote on May 25, 2006, was 479 in favour, 28 against — a landslide, but the 28 opposing MEPs were mostly from the European People’s Party and included key agricultural figures.

Why did it pass in 2006 and not earlier? The timing was shaped by two events. First, the 2004 EU enlargement brought in 10 new member states where health‑claim marketing was even less regulated, increasing pressure for a common standard. Second, the rising obesity crisis — the Commission’s 2005 Green Paper "Promoting healthy diets and physical activity" noted that childhood obesity in the EU had risen by 50% in 15 years — gave political cover for action. The earlier 1997 Commission proposal for a food supplements directive (Directive 2002/46/EC) had opened the door for a broader claims framework, but it took the combination of a public health emergency and internal market logic to push through the NHCR.

## Timeline

- **1999** — The Commission’s White Paper on Food Safety calls for EU rules on claims, citing national divergences.
- **2002** — Self‑regulation attempt by CIAA collapses; industry fails to agree on a common code for health claims.
- **July 2003** — Commission proposes Regulation on nutrition and health claims (COM(2003) 424), including nutrient profiling.
- **2004** — EFSA is formally established and begins preparatory work. Commissioner Byrne leaves office; the new Commissioner Markos Kyprianou takes over.
- **May 2005** — European Parliament adopts first reading position, strengthening the nutrient‑profiling requirement — 479 votes in favour, 28 against.
- **October 2006** — Council adopts the regulation after a compromise that delays full implementation of nutrient profiles by five years.
- **1 July 2007** — Regulation enters into force; transitional period begins.
- **2010** — EFSA issues first scientific opinions on health claims; over 80% of submitted claims are rejected for insufficient evidence.
- **14 December 2012** — Deadline for all health claims to be approved; unauthorised claims are banned. The EU Register of nutrition and health claims is published.
- **2020** — Commission report on the application of the NHCR concludes that nutrient profiling has not yet been adopted due to political deadlock; a reform is promised.

## Impact and consequences

The NHCR fundamentally reshaped food marketing in Europe. According to an internal Commission evaluation in 2008, about 30% of products carrying claims on the EU market had to be relabelled or reformulated within the first year. EFSA’s rigorous assessments rejected roughly 2,400 of the 2,700 health claim applications by 2012 (EFSA, 2012 summary report). For example, Danone’s "Actimel" claim that it "reinforces the body’s natural defences" was initially rejected; only after resubmission with stronger human studies was a narrowly worded claim approved. Kellogg’s lost its UK "Start the day right" campaign after EFSA found no evidence linking breakfast cereals to improved concentration.

The "low fat" wars ended decisively: a product can now only bear a "low fat" claim if its total fat content is ≤3 g per 100 g (for solids) or ≤1.5 g per 100 ml (for liquids). Equally, a "low fat" product must not be high in sugar — the nutrient‑profiling criteria, though stalled, still serve as a guiding principle in enforcement actions.

Industry adjusted by shifting to "source of fibre" or "high protein" claims instead. Small and medium‑sized enterprises struggled most with the cost of EFSA applications — estimated at up to €150,000 per dossier by a 2009 Commission impact assessment — leading to a decline in functional food innovation in smaller companies. Meanwhile, the regulation gave larger players like Unilever and Nestlé a competitive advantage, as they could afford the science.

Consumer awareness improved: a 2013 Eurobarometer survey found that 67% of EU citizens read nutrition labels, up from 50% in 2006. However, the lack of finalised nutrient profiles meant that loopholes persisted — e.g., products high in saturated fat but low in sugar could still carry a "no added sugar" claim.

## Key questions answered

### Why can’t a yogurt company just claim its product “boosts immunity” without approval?

Under the NHCR, any health claim must be backed by “generally accepted scientific evidence” and approved by EFSA before use. The regulation was introduced precisely because companies like Danone and Yakult ran global campaigns with vague language (e.g., “helps your body’s defences”) that consumers interpreted as medical benefits. EFSA now requires at least one well‑controlled human study. Unapproved claims are illegal and national enforcement authorities can fine companies or order product recall.

### What happened to the “low fat” label after the regulation?

The NHCR defined “low fat” as ≤3 g per 100 g for solids and ≤1.5 g per 100 ml for liquids. More importantly, the regulation’s principle of nutrient profiling meant that a product can be low in fat but still be banned from making any claims if it is high in sugar, salt, or saturated fat. This ended the practice where “low fat” labels were placed on sugary cereals or sweetened yogurts. The European Court of Justice in Case C‑609/17 (TÜV Rheinland) later upheld that a “low fat” claim must not mislead about the product’s overall nutritional profile.

### Did the food industry succeed in blocking nutrient profiling?

Yes, partially. The regulation included a requirement for the Commission to adopt nutrient profiles by January 2009, but industry lobbying and member state disagreements (especially from countries like Italy and Poland, which feared for traditional cheeses and cured meats) stalled the process. In 2020, the Commission officially admitted it had not adopted profiles, and a 2022 revision announced by the Farm‑to‑Fork Strategy has not yet produced a final text. As a result, products high in fat, sugar, or salt can still carry claims as long as their specific claim is approved — a gap that consumer groups continue to criticise.

### How did small food companies cope with the new rules?

Many small producers could not afford the EFSA application fees (€50,000–€150,000, according to the 2009 Commission report) and the cost of commissioning clinical studies. As a result, they either withdrew health claims from their labels or switched to generic nutrition claims (e.g., “source of vitamin C”) which have pre‑defined conditions and do not require EFSA approval. A 2012 study by the European Association of Food and Drink SMEs (CEFIC) found that 62% of small and micro‑enterprises reported reduced product innovation after the NHCR came into full force.
