## What is it?

The EU Merger Regulation (ECMR) is the legal instrument that gives the European Commission exclusive authority to review mergers and acquisitions that meet certain turnover thresholds, primarily those with a "Community dimension." It requires companies to notify the Commission of qualifying transactions before they are implemented and allows the Commission to approve them conditionally, unconditionally, or block them outright.

The regulation established a "one-stop shop" principle: if a merger has sufficient cross-border impact within the EU, it falls under the Commission's sole jurisdiction, not the individual review of 27 national competition authorities. It set strict timelines (typically 25 working days for Phase I, 90 working days for Phase II) and introduced the "significant impediment to effective competition" (SIEC) test, which broadened the basis for intervention beyond the earlier "dominance" test.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the European Commission's near-total loss in court. In 2002, the Court of First Instance (now the General Court) annulled the Commission's prohibition of three major mergers in rapid succession: Airtours/First Choice (June 2002), Schneider Electric/Legrand (October 2002), and Tetra Laval/Sidel (October 2002). The court found the Commission's economic analysis was "vitiated by errors" in its assessment of coordinated effects and conglomerate effects. Mario Monti, then Competition Commissioner, faced a credibility crisis: the Commission had been overturned in 6 of 18 merger prohibition decisions since 1990, but never three in one year.

The structural tension was that the original 1989 Merger Regulation (Regulation 4064/89) had been designed for a 12-member Community dominated by manufacturing and heavy industry. By 2001, the EU was set to enlarge to 25 members, the economy had shifted toward services and technology, and the "dominance" test — which only allowed intervention if the merged entity would hold a dominant market position (typically over 40% market share) — was failing to capture anti-competitive effects in oligopolistic and vertically integrated markets. Cases like Boeing/McDonnell Douglas (1997) and GE/Honeywell (2001) had exposed the difficulty of applying the old test to complex global deals.

Coalition dynamics were tightly contested. The Commission's 2001 Green Paper on merger review received 120 responses. The business community, led by the European Round Table of Industrialists and major law firms, pushed for faster procedures and legal certainty. Large companies like Siemens and Deutsche Telekom argued the old regulation caused delays and jurisdictional uncertainty. National competition authorities, especially Germany's Bundeskartellamt and the UK's Office of Fair Trading, were split: some wanted to keep the dominance test, others wanted the new SIEC test that would allow reviews of oligopolistic coordination without requiring proof of dominance.

The decisive break came from two events. First, the Court of First Instance's triple annulment in 2002 was a political earthquake. As Monti later told the European Parliament in January 2003, the judgments "demonstrated that the current legal framework… no longer provides a sufficiently clear basis for the Commission's merger control decisions." Second, the impending 2004 enlargement was the Commission's strongest argument: 25 different national merger regimes would create chaos for cross-border deals. The Irish Presidency of the Council in early 2004 made final adoption a priority.

The alternative considered most seriously was a voluntary referral system rather than mandatory notification. The Commission's 2001 Green Paper debated this, but industry opposed it as creating uncertainty — they wanted a single, predictable system. The Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Peter Liese (EPP-ED, Germany), pushed for stronger due process rights for companies and victims of anti-competitive mergers.

The final regulation was adopted by the Council on 20 January 2004. The Council vote was unanimous, but it followed five years of debate within the Commission's Legal Service and Directorate-General for Competition. The European Parliament approved it by a vote of 474 in favour, 56 against, and 22 abstentions on 9 March 2004.

## Timeline

- **1989** — Original Merger Regulation (4064/89) adopted, effective 21 September 1990
- **1997** — Council adopts Regulation 1310/97, extending Commission jurisdiction to all "concentrations with a Community dimension"
- **2001** — Commission publishes Green Paper on merger review; 120 submissions received
- **June-October 2002** — Court of First Instance annuls Commission prohibitions in Airtours/First Choice, Schneider/Legrand, and Tetra Laval/Sidel
- **December 2002** — Commission publishes draft proposal for new regulation
- **January 2004** — Council unanimously adopts Regulation 139/2004
- **March 2004** — European Parliament endorses the regulation (474-56-22)
- **1 May 2004** — Regulation enters into force (coinciding with EU enlargement to 25 members)
- **2005** — First use of SIEC test to block a merger: Commission prohibits OVAG/VNG (gas market) — later withdrawn by parties before final decision
- **2007** — First use of SIEC test to block a merger after Phase II: Commission prohibits Ryanair/Aer Lingus (Irish air transport)
- **2014** — Commission publishes a White Paper reviewing the regulation's functioning; proposes changes for minority shareholdings
- **2021** — Commission adopts revised procedural rules and updated market definition guidance

## Impact and consequences

The regulation fundamentally changed how mergers are reviewed in the EU. Between its entry into force in 2004 and 2023, the Commission reviewed over 7,000 notified mergers. According to the Commission's 2021 annual report on competition policy, only about 1% of notified mergers (around 70) were blocked or forced to abandon after Commission intervention in that period, but many more were modified through remedies in Phase I or Phase II.

The SIEC test proved more effective in oligopolistic markets. In the first case applying it, the Commission's 2005 OVAG/VNG decision (later withdrawn by the parties) analysed coordinated effects in the German gas market. The test was used in high-profile cases: the 2007 Ryanair/Aer Lingus prohibition (where the Commission found the merged entity would have a dominant position on 22 routes, but also used SIEC to analyse the market for passengers not switching to other airlines), and the 2014 prohibition of Telefónica Deutschland/E-Plus (German mobile telecoms), which the Commission blocked because the merger would have reduced the market from four to three players, a classic SIEC scenario.

National authorities gained a stronger role through the referral system created by Article 9 and Article 22. Between 2004 and 2021, 550 requests for referral were made by member states. The system allowed smaller deals with local impact to be handled nationally while maintaining the one-stop shop for large ones. However, the Commission's 2014 White Paper noted that minority shareholdings not triggering full notification remained a "gap" in the system — a gap the Commission addressed through a 2021 initiative, but without amending the regulation itself, instead using existing Article 22 powers.

The regulation had significant unintended consequences. The tight timelines (25 working days for Phase I) meant the Commission's case teams were chronically under-resourced. The 2004 reform introduced a "pre-notification" phase allowing informal discussions with companies, which became crucial for complex deals. The Commission's Legal Service argued internally that the new SIEC test gave the Commission too much discretion, potentially inviting more litigation — a fear that proved unfounded: the number of appeals against merger decisions remained roughly stable compared to the pre-2004 period.

Companies adapted by hiring more Brussels-based merger control lawyers and by structuring deals to stay below the turnover thresholds — a practice known as "transaction engineering." The regulation also forced global convergence: countries from Brazil to South Africa adopted SIEC-style tests, and the ICN (International Competition Network) used the ECMR as a model for its recommended practices.

## Key questions answered

### Why didn't EU merger rules just keep using the old 'dominance' test?

The old dominance test prevented intervention against anti-competitive effects in oligopolistic markets that lacked a single dominant player. In economics-laden language, the Commission's 2001 Green Paper argued that the dominance test was "ill-suited to deal with oligopolistic market structures where collective dominance could be established without any single firm holding a dominant position." The SIEC test allowed the Commission to block mergers that would significantly impede competition even if no single firm would hold over 40% market share — for example, when merging two smaller firms in a four-player market would reduce competitive pressure.

### Why did those 2002 court rulings change how the EU reviews big company mergers?

These rulings were not just legal losses — they were institutional humiliations. The court found the Commission had engaged in "manifest errors of assessment" and had "adduced insufficient evidence" to meet the legal standard of proof. In the Airtours case, the court annulled the Commission's finding that the travel industry would see coordinated effects, saying the Commission had "failed to establish to the requisite legal standard" that coordination was likely. This forced Commissioner Monti to overhaul the entire merger review process, including creating a Chief Economist position in DG Competition (filled by Lars-Hendrik Röller in 2003) and strengthening the internal peer review system to ensure economic evidence met court standards.

### Why was 2004 the right time to update how Europe approves or blocks corporate takeovers?

Two factors converged: enlargement and the Monti crisis. The EU was to expand from 15 to 25 members on 1 May 2004. Each new member had its own merger control regime, creating fragmentation the Commission argued would cost businesses billions in legal fees and delays. Simultaneously, Monti needed a legislative win to restore DG Competition's credibility after the 2002 court losses. The Irish Council Presidency (January-June 2004) made merger control reform a priority, and the European Parliament was eager to finalise the reform before the European elections in June 2004. The regulation entered into force on the same day as enlargement — 1 May 2004 — symbolically linking the two.
