## What is it?

AFIR is the EU's first comprehensive regulation setting mandatory deployment targets for publicly accessible alternative fuels infrastructure. It replaces the 2014 Directive on the deployment of alternative fuels infrastructure (2014/94/EU), which relied on non-binding national targets and produced widely uneven results.

The regulation requires EU member states to ensure that by 2025, at least one public charging station for electric cars is installed for every 10 electric vehicles on their territory. By 2030, that ratio tightens to one station per eight vehicles. For heavy-duty trucks, the regulation mandates dedicated fast-charging hubs every 60 km along the Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) core network by 2025, and every 100 km on the comprehensive network by 2035.

Hydrogen refuelling stations must be deployed every 200 km along the TEN-T core network by 2030. The regulation also sets requirements for shore-side electricity at maritime ports and airports. Operators must provide transparent pricing, accept ad-hoc payments (via payment cards or contactless devices), and give real-time data on station availability through an open-access platform.

## Why was it introduced?

In February 2019, the European Commission's own evaluation of the 2014 Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Directive showed that only 145,000 public charging points existed across the entire EU — far short of the one million needed. The Netherlands alone accounted for one-third of them, while 12 member states had fewer than 500 charging stations each. The evaluation concluded that the directive's voluntary approach "has not delivered the necessary infrastructure for a mass market."

The structural tension was clear: the 2014 directive gave member states non-binding "national policy frameworks" that most treated as aspirational. At the same time, car manufacturers were launching electric vehicles at scale. Volkswagen announced in November 2019 its plans to invest €60 billion in electrification; by 2021, Tesla had delivered over 930,000 vehicles globally. Yet drivers in southern and eastern Europe faced a patchwork of charging deserts, while those in Scandinavia had dense networks. This chicken-and-egg problem threatened both consumer adoption and the EU's 2030 climate targets.

The political breakthrough came with the European Green Deal, announced by European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019. The 2030 Climate Target Plan, published in September 2020, set a legally binding 55% emissions reduction target for 2030. But the Commission's own impact assessment — published on 14 July 2021 as part of the "Fit for 55" package — calculated that achieving this target required 3 million public charging points by 2030, rising to 16 million by 2050. The 2014 directive, which had generated only 250,000 points by mid-2021, was clearly unfit.

The coalition for the regulation was unusually broad. The European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA) — representing BMW, Daimler, Volkswagen, and others — had been publicly calling for binding targets since 2018. In a March 2021 letter to the Commission, ACEA wrote that "the current voluntary approach is not enough. We need binding targets for infrastructure in line with the EU's climate ambitions." The electric vehicle charging industry — companies like Allego, E.ON, and Tesla — also pushed for mandates, as uncertainty deterred investors from building networks in laggard member states.

Opposition came from countries with low electric vehicle uptake. Poland, Romania, and Bulgaria argued that mandatory targets would force them to build expensive infrastructure that nobody would use. The European Parliament's transport committee, led by Italian MEP Ismail Ertug (S&D, initially rapporteur), struggled to reconcile these views. Ertug's first draft report, leaked in January 2022, proposed even stricter targets than the Commission's. After intense negotiations within Parliament — including pushback from EPP (centre-right) and Renew Europe groups — the final Parliament position, adopted on 19 October 2022 by 470 votes in favour, 75 against, and 106 abstentions, moderated some requirements but kept binding targets.

The final trilogue negotiations, concluded on 28 March 2023, produced a compromise: the European Parliament successfully pushed for binding targets on member states, while the Council (representing national governments) secured flexibility — including the ability for countries to reduce charging point requirements if they met maximum distance criteria for heavy-duty vehicles.

The final text was adopted by the Council on 25 July 2023. It entered into force on 13 October 2023, replacing the 2014 directive. Crucially, the 2023 regulation is directly applicable in all member states — meaning that a citizen or company can invoke it in national courts, unlike a directive which requires transposition into national law.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects the 27 EU member states plus Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein. Industry estimates from ChargeUp Europe, the industry association representing charging point operators, projected in June 2023 that AFIR's binding targets would unlock €20-30 billion in investment by 2030.

The most immediate consequence is the obligation on member states to create publicly accessible charging infrastructure. Germany, which had the highest number of public charging points (about 87,000 in 2023) still faced a shortfall under AFIR's ratio requirement. The Federal Ministry for Digital and Transport estimated in early 2024 that Germany needed to install an additional 1.5 million public charging points by 2030 to meet the regulation's targets.

Countries with low electric vehicle adoption faced the greatest challenge. Poland had only about 3,000 public charging points in 2023, yet its vehicle parc of electric cars was growing. The Polish Ministry of Climate and Environment warned in November 2023 that meeting AFIR's ratio requirement would require a tenfold increase in infrastructure within five years — a target that independent analysts considered unrealistic without significant EU funding.

For the hydrogen refuelling industry, the 200 km TEN-T target by 2030 represents both an opportunity and a risk. Hydrogen Mobility Europe, the industry consortium, stated in April 2023 that existing hydrogen refuelling stations across Europe operated at only 20-30% capacity. The new requirement could lead to stranded assets if hydrogen fuel cell vehicle adoption fails to materialise as expected.

A key legal innovation in AFIR is the obligation on charging point operators to accept ad-hoc payments — a direct response to consumer complaints that many stations required subscriptions or specific apps. The regulation also mandates real-time data sharing through the National Access Points, enabling third-party apps like PlugShare or ABRP to show accurate availability, which was previously impossible in many countries.

First enforcement actions are expected in late 2024 and 2025, when member states must submit their first progress reports to the Commission. The Commission has the power to launch infringement procedures against member states that fail to meet the requirements.

## Key questions answered

### Who pays for electric vehicle charging infrastructure — taxpayers or private companies?

The regulation imposes the obligation on member states to ensure infrastructure exists, but it does not dictate who builds or finances it. In practice, the charging station operators — companies like Ionity (a joint venture of BMW, Daimler, Ford, and Volkswagen), Allego, Fastned, and Tesla — build and operate the stations, with member states responsible for regulatory frameworks and, where necessary, subsidies for uneconomic locations. AFIR requires member states to report on their "national policy frameworks" every two years, including funding plans.

### Why do some EU countries have almost no public chargers while others are saturated?

The 2014 directive gave member states complete discretion over infrastructure spending, with no binding targets. Nordic countries like Norway and Sweden early on offered generous purchase subsidies for electric cars and invested in charging networks. Southern and eastern European countries either provided no subsidies or allocated them to other technologies (such as natural gas vehicles). AFIR changes this by setting binding ratios that apply to all member states, regardless of current adoption levels, though flexibility provisions allow countries with fewer electric vehicles to phase in the requirements more slowly.

### Can I pay with a credit card at any EU charging station?

Not yet, but AFIR requires all new charging stations installed after 13 April 2024 to accept ad-hoc payments via payment cards or contactless devices. Existing stations have until 13 October 2027 to comply. The regulation explicitly prohibits charging station operators from requiring a subscription or membership for access. This provision was a direct response to consumer complaints documented in the Commission's 2019 evaluation, which found that over 60% of public charging stations required a specific app or RFID card that only worked with a single operator's network.

### How does the EU enforce its targets for public electric vehicle chargers?

AFIR gives the European Commission the power to assess member states' progress through biennial reports. If a member state fails to meet the binding targets, the Commission can open infringement proceedings under Article 258 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, potentially leading to fines. The regulation also includes a review clause requiring the Commission to assess the regulation's effectiveness and propose amendments if necessary by 2027, with a particular focus on whether the heavy-duty charging and hydrogen refuelling targets are adequate.
