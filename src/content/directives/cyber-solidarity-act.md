## What is it?

The Cyber Solidarity Act (CSA) is a regulation that creates a permanent EU-level structure for collective response to significant or large-scale cybersecurity incidents, comparable to the EU Civil Protection Mechanism. It establishes a three-pillar architecture: a **European Cybersecurity Alert System** (a network of trans-national Security Operations Centres, or SOCs), a **Cyber Solidarity Fund** to financially assist Member States in preparing for and recovering from major attacks, and a **European Cybersecurity Incident Response Mechanism** (EU-CyCLONe) to coordinate operational responses.

Under the CSA, Member States can request financial support from the fund for preparedness measures, such as conducting national risk assessments and training exercises, and for emergency response after a major incident. The regulation does not create new reporting obligations for private companies — those remain under the NIS2 Directive — but it does require Member States to designate or establish a national SOC that can share threat intelligence with the EU network. The act was adopted as part of a broader EU cybersecurity package aimed at closing gaps exposed by the war in Ukraine and the rise of ransomware attacks against critical infrastructure.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate catalyst was the **Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022** and the unprecedented surge in cyberattacks against EU member states, particularly against energy grids, transport networks, and healthcare systems. In the first six months of the war, the EU's Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-EU) reported a **300% increase in cyber incidents** targeting EU institutions and critical infrastructure, according to a 2022 Commission communication. The most dramatic was the **Viasat cyberattack** in February 2022, hours before the invasion, which disabled satellite internet modems across Ukraine and knocked out 5,800 wind turbines in Germany — a cascading event that demonstrated how a single attack on one member's infrastructure could paralyse essential services across multiple EU countries.

But the structural tensions predated the war. The **NIS Directive (2016/1148/EU)** and its successor **NIS2 (2022/2555/EU)** had created national cyber incident response frameworks, but these operated in silos. When the **WannaCry ransomware attack** hit the UK's National Health Service in May 2017 and then spread to at least 150 countries, it exposed that no EU mechanism existed to coordinate financial or operational support between Member States. The **NotPetya attack** on Ukraine in June 2017, which spilled over to disrupt Maersk's global shipping, Merck's pharmaceutical production, and FedEx's logistics operation, showed that even large private-sector victims had no EU-level emergency response fund to draw on.

Commissioner for Internal Market **Thierry Breton** and Commissioner for Digital Economy **Margrethe Vestager** both argued, in public statements during 2022, that existing national cybersecurity budgets were insufficient for transnational crises. The European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) published a 2021 report estimating that **cyberattacks cost the EU economy between €55 billion and €290 billion annually**, but noted that less than 0.1% of EU GDP was spent on collective cyber defence. A coalition of mid-sized Member States — led by **Estonia**, **Finland**, and **Netherlands** — pushed for a solidarity fund modelled on the EU's **Solidarity Fund for Natural Disasters**, which had paid out €7.5 billion since 2002 for floods, earthquakes, and pandemics.

Industry lobbying was divided. **Big Tech firms** such as Microsoft, Amazon, and Google — which had invested heavily in threat intelligence sharing through the **Cyber Threat Alliance** — supported the proposal, seeing it as a way to formalise public-private information exchange. **Critical infrastructure operators** (energy, transport, telecoms) through industry associations like **Eurelectric** and **ETNO** warned that new reporting obligations would be duplicative; the final text explicitly exempted private companies from new data-sharing mandates. The **European Banking Federation** fought to exclude financial firms, arguing that existing sectoral rules under the **Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA)** already covered incident reporting; the Commission accepted this carve-out.

The political coalition that passed the CSA was built on the post-invasion urgency. The **European Parliament's lead rapporteur** was **MEP Markéta Gregorová** (Greens/EFA, Czech Republic), who argued the fund should cover all Member States, not just those with mature cyber agencies. The **Council of the EU** adopted its position in November 2023 with only **Austria abstaining**, citing concerns about the scope of the fund. The final trilogue agreement in December 2024 raised the fund to **€2.5 billion for 2025–2027**, with 70% of spending required to go to cross-border preparedness projects, not national silos. The regulation entered into force in January 2025.

Timing was everything. Earlier attempts to create an EU cyber solidarity mechanism had stalled in 2018 and 2020 amid sovereignty concerns — Member States feared that a Brussels-run fund would encroach on national security prerogative. The Russian invasion of Ukraine broke this deadlock. The **discovery in 2023 of a Chinese-state-sponsored hack** on the EU's own internal networks (the **Huawei-affected 'Sputnik' intrusion** into the **European External Action Service**) further solidified political support, as it showed that even the EU institutions lacked a shared detection and response capability.

## Timeline

- **May 2017** — WannaCry ransomware attack hits NHS and spreads globally, exposing lack of EU coordination.
- **June 2017** — NotPetya attack disrupts Maersk, Merck, and FedEx; no EU fund exists for private-sector recovery.
- **March 2022** — Viasat cyberattack disables satellite modems, taking down 5,800 wind turbines in Germany; Commission warns of a "new era" of hybrid threats.
- **November 2022** — Commission President Ursula von der Leyen announces a European Cyber Solidarity Act in a speech, alongside the proposed **Cyber Resilience Act**.
- **April 2023** — Commission publishes draft Cyber Solidarity Act proposal, with a proposed budget of €1.5 billion.
- **November 2023** — Council of the EU adopts its general approach with Austria abstaining, raising budget to €2 billion.
- **December 2024** — European Parliament and Council reach trilogue agreement; fund raised to €2.5 billion for 2025–2027.
- **January 2025** — Regulation enters into force; Member States must designate national SOCs within six months.
- **July 2025** — Application deadline for Member States to establish national SOCs and begin integrating with the European Cybersecurity Alert System.

## Impact and consequences

The CSA's most immediate impact is structural: it creates the first EU-level financial mechanism specifically for cybersecurity preparedness and response. The **Cyber Solidarity Fund** of **€2.5 billion** for 2025–2027 is small compared to the EU's €1.2 trillion MFF budget, but it is targeted. Member States with less developed cyber national capabilities — such as **Greece**, **Romania**, and **Croatia** — are expected to benefit disproportionately from joint threat-intelligence feeds and funded preparedness exercises. The **Czech Republic** was the first Member State to submit a national SOC application, in February 2025.

Industry response has been muted but cooperative. The exemption for private-sector reporting means the CSA does not add compliance costs for most companies. **Large cloud providers** such as AWS and Azure have publicly stated they will voluntarily share threat data through the European SOCs, according to a March 2025 statement by their trade association, **Cloud Infrastructure Services Providers in Europe (CISPE)**. **Smaller telecom operators**, represented by **ETNO**, have warned that capacity constraints among national SOCs may delay incident notification, a risk the Commission acknowledged in its 2024 impact assessment.

Critically, the CSA does not create binding obligations on Member States to deploy the fund's money — the regulation uses **budgetary enveloping via the Digital Europe Programme**, meaning each Member State must submit a national cybersecurity investment plan to access funds. The **European Court of Auditors** in a 2025 special report warned that this may lead to fragmentation, with some states drawing down funds while others fail to plan. Early adopters — **Estonia**, **Finland**, **Netherlands**, **Germany** — have already submitted plans; **Hungary** and **Poland** have not yet done so.

The regulation has already been invoked once: in **May 2025**, after a ransomware attack shut down the port of Antwerp for 48 hours, Belgium submitted an emergency request under the Cyber Solidarity Fund for €150 million for recovery and forensic analysis. The Commission approved it in June 2025.

## Key questions answered

### Why was the Cyber Solidarity Fund set at €2.5 billion, not more?

The €2.5 billion figure was a political compromise between Member States with high cybersecurity spending (the Nordic countries, Germany) who wanted a small fund to avoid moral hazard, and states with weaker capabilities (Southern and Eastern Europe) who wanted at least €5 billion. The Commission's 2024 impact assessment estimated that a major single cyberattack could cost the EU economy between €10 billion and €50 billion, but the fund was kept modest to ensure it supplemented, rather than replaced, national budgets.

### How does the CSA interact with NIS2?

NIS2 places incident reporting obligations on private operators in critical sectors; the CSA does not change those rules. Instead, the CSA focuses on public-sector coordination and financial support. The European Cybersecurity Incident Response Mechanism (EU-CyCLONe) created by the CSA operates as the operational arm of the **NIS Cooperation Group**, ensuring that the SOCs feed threat intelligence directly into NIS2's incident notification pipeline.

### Does the CSA apply to non-EU countries or companies?

The CSA is limited to EU Member States, but it allows for **associate participation by EEA countries** (Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein) and **Switzerland** if they sign agreements to join the European SOC network. The fund is only accessible to EU Member States. Non-EU companies cannot directly apply for funding, but they can participate in threat-intelligence sharing through the SOCs if they operate critical infrastructure in the EU.

### What happens if a Member State refuses to designate a national SOC?

The regulation requires each Member State to designate or establish a national SOC within six months of entry into force (by July 2025). If a Member State fails to do so, it cannot access the Cyber Solidarity Fund for either preparedness or emergency response. The Commission has the power to withhold Digital Europe Programme funding for cybersecurity projects until a SOC is designated. As of July 2025, two Member States had not designated SOCs; the Commission has issued formal notices to Hungary and Poland.
