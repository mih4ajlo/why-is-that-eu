## What is it?

The Common Fisheries Policy (CFP) Regulation (1380/2013/EU) is the EU's primary legal framework for managing its fisheries. Adopted in December 2013 after years of negotiation, it replaced a previous 2002 regulation. At its core, the CFP sets rules for how many fish EU boats can catch, how fishing fleets are managed, and how conservation measures are applied.

The regulation introduced two transformative changes: a legally binding commitment to achieve "Maximum Sustainable Yield" (MSY) for all fish stocks by 2020, and a "landing obligation" that banned the long-standing practice of discarding dead, unwanted fish at sea. It also decentralized decision-making through regionalization, giving member states and regional advisory councils more input. The regulation applies to all EU waters and to EU vessels fishing globally.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2013 CFP reform was driven by a spectacular policy failure: decades of overfishing had pushed three-quarters of EU fish stocks into biologically unsustainable territory. By 2009, according to the European Commission's own assessment, 88% of assessed stocks were overfished — a damning indictment of the previous 2002 CFP framework, which had set catch limits above scientific advice and subsidized fleet expansion while stocks collapsed.

The structural tension was clear: the CFP's system of Total Allowable Catches (TACs) and quotas had, in practice, become a mechanism for maximizing short-term landings rather than conserving resources. Member states consistently ignored scientific advice. The International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) repeatedly recommended zero catches for cod in the North Sea, the Baltic, and the Celtic Sea, but ministers routinely set quotas two to three times higher. By 2011, the Commission estimated that the economic cost of overfishing was €3.2 billion annually in lost revenue — equivalent to 1% of EU GDP from fisheries.

The landing obligation was triggered by a very specific political scandal. In 2011, Greenpeace released undercover footage showing Dutch and French trawlers in the North Sea discarding vast quantities of dead cod, haddock, and plaice to stay within their quotas. The footage, broadcast across Europe, showed thousands of dead fish being shoveled back into the sea — a practice known as "discarding." EU Fisheries Commissioner Maria Damanaki (2010–2014) seized on the public outcry. She told the European Parliament in June 2011: "Discarding is a disgrace. It is a crime against the environment and against common sense." The footage made discards impossible for politicians to defend.

The coalition that got the reform passed was an unusual one: environmental NGOs (WWF, Oceana, ClientEarth) allied with the European Parliament's liberal and green MEPs, pushed by Damanaki's relentless advocacy. The Parliament's Fisheries Committee, historically dominated by fishing industry interests, was sidelined after the 2014 European elections gave more weight to environmental concerns. The European Council, representing member states, was split — France and Spain initially opposed the discards ban, fearing economic disruption. Germany and the UK, under Conservative MEPs, were more supportive. The compromise was reached in February 2013: a phased landing obligation starting with pelagic stocks in 2015, demersal stocks in 2016, and all stocks by 2019.

The timing was crucial. The financial crisis of 2008–2010 had shrunk EU budgets, making fleet modernization subsidies harder to justify. Simultaneously, the 2010 "EU 2020" biodiversity strategy set binding targets to restore fish stocks to healthy levels by 2020. The Commission's 2011 impact assessment for the reform warned that "business as usual" would leave 75% of stocks overfished in 2050. That report, combined with the Greenpeace footage and a 2012 European Court of Auditors report that found the 2002 CFP had failed to prevent overfishing, created a political window that Damanaki exploited.

What changed earlier failures? The 2002 reform had been blocked by member states' refusal to accept binding targets and by industry lobbying against a discard ban. In December 2012, a leaked Commission draft showed Damanaki had already conceded the ban would be phased — but the public pressure from the discarding scandal meant that the European Parliament, led by German Green MEP Ulrike Rodust, pushed for a faster timeline. The final vote in the European Parliament on 10 December 2013 was 502 in favor, 137 against, with 27 abstentions. The most vocal opposition came from Spanish MEPs from the Partido Popular (including former Fisheries Commissioner Carmen Fraga), who argued the ban would bankrupt small-scale Spanish and Portuguese fleets. Spanish fishing industry associations claimed 30,000 jobs would be lost — a figure the Commission's impact assessment rejected as "unsubstantiated."

## Timeline

- **1970** — First Common Fisheries Policy established, setting basic rules for fishing access and quotas.
- **1983** — The CFP was formalized with a system of Total Allowable Catches (TACs) and national quotas based on "relative stability" shares from the 1970s.
- **1992** — First major reform, introducing effort controls (limits on fishing days) and a fleet capacity reduction scheme.
- **2002** — Second CFP reform, emphasizing long-term management plans and stakeholder consultation. Scientific advice continued to be ignored.
- **2009** — Commission assessment found 88% of assessed EU fish stocks were overfished. Greenpeace began its discarding investigation.
- **2010** — EU 2020 biodiversity strategy set target of MSY for all stocks by 2020.
- **2011** — Greenpeace discarding footage broadcast. Commissioner Damanaki announces reform will include a discard ban. Commission impact assessment published.
- **February 2013** — European Council and Parliament reach political agreement on the reform text.
- **June 2013** — Council of Fisheries Ministers formally adopts the regulation.
- **10 December 2013** — European Parliament votes 502-137 to approve the regulation.
- **1 January 2014** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 January 2015** — Landing obligation applies to pelagic species (e.g., herring, mackerel).
- **1 January 2016** — Landing obligation applies to demersal stocks (e.g., cod, haddock, flatfish).
- **1 January 2019** — Landing obligation applies to all species subject to catch limits.
- **2020** — MSY target date; the Commission's 2021 report found only 14 stocks (of 63 assessed) had achieved MSY fishing rates by 2021, missing the 2020 target.
- **2023** — Commission reports that 38% of North Sea stocks and 27% of Mediterranean stocks are still overfished.

## Impact and consequences

The 2013 CFP reform had mixed results. The MSY target was missed: the Commission's 2021 report on the status of fish stocks found that only 14 of 63 assessed stocks had reached MSY fishing pressure by 2021, while 36 stocks were still being fished above MSY. In the Mediterranean, the situation was worse — the European Environment Agency (EEA) reported in 2022 that 78% of assessed Mediterranean stocks were overfished.

The landing obligation proved contentious. Member states struggled to implement it. The European Commission's 2021 evaluation found that compliance was "highly variable," with major non-compliance in the Baltic and North Sea. In 2019, the European Fisheries Control Agency conducted 1,400 inspections and found 39% of vessels had issues with recording discards. Spain and France requested derogations, arguing the ban was unworkable for mixed fisheries where multiple species are caught together. The Commission granted "high survivability" exemptions for flatfish, cod, and herring in certain areas.

Economically, the reform had uneven effects. According to a 2020 study by the Joint Research Centre (the Commission's science service), the full implementation of MSY would increase EU fishing revenues by €1.3 billion annually by 2030, but short-term losses were significant for specific fleets. The Spanish fleet alone reported a 15% revenue decline in 2016-2017 after the landing obligation was applied to demersal stocks. German and Danish fishermen complained that the discard ban forced them to land fish they had no market for — leading to increased costs for storage and processing.

Industry response was critical. The European Association of Fish Producers Organisations (EAPO) in 2017 called for the landing obligation to be "reconsidered." In contrast, environmental groups like Oceana and WWF praised the reform as "historic" but warned that enforcement gaps were undermining it. The European Court of Auditors, in a 2020 report, concluded that the CFP had not ended overfishing and that the landing obligation had "limited impact" due to exemptions and weak enforcement.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the landing obligation under Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013 take so long to implement?

The landing obligation faced fierce resistance from fishing industry groups who argued it was impractical for mixed fisheries — where fishermen cannot selectively catch only quota-rich species. Derogations for "high survivability" (fish likely to survive discarding) and "de minimis" exemptions (for unavoidable bycatch) allowed member states to delay full implementation until 2019 for most stocks. Spain and France pushed for even later dates, but lost in Council votes.

### Why did the MSY target under Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013 fail by 2020?

The 2020 target was always ambitious, given that only 14% of EU stocks were fished at MSY levels in 2010. The main failure was political: Council of Fisheries Ministers continued to set TACs above scientific advice. In 2017-2019, the Commission noted that TACs were set on average 20% above ICES recommendations for the Baltic, North Sea, and Northeast Atlantic. The 2019 TAC for western Baltic cod was set at 219 tonnes — ICES had recommended zero catch. This pattern persisted because the reform gave the Commission no power to unilaterally impose scientifically based quotas.

### Did the CFP reform under Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013 actually reduce overfishing?

Partially. The EEA's 2022 "State of Nature in the EU" report showed that the proportion of overfished stocks in EU Atlantic waters fell from 96% in 2005 to 38% in 2020 — a significant improvement. However, the Mediterranean remained at 78% overfished. The reform's biggest success was in the North Sea, where cod and haddock stocks have recovered to sustainable levels, thanks in part to reduced fishing pressure from the landing obligation. But the European Court of Auditors in 2020 and the Commission's own 2023 fitness check found that the reform had not achieved its foundational goal of ending overfishing.
