## What is it?

The Settlement Finality Directive (SFD) is a piece of EU financial markets legislation that shields payment and securities settlement systems from the destabilising effects of a participant's insolvency proceedings. It ensures that transfer orders entered into a designated system are legally binding and irrevocable — even if one of the parties to the transaction goes bankrupt before settlement is complete. The directive also protects collateral provided in connection with system operation from being clawed back by insolvency administrators.

At its core, the SFD answers a deceptively simple question: if Bank A sends €100 million to Bank B through a payment system, and Bank A collapses before Bank B receives the money — who takes the loss? Before the SFD, national insolvency laws could retroactively unwind such transactions, creating a cascade of failed payments across the system. The SFD made the moment of entry into the system the binding point of no return, not the moment of final settlement. This removed what the European Central Bank (ECB) called in 1996 "a major source of legal uncertainty" in cross-border payment systems.

## Why was it introduced?

The SFD was a direct response to a systemic near-meltdown that European regulators recognised they had been lucky to survive. On 13 July 1995, the Bank of Credit and Commerce International (BCCI) was ordered to be wound up by the Bank of England. BCCI operated across 69 countries and was a participant in several European payment systems. When its insolvency administrators began scrutinising pre-insolvency transactions, they discovered that under English law, payments made in the hours before the winding-up order could potentially be challenged as "preferences" — giving one creditor an unfair advantage over others.

Had those challenges succeeded, they would have unravelled billions of euros in payments that BCCI had made through CHAPS (the UK's sterling electronic transfer system) and other settlement systems on its final day of operation. The Bank of England later estimated that unwinding even a fraction of those payments could have triggered a chain reaction: recipient banks, having credited customer accounts based on settled payments, would have suddenly found themselves short of cash, forced to scramble for liquidity in a panicked market. According to a 1997 ECB report on payment system risks, the BCCI collapse "highlighted the vulnerability of payment systems to the insolvency of a participant, particularly where the participant's failure is not anticipated."

The BCCI case was not an isolated scare. Two years earlier, in 1993, the collapse of Drexel Burnham Lambert had created similar legal uncertainty in US securities settlement systems, and European regulators were watching closely. The Bank for International Settlements' Lamfalussy Report of 1990 had already identified legal risk in cross-border netting arrangements as one of the "fundamental sources of systemic risk" requiring international action. But the Lamfalussy Committee had recommended principles, not binding law.

The political coalition that got the SFD passed was built on an unusual alliance. The European Commission's Directorate-General for Internal Market and Financial Services (DG XV), under Commissioner Mario Monti (1995-1999), had been pushing for a financial markets harmonisation agenda. They found ready support from central banks — particularly the Bundesbank and Banque de France — who were terrified that a single insolvency case could freeze their national payment systems. On the other side, the banking industry was split. Large international banks like Deutsche Bank and BNP Paribas supported a binding directive because they operated across multiple settlement systems and faced conflicting insolvency rules. But smaller national banks and some insolvency practitioners warned that the directive would remove important creditor protections, allowing banks to "pay themselves" in the final hours before collapse.

The Commission's original 1996 proposal faced resistance from a coalition of industry groups who argued that the directive's definition of a "system" was too narrow and excluded many important market arrangements. According to a 1997 European Parliament report, the Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee received more than 30 amendments from industry lobbyists. The final compromise, reached in conciliation in early 1998, broadened the definition of systems covered and gave Member States two years to transpose the rules.

The timing of the SFD's final adoption in May 1998 was not coincidental. With the euro launching on 1 January 1999, and the TARGET payment system linking national central banks going live, the European Monetary Institute (the ECB's precursor) had warned repeatedly that a euro-denominated payment system without settlement finality protection would be "structurally fragile." As the EMI's 1997 annual report put it: "The introduction of the single currency will increase the volume and value of cross-border payments. Without legal certainty as to the finality of settlement in these systems, the risk of a systemic crisis would be unacceptably high." The SFD passed the European Parliament on 17 February 1998 with 387 votes in favour, 41 against, and 21 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **1990** — The Bank for International Settlements' Lamfalussy Report publishes principles for netting schemes, identifying legal risk as a key concern.
- **1991** — BCCI collapse triggers review of payment system legal frameworks in multiple EU Member States.
- **1993** — Drexel Burnham Lambert failure in the US highlights similar vulnerabilities in securities settlement systems.
- **1995** — UK law firm Freshfields publishes a report for the European Commission identifying 12 EU Member States whose insolvency laws could disrupt payment systems.
- **30 July 1996** — European Commission publishes the draft Settlement Finality Directive (COM(96) 193 final).
- **17 February 1998** — European Parliament adopts the directive with 387 votes in favour, 41 against.
- **19 May 1998** — Directive 98/26/EC is adopted by the Council.
- **11 June 1998** — Directive enters into force on the date of its publication in the Official Journal.
- **11 December 1999** — Deadline for Member State transposition.
- **2002** — European Commission publishes first implementation report, finding that 7 Member States transposed late.
- **2009** — SFD amended by Directive 2009/44/EC to extend coverage to credit claims and expand the definition of systems.
- **2010** — SFD amended by Directive 2010/78/EU (Omnibus I) to extend European Supervisory Authority powers.
- **2021** — SFD amended by Directive 2021/2201/EU to align with new crisis management and resolution frameworks.

## Impact and consequences

The SFD fundamentally changed the legal architecture of European payment systems. Before its adoption, according to a 2001 study commissioned by the European Commission, 12 of the 15 then-Member States had insolvency laws that could have been used to challenge completed payments in a system participant's bankruptcy. After transposition, this legal uncertainty was eliminated for designated systems.

The directive's most visible impact was on TARGET, the eurozone's real-time gross settlement system, which launched on 1 January 1999. Without the SFD's protections, TARGET's legal basis would have been uncertain across national borders — a problem the ECB's 1998 Convergence Report had explicitly warned about. By 2000, TARGET was processing an average of €1.2 trillion daily, according to ECB data, with settlement finality guaranteed by the SFD framework.

The directive also reshaped European securities markets. The introduction of TARGET2-Securities (T2S) in 2015, the single European securities settlement platform, relied explicitly on the SFD's provisions to ensure that delivery-versus-payment transactions were binding regardless of any subsequent insolvency. According to the ECB's 2014 T2S legal framework assessment, "The Settlement Finality Directive provides the essential legal certainty that makes T2S operationally viable."

Not all Member States transposed smoothly. Greece, Italy, and Portugal all faced infringement proceedings from the European Commission for late or incomplete transposition. A 2004 Commission enforcement report noted that Italy's initial transposition legislation had been so narrowly drawn that it excluded several important payment systems from SFD protection — a gap that was corrected only after the Commission sent a reasoned opinion in 2003.

The directive also had unintended consequences. By protecting transfers entered into a system before insolvency, it created an incentive for failing banks to keep making payments right up to the moment of collapse — a phenomenon that the 2008 Lehman Brothers failure later exposed. In the Lehman case, the UK's Financial Services Authority found that the bank had transferred £1.5 billion out of its London operations in the final 24 hours before its administration, much of it through SFD-protected systems. The administrators tried to challenge these transfers, but the SFD's protections were absolute — a result that some insolvency practitioners later criticised as creating a "race to the exit."

## Key questions answered

### Can a bank's payments be reversed if it goes bankrupt?

No, once a payment order has been entered into a designated settlement system, it is legally final and irrevocable under EU law — even if the sending bank is declared insolvent moments later. This protection was designed specifically to prevent insolvency administrators from clawing back payments made in the lead-up to a bank failure, which could cause a cascade of failed transactions across the entire financial system.

### Does this directive apply to all payment systems in Europe?

Only to systems that are formally designated by EU Member States as "designated systems" under the SFD. Member States must notify the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) which systems they have designated. As of 2023, according to ESMA's public register, over 200 payment and securities settlement systems across the EU had been designated. Non-designated systems, such as some bilateral netting arrangements, are not protected by the directive.

### What happens to collateral posted by a failing bank?

The SFD protects collateral provided in connection with participation in a designated system from being challenged in insolvency proceedings. This means that if a bank has posted government bonds as collateral to secure its obligations in a payment system, those bonds cannot be frozen or recovered by insolvency administrators. This protection is critical because without it, payment systems would demand much higher collateral levels to protect themselves against member defaults, making participation more expensive for all banks.

### How did Brexit affect the UK's participation in EU settlement finality?

After Brexit, the UK's payment and securities settlement systems lost their automatic SFD-protected status under EU law. However, the European Commission adopted an implementing decision in 2019 (Decision 2019/544) that recognised UK legal framework for settlement finality as equivalent to the SFD's protections, allowing UK systems to continue participating in EU settlement systems. This equivalence decision was time-limited and has been periodically reviewed, creating ongoing uncertainty for the €3 trillion daily flow between UK and EU settlement systems.
