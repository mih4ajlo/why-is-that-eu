## What is it?

The Package Travel Directive (2015/2302/EU) is an EU law that governs how package holidays and linked travel arrangements are sold to consumers. It replaced the original 1990 Package Travel Directive, which had not anticipated the rise of online booking, dynamic packaging, and low-cost airlines.

The directive requires travel organisers to provide clear pre-contractual information, prohibits misleading pricing (all mandatory charges must be included upfront), grants consumers a 14-day right of withdrawal, and imposes strict liability on organisers for the performance of all travel services in the package. Crucially, it mandates insolvency protection so consumers are not stranded abroad or lose money if a tour operator goes bankrupt.

## Why was it introduced?

**The collapse of the tour operator Thomas Cook in 2019 was the single most dramatic event that exposed the directive’s effectiveness, but the law was actually adopted four years earlier, in 2015.**

The original 1990 Package Travel Directive (90/314/EEC) was written for an era when consumers bought package holidays from a physical travel agent, paid by cheque or cash, and received a paper brochure. By the mid-2000s, the market had transformed. European consumers increasingly booked flights, hotels, and car rentals separately online, often through comparison websites, or bought "dynamic packages" assembled by third-party platforms. The 1990 directive did not cover these arrangements, creating a regulatory gap.

The European Commission’s 2013 impact assessment found that the share of package holidays in the EU travel market had fallen from around 50% in the 1990s to roughly 30% by 2010, while "dynamic packaging" — where consumers combined separate elements themselves — had surged. The Commission estimated that 40% of all EU travel bookings fell outside the old directive's scope, leaving consumers without protection when things went wrong.

Industry lobbying was deeply divided. Traditional tour operators like TUI and Thomas Cook pushed for an expanded directive to cover online platforms, arguing that it would level the playing field. Low-cost airlines such as Ryanair and EasyJet opposed expansion, warning that classifying their websites as potential "package organisers" would impose huge compliance costs. The European Travel Agents' and Tour Operators' Association (ECTAA) favoured extension; the European Low Fares Airline Association (ELFAA) lobbied against it.

The proposal gained traction under Commissioner for Consumer Policy Neven Mimica (2014–2019), who framed the directive as part of the European Consumer Agenda. The European Parliament rapporteur was Birgit Collin-Langen (EPP, Germany), who pushed for strong insolvency protection and clear liability rules. The Parliament adopted the law on 12 April 2015 with 603 votes in favour, 25 against, and 25 abstentions. The Council of the EU followed on 26 June 2015.

The key innovation was the introduction of "linked travel arrangements" — services bought through separate contracts but facilitated by the same trader (e.g., clicking through from a flight booking to a hotel offer on the same website). This closed the gap without making every online aggregator a full package organiser. The directive also introduced a standardised "package travel information form" and required prices to include all mandatory charges from the start of the booking process.

## Timeline

- **1990** — Original Package Travel Directive (90/314/EEC) adopted
- **2008** — European Commission begins public consultation on revision
- **2013** — Commission publishes impact assessment finding 40% of travel bookings outside current scope
- **9 July 2013** — Commission proposes new Package Travel Directive
- **12 April 2015** — European Parliament adopts directive (603 votes in favour, 25 against)
- **26 June 2015** — Council of the EU adopts directive
- **1 January 2018** — Member states must transpose directive into national law
- **1 July 2018** — Directive applies in all member states
- **2019** — Thomas Cook collapses; insolvency protection mechanisms are tested across the EU
- **2022** — European Commission publishes first evaluation report, noting uneven implementation across member states

## Impact and consequences

The directive has had a mixed record. It succeeded in extending consumer protection to a wider range of bookings — by 2023, the Commission estimated that linked travel arrangements covered roughly 15% of all EU travel bookings, up from near-zero before 2018. European consumer organisations, such as BEUC, praised the mandatory insolvency protection and the requirement that prices include all mandatory charges.

However, the Thomas Cook collapse in September 2019, just 14 months after the directive applied, exposed significant weaknesses. While repatriation and refund systems worked in countries like Germany (where the Deutsche Reisesicherungsfonds had been established under the directive), consumers in other member states faced delays. The UK's Civil Aviation Authority (CAA) repatriated 140,000 Thomas Cook customers, but refund processing took months in some cases. The 2022 Commission evaluation noted that at least eight member states had been the subject of infringement proceedings for late or incorrect transposition.

Industry response varied. Major tour operators invested in compliance systems, while smaller travel agencies complained of increased administrative burden. The European tour operator association ECTAA reported in 2020 that compliance costs had risen by an estimated 10-15% for its members. Low-cost carriers like Ryanair successfully argued that their click-through bookings constituted "linked travel arrangements" rather than full packages, limiting their liability.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the Package Travel Directive cover "linked travel arrangements" and not just packages?

The Commission introduced linked travel arrangements in 2015 precisely to close the regulatory gap without imposing the full burden of package organiser liability on platforms and airlines. A linked travel arrangement occurs when a consumer books separate travel services through related online clicks (e.g., booking a flight and then being offered a hotel on the same site), but the trader does not "package" them. This lighter-touch category still requires insolvency protection but does not make the platform liable for the performance of the services. The compromise was driven by industry lobbying from comparison websites and low-cost airlines.

### When does a consumer have the right to cancel a package holiday without penalty under the Package Travel Directive?

Under Article 12, the consumer may cancel the package at any time before the start of the travel, but will pay an appropriate cancellation fee. The fee must not exceed the actual savings made by the organiser from re-selling the package. However, if there are "unavoidable and extraordinary circumstances" at the destination (e.g., a pandemic, natural disaster, or serious security issues), the consumer can cancel without paying any cancellation fee and receive a full refund. This provision was heavily tested during the COVID-19 pandemic, when travel organisers initially issued vouchers instead of cash refunds, leading the Commission to clarify that full cash refunds must be given within 14 days.

### Does the Package Travel Directive apply to business travel?

Generally, no. The directive applies to packages sold to "travellers" — defined in Article 3(6) as any person who seeks to conclude a package. This includes natural persons acting outside their trade, business, craft, or profession. However, member states may choose to extend the protections to business travellers if they wish. The Commission's 2022 evaluation found that some countries (e.g., France, Germany) had extended protections to small businesses, while most had not.

### How does the Package Travel Directive interact with EU air passenger rights (Regulation 261/2004)?

When a package includes a flight, both the Package Travel Directive and the air passenger rights regulation apply, but they cover different aspects. Air passenger rights (Regulation 261/2004) give the passenger rights against the airline for denied boarding, cancellation, or long delays (including fixed compensation amounts). The Package Travel Directive gives the consumer rights against the organiser for the entire package, including repatriation and refunds during insolvency. In practice, a consumer whose flight is cancelled can claim from both the airline (under Regulation 261) and the package organiser (under the directive), but cannot be compensated twice for the same loss.
