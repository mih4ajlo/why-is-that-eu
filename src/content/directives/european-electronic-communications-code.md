## What is it?

The European Electronic Communications Code (EECC) is a single, overarching directive that replaced the 2002 "Telecoms Package" — a set of six separate directives that had governed EU telecoms for over a decade. It entered into force on 20 December 2018, with member states required to transpose it into national law by 21 December 2020. The Code aims to create a single market for electronic communications, incentivise investment in next-generation networks (like 5G and fibre), and enhance consumer protections.

Key provisions include: harmonised rules for spectrum assignment (enabling faster 5G rollout); stronger consumer rights (e.g., maximum one-year contracts, price transparency, and easy switching); a "net neutrality" framework enshrined in the earlier 2015 regulation; and new obligations for over-the-top (OTT) services like WhatsApp and Skype to comply with certain security and emergency-call rules.

## Why was it introduced?

The EECC was driven by a structural tension: the 2002 framework was designed for a world of fixed-line phones and legacy telecom operators, but by the mid-2010s, the landscape had been transformed by mobile broadband, OTT services, and fragmented national spectrum markets. Investment in high-speed networks across the EU lagged behind the US and Asia — according to the Commission’s 2016 impact assessment, the EU was investing €19.7 billion less annually than needed to meet the Digital Agenda targets for 2020.

The political coalition that pushed the Code through was fragile. The **Juncker Commission** (2014–2019), under Digital Single Market chief **Andrus Ansip** and the Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society **Günther Oettinger**, made it a flagship of the Digital Single Market strategy. They were backed by large incumbent operators (Deutsche Telekom, Orange, Telefónica) who demanded lighter regulation to fund network investment, and by major tech companies (Facebook, Google) who wanted rules to curb the operators’ attempts to impose "sector-specific" fees on OTTs.

Opposition came from several fronts. National regulators (like France’s ARCEP and Germany’s BNetzA) feared losing flexibility on spectrum and market regulation. Consumer groups and net neutrality advocates warned that flexibility on "specialised services" could undermine the 2015 net neutrality rules. The European Parliament’s internal market committee (IMCO) passed the draft in March 2017 by a narrow 30-28 vote, reflecting deep divisions. The final text was adopted by the Council (where member states had clashed over spectrum coordination) and Parliament in a complicated trilogue process that stretched into December 2018.

Timing was critical. The EECC succeeded because of the broader push from the **Digital Single Market strategy** (announced in May 2015), which created a political window for an ambitious telecoms reform. Earlier attempts to merge the directives had stalled under the Barroso Commission, where national vetoes over spectrum and market regulation blocked progress. The 2016 **"Connectivity for a Competitive Digital Single Market"** proposal — part of the same package — laid the groundwork. A specific event that unblocked earlier deadlock on spectrum rules was the Commission’s threat to launch infringement proceedings against member states that hoarded spectrum (e.g., Germany in 2014), which forced a compromise on coordinated assignment deadlines.

## Timeline

- **2002** — Original "Telecoms Package" adopted: six directives (Framework, Authorisation, Access, Universal Service, Privacy, and Competition) establish the regulatory framework for e-communications.
- **2009** — Telecoms Reform Package updates the 2002 rules, introduces net neutrality provisions, and strengthens consumer rights.
- **May 2015** — Juncker Commission launches the Digital Single Market strategy, including a commitment to reform telecoms regulation.
- **September 2016** — Commission proposes the European Electronic Communications Code as a merger of four directives, alongside a "Connectivity" regulation for spectrum coordination.
- **March 2017** — European Parliament’s IMCO committee votes 30-28 in favour of the Code, after intense debate on net neutrality and OTT regulation.
- **December 2018** — Final text adopted by the Council (with Poland and Hungary voting against, and several abstentions) and Parliament; enters into force on 20 December.
- **21 December 2020** — Deadline for member states to transpose the EECC into national law. By mid-2021, the Commission had opened infringement procedures against 24 member states for late transposition.
- **2022-2023** — Commission enforces compliance: fines or threats of fines against Belgium, Cyprus, Greece, and others for failing to implement net neutrality or emergency-call rules under the EECC.

## Impact and consequences

The EECC’s impact has been uneven. On consumer rights, it standardised contract durations (maximum 24 months, reduced to 12 months from May 2022 under a later amendment), introduced mandatory price-comparison tools, and simplified switching processes. However, many member states — including Germany, France, and Italy — were late in transposing the rules, leading to Commission infringement proceedings. By 2023, the Commission had issued reasoned opinions against 16 member states, and referred Belgium to the European Court of Justice for non-compliance.

On 5G rollout, the harmonised spectrum rules helped accelerate assignments: by December 2020, the Commission’s 5G Observatory reported that all member states had assigned the 3.6 GHz band, though the high costs of spectrum auctions in some countries (e.g., Germany’s €6.5 billion auction in 2019) drew criticism. The EECC also required member states to allow spectrum trading and leasing, which some national regulators resisted.

The most controversial area was OTT regulation. The Code required services like WhatsApp and Skype to implement security measures and enable access to emergency services (eCall for voice calls, SMS for text). This was heavily resisted by tech firms, who argued it was disproportionate. By 2023, only a handful of member states had enforced these provisions — the UK (now outside the EU) was more aggressive in its own parallel "Online Safety Bill."

Investment in high-speed networks improved marginally. The Commission’s 2022 Digital Economy and Society Index (DESI) showed that fibre-to-the-premises coverage rose from 20% in 2015 to 56% in 2021, but this was driven more by national broadband plans and Recovery Fund money than the Code alone.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take so long for Europe to update its telecom rules?

Earlier attempts under the Barroso Commission failed because of national vetoes on spectrum policy and regulatory flexibility. The Juncker Commission’s Digital Single Market strategy (2015) provided a political umbrella, but final approval in 2018 required painful compromises: net neutrality safeguards (from the 2015 regulation) were mostly preserved, while operators got lighter market-analysis rules for fibre investments.

### Why are internet messaging and calling apps covered by EU telecom regulations?

The Commission argued that caller-ID and emergency-call rules should apply consistently to voice services, regardless of delivery technology. Operators (Deutsche Telekom, Orange) lobbied hard for this, claiming OTT competitors had an unfair advantage if they avoided regulatory costs. The final rules require OTTs to support emergency-call access and security standards, but exempt two-way messaging services.

### What did the new EU telecom rules decide about net neutrality?

The EECC mostly codified the 2015 Open Internet Regulation (2015/2120/EU) — net neutrality rules that banned blocking, throttling, and paid prioritisation of traffic. It introduced a new provision allowing "specialised services" (like 5G network slicing for autonomous cars) if they do not degrade general internet quality. Critics feared this would create a two-tier internet, but by 2023, no major violations had been reported under this provision.

### Why did some EU countries take years to put the new telecom rules into their own laws?

National regulators often lacked political support from their governments — or faced legal hurdles. For example, Germany delayed transposition until June 2021 because of a dispute with the Bundesnetzagentur over spectrum fees; Belgium missed the deadline because of the federal-state division of telecoms competences. The Commission opened infringement cases against 24 member states by mid-2021, with several (including Belgium and Greece) still in the pipeline by 2023.
