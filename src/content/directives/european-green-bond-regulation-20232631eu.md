## What is it?

The European Green Bond Regulation, adopted in November 2023, establishes a voluntary standard for bonds marketed as "European Green Bonds" (EuGB). Any issuer — whether EU-based or from a third country — can use the label if they commit that all proceeds from the bond finance projects fully aligned with the EU Taxonomy for sustainable activities. This is a stricter requirement than existing market standards like the Climate Bonds Initiative or the ICMA Green Bond Principles, which typically allow 90-95% green allocation.

Under the regulation, issuers must publish a "Green Bond Factsheet" before the bond is issued, undergo mandatory external review by registered reviewers, and submit to supervision by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA). After the bond's maturity, an "allocation report" must show that all funds went to taxonomy-aligned projects. If an issuer cannot immediately identify taxonomy-aligned projects for 100% of proceeds, the regulation allows "flexibility" provisions: up to 15% of proceeds can finance economic activities that meet the Taxonomy's "do no significant harm" criteria but are still awaiting the full technical screening criteria to be adopted.

The regulation is part of the broader EU Sustainable Finance Action Plan, first announced by the European Commission in March 2018. It entered into force on 20 December 2023, and will apply from 21 December 2024 for most provisions, with flexibility provisions applying from 21 June 2025.

## Why was it introduced?

The opening shot came not from Brussels but from a May 2021 report by the European Court of Auditors (ECA). The auditors found that voluntary green bond standards across the EU were "fragmented and inconsistent," with no legal requirement that a bond labeled "green" actually finance environmentally sustainable activities. The ECA's special report documented that among the €200 billion in green bonds issued in the EU in 2020, some issuers had classified fossil fuel infrastructure or coal-plant retrofits as "green". There was no single regulator checking the claims.

The structural tension was clear: the green bond market was booming — the Climate Bonds Initiative estimated global issuance at $269.5 billion in 2020 — but the EU's own Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR) and the Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852/EU) had created a regulatory floor for funds and companies while leaving the bond market in a legal vacuum. Banks like BNP Paribas and Société Générale had been calling for a mandatory standard since 2019, fearing that "greenwashing" bond issuances by competitors — particularly from fossil fuel companies — would undermine trust in the entire market. The European Investment Bank (EIB), which had issued the world's first "climate awareness bond" in 2007, pressured the Commission for uniformity: different member states had different definitions of "green", making cross-border investment comparisons impossible.

The coalition that got this passed was unusual. Environmental NGOs (WWF, ClientEarth, Finance Watch) wanted a mandatory 100% taxonomy alignment to prevent "green-hushing" — issuers quietly relabeling brown assets. Investor associations (Eurosif, UN PRI) pushed for the same, wanting clear liability rules. The banking industry split: the European Banking Federation (EBF) supported the mandatory standard for large issuers but opposed applying it to small and medium-sized issuers. The German banking association, Bundesverband deutscher Banken, warned that a strict 100% rule would force many regional banks out of the green bond market entirely.

The timing was decisive. In January 2022, the EU's Platform on Sustainable Finance — the expert group advising the Commission on the Taxonomy — published a warning that "greenwashing is emerging as a systemic risk" in the bond market. This coincided with the Commission's push for the European Green Deal investment plan, which estimated that €1 trillion in sustainable investments would be needed by 2030. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment calculated that the label could "mobilise an additional €100-150 billion in green bond issuance annually by 2025." A failed green bond standard would have made the Green Deal's financing gap politically unsustainable.

Opposition was real but contained by a crucial concession. The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON) initially proposed a mandatory standard for all bonds marketed as "green" — a position that would have overridden existing national labels like France's "Greenfin" label and the German "Green Bond" standard. France's Autorité des Marchés Financiers (AMF) and Germany's BaFin both lobbied heavily against this, arguing it would create a two-tier market hurting their domestic industries. The compromise was struck in February 2023: the EuGB label remained voluntary, but any bond not using it could still be called "green" subject to existing national rules — a decision that left the UK's Financial Conduct Authority and the US SEC fuming, as it created no real cross-border harmonization.

The final vote in the European Parliament was 418-67, with 86 abstentions. The Green Party opposed the flexibility provisions, arguing they allowed non-taxonomy-aligned activities for two years. The far-right Identity and Democracy group voted against the entire regulation, claiming it would "burden corporate Europe with red tape." The regulation was formally adopted by the Council on 28 November 2023, and published in the Official Journal on 19 December 2023.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate effect is jurisdictional: ESMA now directly supervises external reviewers of green bonds, a role previously performed by national competent authorities. As of April 2024, ESMA has registered four external reviewers under the regulation — including DNV GL, S&P Global Ratings, and Moody's — and is processing applications from a further 12 firms. Any external reviewer that issues a misleading opinion faces administrative fines of up to €500,000 under the regulation, with ESMA empowered to impose periodic penalty payments of €20,000 per day for non-compliance.

For issuers, the cost of compliance is significant. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment estimated that the additional due diligence and reporting costs would range from €100,000 for a simple one-off bond to €5 million for a large, multi-issue framework. The European Issuers association warned that this would "disproportionately affect smaller issuers and reduce the number of green bonds in the EU market." By contrast, the large banks that supported the regulation — such as Crédit Agricole, which issued €2.5 billion in green bonds in 2022 alone — saw the costs as manageable and the reputational benefit as outweighing them.

The regulatory environment has been complicated by the ongoing controversy over the Taxonomy's inclusion of nuclear energy and natural gas. In December 2022, the Platform on Sustainable Finance's experts warned the Commission that including nuclear would "severely damage the credibility of the entire Taxonomy and, by extension, the green bond standard." Despite this, the Commission's Delegated Act adding nuclear and gas to the Taxonomy was published in March 2023. This means that a EuGB-labeled bond could theoretically finance a new nuclear power plant or a gas-fired plant, provided it meets the Taxonomy's strict emissions and safety criteria. The German government, which had opposed nuclear inclusion, warned that this "undermines the integrity" of the label.

The impact on market growth is still unclear. The Climate Bonds Initiative reported that EU-labelled green bond issuance reached €135 billion in 2023, but only a small fraction — approximately €12 billion — was expected to qualify for the new EuGB label in 2024, according to an April 2024 analysis by Natixis. The Commission's own monitoring report from January 2024 acknowledged that "uptake may be initially limited due to the strict 100% taxonomy alignment requirement."

A significant precedent was set in March 2024 when the French energy company Engie issued the first bond explicitly marketed as "EuGB-ready," committing to align its internal reporting with the regulation's framework before the full application date. This was seen as a signal that large energy utilities — often the most exposed to greenwashing litigation — see the label as a risk-mitigation tool.

## Key questions answered

### Can companies label their bonds as "green" without this standard?

Yes — the EuGB label is voluntary. Companies can still issue bonds called "green" under national laws or market standards like the ICMA Green Bond Principles. But those bonds cannot be called "European Green Bonds" or use the EuGB logo. The regulation only applies if a company chooses to use the label.

### Why did Europe need a specific law for green bonds?

Because the green bond market had grown to over €200 billion in the EU by 2020 with no consistent definition of what counted as "green." The European Court of Auditors found that some issuers were labelling bonds as green while financing fossil fuel projects. Banks and investors wanted a single, legally enforceable standard so they could compare bonds across countries and be confident the money went to actual environmental projects.

### Is nuclear power allowed under the EU Green Bond label?

Yes, but only in limited circumstances. The regulation defines "taxonomy-aligned" by reference to the EU Taxonomy Regulation, which includes nuclear and natural gas activities under strict conditions. A EuGB bond could finance a nuclear power plant that meets the Taxonomy's emissions and safety criteria, but not conventional fossil fuel infrastructure. This has been highly controversial among environmental groups.

### What happens if an issuer misuses the EuGB label?

ESMA can fine the issuer up to 10% of the bond's face value for intentionally misusing the label. For external reviewers that certify the bonds, ESMA can impose fines of up to €500,000 and withdraw their registration entirely. The regulation also grants investors the right to claim damages if they relied on a false EuGB certification when buying a bond.
