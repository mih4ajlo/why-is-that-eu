## What is it?

The Payment Accounts Directive (PAD) aims to make bank accounts more consumer-friendly across the European Union. It has three main pillars: first, it requires banks to provide a standardised fee information document (FID) to consumers before opening an account, listing the main services and their charges in a common format. Second, it obliges banks to offer a free or low-cost "basic payment account" to any consumer legally resident in the EU, regardless of their financial history. Third, it simplifies the process for switching a payment account from one bank to another, including transferring recurring payments.

The directive sets a single, standardised template for the FID and a glossary of common terms defined across all EU languages, ensuring consumers can compare offers across borders. It entered into application in September 2016, giving Member States two years to transpose it into national law.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to the 2008 financial crisis, which exposed how millions of EU citizens were overpaying for basic banking services or excluded from them entirely. In 2012, the European Commission's Directorate-General for Health and Consumers conducted a consumer market study that found 82% of consumers did not know the total cost of their payment accounts, while 27% had never switched accounts in their life. The Commission's 2013 impact assessment estimated that EU consumers were overpaying between €2.5 and €5 billion annually due to opaque and untransparent fee structures.

The structural tension was clear: despite the single market for goods and services, payment accounts remained almost entirely national markets. Each bank marketed fees differently, used inconsistent terminology, and switching a current account across borders — or even within the same country — was a cumbersome, weeks-long process involving multiple visits and forms. This created a "consumer inertia" that banks exploited to maintain high margins.

The coalition driving the legislation was led by the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) and Commissioner for Internal Market and Services Michel Barnier, who had made consumer protection in financial services a signature issue following the crisis. Barnier declared publicly in 2013 that "consumers need clear, comparable information on fees and must be able to shop around for the best deal." The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, chaired by MEP Sharon Bowles (ALDE, UK), pushed for even stronger provisions, including mandatory fee caps and portability of account numbers.

Opposition came from the European Banking Federation (EBF), which argued that standardised fee disclosure would be costly to implement and that basic accounts would increase fraud risk. In 2013, the EBF published a study claiming that the directive would cost banks €1.8 billion to implement across the EU. Several Member States, particularly Germany and Austria, resisted the basic account requirement, fearing it would attract undocumented migrants and red tape. The Commission pressurised Germany by releasing a 2012 Eurobarometer survey showing that 58 million EU citizens aged over 18 did not have a payment account, with 30 million of those in Germany alone.

The trigger that broke the deadlock was the Eurozone crisis and the rise of social exclusion across southern Europe. In 2012, over 7% of Greek households had no bank account, while in Italy the figure was 9%, according to a World Bank report cited by the Commission. The crisis-era spectacle of pensioners queuing at bank counters for pitiful sums — unable to receive their pensions electronically because they lacked an account — made financial inclusion a moral imperative for the Juncker Commission's social agenda.

## Timeline

- **2007** — The Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) regulation enters force, harmonising credit transfers and direct debits but leaving account opening fees and consumer rights untouched.
- **2011** — The European Commission launches a public consultation on payment accounts, receiving over 4,000 responses — the highest ever for a financial services consultation at the time.
- **8 May 2013** — Commissioner Michel Barnier proposes the Payment Accounts Directive, including the three pillars: fee transparency, switching, and basic accounts.
- **15 April 2014** — The European Parliament adopts the directive in a plenary vote of 611 in favour, 24 against, with 31 abstentions. The EPP and S&D groups provide the core coalition.
- **23 July 2014** — The Council of the EU formally adopts the directive without further amendment.
- **28 September 2014** — The directive is published in the Official Journal and enters into force.
- **18 September 2016** — Member States must transpose the directive into national law.
- **2019** — The Commission publishes its first implementation report, finding that 21 Member States have fully transposed the directive, while four (Belgium, Greece, Portugal, Spain) have partial compliance on the FID template.

## Impact and consequences

By 2019, the Commission reported that 11 million basic accounts had been opened across the EU since the directive's application date, according to national data compiled by the Commission's 2019 implementation report. The number varied widely: France had opened over 2.5 million basic accounts, while Sweden reported just 12,000. The FID template was adopted by all Member States, and the Commission found that 73% of consumers surveyed in 2018 found the document "easy or very easy to understand" — up from 22% for previous fee sheets.

The switching service proved less popular. The 2019 report found that only 5% of consumers used the switching service in the first two years, with most preferring to open a new account entirely rather than transfer an old one. Critics, including BEUC, noted that the directive did not require banks to offer account number portability (like mobile phone numbers), which limited switching's appeal.

The directive also encountered resistance in practice: a 2017 compliance check by the Commission found that 14 banks across 8 Member States were charging more than the "reasonable" fee allowed for basic accounts. The Commission threatened infringement proceedings against Italy, Spain, and Poland in 2018 over excessive fees, leading to adjustments. The German banking association (BdB) launched a legal challenge to the basic account requirement in 2016, arguing it violated property rights; the case was referred to the European Court of Justice in 2017 and is still pending at the time of writing.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU regulate bank account fees instead of leaving it to market competition?

Despite the single market, bank fees were opaque and incomparable across borders because banks used different terminology, service bundles, and pricing methods. A 2012 Commission study showed that consumers could save up to €100 annually by switching accounts, but 82% never tried because the cost in time and complexity was too high. The directive aimed to create genuine price competition by making comparison simple — essentially, the same logic that underpins unit pricing in supermarkets.

### Why are basic payment accounts required?

The 2008 financial crisis and subsequent austerity drove millions of EU citizens into financial exclusion, particularly the unemployed, migrants, and low-income workers. Without an account, people could not receive wages, benefits, or pensions electronically, forcing them to use costly cash-handling services or loan sharks. The Commission estimated that 30 million EU adults had no payment account at all in 2012, making participation in the modern economy impossible. The basic account requirement is explicitly tied to the EU's fundamental right to access essential services under the European Pillar of Social Rights (proclaimed in 2017).

### Why did Germany oppose the basic account provision?

German banks and the federal government argued that a mandatory basic account for all legal residents would increase fraud risk and administrative burden, and that existing social banking arrangements were sufficient. The German Banking Association (BdB) claimed that the directive would cost banks €400 million annually in Germany alone. However, the Commission's impact assessment countered that the cost of basic accounts was minimal — estimated at €10-30 per customer per year — and that many Member States (France, Finland, Sweden) already had similar schemes without problems. The ECJ case challenging the directive's basic account requirement remains pending.
