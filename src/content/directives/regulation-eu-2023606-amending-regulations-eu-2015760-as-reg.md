## What is it?

The 2023 revision of the ELTIF regulation removes many of the restrictions that made the original 2015 ELTIF product unattractive to fund managers and inaccessible to ordinary retail investors. An ELTIF is a type of investment fund that must invest at least 55% of its capital in long-term, illiquid assets — such as infrastructure projects, private equity, real estate, or unlisted companies — that are typically locked up for years. The reform lowers the minimum investment threshold from €10,000 to €500, eliminates the requirement that retail investors hold more than 10% of their portfolio in ELTIFs, and allows funds to use leverage (borrowing) up to 50% of net asset value.

The core change is making ELTIFs a "packaged retail investment product" subject to the simpler PRIIPs key information document rules, rather than the more complex MiFID II suitability requirements. This effectively opens the product to mass-market distribution through banks, insurance companies, and online platforms — not just wealth management advisers. The regulation also expands the list of eligible assets to include securitised loans, venture capital-backed companies, and green bonds, and reduces the fund's redemption window from 12 months to six months.

## Why was it introduced?

The ELTIF framework was a flagship of the Capital Markets Union (CMU) project launched by the Juncker Commission in 2015, but by 2021 it had become an embarrassment. According to the Commission's own 2021 review, only 57 ELTIFs had been launched across the entire EU, managing roughly €2.4 billion in assets — a fraction of the €2 trillion in European infrastructure investment needs the Commission had projected. The original regulation's retail protections — a €10,000 minimum investment and a 10% portfolio cap — were so restrictive that most European households with savings of €50,000 could not legally invest at all. The product had been designed by regulators to prevent retail investors from over-concentrating in illiquid assets, but the effect was to exclude them entirely.

The structural tension was clear: the EU needed to finance its Green Deal and digital transition — estimated by the Commission at €620 billion annually — but the traditional banking sector was retreating from long-term lending after Basel III capital requirements. Meanwhile, European households held over €10 trillion in bank deposits yielding near-zero interest. The existing regulatory framework made it impossible for fund managers to offer a liquid, professionally managed vehicle that could bridge this gap. The ELTIF's failure was not just a market disappointment; it was a CMU credibility crisis.

The coalition that pushed the reform through was unusually broad. On one side, the European Fund and Asset Management Association (EFAMA) and the Association for Financial Markets in Europe (AFME) lobbied heavily, arguing that the original rules made the product "structurally unviable" — as AFME put it in a 2021 position paper. On the other side, the European Commission's Directorate-General for Financial Stability (DG FISMA) under Commissioner Mairead McGuinness needed a visible CMU success before the 2024 EU elections. The European Parliament's rapporteur, German MEP Sven Giegold (Greens/EFA), who had been a critic of deregulation, supported the reform on the condition that strict investor protection rules around liquidity management and redemption windows were maintained.

The key opposition came from consumer protection organisations. Finance Watch warned in a 2022 briefing that lowering the minimum investment to €500 and removing the portfolio cap would trap retail investors in illiquid funds, arguing that "retail investors do not understand the liquidity mismatch between daily pricing and multi-year lock-ups." The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) called the reform "a step backward" for investor protection. However, these groups failed to block it: the vote in the European Council was unanimous, and in the European Parliament, the final text passed 537-24 with 36 abstentions. The only source of real resistance was the German socialist (SPD) MEP Birgit Sippel, who argued in committee that the reform would "return to the culture of misselling complex products to unsophisticated investors."

The timing was decisive. The reform was bundled with the 2021 review of the Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive (AIFMD) and was accelerated by the post-COVID recovery fund (NextGenerationEU), which explicitly called for private capital to co-finance green and digital projects. The Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 added an energy security rationale: ELTIFs that financed European renewable energy infrastructure became politically attractive. The Commission published its proposal on 25 November 2021; political agreement was reached in July 2022; the regulation entered into force on 9 January 2023.

What changed from the failed earlier approach was a fundamental assumption shift. The 2015 regulation had treated retail investors as incapable of understanding illiquid assets, imposing strict portfolio caps and high minimums. The 2023 reform inverted this logic: if funds are properly authorised and supervised under AIFMD, and if clear redemption rules are disclosed in simplified PRIIPs documents, then retail investors can make their own choices. The EU's co-legislators accepted the fund management industry's argument that the real risk to retail investors was not illiquidity but the vacuum of suitable long-term savings products — leaving them in cash deposits losing value to inflation.

## Timeline

- **April 2015** — Original ELTIF Regulation (2015/760/EU) enters into force, setting €10,000 minimum investment and 10% portfolio cap for retail investors.
- **September 2020** — European Commission launches a review and public consultation on ELTIFs, receiving 48 responses from fund managers, consumer groups, and trade associations.
- **October 2021** — The Commission's review report is published, noting that only 57 ELTIFs exist with €2.4 billion in assets under management.
- **25 November 2021** — Commission publishes its legislative proposal to amend the ELTIF regulation, along with an impact assessment.
- **9 March 2022** — The European Council (Council of the EU) adopts its general approach on the proposal.
- **14 July 2022** — The European Parliament's Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs (ECON) adopts the text; MEP Sven Giegold leads negotiations.
- **October 2022** — Political agreement reached between Parliament, Council, and Commission after trilogue negotiations.
- **14 December 2022** — The European Parliament formally adopts the regulation (537-24 vote).
- **3 January 2023** — The European Council adopts the regulation.
- **9 January 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/606 enters into force, with most provisions applying from 10 January 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was a surge in ELTIF launches. According to data from Morningstar, the number of ELTIFs more than doubled from 57 in 2021 to over 130 by mid-2024, with assets under management rising from €2.4 billion to approximately €15 billion. The French asset manager Amundi launched the largest single ELTIF, a €1.5 billion fund focused on European infrastructure and digital assets, distributed through French retail bank networks. The German investment platform Deutsche Börse's Clearstream began offering ELTIFs as part of its fund distribution platform in 2024.

However, uptake has been geographically uneven. According to a 2024 report by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA), 70% of all ELTIFs are domiciled in Luxembourg and Ireland, while Southern and Eastern European countries — Italy, Spain, Poland — have seen fewer launches. ESMA also noted distribution channel bottlenecks: many retail banks and insurance companies initially refused to sell ELTIFs because their internal compliance systems treated the 10 January 2024 application date as requiring new risk-assessments under MiFID II, even though the regulation explicitly exempts ELTIFs from MiFID II suitability rules.

Consumer concerns have partly materialised. In 2024, UK-based fund manager Apex Group issued a warning that some ELTIFs were marketing themselves as "sustainable" under SFDR Article 8 or 9 without meeting the underlying green criteria, creating greenwashing risks. The European Commission has flagged this in its 2025 review of the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation.

The single most consequential outcome has been the shift in industry behaviour: large institutional asset managers — BlackRock, Schroders, Allianz Global Investors — have launched ELTIFs primarily targeting retail distribution through digital platforms. By contrast, the original 2015 ELTIF was almost exclusively a B2B product sold to institutional investors. The reform has effectively created a new retail asset class.

## Key questions answered

### Can ordinary people now invest directly in European infrastructure projects?

Yes, with as little as €500. The 2023 update abolished the €10,000 minimum investment and removed the rule that retail investors could not put more than 10% of their portfolio into ELTIFs. A retail investor can now buy ELTIF units through their bank, an online broker, or an insurance-based investment product. However, the fund still has a mandatory lock-up period — typically 5 to 10 years — and early redemption is limited to twice per year, so the investment is genuinely long-term and illiquid.

### Why did almost no one use the original 2015 'long-term investment fund'?

The original ELTIF was a regulatory failure. It set a €10,000 minimum investment that excluded most European households — only about 6% of EU households had portfolios large enough to meet the rule. It also required a complex suitability assessment by a financial adviser (MiFID II), which most banks refused to offer for a product with such low uptake. Fund managers found the list of eligible assets too restrictive — for example, it excluded green bonds, securitised loans, and venture capital-backed companies — and the prohibition on leverage made returns uncompetitive compared to private equity.

### Is it safe for a pension or retirement saver?

ELTIFs are riskier than bank deposits or government bonds because the underlying assets — infrastructure, private equity, unlisted companies — are illiquid and difficult to value daily. The regulation requires funds to have a clearly stated redemption policy and to hold liquidity buffers, but there is no deposit guarantee scheme (unlike bank accounts up to €100,000 under the Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive). ESMA has warned that retail investors should only invest money they can afford to lock away for at least five years.
