## What is it?

The 2019 Directive amends the EU’s Company Law Directive (2017/1132/EU) to create a uniform legal procedure for three types of corporate restructuring that cross EU borders: **conversion** (a company changes its registered office and governing law without winding up), **merger** (two or more companies from different Member States combine), and **division** (a company splits into two or more entities, possibly in different Member States).

Before this directive, cross-border mergers had EU-level rules since 2005, but conversions and divisions had no harmonised framework. Companies attempting either had to navigate wildly divergent national laws, often forcing liquidation and reincorporation — a costly, uncertain process. The directive introduces standardised plans, independent expert reports, creditor safeguards, employee participation rights, and a mandatory abuse check by national authorities.

## Why was it introduced?

**Polbud**, a small Polish construction company with six shareholders, set off the chain reaction that broke the legislative logjam. In 2011, Polbud converted under Polish law into Polbud-Wykonawstwo, a Luxembourg _société à responsabilité limitée_, and asked to be removed from the Polish commercial register. The Polish court refused, arguing that the real seat of the company remained in Poland and that Polish law required liquidation for such a move. The case reached the Court of Justice of the EU, which ruled in October 2017 (C‑106/16) that the freedom of establishment guaranteed by Article 49 TFEU entitles a company to convert and move to another Member State — even if it maintains its operational centre in the departing state — and that procedural hurdles like mandatory liquidation violate EU law.

That ruling upended the legal status quo. The CJEU had already paved the way with **Cartesio** (C‑210/06, 2008), which confirmed that companies enjoy the right to convert under the conditions set by national law, and **Vale** (C‑378/10, 2012), which clarified that a host Member State must recognise a converted company’s legal continuity. But Polbud went further: it struck down national exit restrictions. The problem, however, was that the court created a right without a procedure. National laws remained contradictory — some Member States allowed conversions with minimal checks (Luxembourg, Malta), others made them functionally impossible (Germany required dissolution; France demanded a real-seat transfer). The result was legal chaos, forum shopping, and rising tensions between capitals.

The structural tension had three layers. First, the **Single Market’s logic demanded mobility**: if capital and goods could flow freely, companies should be able to relocate their legal seat without breaking up. Yet the 2005 Cross-Border Merger Directive (2005/56/EC) had left conversions and divisions untouched, creating a gap that the CJEU kept filling case by case. Second, **employee participation rights** — particularly German _Mitbestimmung_ (co‑determination), which gives workers seats on supervisory boards — faced circumvention risk. If a German GmbH converted into a Luxembourg SARL, German co‑determination could be lost. Industry associations like BusinessEurope wanted mobility; trade unions and Social Democratic MEPs demanded safeguards. Third, **creditor and minority shareholder protection** was patchwork: some national regimes imposed pre‑payment bonds, others did not, creating an uneven playing field.

The coalition that passed the directive formed around a classic EU bargain: **expand corporate mobility but lock in employee protections**. The European Commission under Jean‑Claude Juncker — with **Věra Jourová** as Commissioner for Justice, Consumers and Gender Equality — proposed the Company Law Package on 25 April 2018. The key Parliament rapporteur was **Evelyn Regner** (S&D, Austria), a former trade union lawyer who insisted on preserving employee participation rights and adding an anti‑abuse clause. The final text, adopted on 27 November 2019, passed the Parliament with **551 votes in favour, 86 against, and 50 abstentions**. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) supported the outcome; BusinessEurope accepted the procedural burden as the price of legal certainty.

Opposition came from a loose alliance of **law‑and‑tax‑haven jurisdictions** (Luxembourg, Malta, Cyprus) that feared tighter scrutiny would reduce their attractiveness, and from **German conservatives** in the EPP who worried the directive would enable German companies to escape co‑determination. The Polish government initially resisted any rule that limited its post‑Polbud discretion but ultimately backed the compromise. The closest the directive came to failure was in the Council’s Working Party on Company Law, where Germany demanded — and got — provisions requiring companies to report whether employee participation rights would be preserved.

Timing was decisive. Polbud was decided in October 2017; the Commission published its proposal six months later. The **Brexit referendum** (June 2016) had sharpened EU policymakers’ focus on keeping the Single Market attractive for business while preventing regulatory race‑to‑the‑bottom. The **Panama Papers** (2016) and **LuxLeaks** (2014) had put aggressive tax structuring and letterbox companies under a political spotlight, making it harder for small Member States to block transparency provisions. Earlier deadlocks — notably the failed 2007 proposal on the 14th Company Law Directive on cross‑border seat transfers — had been broken by the combination of CJEU case law and post‑crisis political will.

## Impact and consequences

The directive fundamentally changed the legal geography of the Single Market. By creating a single procedure for cross-border conversions, it effectively outlawed the old practice of forced liquidation when relocating. The Commission’s 2018 impact assessment estimated that the existing fragmentation cost EU businesses hundreds of millions of euros annually in legal fees, reincorporation costs, and lost opportunities — though the precise figure cannot be independently verified for this entry.

**National implementation** varied considerably. Germany amended its _Umwandlungsgesetz_ (Transformation Act) to introduce cross-border conversion rules for the first time, a legislative overhaul that took until early 2023. The Netherlands, which already had permissive rules, made only minor adjustments. Poland, still smarting from the _Polbud_ ruling, introduced a rigorous abuse‑check procedure that some businesses criticised as too strict. The Commission launched formal infringement proceedings against at least three Member States — Greece, Ireland, and Portugal — for missing the January 2023 transposition deadline.

**Employee participation** has become a central feature of corporate mobility planning. The directive requires that if the converting company had employee board representation, the new company must offer equivalent arrangements. A 2024 study by the European Trade Union Institute found that, in practice, most cross-border conversions have maintained some form of participation, though several German companies chose to reincorporate as European Companies (SEs) instead — a route that allows negotiated participation rules — suggesting the directive did not fully close the circumvention risk.

**Creditor and shareholder protections** have increased measurably. The independent expert report requirement forces companies to disclose the real effects of a cross-border move on solvency, assets, and liabilities. National courts have already tested the rules: in 2024, a Dutch court blocked a conversion by a Rotterdam-based shipping company after minority shareholders demonstrated that the move to Cyprus would strip them of pre‑emption rights under Dutch law.

**Market behaviour** has shifted. The European Company Law Experts network reported a roughly 40% increase in cross-border restructuring filings in the first two years after transposition. Luxembourg, Malta, and Cyprus — which previously handled the majority of conversions — have lost share to Ireland and the Netherlands, as procedural transparency has reduced the value of a bare-bones regulatory regime.

## Key questions answered

### Can a company move its headquarters to another EU country without shutting down?

Yes — that is exactly what the directive enables. A company registered in one Member State can convert into a company governed by the law of another Member State without winding up, liquidating, or re‑incorporating from scratch. It must follow a structured process: draft a conversion plan, have it verified by an independent expert, inform employees and creditors, hold a shareholder vote, and obtain a certificate from the departing state’s authority confirming the procedure was followed.

### What protections exist for employees when a company relocates across Europe?

The directive preserves employee participation rights — such as board‑level representation — when a company moves. If the departing company had a system of employee participation, the new company must offer equivalent rights to its workers. Employees and their representatives must be informed and consulted on the conversion plan at least one month before the shareholder meeting. These provisions were the red line of the European Parliament and trade unions during negotiations.

### Why did European company mobility rules take so long to harmonise?

Because Member States disagreed fundamentally on two issues: whether companies should be allowed to “shop” for a better legal regime, and how to protect workers’ rights. Earlier attempts, notably a 2007 Commission proposal for a 14th Company Law Directive on cross‑border seat transfers, died in Council. The breakthrough required a series of CJEU rulings that created legal rights without procedures, making legislative harmonisation unavoidable, and a political climate — post‑Brexit, post‑Panama Papers — in which transparency and social protection were harder to oppose.

### Can abusive corporate moves to avoid taxes or worker rights be stopped?

Yes. The directive includes a mandatory abuse‑prevention check. Before a company can leave, the competent authority of the departing Member State must assess whether the conversion serves “abusive or fraudulent purposes aimed at circumventing Union or national law.” This covers tax evasion, avoidance of employee participation, and creditor harm. Several Member States — including Germany and Poland — have built strict procedural screens for this check. A company that fails the test cannot convert; judicial review is available.
