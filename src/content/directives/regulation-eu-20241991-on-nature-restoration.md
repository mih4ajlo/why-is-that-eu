## What is it?

The Nature Restoration Regulation requires EU member states to put recovery measures in place on at least 20% of the bloc's land and sea areas by 2030, and on all ecosystems needing restoration by 2050. Unlike the 1992 Habitats Directive, which obliged states to *protect* designated sites, this law obliges them to *improve* the condition of forests, wetlands, rivers, marine habitats, urban green spaces and farmland — even outside Natura 2000 zones.

It sets concrete sub-targets: rewetting 30% of drained peatlands by 2030 (rising to 50% by 2050), reversing pollinator decline by 2030, planting three billion additional trees, restoring 25,000 km of free-flowing rivers, and ensuring no net loss of urban green space. Each member state must submit a National Restoration Plan to the Commission detailing how it will hit these targets.

The regulation entered into force on 18 August 2024 and applies directly across all 27 member states, though most obligations are implemented through national plans due by mid-2026.

## Why was it introduced?

When Frans Timmermans unveiled the proposal in June 2022, he framed it as the missing piece of the European Green Deal: the EU had pledged climate neutrality by 2050 and a Farm to Fork strategy, but 81% of protected habitats in Europe were still in poor condition according to the European Environment Agency's 2020 State of Nature report. Pollinator populations had collapsed — one in three bee, butterfly and hoverfly species was in decline — and 71% of EU fish stocks were classed as below healthy levels. Timmermans argued protection alone had failed; restoration was now mandatory under the UN's 2022 Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, which the EU had championed.

What followed was the most acrimonious environmental file of the mandate. The European People's Party, led by Manfred Weber, broke with the Commission of its own president Ursula von der Leyen and campaigned actively to kill the law, arguing it would slash farm output and threaten food security amid the post-Ukraine grain price shock. Copa-Cogeca, the EU farming lobby, claimed peatland rewetting would expropriate farmers; the European Landowners' Organization joined in. On the other side, more than 6,000 scientists signed an open letter in July 2023 dismissing the food-security argument, and companies including IKEA, Nestlé, Unilever and Iberdrola publicly backed the law through the Business for Nature coalition.

The Parliament vote on 12 July 2023 was knife-edge: 336 in favour, 300 against, 13 abstentions, after EPP amendments to reject the file outright failed by just 12 votes. The text was watered down significantly — agricultural ecosystem indicators softened, an emergency brake added on food security grounds. Then it nearly died again in Council: Hungary withdrew support in March 2024, leaving the file without a qualified majority. It was rescued on 17 June 2024 when Austria's Greens environment minister Leonore Gewessler voted yes despite Chancellor Karl Nehammer's ÖVP publicly forbidding her to do so — triggering a constitutional complaint and a criminal referral against her in Vienna. The regulation passed Council 20-6 with Austria's vote counted.

## Impact and consequences

Member states are now drafting National Restoration Plans, with peatland-heavy countries — Germany, Ireland, Finland, the Netherlands — facing the steepest adjustments. Ireland alone has roughly 1.5 million hectares of drained peat that will need partial rewetting, much of it under cattle and dairy. The Dutch government, already convulsed by farmer protests over nitrogen rules, signalled it would seek maximum use of the food-security emergency brake.

Industry response split sharply. Forest owners in Sweden and Finland, represented by CEPF, warned of conflicts with national bioeconomy strategies; both governments voted against the file in Council. Conversely, renewable developers welcomed Article 6, which classifies restoration projects as serving overriding public interest — easing permitting in much the same way the 2023 RED III revision did for wind farms. The European Investment Bank announced in late 2024 it would expand its Natural Capital Financing Facility to help fund national plans, with the Commission estimating restoration needs at €154 billion through 2050 against benefits valued at €1,860 billion.

Politically, the law's near-death experience reshaped the 2024 European elections. Von der Leyen distanced herself from parts of the Green Deal during her campaign, and her second Commission, sworn in December 2024, dropped the standalone "Green Deal" portfolio. Environment commissioner Jessika Roswall now oversees implementation alongside a new "competitiveness" framing.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EPP try to kill a major EU nature restoration plan proposed by a Commission led by their own party?

Weber's calculation was electoral: with farmer protests spreading from the Netherlands to France and Germany in 2023-24, the EPP positioned itself as the rural counterweight to Greens and Socialists ahead of the June 2024 European elections. By breaking with von der Leyen on this single file, Weber signalled to centre-right voters and farming constituencies that the EPP would brake further green legislation — a strategy that paid off in the elections, where the EPP gained seats while Greens lost a quarter of theirs.

### Why was restoring drained peatlands by rewetting them so controversial in the EU nature restoration law?

Drained peatlands cover only about 3% of EU land but emit roughly 5% of total EU greenhouse gases, making them among the cheapest decarbonisation options. But most drained peat in Ireland, Germany and the Baltics is now productive farmland or commercial forest, and rewetting changes what can grow there. Farming lobbies framed this as forced land-use change; the final regulation accordingly made rewetting voluntary for private landowners, with member states meeting targets via public land or incentive schemes.

### Why is the EU nature restoration law a directly binding regulation instead of a directive that countries can adapt?

The Commission chose a regulation — directly applicable, no national transposition — because the 1992 Habitats Directive's patchy implementation was the very problem it sought to fix. After 30 years, only 15% of habitats covered by the directive were in good status. By using a regulation with quantified, time-bound targets and Commission oversight of National Restoration Plans, Brussels gains tools closer to those it uses for climate compliance under the Effort Sharing Regulation.
