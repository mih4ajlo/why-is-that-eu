## What is it?

The European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF) is an EU solidarity fund that co‑finances active labour market measures for workers who have been dismissed because of major structural changes — originally globalisation-driven trade shocks, and since 2021 also automation, digitalisation, and even temporary crises like the COVID‑19 pandemic. It does not directly give money to individuals; instead, it reimburses member states up to 60% of the cost of tailored re‑employment services: retraining, career counselling, job‑search assistance, entrepreneurship grants, and education allowances.

Member states submit applications when a large‑scale redundancy event occurs (at least 200 workers dismissed in one region or sector) and can show the dismissals were caused by a shift in global trade patterns, a financial or economic crisis, or the introduction of new technologies. The European Commission approves the programme, and the European Parliament and Council jointly decide on funding. The scheme is temporary and crisis‑specific, with a total annual ceiling of €186 million (2021–2027).

## Why was it introduced?

The original EGF was created in 2006 under the Barroso Commission, driven by a wave of industrial closures in textiles, automobiles, and electronics after the EU’s eastern enlargement and China’s accession to the WTO in 2001. In 2005 alone, over 40,000 textile jobs were lost in France, Italy, and Portugal — with little support from existing national safety nets. Commissioner Vladimír Špidla (Employment, Social Affairs) argued that the EU needed a visible solidarity fund to show that globalisation could be managed fairly.

During the 2014 revision, the fund was extended to cover temporary economic crises (like the euro‑zone recession) and became permanent rather than reviewed every seven years. But by the late 2010s, a second pressure was clear: automation and digitalisation were causing redundancies even without trade shocks. In 2018, the Commission’s own impact assessment estimated that 14% of jobs in OECD countries were at high risk of automation. Industry groups such as DigitalEurope welcomed the idea as a way to make technological change politically palatable, while trade unions — notably the European Trade Union Confederation — pushed for a broader definition.

The key turning point came in 2019–2020. The von der Leyen Commission’s ambitious Green Deal and Digital Agenda required a social safety net for workers displaced by the twin transitions. At the same time, the COVID‑19 pandemic triggered mass layoffs in sectors like tourism and retail — the EGF was suddenly used for a global health crisis. Commissioner Nicolas Schmit (Jobs and Social Rights) led the legislative revision, which passed in May 2021. The European Parliament was the strongest advocate for including automation and crisis‑related redundancies, while member states in the Council — wary of budget increases — accepted the change as part of the EU’s expanded social response to the pandemic.

## Timeline

- **2006** — Original European Globalisation Adjustment Fund created (Regulation 1927/2006) with a €500 million annual ceiling for trade‑related job losses.
- **2014** — Revision makes EGF permanent, extends to economic crises, lowers threshold from 1,000 to 500 workers.
- **2018–2019** — Commission evaluation shows that 60% of EGF applications since 2014 were for financial‑crisis layoffs, not trade. Automation emerges as a growing cause.
- **May 2020** — Commission proposes revision to explicitly include automation, digitalisation, and temporary crises like COVID‑19.
- **May 2021** — Regulation 2021/691/EU adopted by Parliament (large majority, with S&D and Greens leading) and Council. Entered into force retroactively from 1 January 2021.
- **2022–2023** — First applications under the new rules: Estonia (automation in IT services), Spain (retail closures due to digital shift), Belgium (auto sector restructuring).

## Impact and consequences

Between 2007 and 2020, the EGF had supported over 170,000 workers across 24 member states, according to the Commission’s 2020 evaluation report. However, the fund has been used unevenly: Belgium, France, and Germany account for more than half of all applications, while Eastern European countries rarely apply — partly because they find the co‑financing requirement (40% national contribution) onerous.

The 2021 revision softened that by allowing a higher EU reimbursement rate (up to 80%) for applications linked to crises recognised under the EU’s economic governance framework. Early evidence suggests the automated‑redundancy clause has been used rarely, mostly in digital‑services firms. Critics — including the European Parliament’s budgetary control committee — have pointed out that the application process remains slow (often 12–18 months) and that workers are sometimes re‑trained for jobs that do not exist locally. Nevertheless, the EGF remains the EU’s most visible instrument of labour‑market solidarity and is often cited as a model for a possible future European Unemployment Reinsurance Scheme.

## Key questions answered

### What is the EU doing to help workers who lose jobs because of trade or automation?

The European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF) co‑finances national retraining and job‑search programmes for workers laid off in large‑scale redundancies caused by globalisation, trade shocks, automation, or major economic crises. Member states design the support — training courses, counselling, start‑up grants — and the EU reimburses up to 60% (or 80% in crisis times). It is not a universal benefit; the redundancy must involve at least 200 workers and be clearly linked to structural change.

### Does the EU have a programme for workers displaced by new technology like AI or robots?

Since 2021, the EGF explicitly covers dismissals caused by automation and digitalisation — not just trade. This could include workers replaced by robots in manufacturing, or call‑centre staff displaced by AI‑powered chatbots. The first application under this category was filed by Estonia in 2022 for IT‑services workers made redundant after a switch to cloud‑based automation. The fund is, however, reactive: it only kicks in after mass layoffs have already occurred.

### How does a worker actually get help from the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund?

Workers do not apply directly. Their employer must declare a mass redundancy (at least 200 dismissals) to the national government, which then decides whether to prepare an EGF application. If approved by the Commission and the EU budget authority, the national public employment service organises individualised support. The worker receives no cash payment from the EU — instead, they get free training, job‑search coaching, and sometimes a monthly allowance for attending courses.

### Why do some EU countries use the Globalisation Adjustment Fund more than others?

The fund is unevenly used because applying requires administrative capacity and a 40% national co‑financing share. Wealthier member states like Germany, France, and Belgium have dedicated teams to draft applications and can afford the national contribution. Smaller or poorer states often lack the resources or find it cheaper to absorb layoffs through their existing unemployment systems. The 2021 revision tried to lower the co‑financing burden, but uptake in Eastern Europe remains low.
