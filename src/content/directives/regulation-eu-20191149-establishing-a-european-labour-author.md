## What is it?

The European Labour Authority (ELA) is an EU agency that began operations in October 2019 with a mandate to help member states enforce labour rules when workers, employers, or social security contributions cross borders. It does not impose sanctions or override national inspectors; instead it organises joint inspections, exchanges data between national authorities, and mediates disputes over which country's labour law applies. The agency is headquartered in Bratislava and had a budget of roughly €50 million for 2023.

The ELA covers the entire field of EU labour mobility: posting of workers, social security coordination, access to social benefits for mobile citizens, and undeclared work. It can launch “concerted and joint inspections” at the request of two or more member states, and it maintains a set of electronic tools such as the European Social Security Pass to help inspectors verify a worker’s status in real time.

## Why was it introduced?

The ELA was a direct response to a string of enforcement failures that had made a mockery of EU labour law. The most dramatic trigger came in 2011–2012, when European Court of Justice rulings in *Laval*, *Viking*, and *Rüffert* severely restricted the ability of trade unions to strike against posted workers employed below local standards. Then, in 2015, the Commission’s own impact assessment for the revision of the Posting of Workers Directive found that national inspectors checked fewer than 1% of posted workers – a figure that the Commission's 2017 analysis repeated, calling it “a systemic enforcement deficit”. The ELA’s chief architect, Commissioner for Jobs and Social Rights Marianne Thyssen (2014–2019), repeatedly cited this 1% figure in internal debates and public hearings.

The structural tension was plain: EU rules on free movement and posting had created a market where companies could shop for cheaper labour, but national authorities had neither the legal tools nor the resources to chase the abuses that followed. A Belgian minister told the Council in 2017 that his country had identified 15,000 “false self-employed” construction workers from Eastern Europe in a single year, but could not recover the lost social contributions because the workers’ home states refused to cooperate.

The coalition that pushed the ELA through was unusual. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) had been demanding a “European labour inspectorate” since the early 2000s, while employer groups such as BusinessEurope opposed any new agency, arguing it would duplicate national efforts and raise compliance costs. The compromise – an agency that facilitates cooperation rather than inspects directly – was brokered by the Austrian Presidency in late 2018. The European Parliament adopted the regulation on 16 April 2019 with 477 votes in favour, 183 against, and 54 abstentions, after core social-Parliament groups (S&D, ALDE, Greens) secured a stronger mediation role and a longer list of tasks. The Council adopted it on 13 June 2019.

Timing was everything. The Juncker Commission had made the European Pillar of Social Rights its signature initiative in 2017, and the ELA was presented as the operational arm of that pillar – a concrete deliverable after years of declamatory documents. The 2018 revision of the Posting of Workers Directive had already shifted the political centre of gravity; member states that had blocked earlier enforcement proposals (notably the 2012 enforcement directive) now accepted that a weak agency was better than the legitimacy crisis of failed enforcement.

## Timeline

- **2012** — The European Commission issues a report on the application of the Posting of Workers Directive, finding that fewer than 2% of posted workers are inspected annually.
- **2017** — The European Pillar of Social Rights is proclaimed at the Gothenburg summit; Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker announces plans for a “European Labour Authority” in his State of the Union speech.
- **13 March 2018** — The Commission publishes its legislative proposal for the ELA (COM(2018) 131 final).
- **April 2019** — The European Parliament and Council reach a political agreement.
- **16 April 2019** — European Parliament votes 477 to 183 in favour.
- **20 June 2019** — Council adopts Regulation 2019/1149.
- **31 July 2019** — Regulation enters into force.
- **16 October 2019** — Executive Director designate (Cosmin Boiangiu) takes office; agency becomes operational.
- **2020–2022** — First joint inspections launched, covering road transport and construction; inspections in the meat-processing sector follow after COVID-19 outbreaks are traced to overcrowded worker dormitories.

## Impact and consequences

The ELA has had a modest but measurable effect on cross-border enforcement. By 2023 it had facilitated over 40 joint inspection campaigns, covering sectors such as construction, road haulage, and agriculture. In a 2021 joint campaign on “undeclared work in the cleaning sector”, 11 member states participated, and authorities found irregular employment status for 12% of the workers checked. The agency also launched a mediation procedure for social security coordination disputes – a mechanism that had been blocked for decades by national reticence.

Industry response has been mixed. BusinessEurope continues to argue that the ELA duplicates the work of the Administrative Commission for Social Security Coordination; the ETUC calls it too weak to enforce sanctions. Member states have used the agency mostly as a data-sharing platform: the number of spontaneous exchanges of information between labour inspectorates increased by 35% between 2019 and 2022, according to the Commission’s 2023 evaluation report.

Critically, the ELA does not have investigative powers of its own, and it cannot impose fines. Its greatest impact may be structural: by running joint inspections, it forces national authorities to coordinate procedures they previously ignored. The Commission’s 2023 evaluation concluded that the agency had “contributed to greater mutual trust”, but noted that its budget and staff (around 100 by 2023) were “insufficient to cover the full scope of its mandate”.

## Key questions answered

### Why can’t EU countries stop companies from paying posted workers less than local wages on their own?

National labour inspectors have no jurisdiction outside their own territory, and they often lack the language skills, legal knowledge, or diplomatic channels to enforce claims against a foreign employer. A Polish construction worker posted to Germany is covered by German law on minimum wage and working hours, but if the employer returns to Poland, the German inspector cannot follow. The ELA helps national agencies share information and organise joint raids, so that an employer cannot evade enforcement by changing countries.

### What is the difference between the European Labour Authority and a European labour inspectorate?

The ELA has no power to enter workplaces, issue fines, or overrule national authorities. It is a coordination agency: it organises joint inspections, mediates disputes, and runs IT systems. A true European labour inspectorate, which trade unions called for, would have its own inspectors qualified to operate in any member state. That proposal was rejected by the Council as a violation of national sovereignty over labour law. The ELA is a far weaker compromise.

### Does the European Labour Authority cover all types of worker mobility?

Yes, but its mandate is limited to three specific areas: posting of workers, social security coordination, and undeclared work. It does not cover collective bargaining agreements, occupational health and safety (handled separately by EU-OSHA), or labour migration from outside the EU. Its focus is on enforcement gaps created by internal mobility, not immigration.

### Has the European Labour Authority ever forced a company to repay unpaid wages?

No. The ELA cannot order repayment or impose sanctions. Its role is to coordinate investigations; any fines or recovery of wages must be initiated by the national authority of the member state where the work took place. In a 2021 joint inspection in the Netherlands and Romania, the Dutch labour inspectorate used evidence shared through the ELA to recover €300,000 in unpaid social contributions from a Romanian transport company – but the recovery was a national act, not an ELA sanction.
