## What is it?

The Industrial Emissions Directive (IED) is the EU's primary legislative instrument regulating pollution from large industrial and agricultural installations. It operates on an integrated permitting approach, meaning that permits must consider the entire environmental performance of a facility — covering emissions to air, water, and soil, as well as waste generation, energy efficiency, accident prevention, and site restoration upon closure.

The IED requires all covered installations to operate according to "Best Available Techniques" (BAT), which are defined through a technical exchange process with industry, Member States, and environmental NGOs. The directive covers around 50,000 installations across sectors including energy, metal production, minerals processing, chemicals, waste management, and intensive livestock farming.

## Why was it introduced?

The IED's story begins not with a single scandal, but with a decade of fragmentation. By the late 1990s, EU industrial pollution regulation operated through seven separate directives — covering everything from large combustion plants to titanium dioxide production — each with its own permitting logic, enforcement culture, and emission limits. The result was a patchwork: a cement plant in Germany faced different emission caps than one in Spain, and regulators lacked the tools to compare environmental performance across Member States.

The structural tension was clear: the internal market meant industrial competition was European, but pollution control remained nationally interpreted. The 1996 Integrated Pollution Prevention and Control (IPPC) Directive had attempted to harmonise permitting, but its reliance on loosely defined "best available techniques" allowed wide national discretion. By 2000, the Commission's own implementation reports showed that only 12 Member States had fully transposed the IPPC Directive, and permit quality varied dramatically — some countries issued permits without any reference to BAT at all.

The coalition for reform built slowly. On one side, the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) and ClientEarth pushed for binding emission limit values, not just BAT reference documents. On the other, industry federations like CEFIC (chemicals) and Eurofer (steel) warned that harmonised limits would erode competitiveness and force plant closures. The European Parliament's Environment Committee, led by rapporteur Holger Krahmer (ALDE, Germany), initially favoured flexibility, but the 2008 financial crisis shifted the political calculus: as production fell across Europe, the Commission argued that inefficient, dirty plants would not survive the downturn anyway.

The timing was driven by the 2007-2009 legislative cycle under the Barroso I Commission. Environment Commissioner Stavros Dimas (2004-2010) made the IED his flagship file, arguing that consolidation would reduce administrative burden for both industry and regulators by 20-30%. The Commission's 2007 impact assessment estimated that the seven existing directives imposed €5-10 billion in compliance costs annually, much of it spent on duplicative permitting procedures. A single directive, Dimas argued, would cut that by €1-2 billion.

What changed to break the deadlock was the 2009 European Parliament elections. The Parliament's 2007 first reading had produced 427 amendments, many weakening the Commission's proposal. But the 2009 election shifted the political centre — the EPP-ED and S&D groups negotiated a deal that preserved binding BAT conclusions while allowing Member States to grant time-limited derogations for specific local conditions. The Council, led by the Swedish Presidency in late 2009, pushed for a final agreement that included agriculture — intensive poultry and pig farms (over 40,000 places for poultry or 2,000 places for pigs) would enter the IED scope from 2013.

The final vote in the European Parliament on 7 July 2010 was 561 in favour, 91 against, with 47 abstentions. CEPS (Centre for European Policy Studies) analysis showed the opposition was concentrated among MEPs from Member States with large livestock sectors, particularly Poland and Spain, where small-scale pig farms feared closure.

## Timeline

- **1996** — Integrated Pollution Prevention and Control (IPPC) Directive (96/61/EC) adopted, introducing the BAT principle
- **2003** — Commission report finds only 12 Member States fully transposed IPPC, with wide variation in permit quality
- **2005** — Commission publishes "Roadmap for the Review of the IPPC Directive", signalling intent to consolidate
- **21 December 2007** — Commission (Barroso I, Commissioner Dimas) proposes the IED, merging seven directives
- **2009** — European Parliament adopts first reading position with 427 amendments; June European elections shift composition
- **2010** — Council and Parliament reach informal agreement on compromise text; 7 July: Parliament votes 561-91-47 in favour
- **24 November 2010** — Council formally adopts the IED (2010/75/EU)
- **6 January 2011** — IED enters into force; Member States have 2 years to transpose
- **7 January 2013** — Transposition deadline; Commission opens infringement proceedings against 14 Member States for late transposition
- **2017** — Commission's "Fitness Check" of environmental monitoring finds IED is broadly effective but notes gaps in enforcement
- **2022** — Commission proposes IED revision (part of European Green Deal package) to include mining, battery manufacturing, and digital permitting
- **2024** — Revised IED (2024/1785/EU) adopted, adding livestock farms as small as 150 livestock units to scope

## Impact and consequences

The IED transformed industrial permitting across Europe. By 2020, the Commission reported that 90% of covered installations had BAT-based permits, compared to an estimated 40% under the IPPC Directive. The European Pollutant Release and Transfer Register (E-PRTR), established alongside the IED, created public transparency: any EU citizen can now look up the emissions of any IED-regulated plant.

The biggest impact was on coal power. The IED's Large Combustion Plant BREF (Best Available Techniques Reference Document), adopted in 2017, set emission limits for NOx, SO2, and dust that forced the closure of around 50 GW of coal-fired capacity across the EU — approximately one-third of the fleet operational in 2010. The Commission's 2020 report on "Energy Union and Climate Action" specifically credited the IED for reducing SO2 emissions from power plants by 80% between 2004 and 2018.

However, enforcement remained patchy. The Commission's 2020 implementation report found that 14 Member States still had not fully transposed the revised BREFs, and infringement proceedings were opened against 9 countries for failing to update permits within the required 4-year window. Italy and Poland accounted for the majority of delayed permit updates, with Polish authorities arguing that requiring BAT for all coal plants within 4 years was economically impossible given the country's 80% coal-dependent electricity system.

Industry responses varied. The steel sector — particularly ArcelorMittal and ThyssenKrupp — invested heavily in BAT-based technologies for sintering plants and coke ovens, arguing that regulatory certainty justified the capital expenditure. The chemicals sector, represented by CEFIC, focused on defending "site-specific" flexibility, winning provisions that allow derogations for plants where applying BAT "would lead to disproportionately higher costs compared to the environmental benefits".

The most controversial change came in 2024: the IED revision extended the directive's scope to cover livestock farms with as few as 150 livestock units (previously 40,000 poultry or 2,000 pigs). This brought up to 180,000 additional farms under European regulation, predominantly in the Netherlands, Denmark, and France. The Dutch government, already confronting a nitrogen crisis that had halted construction permits, estimated that 60-70% of its pig and poultry farms would now require IED permits, triggering industry protests and a legal challenge from the Dutch agricultural union LTO Nederland.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU rules for industrial pollution use "best available techniques" instead of fixed emission limits?

The IED's architects deliberately avoided rigid EU-wide emission caps because industrial processes vary too widely. A cement kiln in Greece (using limestone with high chlorine content) cannot meet the same limits as a kiln in Germany using cleaner raw materials without excessive cost. Instead, BAT conclusions — negotiated through the Seville Process with industry, NGOs, and Member States — set reference levels that regulators use to issue site-specific permits. The CJEU's 2018 ruling in *ClientEarth v. Commission* (C-629/16) clarified that Member States cannot grant derogations from BREF conclusions without demonstrating specific local circumstances justifying the deviation.

### Why did it take from 1996 to 2010 to replace the old EU rules on industrial pollution?

Three factors delayed consolidation. First, industry opposition: the chemicals, steel, and cement sectors argued that binding BAT conclusions would damage competitiveness, and their lobbying delayed the Commission proposal until late 2007. Second, the 2008 financial crisis: Member States insisted on incorporating "crisis derogations" allowing time-limited exemptions for plants facing economic hardship. Third, agricultural scope: disagreement over whether intensive livestock farms should be included — the Commission proposed 40,000-poultry threshold, but the Council wanted higher — consumed 18 months of negotiations.

### How do these industrial pollution rules fit with other EU environmental laws?

The IED serves as the operational backbone for several EU environmental priorities. Its emission limits support compliance with the Ambient Air Quality Directive (2008/50/EC) by reducing transboundary air pollution. Its water provisions align with the Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC), requiring permits to prevent groundwater contamination. And its accident prevention rules connect to the Seveso III Directive (2012/18/EU) on major industrial hazards. Since 2021, the Commission has required IED permits to include decarbonisation roadmaps, linking industrial pollution control to the European Green Deal's 2050 net-zero target.

### What happens if a country doesn't enforce the EU's industrial pollution limits?

The Commission maintains a dedicated IED compliance unit that monitors transposition and enforcement. Between 2013 and 2023, the Commission opened infringement proceedings against 22 of the 27 Member States for late transposition or failure to update permits. In 2021, the CJEU fined Poland €70 million plus a daily penalty of €480,000 for failing to close or retrofit the Turów coal mine and power plant — a ruling that turned on IED compliance as well as water abstraction and air quality. The relatively high attention from Brussels reflects the IED's centrality to the EU's environmental acquis: non-compliance in one Member State undermines emission reductions across the entire EU internal market.
