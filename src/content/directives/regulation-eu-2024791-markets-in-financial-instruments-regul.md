## What is it?

The 2024 MiFIR review creates Europe's first legally mandated consolidated tape — a single, real-time electronic feed combining trading data from all venues (stock exchanges, multilateral trading facilities, systematic internalisers) for equities, bonds, and derivatives. It also requires market data providers to offer data "on a reasonable commercial basis" and caps equity pre-trade transparency data costs at a fraction of current levels.

The package represents the most significant reform of EU market infrastructure since MiFID II/MiFIR entered force in 2018. It mandates a single "consolidated tape provider" (CTP) per asset class, selected via competitive tender by ESMA, and funded through mandatory contributions from market participants. The regulation also introduces a new "data reporting services provider" (DRSP) framework and tighter rules on trading venue data charges.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform's immediate trigger was not a scandal but a slow-motion competitiveness crisis. By 2020, EU capital markets remained fragmented across 300+ trading venues, while investors could not get a single real-time view of prices. The European Commission's 2020 Capital Markets Union action plan estimated that EU equity market data costs were up to 10 times higher than in the United States, where a consolidated tape has existed since 1975. The cost of accessing bond and derivative data in Europe was even higher, with no tape at all.

Political momentum built from an unlikely coalition. The European Fund and Asset Management Association (EFAMA) — representing €25 trillion in assets — led the push, arguing that data costs ate into fund returns. The Dutch and French governments, under finance ministers Wopke Hoekstra (Netherlands) and Bruno Le Maire (France), made the tape a priority in Council negotiations. They were joined by the European Central Bank, which in its 2021 Market Contact Group statement warned that fragmented data was "a structural obstacle" to shifting EU savings from bank deposits to capital markets.

The opposition was fierce and concentrated. Stock exchanges — Deutsche Börse, Euronext, London Stock Exchange Group (which lost post-Brexit relevance but held continental data assets), and the Spanish BME — argued through the Federation of European Securities Exchanges (FESE) that forcing them to sell proprietary data at regulated prices would destroy their business models. They warned of up to €1.8 billion in lost revenue, according to an Oxera study commissioned by FESE in 2020.

A series of technical failures by the existing voluntary tape model sealed its fate. Under MiFID II, the Commission had hoped exchanges would voluntarily create consolidated tapes. They did not: by 2022, the only operational tape was for post-trade bond data, run by Bloomberg, and it covered only a fraction of transactions. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA), under Chair Steven Maijoor, told the European Parliament in January 2022 that a voluntary approach had "completely failed."

Then came a specific political unblocking event. In December 2022, the trilogue negotiations collapsed when the European Parliament's lead negotiator, Dutch MEP Paul Tang (S&D), insisted on a mandatory tape for equities and bonds, while the Council — led by the Czech Presidency — wanted to keep the regime voluntary for derivatives. The logjam broke in June 2023 when the Swedish Presidency brokered a compromise: mandatory tapes for all asset classes, but with staggered timelines — equities by 2025, bonds by 2026, and derivatives by 2028. The European Parliament approved the deal in March 2024 with 479 votes in favour, 135 against, and 44 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **1975** — US Congress establishes the consolidated tape for equities through the Securities Acts Amendments; EU has no equivalent for 45 years.
- **2018** — MiFID II/MiFIR enter force; the Commission expects voluntary industry tapes to emerge but none materialise for equities.
- **September 2020** — Commission publishes Capital Markets Union action plan, promising legislative action on consolidated tape "if industry fails to deliver."
- **January 2022** — ESMA Chair Steven Maijoor tells European Parliament that voluntary approach "completely failed."
- **December 2022** — Commission tables MiFIR review proposal; trilogues collapse on mandatory vs. voluntary tape for derivatives.
- **June 2023** — Swedish Council Presidency brokers compromise: mandatory tapes for all asset classes on phased timeline.
- **March 2024** — European Parliament adopts final text (479-135-44); Council adopts in April.
- **June 2024** — Regulation published in Official Journal (2024/791/EU).
- **2025** — Equities consolidated tape operational; pre-trade transparency data price cap applies.
- **2026** — Bonds consolidated tape operational.
- **2028** — Derivatives consolidated tape operational; full review by Commission.

## Impact and consequences

The reform forces stock exchanges to unbundle and compete on data pricing for the first time. Deutsche Börse, Euronext, and the London Stock Exchange Group (which still operates EU data services) must now sell their proprietary data feeds alongside a cheaper, standardised consolidated tape. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated that investors could save €200-300 million annually in data costs, though exchanges warned of a 25-40% revenue hit to their data businesses.

The CTP selection process creates a new regulatory dynamic: ESMA will choose a single commercial provider per asset class through a competitive tender. The first equity CTP tender, launched in November 2024, attracted interest from Bloomberg, Euronext's "Optiq" unit, and a consortium of Italian and Spanish exchanges. The bond CTP tender is expected in 2025.

National regulators face new supervisory demands: tracking whether data is sold "on a reasonable commercial basis" — a fuzzy legal standard that ESMA will flesh out through technical standards. Smaller venues, such as the Warsaw Stock Exchange and Vienna's Wiener Börse, could lose a significant revenue source if their data becomes less attractive compared to the tape.

The reform also creates a new category of regulated entity: the "data reporting services provider" (DRSP), which must comply with organisational, outsourcing, and business continuity rules. This brings high-frequency trading analytics firms and data vendors under direct ESMA supervision for the first time.

## Key questions answered

### Why are trading data costs in Europe so much higher than in the US?

The US has had a legally mandated consolidated tape since 1975, which provides a single real-time view of prices and caps data costs. The EU had no equivalent — each exchange sold proprietary data at market-driven prices, creating a fragmented system where investors had to buy multiple feeds. A 2020 Commission study found EU equity market data costs were up to 10x US levels.

### Who fought against the consolidated tape and why?

Europe's major stock exchanges — Deutsche Börse, Euronext, and BME — lobbied intensely against mandatory data sharing through the Federation of European Securities Exchanges (FESE). They argued data sales generated €1.2-1.8 billion annually that funded exchange technology and regulatory compliance. The exchanges warned that forced data sharing would weaken their ability to invest in trading infrastructure and potentially drive them to relocate activities outside the EU.

### When will investors actually see the first consolidated tape data?

The equities tape is scheduled to go live in 2025, after a competitive tender process run by ESMA. Bonds follow in 2026, derivatives in 2028. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated that once fully operational, the tapes could reduce the time investors spend searching for liquidity by 30-50%, potentially increasing trading volumes in less liquid asset classes like corporate bonds.

### Does the reform affect how individual retail investors trade?

Indirectly yes. Retail investors using online brokers should benefit from tighter spreads and better execution prices as institutional investors access more complete market data. However, the most tangible impact will be on the cost of index funds and ETFs — lower data costs for asset managers could reduce expense ratios by a few basis points over time.
