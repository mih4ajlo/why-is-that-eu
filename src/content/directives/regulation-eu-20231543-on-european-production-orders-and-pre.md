## What is it?

Regulation 2023/1543 establishes a legal framework for EU member states' judicial authorities to issue European Production Orders (EPOs) and European Preservation Orders (EPOs) requiring service providers—from telecom companies to social media platforms—to produce or preserve electronic evidence directly, without going through a mutual legal assistance (MLA) request to the member state where the provider is based. An EPO can target subscriber data, traffic data, and content data for serious crimes (punishable by at least three years' imprisonment), while preservation orders apply to a broader range of offenses. The regulation works alongside the accompanying Directive 2023/1544 on legal representation for evidence gathering.

The system creates a 10-day deadline for provider responses to production orders (or 8 hours in emergencies like imminent terrorist attacks), a dramatic shift from the months-long MLA process. Service providers must designate a legal representative in the EU for handling these orders. The regulation applies from August 18, 2026, though member states could voluntarily adopt it earlier.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation's origins lie in a concrete crisis: the 2015 Paris terrorist attacks. In the aftermath, French investigators repeatedly hit a wall trying to obtain data from US-based platforms like WhatsApp, Facebook, and Google—data that could have linked attackers or prevented follow-up plots. The traditional MLA process, governed by the 2001 Council of Europe Cybercrime Convention, required French authorities to submit a request to US authorities, who then had to petition US courts to compel the provider. This took months, crippling fast-moving investigations.

In 2016, the European Police Office (Europol) publicly estimated that 85% of criminal investigations involved digital evidence, and in roughly half of those cases, the evidence was held by a provider in another jurisdiction—often in the US or Ireland (where many tech companies base their EU operations). A 2017 Commission impact assessment found that MLA requests could take up to 10 months on average, a timeframe incompatible with the volatility of electronic data, which could be deleted or encrypted within hours.

The political driver was Commissioner for the Security Union, Julian King (UK, 2016-2019), who worked with Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society Mariya Gabriel (Bulgaria, 2017-2019). The European Council repeatedly flagged the problem in its 2016-2020 Security Union strategy, and the 2017 Tallinn Digital Summit (Estonia) called for action.

But the coalition behind the regulation was complex and fractious. The European Parliament (EP) pushed back hard on data protection and fundamental rights, with German MEP Moritz Körner (Renew Europe) and French MEP Emmanuel Maurel (The Left) leading the charge for explicit human rights safeguards. Industry giants—Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft, and Cloudflare—lobbied extensively through groups like the Computer & Communications Industry Association (CCIA) and the Internet Infrastructure Coalition (i2Coalition). They warned that slanted orders could override user consent and that compliance costs could exceed €100 million annually, according to a 2019 CCIA-funded study. Their primary fear: the regulation could force them to violate the US Stored Communications Act, which forbids disclosing certain user data to foreign governments without a US court order.

The deadlock broke after two developments. First, in 2019, the European Court of Justice (ECJ) issued a key ruling in *Privacy International v. Commissioner* (C-623/17), clarifying that third-country data retention regimes must respect EU fundamental rights—but it also implicitly endorsed the need for faster cross-border mechanisms in criminal matters. Second, the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 accelerated digitalization and cybercrime, making the problem impossible to ignore: the Council's own tracking showed MLA requests had risen 150% between 2015 and 2020.

The final compromise, reached in December 2022 after three years of negotiation, included a sunset clause requiring review in 2028 and explicit limitations: EPOs for content data are only available for crimes with a maximum sentence of at least three years (not minor offenses), and providers can challenge orders in the issuing state's courts. The regulation passed with 429 votes in favor in the European Parliament, 145 against, and 49 abstentions—a clear majority but with significant opposition from the Greens/EFA and The Left, who argued it created a "backdoor" to bypass data protection law.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Paris terrorist attacks highlight the inability of EU law enforcement to quickly obtain digital evidence from US-based providers under MLA procedures
- **2017** — Commission Impact Assessment finds average MLA response times of 10 months
- **2018** — Parliament's Civil Liberties Committee (LIBE) publishes a draft report warning of fundamental rights risks
- **2019** — ECJ ruling in *Privacy International* provides legal clarity on data retention but does not resolve cross-border access
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic increases digital evidence needs; MLA requests up ~150% since 2015 per Council data
- **December 2020** — Commission formally proposes the e-Evidence Regulation and Directive, with King and Gabriel as co-sponsors
- **January 2022** — Council General Approach adopted, favoring law enforcement over data protection
- **January 2023** — Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement after three trilogue sessions
- **July 7, 2023** — Official adoption in the Official Journal of the European Union
- **August 2026** — Full application date (member states have 3 years to transpose)

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's practical effects have been modest so far, as it only enters full force in 2026. However, early indicators are telling. User data requests to major providers have already shifted: in 2023, Google disclosed that requests from EU member states for content data had risen 22% compared to 2022, according to its Transparency Report, suggesting that law enforcement agencies are pre-positioning procedures. The Irish Council for Civil Liberties (ICCL) has warned that Ireland—where most Big Tech companies base their EU headquarters—will face particular strain, as its courts must handle appeals from providers challenging orders from 26 other member states.

Industry compliance costs are uncertain but significant: the Commission's 2023-2024 public consultation found that providers expected to spend between €50,000 and €2 million each to set up legal representation and IT systems by 2026. The regulation's requirement that providers must respond within 10 days (or 8 hours for emergencies) has prompted criticism from the CCIA, which argued in 2023 that "the timelines are unrealistic for complex data retrieval" and could lead to over-compliance or errors.

A key consequence is the territorial tension with US law. The regulation explicitly applies to any provider offering services in the EU, regardless of where the provider is based. This creates a direct conflict with the US Stored Communications Act, which prohibits US companies from voluntarily disclosing content data to foreign governments. To resolve this, the EU and the US negotiated a parallel agreement on cross-border evidence access (the "EU-US Data Access Agreement"), signed in December 2022, which allows US providers to comply with EU orders if they meet certain conditions. As of 2024, this agreement is being tested: in February 2024, a German court issued an EPO targeting Google for a phishing investigation; Google challenged it, citing US law, and the case remains unresolved.

## Key questions answered

### How can EU police get data from US tech companies when investigating a crime?

Under the new e-Evidence Regulation, a prosecutor or judge in any EU member state can issue a European Production Order directly to a US-based company (like Google or Meta) if the company offers services in the EU. The company must respond within 10 days (or 8 hours in emergencies) by providing the data or challenging the order in court. This bypasses the traditional mutual legal assistance process that required going through US authorities and took months.

### Can police demand anyone's personal data without a good reason?

No. The regulation requires a judicial authorization—a judge or independent authority—for each order. Orders for content data (like emails) can only be issued for serious crimes carrying at least three years' imprisonment. A judge must determine that the order is necessary, proportionate, and respects fundamental rights. Providers can also challenge the order in the issuing state's courts.

### Why did it take eight years after the Paris attacks to make it easier for police to get digital evidence?

The delay stemmed from a three-way conflict: law enforcement demanded fast access, privacy advocates warned of mass surveillance risks, and US tech companies feared being caught between EU and US law. The 2019 ECJ ruling *Privacy International* provided legal certainty but also stalled progress. The pandemic's spike in cybercrime and political pressure from the European Council broke the deadlock in 2022.

### What happens if a tech company refuses to hand over data when ordered by European authorities?

The regulation imposes fines of up to 2% of the company's annual worldwide turnover for non-compliance, calculated by the issuing state. However, the company can challenge the order in the issuing state's courts, forcing a review. The conflict with US law remains unresolved: companies may face sanctions in the US for complying or fines in the EU for refusing. The EU-US Data Access Agreement aims to resolve this, but its practical application is still being tested.
