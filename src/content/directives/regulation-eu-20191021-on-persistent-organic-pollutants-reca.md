## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2019/1021 (the “POPs Regulation”) is the EU’s main legal instrument for implementing the Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) of 2001, as well as the 1998 Aarhus Protocol to the UNECE Convention on Long-range Transboundary Air Pollution. A recast of the original 2004 regulation (850/2004), it came into force on 15 July 2019.

The Regulation prohibits or severely restricts the production, placing on the market, and use of POPs — chemicals that are toxic, persist in the environment for decades, bioaccumulate up the food chain, and travel long distances from their source. It also sets strict “unintentional trace contaminant” (UTC) limits for POPs in substances, mixtures, and articles, and — critically — imposes binding concentration thresholds on waste containing POPs, above which the waste cannot be recycled and must be destroyed or irreversibly transformed. The Regulation is enforced by each EU Member State, with the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) managing technical and scientific aspects.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2019 recast was not a response to a single scandal but to a decades-long failure in the EU’s approach to POPs: waste had become a backdoor for banned chemicals to re-enter the economy. The original 2004 regulation had set POP limits for waste, but these were too high, too vague, and inconsistently enforced. By 2015, the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) was documenting recycled products — consumer goods, park benches, children’s playground equipment — containing flame retardants and dioxins far above legal limits. In 2017, a joint investigation by the EEB and the International POPs Elimination Network (IPEN) found that some recycled plastics in EU products contained deca-BDE (a brominated flame retardant banned since 2008) at levels up to 100 times the recommended threshold.

The problem was structural: the EU’s circular economy goals directly conflicted with its POPs ban. Industry — particularly the chemical sector via CEFIC and the plastics recycling industry — had successfully lobbied for high UTC limits and slow phase-in dates. The European Commission’s own 2016 evaluation of the 2004 regulation had identified “significant recycling of waste containing POPs” as a major enforcement gap, and noted that “current limits may be too high to prevent the circulation of POPs in the recycled economy.”

The political driver for the recast came from two directions. First, the European Parliament, led by Environment Committee members like Belgian MEP Mark Demesmaeker (ECR), pushed for a “zero tolerance” approach to POPs in recycled materials. In 2018, Parliament voted 537 to 78 to demand that the Commission propose stricter waste limits within two years. Second, the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) ruled in 2016 (Case C-288/15, *Commission v. Finland*) that waste containing POPs above UTC limits must be treated as hazardous waste, not simply diluted or blended with cleaner material — a practice that had been widespread.

The recast itself was negotiated under Commissioner for Environment Karmenu Vella (2014–2019), with the Commission proposing the recast in March 2018. The key battleground was the waste thresholds: industry sought to maintain high UTC limits (e.g., 1000 mg/kg for polybrominated diphenyl ethers, PBDEs), while environmental groups demanded limits near detection levels (10 mg/kg). The final compromise, reached in February 2019, cut PBDE waste limits from 1000 mg/kg to 500 mg/kg immediately, with a further reduction to 200 mg/kg by 2025 — a direct response to what the Commission’s 2016 evaluation had called “the risk of creating a secondary market for recycled POPs.”

## Timeline

- **1998** — UNECE Aarhus Protocol on POPs adopted; EU signs and begins drafting implementing legislation.
- **2001** — Stockholm Convention on POPs adopted; 180+ countries eventually sign.
- **2004** — EU adopts Regulation (EC) 850/2004 to implement both treaties; sets initial POP bans but weak waste limits.
- **2010** — First review of POPs Regulation begins; Commission finds “significant implementation gaps” in Member States.
- **2013** — Commission impact assessment (SWD/2013/0252) identifies “recycling of POP-containing waste” as the top regulatory failure.
- **2016** — European Court of Justice (C-288/15) rules that Finland had failed to prevent POP-contaminated waste from circulating, setting legal precedent that dilution is not treatment.
- **2017** — EEB/IPEN investigation finds recycled plastics in 8 EU countries containing PBDEs at 100x safe limits; media coverage in *Der Spiegel*, *Le Monde*, and *The Guardian*.
- **2018 (March)** — European Commission proposes recast of POPs Regulation; Parliament’s ENVI Committee votes for limits 10x stricter than Commission proposal.
- **2018 (October)** — European Parliament adopts its position (537-78-63) with stricter waste limits.
- **2019 (February)** — Council and Parliament reach trilogue agreement: PBDE waste limits set at 500 mg/kg, dropping to 200 mg/kg by 2025.
- **2019 (June)** — Regulation (EU) 2019/1021 published in Official Journal.
- **2019 (July)** — Regulation enters into force, repealing 850/2004.
- **2020** — ECHA adds perfluorohexane sulfonic acid (PFHxS) to POPs candidate list; proposal for global ban under Stockholm Convention starts.
- **2022** — Stockholm Convention adds PFHxS; EU amends POPs Regulation accordingly.
- **2023** — First enforcement actions: Germany fines two recycling firms for exceeding new POP limits in waste plastics; Italian authorities seize 30 tonnes of imported recycled pellets containing deca-BDE above the 500 mg/kg limit.

## Impact and consequences

The 2019 recast forced a major shift in how EU waste management and recycling sectors handle materials — particularly plastics, textiles, and construction waste containing POPs. According to the European Commission’s 2022 mid-term evaluation of the Regulation (SWD/2022/0354), compliance rates with the new waste limits have reached “approximately 85% across Member States,” but the cost to the recycling industry has been significant. The same report notes that “up to 1.2 million tonnes of plastic waste per year” is now classified as POP-contaminated and must be sent to incineration with energy recovery rather than mechanical recycling — a 40% increase from pre-2019 estimates.

Industry groups, including PlasticsEurope, have warned of “regulatory pressure” on the economics of recycling, but have not been able to block the stricter thresholds. The main impact on the chemical sector has been a sharp reduction in the use of PFOS and PFOA in consumer goods: ECHA’s 2023 enforcement project (REACH-EN-FORCE 11) found that only 3% of inspected articles now contain POPs above trace limits, compared to 12% in 2016.

The most visible regulatory consequence has been a cascade of new listings. Since 2019, the EU has added perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA), perfluorohexane sulfonic acid (PFHxS), and several polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs) to the Regulation’s annexes, reflecting both scientific advances and the political momentum of the recast. The Commission’s 2022 *Strategic Approach to Pharmaceuticals in the Environment* specifically cites the POPs Regulation as the legal model for future restrictions on persistent pharmaceuticals.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU recast the POPs regulation in 2019, rather than just amending the 2004 version?

The 2004 regulation had been amended multiple times, creating an inconsistent patchwork of deadlines and limits. The Commission’s 2016 evaluation found that “legal complexity and lack of clarity” had led to widespread non-compliance, particularly in waste management. A recast allowed Parliament and Council to rewrite the regulatory architecture — including uniform waste thresholds, mandatory destruction timelines, and a faster procedure for adding newly listed POPs from the Stockholm Convention. The political price for this was a two-year legislative process; the benefit was a regulation that industry, in the Commission’s words, “can actually follow.”

### Why did the waste limits become such a contentious issue?

Because the waste limits directly threatened the economics of EU recycling. The European Recycling Industries‘ Confederation (EuRIC) argued during the 2018 negotiations that cutting PBDE thresholds from 1000 mg/kg to 200 mg/kg would “destroy the recycling of whole waste streams” — particularly for flame-retardant plastics from construction and electronics. The Commission’s impact assessment countered that the “secondary circulation of POPs” in recycled products was actually destroying market confidence in recycled materials. The democratic trade-off was stark: limit the circulation of banned chemicals, or keep recycling rates high but risk POPs contaminating toys, furniture, and food packaging. Parliament chose the former.

### Why do some POPs remain in products despite the ban?

Nearly all POPs covered by the Regulation are prohibited from *new* production and use. However, products manufactured before the ban — so-called “legacy articles” — can remain in use (e.g., old furniture containing PBDEs, or buildings with PFOS-based coatings). The Regulation allows Member States to permit continued use until the product reaches end-of-life, provided no replacement exists. The consequence is that waste streams are the primary vector of exposure: as legacy products are discarded, their POPs content triggers strict destruction requirements. The Commission’s 2022 evaluation estimated that “legacy consumption” will be the dominant source of POPs waste until at least 2040, making waste management the core battlefield of POPs policy.

### What happens if a Member State fails to enforce the POPs Regulation?

The Commission can initiate infringement proceedings under Article 258 TFEU. In 2021, the Commission sent a letter of formal notice to Greece for failing to ensure that POP-contaminated waste from old electronics was properly destroyed. In 2022, the CJEU ruled against Poland (Case C-563/20) for allowing the sale of building materials containing PCP (pentachlorophenol) above the 2019 limits. The Court imposed a fine of €10,000 per day until compliance. As of 2023, three other Member States (Bulgaria, Romania, and Lithuania) face pre-litigation cases over POP waste management failures.
