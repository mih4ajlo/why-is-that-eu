## What is it?

The Just Transition Fund (JTF) is a dedicated EU financial instrument designed to mitigate the social and economic shock of decarbonisation – particularly for regions that have built their identity and employment around coal, lignite, peat, and oil shale. It is one of three pillars of the wider Just Transition Mechanism, which mobilises an estimated €55 billion in public and private investment between 2021 and 2027.

Unlike traditional cohesion funds, the JTF focuses on reskilling workers, supporting start-ups, investing in clean energy infrastructure, and revitalising former industrial sites. Each eligible region must draw up a Territorial Just Transition Plan approved by the European Commission. Member States cannot use the fund to finance fossil fuel projects, including natural gas, except in strictly defined cases under the 2021 revision (which allowed some gas infrastructure in certain regions until 2025, but this was later tightened under the 2023 revision). The fund is delivered under shared management with national and regional authorities.

## Why was it introduced?

The JTF’s creation was driven by a single, explosive threat in December 2019: Polish Prime Minister Mateusz Morawiecki signalled that Poland would veto the EU’s objective of reaching climate neutrality by 2050 unless the Union put serious money on the table for coal regions. Poland, home to over 80% of the EU’s remaining coal‑mining jobs (according to the European Commission’s 2020 impact assessment), faced the starkest transition cost. Its Silesian coal basin alone employed roughly 80,000 miners, and hundreds of thousands of indirect jobs depended on the sector.

Structural tension: The EU’s existing Cohesion Policy funds (European Regional Development Fund, Cohesion Fund) were not designed to cushion the rapid labour market upheaval that climate policy required. Regions like Upper Silesia, Western Macedonia in Greece, or the Jiu Valley in Romania faced a decade of industrial collapse without a targeted safety net. At the same time, the European Green Deal – presented by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019 – promised an “inclusive” transition but lacked a concrete budget line. Without the JTF, the Green Deal was a political house of cards on unstable ground.

Coalition dynamics: Eastern European member states – Poland, Czech Republic, Bulgaria, Romania – formed a bargaining bloc during the 2020–2021 budget negotiations. They made JTF funding a condition for supporting both the EU’s Multiannual Financial Framework (2021–2027) and the European Climate Law (2021/1119/EU). The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Manolis Kefalogiannis (EPP, Greece), backed a larger fund and pushed for a binding “do no harm” principle that eventually limited gas investments. The final compromise allocated €7.5 billion from the EU budget (in 2018 prices) plus €10 billion from NextGenerationEU, the pandemic recovery instrument. The political breakthrough came in July 2020, when EU leaders agreed on the recovery plan and tied the JTF to it.

Timing: The confluence of the Green Deal launch, the COVID-19 recession, and the start of the 2021–2027 budget negotiations created a window for the JTF that had not existed before. Earlier efforts, such as the Coal Regions in Transition Platform launched in 2017 under the Juncker Commission, were non‑binding and lacked dedicated funding. The platform’s pilot regions (e.g. Silesia, Lusatia, Western Macedonia) generated detailed transition plans but could not finance them. The 2019 European elections shifted the political centre of gravity: the Greens gained seats, and the new von der Leyen Commission made climate the top priority. The pandemic then provided a “only once in a generation” justification for large‑scale debt‑financed spending.

What changed: The specific event that unblocked the deadlock was the July 2020 European Council summit on the recovery fund. After a marathon four‑day negotiation, leaders agreed to set aside €10 billion in new money for the JTF from NextGenerationEU – effectively doubling the initial Commission proposal. Morawiecki publicly praised the deal, and Poland later approved the Climate Law. Without that direct link, the fund would have remained a symbolic proposal.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Paris Agreement signed; EU pledges 40% emissions reduction by 2030. Coal regions begin to organise around transition demands.
- **2017** — European Commission launches the Coal Regions in Transition Platform, a voluntary knowledge‑sharing network. No money attached.
- **December 2019** — European Green Deal presented; Commission President von der Leyen promises a “Just Transition Mechanism”.
- **January 2020** — Commission proposes the Just Transition Fund as part of the European Green Deal Investment Plan, initially with €7.5 billion.
- **February–July 2020** — Intense negotiations on the EU Multiannual Financial Framework and NextGenerationEU. Poland threatens veto over climate neutrality.
- **21 July 2020** — European Council reaches agreement on the recovery package; JFT is allocated €17.5 billion (€7.5 billion EU budget + €10 billion NextGenerationEU).
- **December 2020** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement on the JTF regulation.
- **24 June 2021** — Regulation (EU) 2021/1056 is formally adopted by the Council.
- **1 July 2021** — Regulation enters into force; Territorial Just Transition Plans start being submitted.
- **2022** — First operational programmes approved for Poland, Germany, Romania, Greece, and others.
- **2023** — Commission revises JTF eligibility to strengthen the “do no significant harm” principle and restrict gas financing.

## Impact and consequences

The JTF redirected EU cohesion spending towards a climate‑transition purpose for the first time. By 2024, over 60 Territorial Just Transition Plans had been approved, covering more than 100 European regions. The largest single beneficiary is Poland, which received approximately €3.5 billion for Silesia, Wielkopolska, and Łódź. Germany’s lignite regions (Lusatia, North Rhine‑Westphalia) got about €1.7 billion.

The fund forced national governments to identify, map, and disclose their most vulnerable fossil‑fuel‑dependent communities – a previously opaque patchwork of state‑owned mines and ageing power stations. However, implementation proved slow. A 2023 European Court of Auditors special report found that by end‑2022, only 1% of JTF funds had actually reached beneficiaries, due to lengthy approval procedures and weak administrative capacity in some regions. The Commission acknowledged the delays but argued that the planning phase was essential to avoid malinvestment.

The JTF also created unintended political friction. Some coal‑dependent regions complained that the planning requirements forced them to “betray” their industrial heritage; others, like the Czech Republic, fought to include nuclear and gas projects, which the Commission largely rejected. The fund’s exclusion of coal mine closures from direct financing (they must be covered by national schemes) left a funding gap that some member states struggled to fill.

## Key questions answered

### Does every coal region in the EU get money from this fund?

No. Only regions that submit an approved Territorial Just Transition Plan are eligible. As of 2024, about 100 regions in 18 member states have received JTF allocations. Regions must demonstrate a clear path away from fossil fuels and invest in new economic activities – not simply keep old coal plants running.

### Why did Poland threaten to block the EU's climate neutrality target?

Because Poland had the most to lose. According to European Commission estimates, the Polish coal sector directly employed around 80,000 workers and indirectly supported hundreds of thousands more. Without a concrete financial safety net, the government argued that the 2050 climate neutrality goal would devastate its economy and trigger massive unemployment. The JTF was the price for Poland’s consent.

### Can the fund be used to build new gas pipelines or power plants?

Largely no. The original regulation allowed limited gas investments in exceptional circumstances for regions with no other fossil fuel alternative, but this loophole was tightened in the 2023 revision after environmental groups and the European Parliament protested. Since then, only renewable energy, district heating, and energy efficiency projects qualify for JTF support. Gas infrastructure is not financed.

### How much money has actually been spent so far?

The European Commission reported that by end-2023, about 15% of the total JTF allocation had been disbursed to member states, with the rest committed to approved projects but not yet paid out. The European Court of Auditors criticised the slow pace, noting that most regions had only completed the planning phase. The first concrete projects – reskilling programmes in Silesia, a hydrogen hub in Lusatia – are expected to start delivering tangible results from 2025 onward.
