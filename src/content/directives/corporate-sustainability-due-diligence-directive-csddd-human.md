## What is it?

The Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) imposes a legal duty on large companies operating in the EU to conduct ongoing due diligence across their own operations and their "chains of activities" — covering direct and indirect suppliers, distribution, transport, storage, and use of their products. Companies must identify actual and potential adverse impacts on rights like child labour, forced labour, freedom of association, and environmental harms like pollution, biodiversity loss, and climate change.

The directive applies in phases from 2027: first to EU companies with over 5,000 employees and €1.5 billion turnover (including non-EU companies reaching that threshold in the EU), then gradually to firms with over 1,000 employees and €450 million turnover by 2029. It requires companies to adopt a climate transition plan aligned with the Paris Agreement, and creates civil liability for companies that fail to prevent harm and do not remedy it. Individual member states designate supervisory authorities to enforce compliance, with penalties including fines up to 5% of net worldwide turnover.

## Why was it introduced?

The CSDDD was driven by the collapse of the Rana Plaza garment factory in Bangladesh on 24 April 2013, which killed 1,134 people and injured over 2,500. The factory supplied major European fashion brands including Primark, Mango, and Benetton, yet no company faced legal consequences in Europe. The disaster exposed a structural failure: EU law had no mechanism to hold companies accountable for human rights abuses deeper than their direct contractual relationships. Victims and their families were effectively barred from European courts.

The political response began in 2013 with the European Commission's non-binding Corporate Social Responsibility strategy, but industry resistance stalled binding measures. In 2017, France passed the Loi de Vigilance (Duty of Vigilance Law) — the first EU member state to mandate human rights due diligence. Germany followed in 2021 with the Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (Supply Chain Due Diligence Act), taking effect in 2023. These national laws created a fragmentation that European business groups like BusinessEurope warned would distort the single market — a key argument for EU-level action.

The legislative breakthrough came in 2020, when European Commissioner for Justice Didier Reynders (Belgium, 2019–2024) announced a legislative initiative on sustainable corporate governance, responding to the European Parliament's 2019 resolution calling for mandatory due diligence (passed with 504 votes in favour, 79 against). The Commission's 2020 impact assessment found that only one-third of EU companies were conducting any human rights due diligence, and fewer than 10% did so for environmental impacts.

The proposal faced fierce opposition from business lobbies, particularly from Germany and Scandinavia. Industry associations representing Volkswagen, BASF, and Siemens warned of "disproportionate administrative burdens" and risk of "competitive disadvantage". The final text was substantially weakened from the Commission's original proposal: the threshold was raised from 500 employees to 5,000 employees, cutting the number of affected companies from roughly 16,000 to fewer than 4,000. The provision on director duties was dropped, and the civil liability clause was narrowed to require a "negligent" failure rather than automatic liability.

The directive was finally adopted on 24 May 2024 after a last-minute political battle. The European Council had failed to approve the text in February 2024 after Germany's liberal FDP party withdrew support, citing costs. An abstention by Belgium's abstention-breaking vote allowed the final approval on 15 March 2024, by a qualified majority of EU member states (15 in favour, 6 against including Germany and Italy, 4 abstentions). In the European Parliament, the final vote was 374 in favour, 235 against, 19 abstentions.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's primary impact is legal certainty for the estimated 4,000 affected companies, replacing a patchwork of 15 member state-level due diligence laws with a single EU regime. However, the tightened threshold means approximately 12,000 fewer companies are covered than the Commission originally proposed — notably excluding most small and medium-sized enterprises, which are only indirectly affected as suppliers.

Civil liability is the directive's most consequential innovation. Under Article 22, victims of human rights or environmental harm can sue a company in EU courts if the company negligently failed to conduct due diligence and that failure caused the harm. This reverses the traditional "chain of contracts" barrier that shielded companies from responsibility beyond their direct suppliers. The first test case is likely to involve a European fashion brand sourcing from textile factories in Bangladesh or Turkey, according to legal analysts at the European Coalition for Corporate Justice.

The climate transition plan requirement (Article 15) is another binding obligation: companies must adopt a plan to ensure their business model is compatible with limiting global warming to 1.5°C under the Paris Agreement. This goes beyond the non-binding climate reporting under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (2022/2464/EU).

Implementation challenges are expected. Member states must designate supervisory authorities with investigative powers and the ability to impose fines — including the power to order companies to stop harming and to publish non-compliance notices. Germany, which already has the LkSG, may need to harmonise its national law, while several Eastern European states with limited administrative capacity are likely to struggle. The Commission estimates transposition costs at €150–200 million across the EU, but industry groups like the German Chamber of Commerce (DIHK) argue the real cost will be three to five times higher due to supply chain mapping and auditing expenses.

First enforcement actions are anticipated from 2028–2029, as the first companies reach the compliance deadline. The Commission will publish guidance and possibly delegated acts to clarify the scope of "chains of activities" and the burden of proof in civil liability cases.

## Key questions answered

### Can a European company be sued in an EU court for human rights abuses by its supplier in another country?

Yes — the CSDDD creates civil liability for companies that negligently fail to conduct due diligence and that failure causes harm, including by suppliers in their "chains of activities". Victims can bring claims in EU courts, but must prove the company was negligent. This is a major shift from current law, where companies could generally avoid liability by claiming they had no direct contractual relationship with the harming entity.

### Does the directive apply to companies based outside the EU?

Yes — non-EU companies with net turnover of at least €1.5 billion generated in the EU are covered, starting in 2027. This captures major international brands, raw material suppliers, and manufacturers that sell large volumes into the European market. They must designate an authorised representative in an EU member state to handle compliance.

### How does this differ from Germany's existing supply chain law?

The CSDDD is broader in scope and stricter in enforcement. Germany's law (LkSG) already covers human rights due diligence but does not require a climate transition plan aligned with 1.5°C, and its civil liability provisions are weaker (it requires loss of obligation, not negligence). The CSDDD also covers environmental impacts more explicitly and has higher maximum fines (5% of worldwide turnover versus 2% under LkSG). Germany must transpose the CSDDD by 2026, likely raising its standards.
