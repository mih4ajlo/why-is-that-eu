## What is it?

The Birds Directive (2009/147/EC) is a European Union law that safeguards all naturally occurring wild bird species within the EU's territory. It covers their eggs, nests, and habitats. The directive bans deliberate killing or capture of birds, destruction of nests and eggs, and disturbance during breeding and rearing. It also lists species that may be hunted, provided hunting does not jeopardise conservation status and occurs outside breeding and migration periods.

Member States must designate Special Protection Areas (SPAs) for species listed in Annex I and for migratory birds. These SPAs form part of the Natura 2000 network. The directive also requires Member States to restore lost habitats and to monitor bird populations. In practice, it has become a powerful tool for environmental NGOs to challenge hunting practices and land‑use decisions in national courts and at the European Court of Justice.

## Why was it introduced?

The Birds Directive owes its existence to a remarkable political gamble by the European Commission in the late 1970s — and to the mass slaughter of migratory birds that had become a symbol of Europe's environmental neglect.

By the early 1970s, Mediterranean countries — particularly Italy, Malta, France, and Greece — tolerated the large‑scale killing of millions of migratory songbirds each spring and autumn. Hunters used nets, traps, glue, and automatic rifles. The Italian hunting lobby, organised under the Federazione Italiana della Caccia, was especially powerful: it boasted over a million members and had close ties to the Christian Democracy party. The conservation case was championed by the International Council for Bird Preservation (now BirdLife International) and by a small but vocal group of European Parliamentarians. The turning point came in 1972, when the United Nations Stockholm Conference on the Human Environment gave environmental issues unprecedented political weight. The European Economic Community (EEC) had just launched its first Environmental Action Programme in 1973, but it had no legal basis for nature protection — the Treaty of Rome did not mention the environment.

The Commission’s Environment Directorate, then a tiny unit within the Industry Directorate, seized the moment. Its director, Italian official Alberto Tridente, and the newly appointed European Commissioner for the Environment, Lorenzo Natali (Italy), pushed for a directive specifically on bird protection. Natali argued that migratory birds were a common heritage and that unilateral hunting bans by some Member States were ineffective if others continued the slaughter.

The proposal faced furious opposition. The hunting lobby, led by the Fédération des Associations de Chasseurs de l’UE (FACE, founded in 1927), warned that the directive would destroy rural traditions and cost tens of thousands of jobs. France, Italy, and Ireland — all with strong hunting cultures — threatened to block the draft in the Council of Ministers, which at the time required unanimity for environmental legislation. The French government insisted on a derogation allowing traditional hunting methods like glue traps and nets. The British government demanded exemptions for certain species still considered pests, such as the carrion crow.

The breakthrough came from an unexpected direction: a 1978 ruling by the European Court of Justice in Case 8/78 (Commission v. France), which held that the EEC had the implied power to adopt environmental measures even without an explicit treaty article, as long as they were necessary for the functioning of the common market. This gave the Commission legal cover. Natali then struck a deal with the French and Italian delegations: the directive would allow limited hunting of listed species, provided it did not threaten conservation. In exchange, France agreed to phase out the most destructive traps and to start creating protected areas.

The directive was adopted on 2 April 1979 as 79/409/EEC. It passed by a qualified‑majority vote in the Council — the first time an environmental law had been adopted without unanimity. The European Parliament, which at the time had only advisory powers, strongly endorsed it. The hunting lobby had lost, but it secured a clause (Article 9) allowing Member States to grant derogations “in the interests of public health and safety” or “for the protection of flora and fauna” — a loophole that would later be exploited repeatedly in the Italian and Maltese courts.

Decades later, the directive was codified in 2009 (2009/147/EC) with no substantive changes. Attempts to revise it in 2006–2008, backed by hunting interests seeking to expand the list of huntable species, collapsed when the European Parliament and environmental NGOs mounted a fierce campaign. The Fitness Check published by the Commission in 2019 concluded that the directive had been “fit for purpose” but that implementation gaps remained.

## Timeline

- **1973** — EEC launches its first Environmental Action Programme, calling for protection of migratory birds.
- **1978** — ECJ ruling in Case 8/78 confirms EEC competence to adopt environmental directives.
- **2 April 1979** — Council adopts Directive 79/409/EEC on the Conservation of Wild Birds.
- **1981–1995** — European Commission brings several Member States before the ECJ for failure to designate sufficient SPAs (e.g. Commission v. Italy, Commission v. France).
- **1992** — Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) extends the SPA network into Natura 2000.
- **2006** — Commission proposes a revision of the Birds Directive to allow hunting of additional species; after fierce opposition from the European Parliament and NGOs, the proposal is withdrawn in 2008.
- **30 November 2009** — Directive 2009/147/EC codifies the 1979 text without substantive changes.
- **2019** — Commission Fitness Check concludes the directive is “fit for purpose” but notes that 39% of bird species in the EU still have poor or bad conservation status (EEA State of Nature report).
- **2021** — The European Court of Justice, in Case C‑583/19 (Commission v. Malta), rules that Malta’s derogation allowing spring hunting of turtledoves violates the directive, citing the species’ status as “unfavourable” under the Red List.

## Impact and consequences

The Birds Directive has been one of the most litigated pieces of EU environmental law. By 2022, the Commission had sent over 350 infringement letters and the ECJ had delivered more than 50 judgments concerning its application. The most frequent violators are Italy, France, Malta, and Greece — all countries with strong hunting traditions.

Economically, the hunting lobby has adapted. FACE pivoted from outright opposition to lobbying for “sustainable hunting” and now represents 7 million hunters across the EU. The directive forced several Member States to ban spring hunting, the use of lime‑sticks, and the trapping of songbirds — though illegal trapping persists in Cyprus and Malta, according to a 2020 Commission report.

For conservation, the results are mixed but positive. The number of SPAs designated grew from 543 in 1985 to over 5,000 by 2020, covering more than 10% of the EU’s land area. The margin for errors, however, is shrinking: in 2022 the ECJ ordered Italy to pay a €4 million lump sum and a period penalty of €10,000 per day for failing to set up an adequate system of combatting illegal bird hunting in Lombardy — the first daily penalty for a nature directive violation.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU create a law specifically for birds and not for other animals?

Birds were chosen because they are highly mobile and cross national borders — unilateral protections by one country were undermined by hunting in another. Birds also had strong public appeal (they are visible, sing, and are monitored by amateur ornithologists) and a well‑organised conservation lobby (BirdLife International). The success of the Birds Directive later paved the way for the Habitats Directive, which covers other species.

### Does the EU allow any hunting of wild birds?

Yes, but only for species listed in Annex II, and only during defined periods outside the breeding and migration seasons. Hunting must not jeopardise the conservation status of the species. Several countries (e.g. France, Malta) have lost ECJ cases for allowing hunting during spring migration. The directive also permits derogations under strictly controlled circumstances — for example, to prevent damage to crops — but these are frequently challenged.

### What happens if a Member State ignores the directive?

The European Commission can sue the country in the European Court of Justice. If the state remains non‑compliant, the ECJ can impose a lump‑sum fine plus daily penalties. In 2022, Italy was ordered to pay €4 million plus €10,000 per day for failing to tackle illegal bird trapping in Lombardy. More commonly, the Commission sends reasoned opinions and infringement letters that often lead to negotiated improvements.
