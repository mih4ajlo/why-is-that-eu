## What is it?

European Union state aid rules under Article 107(1) of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) prohibit governments from granting selective economic advantages that distort competition. But for very small sums, the Commission considers there is no real impact on trade or competition — these are "de minimis" aids that fall below the notification threshold entirely.

The De Minimis Aid Regulation (2023/2831/EU) sets the maximum amount of public support a single undertaking can receive from any Member State over three consecutive fiscal years without needing prior approval from the Commission. The regulation also mandates that Member States establish a national transparency register to record all de minimis aid awards and make the data publicly accessible. It applies from 1 January 2024, replacing the previous 2013 regulation (1407/2013/EU).

## Why was it introduced?

The 2023 revision emerged from a routine review cycle, but it was shaped by a decade of mounting evidence that the old €200,000 ceiling had lost touch with economic reality and that weaknesses in enforcement had allowed some Member States to circumvent the spirit of the rules.

**Structural tension** — The original 2013 regulation had set a ceiling of €200,000 over three years, a figure drawn from earlier case law and economic modelling. By 2020, when the Commission launched its formal evaluation, inflation had chipped away at the real value of that threshold. More critically, the Commission's own "Fitness Check" of State aid rules, published in 2020, found that the administrative burden of complying with the reporting and monitoring requirements for very small aid measures often exceeded the aid amounts themselves. A thriving ecosystem of local business support grants, small training subsidies, and micro-loans was either going unreported or was being processed through cumbersome national schemes that could have been exempted. The tension between the original goal — freeing small assistance from red tape — and the growing cost of playing by the rules had become untenable.

**Coalition dynamics** — The revision enjoyed broad support from Member States, many of whom had argued for years that the ceiling was too low. The European Parliament had called for an increase in its 2021 resolution on competition policy. Industry associations representing small and medium-sized enterprises, such as SMEunited, consistently pushed for a higher threshold to allow local governments to offer targeted support without triggering EU scrutiny. The main opposition came from a handful of larger Member States that feared weaker enforcement could lead to a "race to the bottom" in tax incentives, but this did not coalesce into a blocking minority in the Council. The Commission itself, under Executive Vice‑President Margrethe Vestager, had signalled openness to reform in its 2021 Communication on the "State Aid Modernisation" package.

**Timing** — The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the review. In March 2020, the Commission adopted a Temporary Framework allowing Member States to grant significant amounts of aid without individual notification — including amounts far above the de minimis ceiling. The crisis made clear that the old notification thresholds were unsuitable for emergencies, but it also exposed the weakness of transparency: Member States reported aids under the Temporary Framework inconsistently, and the Commission lacked a centralised tool to verify cumulation. When the Temporary Framework expired in June 2022, the Commission had already begun drafting the new de minimis regulation, embedding lessons on transparency and a higher permanent ceiling.

**What changed** — A previous attempt to raise the ceiling in the 2013 regulation had been resisted because Member States could not agree on a common methodology for calculating "single undertaking" and "cumulation". A 2017 legislative proposal from the Commission to introduce a mandatory central register had stalled. The turning point was the 2020 Fitness Check and the COVID experience. The Commission issued a public consultation in 2021, received over 100 responses, and in December 2023 published the final text. The new regulation raised the ceiling by 50 % to €300,000 and made the transparency register mandatory for all Member States from 2026.

The regulation was adopted without a formal vote in the Council because de minimis regulations fall under the Commission's implementing powers delegated by the Council under Article 108(4) TFEU. The Commission consulted the Advisory Committee on State Aid, and no Member State objected during the examination procedure.

## Timeline

- **2007** — First de minimis regulation (1998/2006/EC) sets the ceiling at €200,000 over three years.
- **2013** — Regulation 1407/2013 renews the same ceiling and clarifies the single-undertaking concept.
- **2017** — Commission evaluation begins; the 2019 "Fitness Check" report highlights erosion of ceiling value and enforcement gaps.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 Temporary Framework adopted; national aid surged, showing shortcomings in transparency and cumulation controls.
- **2021** — Commission opens public consultation on revising the de minimis regulation; 108 submissions received.
- **13 December 2023** — Commission adopts Regulation (EU) 2023/2831.
- **1 January 2024** — Regulation applies (entry into force three days after publication).
- **1 January 2026** — Deadline for all Member States to have an operational central transparency register for de minimis aid.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate practical effect is that any EU Member State can grant a company up to €300,000 in public support over three years without notifying the Commission, compared with €200,000 before. The Commission estimated in its 2023 impact assessment that this would save Member States and undertakings several million euros annually in administrative costs, though it did not publish precise figures.

For small and medium-sized enterprises, the higher ceiling means that micro-grants, start-up vouchers, and local employment subsidies can be deployed faster, without waiting for months of Commission scrutiny. However, the accompanying transparency obligation — each Member State must keep a public register of all de minimis aid — will impose a one-off administrative burden on national governments, especially those that previously relied on paper records or fragmented databases. Countries such as Germany and France already operate registers; others, including several Eastern European Member States, face implementation challenges.

A more subtle consequence concerns "cumulation" — the prevention of multiple small aids adding up to a distortive package. The new regulation tightens the rules by requiring that aid is considered cumulated with any other de minimis aid already received, including from different authorities. The European Competition Network (composed of national competition authorities) has begun coordinating enforcement to ensure registers can cross-check each other, though the system will only be fully operational after 2026.

No fines or enforcement actions specific to the new regulation have been recorded as of mid-2025, but the Commission has signalled it will monitor Member State compliance with the transparency deadline.

## Key questions answered

### Does a small company need to tell the EU every time it receives a local grant?

No — that's the point of the de minimis rule. As long as the total of all such smaller aids to the company does not exceed €300,000 over three years, the national authority can grant the support without notifying the European Commission. The company itself does not have to report anything to Brussels, but the Member State must eventually record it in a public transparency register. The company must keep its own records and inform the granting authority about any other de minimis aid received in the previous three fiscal years.

### Why did the EU raise the small-grant ceiling from €200,000 to €300,000?

Inflation had eroded the real value of the old ceiling since 2013. The Commission's own 2020 Fitness Check found that the administrative costs of monitoring aid just below €200,000 often outweighed the benefits of controlling it. Raising the ceiling by 50 % allowed the regulation to keep pace with nominal economic growth and simplified life for local authorities that regularly grant micro‑support for training, consultancy, or small investments. The change was also supported by a clear majority of Member States and business associations during the public consultation.

### What stops a country from giving the same company €300,000 every three years to avoid EU scrutiny?

The rules on cumulation and the single‑undertaking concept. The regulation treats all entities that are linked by ownership or control (parent–subsidiary, sister companies under common control) as one "single undertaking", so the €300,000 cap applies across the whole group. The transparency register makes it easier to check whether a company has already received aid from another source. If a Member State knowingly exceeds the ceiling, the entire aid is considered illegal state aid and must be recovered with interest, plus the Commission can open an infringement procedure.

### Will the new transparency register mean my competitor can see what subsidy my firm received?

Yes, that is the intention. From 1 January 2026, each Member State must operate a centralised, publicly accessible database listing the beneficiary of each de minimis aid, the amount, and the legal basis. The goal is to ensure citizens and competing businesses can verify that no company is illegally accumulating small aids beyond the ceiling. Data protection rules under the GDPR apply, but the Commission's 2023 impact assessment noted that the public interest in transparency outweighs privacy concerns for business aid. Your competitor could indeed see that your company received, say, a €50,000 training subsidy.
