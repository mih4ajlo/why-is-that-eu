## What is it?

The Drinking Water Directive recast (2020/2184/EU) updates and replaces the original 1998 directive that set basic safety standards for tap water across the European Union. Its most striking innovations are two-fold: it establishes the EU's first legally binding maximum limits for PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances), a family of persistent industrial chemicals linked to cancers and immune harm, and it formally recognises the human right to access safe drinking water.

The directive requires member states to ensure that water intended for human consumption is free from any micro-organisms, parasites, or substances that present a potential danger to human health. New parameters cover emerging contaminants, including bisphenol A, microplastics, and PFAS. For PFAS, the directive sets a parametric value of 0.1 µg/L for the sum of 20 specified PFAS compounds, with an alternative option of 0.5 µg/L for "total PFAS" measured by a different method. The legislation also obliges member states to take measures to improve access to water for vulnerable and marginalised groups, such as installing public fountains and providing water in public buildings.

Member states had until 12 January 2023 to transpose the directive into national law. The PFAS limits will need to be monitored from 2024 onward. The recast also introduces a risk-based approach to water quality management, shifting from end-of-pipe testing to preventive measures across the supply chain.

## Why was it introduced?

The recast was triggered by a political earthquake: the first-ever successful European Citizens' Initiative (ECI). In 2013, the "Right2Water" campaign, backed by trade unions and NGOs like the European Federation of Public Service Unions (EPSU), collected over 1.6 million signatures demanding that the EU guarantee universal access to clean water and that water services not be liberalised. The ECI forced the European Commission to respond, and in 2015 the Commission acknowledged that the 1998 directive was outdated—it did not cover emerging contaminants such as PFAS, pharmaceuticals, or endocrine disruptors, and it had no explicit right to water.

Structural tension had been building for years. The original directive from 1998 was written before PFAS were widely detected in European water supplies. By the mid-2010s, contamination hotspots were emerging across Europe: the Veneto region of Italy, where a Miteni factory had discharged PFAS for decades; the Ruhr area in Germany; and parts of Belgium and the Netherlands. A 2016 investigation by the European Environment Agency found PFAS in groundwater and drinking water in multiple member states, with concentrations exceeding 0.1 µg/L in some areas. Industry lobbied against strict limits, arguing that PFAS were essential for manufacturing and that detection methods were insufficient. The European Chemical Industry Council (Cefic) and individual companies like 3M and Solvay warned that overly stringent values could disrupt production and increase costs for water utilities.

The coalition that pushed the recast through was a broad one. The European Parliament, particularly the Environment Committee and rapporteur Christophe Hansen (EPP, Luxembourg), demanded ambitious PFAS limits. The European Commission under Jean-Claude Juncker, with Environment Commissioner Karmenu Vella initially and then Virginijus Sinkevičius, supported the recast as part of the Circular Economy Action Plan and the broader "zero pollution" ambition. The Council of the EU, where member states had divergent interests, eventually agreed to a compromise: a mandatory 0.1 µg/L limit for the sum of 20 PFAS, but with a longer transition period and an alternative "total PFAS" approach for states that could not initially meet the sum method.

Timing was crucial. The recast proposal was published in February 2018, just as public awareness of PFAS exploded following the release of the film *Dark Waters* and high-profile contamination cases in the US and Europe. The European Court of Justice had also issued rulings on the precautionary principle that strengthened the case for action. The final adoption in December 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, reinforced the importance of public health and safe water. A key unblocker was the Commission's own impact assessment, which estimated that the economic benefits of stricter water safety standards would outweigh costs by at least 3:1, particularly by reducing long-term health spending.

The right to water clause was a direct outcome of the Right2Water initiative. The Commission initially resisted making it a legally binding obligation, but the Parliament insisted. The final text requires member states to "take measures to improve access to water" for "vulnerable and marginalised groups" and to provide "free access to drinking water in public buildings and events." While not a fully justiciable right akin to the UN's 2010 resolution, it marks the first time EU legislation explicitly frames water access as a right rather than a service.

## Timeline

- **2013** — The Right2Water European Citizens' Initiative collects 1.6 million signatures, the first ECI to meet the threshold; forces Commission response.
- **2015** — Commission publishes a report acknowledging the need to revise the Drinking Water Directive, citing emerging contaminants and the right to water.
- **2016** — European Environment Agency report finds PFAS in groundwater across several member states, including Italy, Germany, and the Netherlands.
- **1 February 2018** — European Commission proposes the recast directive, including a PFAS limit of 0.1 µg/L for 20 substances.
- **2019** — European Parliament adopts its position, strengthening PFAS limits and adding a right-to-water clause; Council agrees a compromise.
- **16 December 2020** — Directive (EU) 2020/2184 formally adopted by the Council and Parliament.
- **12 January 2021** — Directive enters into force.
- **12 January 2023** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2024** — PFAS monitoring becomes mandatory under the directive.

## Impact and consequences

Water utilities across the EU have had to invest in new filtration technologies, such as granular activated carbon and reverse osmosis, to meet the PFAS limits. According to EurEau, the European federation of water service associations, some 20–30% of water supply zones in countries like Germany, the Netherlands, and Sweden required upgrades to comply with the 0.1 µg/L standard. The French water industry association FP2E estimated that compliance costs could reach €500 million nationally over a decade. However, the Commission's impact assessment had projected health benefits of €2.5 billion per year from reduced cancer rates and immune-system harms.

Industrial users of PFAS, particularly in the electronics, textile, and chemical sectors, have faced pressure to phase out the substances. The directive accelerated existing regulatory trends, including the 2021 PFAS restriction proposal under REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals). Companies like 3M announced in 2022 that they would stop producing PFAS entirely by 2025, partly citing the regulatory environment in Europe.

Member states have struggled to meet the transposition deadline. As of mid-2023, the Commission had launched infringement proceedings against at least seven member states, including Belgium, Poland, and Spain, for failing to fully transpose the directive. The right-to-water provisions have prompted concrete measures: several cities, such as Paris and Barcelona, have installed additional public fountains, and some member states now require free water in restaurants and public buildings.

The directive has also influenced global standards. In 2022, the World Health Organization aligned its draft PFAS guidelines with the EU's 0.1 µg/L level for PFOA, signalling the EU's role as a regulatory pioneer. Environmental NGOs like the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) have praised the PFAS limits but criticised the 20-compound list as too narrow, noting that over 4,700 PFAS exist.

## Key questions answered

### What are 'forever chemicals' and why are they in European drinking water?

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are a family of more than 4,700 synthetic chemicals used for their water- and grease-resistant properties in products like non-stick pans, waterproof clothing, firefighting foams, and food packaging. They are called 'forever chemicals' because they do not break down naturally and accumulate in the environment and in human bodies. In Europe, PFAS enter drinking water mainly through industrial discharges from factories (e.g., the Miteni plant in Italy), airports and military bases using firefighting foam, and landfills. A 2019 EEA assessment found PFAS in groundwater across all 28 EU member states, with concentrations above 0.1 µg/L in at least 2% of monitoring sites. The recast directive sets the first EU-wide legal limit to protect public health.

### Does EU law guarantee a human right to water?

The Drinking Water Directive recast introduces the first explicit EU legal recognition of a right to water. Article 16 requires member states to "take measures to improve access to water for all, in particular for vulnerable and marginalised groups." This is not a fully justiciable right like in some national constitutions, but it obliges governments to act—for example, by installing public fountains, providing free water in public buildings, and ensuring that water shutoffs do not disproportionately affect low-income households. The provision stems directly from the 2013 Right2Water European Citizens' Initiative, which gathered 1.6 million signatures and forced the Commission to act. The EU also funds projects through the European Regional Development Fund to improve access in underserved areas, but water remains publicly or privately managed at national level.

### How strict are the new PFAS limits compared to other regions?

The EU limit of 0.1 µg/L for the sum of 20 PFAS compounds is one of the strictest in the world. It is more stringent than the US Environmental Protection Agency's 2016 health advisory level of 0.07 µg/L for PFOA and PFOS individually (though the EPA proposed a stricter 0.004 µg/L for PFOA in 2022). The directive also allows member states to set lower limits if necessary for health protection. By contrast, the World Health Organization's 2022 draft guideline values are 0.1 µg/L for PFOA and 0.1 µg/L for PFOS, roughly aligning with the EU. China and Canada have older guidelines of 0.4 µg/L and 0.6 µg/L respectively. The EU's decision to regulate 20 PFAS as a sum, rather than individual compounds, addresses concerns about unidentified mixtures.

### Could a citizens' petition force the EU to change its laws again?

Yes, but the bar is high. The European Citizens' Initiative (ECI) allows at least one million EU citizens from at least seven member states to ask the Commission to propose legislation. Since its creation in 2012, only seven out of over a hundred ECIs have succeeded—with Right2Water being the first. The Commission must formally respond but is not obliged to legislate. However, a successful ECI can publicly shame the Commission or shift the political debate. In the water case, the Commission's 2015 response committed to a review of the Drinking Water Directive, which eventually turned into the recast. The ECI process was reformed in 2019 to make it easier to launch, but turning signatures into binding law still requires a pro-active Commission and supportive Parliament and Council.
