## What is it?

REACH — Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals — is an EU regulation adopted in 2006 that governs how chemical substances are produced, imported, sold and used across the European Economic Area. It applies to virtually every chemical on the market, from industrial solvents to substances embedded in consumer goods like toys, textiles and car parts.

The regulation operates through four mechanisms reflected in its name. Companies must **register** every substance they manufacture or import above one tonne per year, submitting safety data to the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) in Helsinki. ECHA and member state authorities then **evaluate** dossiers and substances of concern. The most hazardous substances — carcinogens, mutagens, reproductive toxicants, persistent bioaccumulative pollutants and endocrine disruptors — are placed on an **authorisation** list (Annex XIV), meaning they cannot be used without specific permission. Other dangerous uses can be **restricted** entirely via Annex XVII, which lists banned or limited substances by application.

REACH is regularly updated. Commission Regulation (EU) 2026/859, for example, amended Annex XVII to tighten restrictions on 2,4-dinitrotoluene — a carcinogenic chemical found in some vehicle parts and military equipment — limiting its concentration in articles placed on the EU market.

## Why was it introduced?

Before REACH, EU chemicals law was a patchwork of more than 40 different directives that distinguished sharply between "existing" and "new" substances. Around 100,000 substances had been on the European market before September 1981 and were grandfathered in with virtually no safety data required. Only post-1981 "new" substances faced serious testing requirements. The result was a regulatory absurdity: the chemicals causing the most exposure — because they were the most widely used — were the least studied.

Public health pressure built through the 1990s as scientific evidence accumulated linking chemical exposure to cancer, fertility problems, asthma and developmental disorders. Environmental groups, WWF in particular, ran "biomonitoring" campaigns showing that MEPs and even Commissioners had dozens of industrial chemicals in their blood — including phthalates, brominated flame retardants and PFCs. High-profile crises like the 1999 Belgian dioxin contamination of animal feed reinforced the sense that the EU's chemical risk management system was reactive rather than preventive.

The political turning point came under the Prodi Commission. In February 2001, Environment Commissioner Margot Wallström and Enterprise Commissioner Erkki Liikanen published a White Paper on a future chemicals strategy, proposing to flip the burden of proof: industry, not governments, would have to demonstrate that substances were safe ("no data, no market"). The chemical industry — led by Germany's BASF, Bayer and the trade association CEFIC — lobbied fiercely against it, supported by Chancellor Gerhard Schröder and French President Jacques Chirac, who in a joint 2003 letter warned of catastrophic competitiveness losses. American industry and the Bush administration also lobbied against REACH, with leaked diplomatic cables showing coordinated US pressure.

Counter-pressure came from Nordic governments (Sweden and Denmark in particular), trade unions worried about occupational cancer, and the European Parliament's environment committee. After more than 5,000 amendments and one of the most intensive lobbying campaigns in EU history, a compromise text was adopted in December 2006 and entered into force on 1 June 2007. The regulation explicitly invokes the precautionary principle and identifies the protection of human health and the environment, while ensuring the free circulation of substances and enhancing competitiveness and innovation, as its core objectives.

## Timeline

- **1967** — Directive 67/548/EEC on classification and labelling of dangerous substances, the foundation of EU chemicals law.
- **1981** — Cut-off date dividing "existing" (untested) from "new" substances under Directive 79/831/EEC.
- **1998** — EU Environment Council concludes existing chemicals legislation has failed to protect health and the environment adequately.
- **2001** — Commission White Paper "Strategy for a future Chemicals Policy" proposes REACH.
- **2003** — Schröder–Chirac–Blair letter warns Commission against damaging industry competitiveness; draft REACH text published.
- **2006** — Regulation (EC) No 1907/2006 adopted on 18 December after three years of negotiation.
- **2007** — REACH enters into force on 1 June; ECHA established in Helsinki.
- **2008–2018** — Three registration deadlines (2010, 2013, 2018) phase in substances by tonnage band.
- **2011 onwards** — Successive Annex XIV authorisation lists target substances of very high concern (SVHCs) including phthalates, chromium(VI) compounds and lead chromates.
- **2020** — Commission's Chemicals Strategy for Sustainability commits to a "REACH revision" focused on endocrine disruptors and combination effects.
- **2026** — Commission Regulation (EU) 2026/859 restricts 2,4-dinitrotoluene in articles, including vehicle parts and military equipment, due to its carcinogenicity.

## Impact and consequences

REACH transformed the global chemicals industry. By 2023, ECHA had received more than 100,000 registration dossiers covering some 23,000 unique substances. Compliance costs ran into tens of billions of euros over two decades, with disproportionate burdens on SMEs and on importers of specialty chemicals. Many older or low-volume substances were withdrawn from the EU market simply because registration was uneconomic — a deliberate intended effect, since lack of data was treated as itself a risk.

The "Brussels effect" was profound. Because non-EU manufacturers exporting to Europe must comply, REACH pushed global supply chains toward EU standards. South Korea's K-REACH (2015), Turkey's KKDIK (2017) and amendments to chemical laws in China, Taiwan and the UK (UK REACH after Brexit) all borrowed REACH's architecture. Downstream industries — electronics, automotive, textiles, toys — had to map their entire supply chains for substances of very high concern and disclose them under Article 33.

Industry adaptation has been uneven. Large chemical producers built specialised regulatory affairs departments and absorbed costs; many small formulators and article importers struggled. Critics argue REACH has driven manufacturing offshore without commensurate environmental gains; supporters point to dozens of dangerous substances now phased out, including several phthalates and flame retardants that biomonitoring studies show are declining in human blood.

## Key questions answered

### Why does REACH cover articles, not just chemicals?

Because hazardous substances reach consumers and the environment mostly through products — a child's toy, a car seat, a piece of clothing — not through bulk chemical sales. Restricting only the substance itself would leave huge exposure pathways open. That is why Annex XVII restrictions, like the 2026 measure on 2,4-dinitrotoluene, set concentration limits in finished articles such as vehicle parts.

### Why was REACH so controversial?

It reversed a fundamental principle of post-war industrial regulation: instead of regulators having to prove a chemical was harmful before restricting it, industry now had to prove it was safe before selling it. This "no data, no market" rule meant tens of thousands of substances faced testing costs, and the chemicals lobby — one of Europe's largest industrial sectors — fought it intensively for five years.

### Why is ECHA in Helsinki?

When REACH was adopted, EU agencies were distributed across member states partly to give newer or smaller members visible EU institutions. Finland, which had joined in 1995, won the bid in 2003 over competing offers from Italy, Spain and others, partly on the strength of its administrative reputation and language facilities.

### Why does REACH keep being amended?

The regulation is a framework: the lists of restricted substances (Annex XVII) and authorised substances (Annex XIV) are continuously updated as ECHA and member states identify new substances of very high concern. The 2026 amendment on 2,4-dinitrotoluene is one of dozens of such targeted updates each year, reflecting evolving scientific evidence on carcinogenicity, persistence and endocrine disruption.
