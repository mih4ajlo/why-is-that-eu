## What is it?

The Data Act is a horizontal EU regulation that clarifies who owns, can access, and can share data generated by internet-of-things (IoT) devices — from smart home appliances and connected cars to industrial machinery. It gives users of those products (whether individuals or companies) the right to access and share the data they generate, breaking manufacturer-controlled data silos.

At its core, the Act establishes a principle: the person who owns or rents a connected device has the right to access the data it produces, and can share it with third-party service providers. Manufacturers must design products to make such data accessible by default, and cannot lock users into their own aftermarket services. For business-to-business data sharing, it sets rules on fair, reasonable and non-discriminatory (FRAND) terms.

The regulation also tackles business-to-government data sharing in emergencies (like floods or pandemics), imposes interoperability requirements for data spaces, and includes safeguards to protect trade secrets. Non-compliance can result in fines up to 4% of global annual turnover — matching the GDPR's maximum penalty.

## Why was it introduced?

The Data Act was born from a structural contradiction: while the EU's 2018 data strategy set an ambition to create a single market for data, most industrial and consumer data remained locked inside proprietary ecosystems of device manufacturers. A John Deere tractor generated terabytes of data per harvest — but the farmer could not access it without a dealership contract. A connected thermostat in a Berlin apartment logged temperature preferences — but the tenant could not share that data with a new energy provider.

The political trigger was the failure of voluntary industry initiatives. In 2018, the European Commission had hoped the automotive sector would self-regulate on in-vehicle data access. Instead, carmakers like Volkswagen and BMW built proprietary telematics platforms that blocked independent garages from offering connected services. ACEA, the European Automobile Manufacturers' Association, fought any mandate. By 2020, the Commission's impact assessment found that only 20% of industrial data was being reused across sectors, with data lock-in costing the EU economy an estimated €10-15 billion annually in lost innovation.

The coalition that got the Data Act passed was unusual. It united consumer rights groups (BEUC), independent service providers (like leasing companies and independent repair shops), and parts of European manufacturing — particularly machinery makers like Siemens and Bosch, who feared losing aftermarket business to US and Chinese competitors with better data access. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Maltese MEP Alex Agius Saliba (S&D), pushed for strong user rights against fierce lobbying from the automotive, agritech, and smart home appliance industries.

Opposition came from manufacturers who argued that mandatory data sharing would expose trade secrets and reduce incentives to collect data. DigitalEurope, representing companies like Apple, Siemens, and Deutsche Telekom, warned the rules would "undermine contractual freedom and innovation in data-driven products." Apple specifically feared the Act would force it to open iPhone sensor data to competitors. The final text was a compromise: it preserved strict trade secret protections and excluded data shared in cloud computing services from the core access provisions.

The timing was critical. The Act was proposed in February 2022, just weeks after the EU's Digital Markets Act and Digital Services Act had established the new regulatory framework for Big Tech. Commission Executive Vice-President Margrethe Vestager used the momentum of Europe's push for "digital sovereignty" to frame the Data Act as necessary to prevent a new wave of data monopolies in the IoT sector. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 also accelerated provisions on business-to-government data sharing for emergency response.

After the European Parliament adopted its position in March 2023 (with 500 votes in favour, 110 against, and 20 abstentions), the Council formally approved the regulation in November 2023. The short trilogue was driven by urgency ahead of the 2024 European elections.

## Timeline

- **2018** — European Commission publishes "Towards a common European data space" communication, identifying data access as a priority
- **February 2020** — Commission publishes European Strategy for Data, promising legislation on B2B and B2G data sharing
- **February 2022** — Commission proposes the Data Act, with Vestager and Commissioner Thierry Breton leading
- **March 2023** — European Parliament adopts position (500-110-20)
- **November 2023** — Council formally adopts the regulation
- **December 2023** — Data Act enters into force (20 days after publication in Official Journal)
- **September 2025** — Application date for most provisions (20 months after entry into force)
- **September 2028** — Full application including product design requirements for new devices

## Impact and consequences

The Data Act has not yet been enforced (its application date is September 2025), but its effects are already visible. Several automotive manufacturers — including Stellantis and Renault — have announced API access programmes for vehicle data, preemptively offering what the law will mandate. German agricultural equipment maker CLAAS has piloted data-sharing platforms for farmers, anticipating competition from independent agri-analytics startups.

The most contentious consequence concerns trade secrets. The Act requires manufacturers to share data with third-party service providers without identifying which data qualifies as a trade secret until requested. Critics in the European Technology Chamber argued this creates legal uncertainty: a company may have to prove confidentiality only after a breach has occurred. The Commission responded by issuing guidance in June 2024 emphasising that trade secret protections remain fully applicable.

Small and medium-sized enterprises received a partial exemption: manufacturers are not required to share data with micro-enterprises unless the SME can offer a service the manufacturer cannot. This was a last-minute addition to address German Mittelstand lobbying.

The regulation has already influenced global standards. Japan's Digital Agency cited the Data Act when designing its 2024 smart home data-sharing framework. California's 2024 right-to-repair legislation includes connected device data access provisions inspired by the EU model.

## Key questions answered

### Who actually owns the data my connected car generates?

Under the Data Act, you don't "own" data in a property sense — no EU law creates property rights over raw data. Instead, you have a legally enforceable right to access and share the data generated by products you own or use. If your car generates location, driving behaviour, and diagnostic data, you can require the manufacturer to make that data available to you or to a third party — like an independent garage or an insurance company — with minimal friction.

### Can a smart home appliance company lock me into its own services?

No, and this is one of the Act's core prohibitions. Manufacturers cannot use technical or contractual barriers to prevent you from switching to a competitor's service. If your smart thermostat collects temperature and usage data, you can share that data with a different energy management platform. The manufacturer must provide the data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format. This directly targets the business model of companies like Nest (Google) and Hive (Centrica), who rely on data stickiness.

### Does the Data Act apply to personal data or industrial data?

Both — but differently. For personal data (like your health tracker logs), the GDPR already gives you portability rights; the Data Act extends those rights to non-personal data generated alongside it. For industrial data (like manufacturing robot performance logs), the Act creates entirely new access rights that did not exist before. A factory owner can now demand data from the machine's manufacturer and share it with predictive maintenance software vendors — without revealing trade secrets.

### What happens if a company refuses to share data?

The Act gives national enforcement authorities — typically data protection agencies or competition authorities — the power to impose fines up to 4% of the company's global annual turnover. In cases where a company blocks data access using contractual terms, the user can bring a court action. The Commission estimated in its 2022 impact assessment that compliance costs would be offset by a projected €270 billion in additional GDP by 2028 from increased data reuse.
