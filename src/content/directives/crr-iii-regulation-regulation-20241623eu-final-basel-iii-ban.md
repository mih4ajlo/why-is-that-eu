## What is it?

CRR III (formally Regulation 2024/1623/EU) is the final legislative step in the European Union's adoption of the Basel III international banking standards, negotiated by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision after the 2008 global financial crisis. It amends the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR, Regulation 575/2013) and the Capital Requirements Directive (CRD VI), which together form the EU's prudential framework for banks.

The regulation introduces several major changes: an "output floor" that prevents banks using internal models to calculate capital requirements from falling below 72.5% of what standardised methods would produce; tighter rules for market risk (the "Fundamental Review of the Trading Book"); revised treatment of credit risk, operational risk, and credit valuation adjustment risk; and new reporting and disclosure requirements for exposures to crypto-assets. After years of industry lobbying and political debate, it entered into force on 9 July 2024, with most provisions applying from 1 January 2025.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for CRR III was the completion of the Basel III "endgame" reforms, finalised by the Basel Committee in December 2017. But the political drama that made the regulation EU law began with a 2022 scandal that shattered industry trust in internal models. In January 2022, **Danske Bank** admitted that its internal risk-weight models for credit risk—approved by Danish regulators under the CRR framework—had been systematically misreporting capital requirements for years, effectively allowing the bank to hold less capital than prudently necessary. The Danish Financial Supervisory Authority fined Danske Bank DKK 3.5 billion (€470 million) in September 2022 for "serious and systemic" failures in its internal models, the largest fine ever imposed by a Danish financial regulator. This was not an isolated case: **Barclays** (2021), **Credit Suisse** (2021), and several German Landesbanken had all faced sanctions or model failures. The **European Banking Authority (EBA)** noted in its 2022 risk assessment that internal model approvals had become "a patchwork" across member states, with some national supervisors applying far laxer standards than others.

The structural tension had been building for years. Under the original CRR/CRD IV framework from 2013, large banks could use internal models (IRB approaches) to estimate risk weights, often producing capital requirements far lower than standardised approaches—sometimes by 40-50%. This created a competitive advantage for banks with sophisticated models and aggressive assumptions. European industry—led by **Deutsche Bank**, **BNP Paribas**, and the **European Banking Federation (EBF)**—fought hard against the output floor, warning it would force them to hold an extra €120-150 billion in capital, reducing lending capacity. The **Association for Financial Markets in Europe (AFME)** ran a sustained lobbying campaign targeting MEPs from Germany and France, arguing EU banks would be at a disadvantage versus US banks if the US delayed Basel III implementation (which it did: the Federal Reserve's proposed output floor rule was only published in July 2023, then withdrawn in September 2024).

The coalition that got CRR III passed was fragile and surprising. **Commissioner Mairead McGuinness**, who took over financial services in October 2020, made Basel III completion a personal priority. She faced fierce resistance from **Markus Ferber** (EPP, Germany), the European Parliament's lead rapporteur for banking files, who along with **Herbert Dorfmann** (EPP, Italy) proposed delaying the output floor to 2032—effectively killing it. The tide turned in 2023 when the **European Central Bank (ECB)** —under **Andrea Enria** (Chair) and **Claudia Buch** (later Chair from 2024)—published a blistering analysis showing that EU banks' internal models had produced average risk weights of 12% for large corporate exposures, versus 45% under standardised rules, creating a threefold capital advantage. The ECB's **Supervisory Board** warned that without the output floor, EU banks would remain "structurally under-capitalised compared to global peers." The **IMF** and **Financial Stability Board** also publicly supported the floor.

Timing was everything. The geopolitical context of Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine and subsequent energy crisis shifted political attention away from banking deregulation and toward stability. France—historically aligned with German banks in opposing tight constraints—changed position after **Société Générale** and **BNP Paribas** suffered significant losses on Russian exposures in 2022, exposing weaknesses in internal model calculations. **President Macron** personally supported a strong output floor during the June 2023 EU summit, surprising many observers.

The final legislative text was agreed in trilogue on 9 November 2023, with a compromise: the output floor applies from 1 January 2025 but with a phased implementation until 2032. The **European Council** adopted CRR III on 19 June 2024, with **26 out of 27 member states** voting in favour; Hungary abstained, citing concerns about sovereign debt treatment. The **European Parliament** adopted it on 24 April 2024, with 487 in favour, 150 against, and 38 abstentions. The main opponents were MEPs from Germany and Austria who argued the floor would damage small and medium-sized bank lending.

- **July 2019** — EBA publishes transitional impact assessment estimating EU banks would need to raise €135 billion in additional capital to meet output floor.
- **October 2020** — Commissioner Mairead McGuinness takes office, prioritises CRR III proposal.
- **October 2021** — European Commission publishes legislative proposal for CRR III and CRD VI.

- **September 2022** — Danish FSA fines Danske Bank DKK 3.5 billion for model misreporting; ECB Supervisory Board warns of systemic model weaknesses.
- **July 2023** — Federal Reserve issues US proposal for Basel III implementation, but with a lower output floor (65%) and phased timeline to 2032.
- **November 2023** — Trilogue agreement reached on CRR III: output floor set at 72.5% with phase-in to 2032.
- **24 April 2024** — European Parliament adopts CRR III (487-150-38).

- **9 July 2024** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 January 2025** — Most provisions apply (except phased output floor).
- **January 2032** — Full output floor applies.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects all EU credit institutions holding capital above €5 billion; smaller banks are exempt from the output floor. The **EBA** estimates that large banks will need to increase Common Equity Tier 1 capital by an average of 15-20% to comply fully by 2032. **Deutsche Bank** stated in its 2023 annual report that the output floor would add €4-5 billion to its capital requirements. **BNP Paribas** estimated €3-4 billion. The **EBF** had previously claimed the floor would reduce EU GDP by 0.3% over five years due to constrained lending—a figure heavily contested by the ECB's **Philip Lane** (Chief Economist), who argued that better capitalised banks actually increase lending during downturns.

Industry response has been mixed. Several large banks—including **HSBC**, **Santander**, and **ING**—have accelerated model upgrades to close the gap with standardised approaches. Others—notably **Commerzbank** and **UniCredit**—have reduced trading book activities to lower market risk capital charges. The **Basel Committee's** 2024 monitoring report noted that EU banks' risk-weighted assets increased by 12% on average in the first year after implementation, with trading book exposures rising over 20%.

The most contested provision concerns crypto-asset exposures. CRR III introduces a 1250% risk weight for unbacked crypto-assets (like Bitcoin) and a qualifying threshold for stablecoins, reflecting the **Financial Stability Board's** 2023 recommendations after the collapse of **FTX** and **Terra/Luna**. **Circle** (USDC issuer) and **Crypto UK** had lobbied for lighter treatment, arguing that standardised 1250% weights would "kill European crypto adoption." The Parliament's compromise kept the 1250% floor but allowed lower risk weights if stablecoins meet strict collateral and redemption conditions.

Several member states face implementation challenges. **Greece** and **Cyprus** have the highest proportion of banks using internal models relative to their banking sector size, and their national supervisors—the **Bank of Greece** and **Central Bank of Cyprus**—have warned of disproportionate impacts on local lending. The Commission allowed a one-year delay for Greek banks (to 2026) as part of a broader memorandum of understanding following Greece's exit from enhanced surveillance in 2022.

## Key questions answered

### What exactly is the "output floor" that banks opposed so fiercely?

The output floor is a rule that prevents a bank from using its own internal risk models to calculate capital requirements that fall below 72.5% of what the standardised regulatory method would require. Banks with sophisticated models often produce much lower capital numbers, creating an advantage over smaller competitors. The floor essentially says: even if your fancy model says you need less capital, you must hold at least 72.5% of the baseline that applies to every bank, ensuring a minimum level of prudence.

### Does this regulation affect how much I can borrow from my bank?

Indirectly, yes. CRR III forces banks to hold more capital against their loans, particularly for large corporate exposures and trading activities. Some banks—especially in Germany, France, and Italy—warned this would reduce lending to small and medium-sized businesses. However, the ECB's research suggests that better-capitalised banks tend to maintain more stable lending during economic downturns, potentially making credit more available over the business cycle. The phase-in period to 2032 allows banks time to adjust.

### Why did the Danske Bank scandal matter for this regulation?

Danske Bank's 2022 admission that its internal models had been systematically misreporting capital requirements—resulting in a €470 million fine—destroyed the credibility of banks' arguments that their sophisticated models were superior to standardised rules. The scandal showed that even a major, regulated bank could "game" internal models with impunity, producing capital levels far below what safety required. This gave the ECB and Commissioner McGuinness the political ammunition to overcome industry opposition to the output floor.

### Does CRR III regulate crypto differently from traditional assets?

Yes, and dramatically so. CRR III assigns a 1250% risk weight to unbacked crypto-assets like Bitcoin, meaning banks must hold €1.25 in capital for every €1 of exposure—essentially making it uneconomical for banks to hold such assets. Stablecoins can get lower risk weights only if they meet strict conditions including full backing by high-quality liquid assets and redemption at par. This puts EU banks in a much tighter position than US banks, where the Federal Reserve has proposed lighter treatment pending further study.
