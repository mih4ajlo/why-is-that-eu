## What is it?

The Transparent and Predictable Working Conditions Directive (EU) 2019/1152 updates and replaces the 1991 Written Statement Directive. It requires employers to provide every worker, regardless of contract type or hours, with a detailed written statement of their essential working conditions within the first day of work — not the old two-month deadline. The statement must include information on pay, working hours, probation period, and, crucially, the "reference hours and days" for on-demand or zero-hour workers.

The directive sets EU-wide minimum standards on predictable work: it prohibits exclusivity clauses that prevent workers from taking other jobs, caps probationary periods at six months, and requires that any cancellation of a scheduled shift without adequate notice triggers compensation. It also gives workers the right to request a more predictable and secure form of employment and to receive a reasoned written response from their employer. The directive applies to all workers, explicitly including domestic workers, on-demand workers, voucher-based workers, and platform workers.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive is a direct legislative response to the explosion of precarious, non-standard work in Europe after the 2008 financial crisis — and the failure of the 1991 Written Statement Directive, which had been written for a world of permanent full-time jobs. By 2015, Eurofound estimated that nearly 40% of all new jobs created in the EU since 2010 were non-standard: part-time, temporary, zero-hour, on-call, or platform-based. The old directive, written when fax machines were a novelty, gave employers up to two months to issue a written statement, made no mention of on-demand scheduling, and had no teeth regarding probation periods.

The structural tension was stark: the EU had created an internal labour market with free movement, but workers in flexible arrangements had no basic right to know when they would work, how much they would earn, or whether they could take another job. The 1991 directive had been transposed in 27 different ways, with enforcement ranging from non-existent (Greece had no penalty for failing to provide a written statement) to rigorous. Industry groups — particularly platform economy giants like Uber, Deliveroo, and food-service franchisors — opposed any obligation to provide guaranteed hours or compensation for last-minute cancellations, arguing it would destroy the flexibility that workers in the gig economy supposedly valued. The European Commission's 2017 impact assessment cited a 2015 Eurofound survey showing that 3.7 million EU workers in "vulnerable" forms of employment had no written statement at all, and 64% of those workers reported unpredictable working hours.

The coalition that got this passed was surprising. The Socialists & Democrats (S&D) and the Greens/EFA pushed for robust protections, but the decisive shift came from the centre-right European People's Party (EPP), whose labour spokesperson, MEP Agnes Jongerius (S&D, Netherlands), struck a deal with the Parliament's Rapporteur, MEP Sirpa Pietikäinen (EPP, Finland). The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) ran a targeted campaign naming and shaming governments with weak enforcement, while employer organisations like BusinessEurope and the International Organisation of Employers (IOE) fought hard to keep the directive's scope limited to "employees" as defined by national law. The compromise that broke the logjam: the directive would apply to "workers," a broader term than "employees," but the six-month probation cap could be extended up to 12 months for some sectors if justified by collective agreements.

Timing was everything. The proposal was tabled by the Juncker Commission in December 2017, part of the European Pillar of Social Rights proclaimed at the Göteborg Summit in November 2017. Juncker's Commission had made social fairness a core priority after years of austerity — and the 2017 Swedish election, where the far-right Sweden Democrats surged on an anti-EU platform, had put pressure on mainstream parties to show the EU delivered tangible benefits. The legislative process was remarkably fast: the Commission published its proposal on 21 December 2017, the European Parliament and Council reached political agreement in June 2018, and the directive was formally adopted in June 2019 — just 18 months from proposal to adoption, unusually swift for social policy. The key breakthrough was the Parliament's Employment and Social Affairs Committee vote in April 2018, where a cross-party compromise on zero-hour contracts was adopted by 35 votes to 7, with 1 abstention.

What changed the political calculus was a series of high-profile court cases and scandals. In 2016, the UK Court of Appeal ruled in *Uber BV v. Aslam* that Uber drivers were "workers" entitled to minimum wage and holiday pay — a ruling that sent shockwaves through EU platform companies. In 2017, the Dutch Supreme Court ruled that Deliveroo riders were employees, not independent contractors. The European Committee of Social Rights found in 2015 that Greece's labour law reforms violated the European Social Charter by abolishing sectoral collective agreements. These cases stripped away the argument that platform work was a positive new form of flexibility, showing instead that it was a mechanism for avoiding basic labour law. The Commission's own 2017 public consultation on the European Pillar of Social Rights received over 16,000 responses, with 71% of individual citizens saying they felt insecure about their working conditions — figures that were widely reported and shifted public opinion.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Written Statement Directive (91/533/EEC) adopted, requiring employers to provide written information on working conditions within two months
- **2008-2015** — Post-financial crisis sees explosive growth of non-standard work; Eurofound reports nearly 40% of new EU jobs are non-standard
- **July 2015** — European Committee of Social Rights rules Greek labour reforms violate the European Social Charter
- **November 2015** — European Commission's *Better Regulation* process begins evaluation of the 1991 directive
- **October 2016** — UK Court of Appeal rules in *Uber v. Aslam* that Uber drivers are workers
- **November 2017** — European Commission publishes European Pillar of Social Rights at Göteborg Summit
- **21 December 2017** — Commission proposes Transparent and Predictable Working Conditions Directive
- **April 2018** — European Parliament's Employment Committee votes 35-7 for the compromise text
- **June 2018** — Parliament and Council reach political agreement
- **11 June 2019** — Directive 2019/1152 formally adopted by Council and Parliament
- **1 August 2019** — Directive enters into force
- **1 August 2022** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law
- **2023-2024** — Commission begins infringement proceedings against Member States that missed the transposition deadline; at least 12 countries had not fully transposed by mid-2023

## Impact and consequences

The directive is still in its early implementation phase, but its effects are already visible. In Ireland, the transposing legislation — the Employment (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2021 — abolished zero-hour contracts nearly entirely for most workers, requiring employers to pay for cancelled shifts and banning exclusivity clauses outright. The Irish law forced companies like Domino's Pizza and Burger King to overhaul their scheduling practices, with one large franchise operator telling the Irish Times that its labour costs rose by 8% as a result.

In France, the directive accelerated the *Loi pour la liberté de choisir son avenir professionnel* (2018), which already required platform companies to provide social protection contributions for workers. The French government extended the directive's transparency obligations to platform workers in January 2023, requiring platforms like Uber Eats and Deliveroo to display estimated earnings and working hours before a worker accepts a delivery. The German government's transposition law, the *Nachweisgesetz*, took effect on 1 August 2022 and immediately sparked legal challenges from employer associations representing the hospitality sector, who argued the seven-day notice requirement for shift cancellations was impractical for restaurants. The Federal Labour Court is expected to rule on the constitutionality of the probation cap provisions by 2025.

The platform companies have responded by restructuring their business models rather than complying directly. Uber introduced in 2022 a "Worker Information Centre" in its app across the EU, providing workers with a written statement of their relationship with Uber — but carefully describing them as "independent partners" rather than workers. Deliveroo announced it would exit the Dutch market in 2022, citing the cumulative burden of the directive and the 2021 Dutch *Wet arbeidsmarkt in balans* (Balanced Labour Market Act). The company's CEO, Will Shu, told investors the regulatory environment had become "challenging" for the platform model.

A 2023 study by the European Foundation for the Improvement of Living and Working Conditions (Eurofound) found that while the directive had been transposed in all 27 Member States, enforcement varied widely. Only nine countries had established a dedicated labour inspectorate unit for platform work; the Commission had opened infringement procedures against 12 Member States for incomplete or late transposition. The same study estimated that the directive would cover approximately 2 million previously unprotected workers across the EU — mostly platform workers, domestic workers, and temporary agency workers — but noted that the self-employed exemption continued to leave an estimated 4.5 million workers in the grey zone between employment and self-employment.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU rules on fair working conditions focus on giving workers written info when the real problem is making sure those rules are actually followed?

The directive deliberately uses transparency as a regulatory lever. The Commission's 2017 impact assessment noted that a 2015 Eurobarometer survey found 63% of EU workers did not know their basic employment rights — and that written information was a prerequisite for workers to understand and enforce those rights. Without a written statement from day one, a worker cannot prove their working hours, their probation period, or their pay rate in a legal claim. The directive's drafters assumed that enforcement would improve organically as workers became aware of their rights and could produce evidence.

### Why didn't EU rules on fair working conditions completely ban zero-hour contracts?

The political compromise was explicit: the EPP and employer groups would not accept a full ban, arguing that zero-hour contracts provided flexibility for students, retirees, and workers with care responsibilities. Instead, the directive imposed constraints that make zero-hour contracts less attractive: employers must compensate for cancelled shifts, cannot prevent workers from taking other jobs, and must provide a written statement of the "reference hours and days" — effectively forcing employers to declare a minimum commitment. This is a "soft ban" approach, similar to the UK's law after the 2016 *Uber* ruling, which requires zero-hour workers to be paid for cancelled shifts without notice.

### Why did it take 18 months to update EU worker information rules when the original 1991 version took 12 years?

The speed reflected a rare consensus between the Commission, Parliament, and Council. The Juncker Commission's emphasis on social fairness had strong backing from the German and French governments, both facing domestic pressure on precarious work. The 2017 Göteborg Summit had set a political deadline for delivering the Pillar of Social Rights before the 2019 European Parliament elections. And crucially, the opposition was disorganised: BusinessEurope's lobbying campaign failed to unite Member States, as several Eastern European countries — notably Poland and Hungary — actually supported stronger protections to reduce labour migration to Western Europe. The 2018 Portuguese presidency of the Council made social policy a priority, and the text was finalised under the Austrian presidency in June 2018 with only the Netherlands and Malta voting against.
