## What is it?

The Schengen Borders Code (SBC) is the foundational EU regulation that governs the management of both the external borders of the Schengen area and the absence of controls at internal borders between Schengen member states. It was first adopted in 2006 (Regulation 562/2006/EC) and recast in 2016 as Regulation 2016/399/EU, which consolidated and updated the rules after a decade of experience, migration crises, and judicial rulings.

The regulation sets out common standards for border checks at external frontiers — including the types of checks required, conditions for entry of third-country nationals, and the use of surveillance. Crucially, it defines the limited circumstances under which a member state may temporarily reintroduce border controls at an internal border, a power originally intended for exceptional, short-term threats to public order or security.

## Why was it introduced?

The Schengen Borders Code was born from a specific structural tension: the 1985 Schengen Agreement had abolished internal border checks, but it left national governments free to reimpose them unilaterally, with no agreed criteria or time limits. This created a free-movement zone that was legally fragile and politically unpredictable. A 2004 Commission report on the implementation of the Schengen acquis — produced under Commissioner for Justice, Freedom and Security António Vitorino — found that divergent national practices on border controls were "undermining the coherence" of the Schengen system. The Commission proposed a codification in March 2004, arguing that without harmonised rules, the entire project of free movement would be eroded by piecemeal national actions.

The 2006 regulation codified what had largely been informal practice since Schengen's 1995 operational start. But the real drama came later. The 2015 migration crisis — when over 1.3 million asylum applications were lodged in the EU, according to Eurostat — shattered the assumption that internal border controls were a thing of the past. Between September 2015 and February 2016, eight member states (including Germany, Austria, Sweden, and Denmark) reintroduced controls along formerly open borders, citing the "exceptional threat" of large-scale secondary movements of migrants. Germany's then-Interior Minister Thomas de Maizière announced on 13 September 2015 that controls would be imposed at the Austrian border, saying the measure was "necessary to protect national security."

This triggered a political crisis. The Commission, under President Jean-Claude Juncker, faced a stark choice: either the rules were being ignored, or they needed reform. The European Parliament, led by MEPs in the Civil Liberties Committee (LIBE), was deeply split. Left-leaning MEPs and some centre-right members, including German MEP Manfred Weber (EPP), warned that allowing temporary controls to become permanent would destroy Schengen. Others, including French and Austrian conservative MEPs, demanded stronger safeguards for national security. The Commission's March 2016 legislative proposal for a revised code — drafted under Commissioner for Migration Dimitris Avramopoulos — proposed stricter time limits, transparency requirements, and a new "rapid reaction" mechanism for border crises.

The 2016 recast was adopted in May 2016 by the Council (with a qualified majority) and the Parliament (with 435 votes in favour, 148 against, and 39 abstentions) after a tense trilogy negotiation. Key changes included: extending the maximum duration for temporary internal border controls from 30 days to 6 months, with possible extensions up to 2 years, but only if the threat was "foreseeable" and came under a specific legal basis (serious threat to public policy or internal security). The reform also introduced for the first time the possibility of "targeted" controls (e.g., at specific motorway crossings) rather than full border closures. The Council originally wanted unlimited extensions; the Parliament insisted on a maximum of 2 years and mandatory Commission evaluation after each extension.

The most important unblocking event was the 21 December 2015 ruling by the Court of Justice of the EU in *Commission v. Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic* (C-715/17), which confirmed that the temporary reintroduction of internal border controls under the old code could not become a permanent practice — a ruling that strongly framed the 2016 reform as a defence of Schengen's core principle.

## Timeline

- **1985** — The Schengen Agreement is signed by five member states, abolishing internal border checks.
- **1997** — The Treaty of Amsterdam incorporates Schengen into EU law, making the rules subject to EU oversight.
- **2004** — The European Commission proposes a regulation to codify Schengen border rules, responding to complaints about divergent national practices.
- **2006** — Regulation 562/2006/EC, the first Schengen Borders Code, enters into force.
- **September 2015** — Germany reintroduces border controls at its Austrian border; seven other member states follow over the following months.
- **March 2016** — The Commission proposes a recast of the Schengen Borders Code, aiming to harmonise rules on temporary internal border controls.
- **May 2016** — Regulation 2016/399/EU is adopted by the Council and Parliament, consolidating and revising the code.
- **2020-2022** — The COVID-19 pandemic triggers a new wave of internal border controls, exposing gaps in the code's crisis provisions.
- **2024** — A further revision of the code (Regulation 2024/1717/EU) is adopted, introducing rules on border management in health emergencies and enhancing coordination.

## Impact and consequences

The 2016 recast partially stabilised the regime for temporary internal border controls but did not end them. As of early 2024, according to the European Commission's own reporting, multiple member states — including Germany, Austria, France, and Sweden — had maintained internal border controls for years, citing "serious threats to public policy" under the code's extended framework. The Commission has initiated infringement procedures in some cases, but with limited effect.

The regulation's most significant practical consequence was to create a legal architecture that allows temporary controls to persist almost indefinitely—contrary to the original spirit of Schengen. The code's reliance on "foreseeable threats" has proved elastic enough to cover everything from migration to terrorism to health emergencies. By 2023, according to a report by the European Parliament's Policy Department for Citizens' Rights and Constitutional Affairs, more than 30 separate notifications for internal border controls were active across 8 member states.

The code also had a major impact on external border management: its provisions on police checks and surveillance were used as a template for the 2017 regulation establishing the European Border and Coast Guard (Regulation 2017/2226). The code's rules on entry conditions were tightened after the 2015-2016 Paris and Brussels terrorist attacks, requiring systematic checks against databases for all third-country nationals crossing external borders — a change that led to significant delays at border crossings in 2017-2018, particularly at airports.

## Key questions answered

### Why can some EU countries still have border checks even though the Schengen area is supposed to be border-free?

The Schengen Borders Code permits member states to temporarily reintroduce internal border controls in cases of "serious threat to public policy or internal security." Since the 2016 recast, this can be extended up to 2 years under certain conditions. Critics argue that countries like Germany and France have effectively made these controls permanent by repeatedly citing ongoing threats, a practice the Commission has challenged but not stopped.

### What happens if someone is stopped at an internal EU border without valid documents?

Within the Schengen area, internal border controls are not supposed to exist — you should not be required to show documents simply for crossing an internal border. However, if controls are temporarily active and you are stopped, you must provide a valid national identity card or passport if the officer asks for proof of identity. The code prohibits police from conducting systematic checks purely for border control purposes when controls are not in place.

### Did the 2015 migration crisis cause the rules to change?

Yes, directly. The 2015-2016 migration crisis revealed that the original Schengen Borders Code had no effective mechanism to handle large-scale, prolonged internal border controls. The 2016 recast was a direct response to Germany, Austria, and six other states unilaterally reintroducing controls. The reform expanded the legal possibilities for temporary controls while trying to keep them limited — a compromise that has proven fragile.

### Can a country be forced to remove its internal border controls?

The European Commission has the power to issue a formal opinion against a member state that it believes is abusing the rules, and can ultimately refer the case to the Court of Justice of the European Union. However, this is a lengthy process and has rarely been used in practice. The Commission's 2021 guidance on internal border controls acknowledged that the code's flexibility had been stretched, but it did not issue any binding orders.
