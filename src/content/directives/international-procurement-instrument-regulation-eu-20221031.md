## What is it?

The International Procurement Instrument is an EU regulation that gives the European Commission the legal authority to impose "measures" — ranging from price penalties to outright exclusion from bids — on companies from third countries that maintain restrictive or discriminatory procurement practices against EU businesses. It is targeted, sector-specific retaliation tool, not a blanket ban.

The IPI applies to public procurement procedures above certain value thresholds (€15 million for works and concessions, €5 million for goods and services). The Commission investigates complaints or own-initiative cases to determine whether a specific foreign measure restricts EU access. If confirmed, it can impose a "IPI score adjustment" — effectively a 20-50% price penalty on bids from that country's companies, or exclude them entirely from specific EU tenders.

## Why was it introduced?

The IPI was born from two decades of failed diplomacy and a single trade statistic that successive European Commission president, José Manuel Barroso, Jean-Claude Juncker, and Ursula von der Leyen, all found politically untenable: the EU had opened roughly €2 trillion of its procurement market annually to global bidders, while major trading partners — notably China — kept their own markets virtually closed. A 2019 Commission staff working document estimated that only €12 billion of EU procurement was won by non-EU companies, a tiny fraction of total EU procurement, while EU companies secured practically nothing from Chinese public tenders.

The structural tension was stark: the EU's own procurement directives, particularly those from 2014, had liberalized access unilaterally without reciprocity. The underlying logic — that liberalization by example would be reciprocated — had failed.

The political breakthrough came in 2021, driven by a coalition of industrial member states — Germany, France, Italy, and Spain — who faced growing frustration from their export champions. Siemens, Alstom, and Vinci had watched Chinese competitors win high-speed rail and infrastructure contracts in non-EU markets under opaque bidding rules. Meanwhile, Chinese telecom equipment giant Huawei had successfully participated in EU 5G tenders, triggering security concerns that further poisoned the reciprocity debate.

Timing was crucial. The COVID-19 pandemic had exposed EU dependency on external suppliers and protectionist moves by the US and China. But the specific event that unblocked years of deadlock was the political shift in the European Parliament and Council under the von der Leyen Commission. In a June 2021 Council meeting, Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton — a former French industry minister — personally pushed for the file's prioritization, arguing that the EU's 'open but assertive' trade agenda required tangible, not rhetorical, instruments.

The Parliament had supported the IPI earlier in 2021, but the Council had long been divided. The key opposition came from Nordic member states — especially Denmark and Sweden — and from the Netherlands, who argued the instrument would invite retaliation and undermine the EU's WTO commitments. Germany had previously been cautious, but by 2021, after years of failed WTO reform talks and increasing Chinese state-directed procurement, Berlin switched to supporting the measure. The final Council vote in June 2022 was not unanimous — Denmark and Luxembourg voted against — but the qualified majority held.

Three earlier attempts to pass a similar law had failed: a 2012 Commission proposal stalled in the Parliament due to concerns about legal complexity and trade relations; a 2016 revised version died in the Council because Germany and the UK blocked it; and a 2019 attempt was derailed by the Brexit negotiations and a new Parliament election.

## Timeline

- **2012** — European Commission first proposes a regulation on access of third-country goods and services to the EU's internal market in public procurement
- **2016** — Commission revises the proposal with a more targeted approach, but it stalls in Council
- **2019** — Juncker Commission attempts revival; Brexit and European elections halt progress
- **2021** — European Parliament adopts its position on the IPI in May; Council reaches general approach in June after German government switches support
- **2022** — Regulation formally adopted by Council and Parliament votes in March; published in Official Journal on June 29; enters into force on August 29
- **2023** — Commission publishes implementing regulation on IPI investigations and operational details
- **2024** — First notifications from member states about potentially discriminatory foreign measures

## Impact and consequences

The IPI's actual trade impact remains unproven as of late 2024. No IPI investigation has yet concluded, though the Commission has received several informal notifications from member states about foreign practices. Deutsche Bahn and SNCF have privately warned that the instrument could provoke retaliation from China, which might block their future bids for Chinese high-speed rail projects.

The most significant consequence has been diplomatic: China's Mission to the EU formally protested the regulation in early 2023, describing it as "protectionism dressed up as reciprocity." The instrument has also emboldened EU trade hawks, who now push for similar reciprocity tools in other areas, including foreign subsidies and investment screening.

The main critics — Denmark, Sweden, and the Netherlands — warned that the IPI could alienate WTO partners and harm the EU's credibility as a rules-based trade advocate. But the Commission's 2021 impact assessment countered that without such a tool, EU companies would continue losing an estimated €24 billion in procurement opportunities abroad annually.

The instrument's design deliberately avoids a US-style "Buy American" approach: it targets specific countries and sectors, not all foreign bidders. As EU Trade Commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis stated in a 2022 press conference, "This is a tool for negotiation, not for closure."

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU pass a law to block foreign companies from bidding on government contracts?

Because European companies were systematically locked out of major public procurement markets in China, India, and other large economies, while those same non-EU companies could freely bid on EU contracts. The €2 trillion gap between the value of the EU's open procurement and the minuscule share won by non-EU bidders showed the lack of reciprocity. The EU needed a threat — credible retaliation — to negotiate better access abroad.

### Does this law target specific countries?

Yes, but not by name. The IPI is triggered by Commission investigations into specific foreign measures that restrict EU access to a particular country's procurement sector. In practice, the regulatory impact assessment and political debate focused almost entirely on China, whose state-directed procurement market is largely closed to foreign firms. India and several Gulf states were also cited as discriminatory.

### Will this law slow down public infrastructure projects in the EU?

Potentially, but only in a small number of cases. The IPI only applies to contracts above €15 million for works and €5 million for goods and services, meaning it targets large infrastructure projects — bridges, railways, hospitals. The Commission has promised streamlined investigations to minimize delays. Industry associations like BusinessEurope support the IPI, arguing that unclear procurement rules abroad cause more damage to EU companies than any retaliation the IPI might provoke.

### Could these EU procurement rules be challenged at the WTO?

It depends how it is applied. The EU argues the IPI is compatible with the WTO's Government Procurement Agreement (GPA) because it targets only countries that are not GPA signatories or that have specific discriminatory measures. China is not a GPA party. However, if the EU imposed penalties on a country that is a GPA member (like India, which is an observer), legal challenges would be likely. The Commission's 2021 legal assessment acknowledged this risk and designed the IPI with graduated, proportional measures to minimize it.
