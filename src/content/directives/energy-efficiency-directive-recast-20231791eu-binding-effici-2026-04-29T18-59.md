## What is it?

The recast Energy Efficiency Directive (EU 2023/1791) is a binding legislative framework that obliges EU member states to reduce their final energy consumption by 11.7% by 2030 compared to the 2020 reference scenario projections. This translates to a maximum EU final energy consumption of 763 million tonnes of oil equivalent (Mtoe) and a primary energy consumption of 992.5 Mtoe. The directive entered into force on 10 October 2023, replacing the 2018 version (2018/2002/EU).

At its core, the directive enshrines the "energy efficiency first" principle as a legally binding obligation. This means EU countries, when making policy, investment, or regulatory decisions in energy, transport, buildings, and industry, must prioritise energy efficiency measures before considering other supply-side solutions. The directive also requires member states to deliver annual energy savings of 1.49% of final energy consumption from 2024 to 2030 — nearly double the previous 0.8% requirement. It extends national energy-saving obligations to the public sector, requiring yearly renovation of at least 3% of the total floor area of public buildings, and mandates that large enterprises (excluding SMEs) implement an energy management system.

## Why was it introduced?

The recast was driven by a structural failure: the EU's pre-2021 energy efficiency framework was falling far short of what was needed to meet the Paris Agreement goals. The 2018 EED had set a 32.5% efficiency improvement target for 2030, but the Commission's 2020 impact assessment showed that with existing policies, the EU was on track for only about 29% — a gap of 3–4 percentage points. More damningly, the European Environment Agency's 2021 monitoring report found that final energy consumption had actually *increased* in 2021 year-on-year, driven by post-COVID economic recovery.

The political breakthrough came from a perfect storm. The Commission President Ursula von der Leyen had launched the European Green Deal in December 2019, but the 2018 EED was still locked into the earlier 2030 climate framework. The key inflection point was July 2021, when the Commission presented the "Fit for 55" package — a sweeping legislative overhaul to align EU climate and energy law with the newly-agreed 55% emissions reduction target for 2030 (compared to 1990 levels). This package included the proposal for the EED recast, tabled by Energy Commissioner Kadri Simson (Estonia, RE). The timing was shaped by the European Climate Law (2021/1119), which for the first time gave binding legal force to the 55% target. Without the EED recast, the EU would have had no adequate legal mechanism to deliver those reductions.

The coalition that secured the recast was the standard Green Deal alliance: the Socialists & Democrats (S&D), the Greens/EFA, and the Renew Europe group in the European Parliament, backed by the Commission and a qualified majority of member states in the Council. Lead MEP Niels Fuglsang (Denmark, S&D) pushed for the binding 11.7% target, which was $1.5 billion in annual energy savings, according to Commission estimates. Opposition came from conservative and far-right groups (EPP made some concessions, while ECR and ID voted against), arguing that binding targets would hurt industrial competitiveness. Poland, Hungary, and Bulgaria were the most vocal member-state opponents in Council, warning of high implementation costs.

Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine on 24 February 2022 dramatically accelerated the timeline. The plan to save 11.7% of energy — equivalent to nearly 150 million tonnes of oil — was recast as a geopolitical necessity to reduce dependence on Russian gas. The Commission’s REPowerEU communication in May 2022 explicitly tied the EED recast to security of supply. By autumn 2022, what had been a sluggish legislative process turned urgent: the European Council’s October 2022 conclusions called for "rapid adoption" of the efficiency directive. The co-legislators reached a provisional deal in March 2023, and the final text was approved by the Parliament on 11 July 2023 (471 votes to 147) and by the Council on 25 July 2023.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate effect is that all 27 member states must submit updated National Energy and Climate Plans (NECPs) with binding efficiency targets. As of mid-2024, only 10 countries had submitted draft updates — Germany, France, and Italy faced Commission warnings about delays. The 1.49% annual savings obligation (up from 0.8%) means that hospitals, universities, and central government buildings must undergo annual audits and 3% floor-area renovations.

Industry response has been mixed. The European Alliance to Save Energy (EU-ASE) and the Coalition for Energy Savings praised the binding obligations. In contrast, BusinessEurope and the Confederation of European Paper Industries (CEPI) warned that the 1.49% target could increase compliance costs for energy-intensive industries by €3–5 billion annually, according to the Commission's 2021 impact assessment, though the final text softened requirements for SMEs. Large enterprises with over €50 million annual turnover must now implement certified energy management systems (ISO 50001 or equivalent) or face penalties determined by member states.

For the public sector, the 3% annual renovation requirement for public buildings is expected to trigger €10–15 billion in renovations across the EU by 2030, according to the Buildings Performance Institute Europe (BPIE). The directive also mandates priority to "energy efficiency first" in all national planning, meaning new gas-fired power plants, for example, must undergo efficiency screening before approval.

The first enforcement test came in early 2024, when the Commission opened infringement proceedings against seven member states — including Spain, Romania, and Bulgaria — for failing to transpose the 2018 EED provisions on metering and billing, a precursor to the recast's own tougher obligations.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU suddenly require member states to cut energy use by 11.7%?

The 11.7% target emerged from the Commission's 2021 modelling showing that existing voluntary approaches were insufficient to meet the EU's legally binding 55% emissions cut by 2030. The Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022 transformed this technical goal into a geopolitical necessity: reducing energy consumption directly cut demand for Russian gas. The target was calculated against the 2020 reference scenario, not actual 2020 consumption, to avoid penalising countries that had already improved efficiency.

### Does every company in the EU now need an energy audit?

No. Only "large enterprises" — defined as companies with over 250 employees and annual turnover above €50 million — must implement an energy management system and conduct regular audits. SMEs (small and medium-sized enterprises) are exempt from mandatory audits, though member states are encouraged to provide them with free or subsidised energy audits. The directive's annual savings obligations fall on member states, not individual businesses, though states can (and do) pass the burden onto larger companies via national legislation.

### How can the EU enforce the "energy efficiency first" principle?

The "efficiency first" principle is now a legally binding obligation, not just a political slogan. Member states must demonstrate in their NECPs how they prioritised energy efficiency over supply-side investments in major infrastructure decisions. If a country builds a new power plant without showing it studied efficiency alternatives first, the Commission could launch infringement proceedings at the Court of Justice of the EU. However, the principle is procedural, not absolute — a country can still build a gas plant if it can prove efficiency measures were infeasible. The first test case came in 2023 when Poland's plans for new gas units faced Commission scrutiny on these grounds.

### What happens if a country doesn't meet its 1.49% annual savings target?

Member states face the full enforcement machinery of EU law. If the Commission determines a country is falling short, it can issue a formal notice, followed by a reasoned opinion, and ultimately refer the case to the Court of Justice of the EU. The Court can impose lump-sum fines or daily penalty payments — typically ranging from €10,000 to over €100,000 per day, calculated based on the country's GDP and voting weight. In theory, repeated non-compliance could lead to suspension of EU funds, though this has never been applied. The real enforcement pressure comes from the reporting cycle: countries that miss their 2026 interim targets face immediate Commission scrutiny.
