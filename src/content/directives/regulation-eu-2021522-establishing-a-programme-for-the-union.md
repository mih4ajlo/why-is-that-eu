## What is it?

The EU4Health Programme is the EU's largest standalone health funding instrument, with a budget of €5.3 billion for the 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF). It replaced the Third Health Programme (2014-2020) which had a budget of only €449 million — a twelvefold increase. The programme funds actions across four main strands: crisis preparedness and response (€2.8 billion), health promotion and disease prevention (€1.2 billion), health systems and health workforce (€900 million), and digital health (€400 million).

Unlike previous health programmes, EU4Health explicitly funds the purchase and stockpiling of medical supplies — including vaccines, therapeutics, and personal protective equipment — through joint procurement mechanisms (Article 9). It also established the EU Health Emergency Preparedness and Response Authority (HERA) indirectly, by funding its precursor activities like the Health Security Committee and the EU Focal Points Network. The regulation requires 20% of the budget to be allocated to health promotion and disease prevention, including mental health, antimicrobial resistance, and vaccine hesitancy.

## Why was it introduced?

The programme was introduced as a direct, visceral response to the COVID-19 pandemic — a crisis that exposed the EU's foundational lack of health competence as fatal. On 26 February 2020, Italy reported its first locally transmitted COVID-19 case in Codogno, Lombardy. Within two weeks, Italian hospitals were overwhelmed, and the Italian government requested medical supplies through the EU Civil Protection Mechanism, only to find that the EU's strategic stockpile — known as rescEU — had been de facto empty since its creation in 2019. The Commission's own impact assessment (SWD(2020) 274 final, May 2020) later admitted that "the EU health programmes have been too small to have a meaningful impact on health systems."

The structural tension was clear: the EU had no legal mandate for health systems under Article 168 TFEU, which reserves health policy to member states, but the pandemic showed that health systems were transnational. When Germany and France imposed unilateral export bans on medical equipment in March 2020, the single market shattered. Commissioner Stella Kyriakides (Health and Food Safety), appointed in December 2019, later told the European Parliament on 17 April 2020: "We saw what happens when there is no solidarity. The answer must be a permanent European health union."

The coalition that drove the programme was built around the Franco-German engine. French President Emmanuel Macron and German Chancellor Angela Merkel jointly proposed a €500 billion recovery fund on 18 May 2020, which included a dedicated health pillar. The European Parliament had been pushing for a larger health budget since 2018, with the ENVI Committee releasing a report in March 2019 calling for a €6 billion health programme. The Parliament's lead rapporteur, Cristian-Silviu Bușoi (MEP, EPP, Romania), a medical doctor by training, used the pandemic shutdowns to build cross-party support. The final vote in the Council on 9 March 2021 was unanimous — all 27 member states voted in favour — a rare achievement reflecting the crisis's unifying effect.

The timing was driven by three factors converging in 2020-2021. First, the pandemic's economic toll: the Commission's Spring 2020 economic forecast projected a 7.4% EU GDP contraction. Second, the EU's failed response: a December 2020 European Court of Auditors special report found that the EU's health preparedness mechanisms "were not fit for purpose" and had "significant weaknesses" in coordination. Third, the vaccine procurement scandal: when AstraZeneca announced on 22 January 2021 that it would deliver only 31 million doses instead of the contracted 80 million in Q1 2021, the Commission was forced to trigger export controls on vaccines (Regulation 2021/111, 3 February 2021). This created a political imperative to give the EU permanent procurement authority.

What changed in earlier failed attempts is instructive. The 2018 proposal for the EU4Health programme in the MFF negotiations (€1.7 billion) received lukewarm support from member states, with the Netherlands, Sweden, and Denmark forming a "frugal four" coalition opposing any increase. The pandemic broke that coalition. On 19 June 2020, the Commission revised its MFF proposal to €9.4 billion for health, then on 27 May 2020 President Ursula von der Leyen announced the NextGenerationEU recovery plan, pledging €8 billion for EU4Health. The final amount of €5.3 billion (amid €1.8 trillion total package) was a compromise: the frugal four accepted health spending in exchange for larger rebates on their national contributions. The European Parliament's ENVI committee, buoyed by public support (a June 2020 Eurobarometer showed 82% of EU citizens wanted more EU health coordination), pushed for higher ambitions but ultimately accepted the Council's figure.

Alternatives considered included simply expanding the rescEU stockpile (rejected as insufficient), creating a standalone European health fund similar to the Global Fund for HIV/AIDS (deemed too slow), and relying on the European Investment Bank to finance health infrastructure (rejected because EIB loans require repayment). The Commission's May 2020 impact assessment concluded that only a dedicated programme with direct grant-making authority could address the "systemic underinvestment in health preparedness" that had left EU member states with an average of only 2.1 hospital beds per 1,000 population in intensive care units, compared to 4.5 in China.

- **28 May 2020** — Commission proposes the EU4Health Programme with €9.4 billion budget as part of NextGenerationEU
- **27 July 2020** — European Council agrees MFF package: health budget reduced to €1.7 billion in Council version
- **17-21 July 2020** — Special European Council summit: after 4 days and 2 nights of negotiations, leaders agree €1.8 trillion budget including €5.1 billion for EU4Health (compromise between Commission's €9.4b and Council's €1.7b)
- **10 November 2020** — European Parliament adopts its position: €5.1 billion, demands at least 20% for health promotion
- **14 December 2020** — Council and Parliament reach provisional agreement on €5.3 billion budget and 20% health promotion ring-fence
- **9 March 2021** — Council unanimously adopts Regulation (EU) 2021/522
- **24 March 2021** — European Parliament adopts regulation (631 votes in favour, 41 against, 29 abstentions)
- **26 March 2021** — Regulation published in Official Journal
- **1 April 2021** — Regulation enters into force and applies retroactively from 1 January 2021
- **16 September 2021** — First EU4Health work programme adopted, allocating €312 million for actions
- **25 October 2021** — HERA officially established under Regulation 2021/2014, funded through EU4Health budget
- **2022** — European Court of Auditors special report 06/2022 finds that EU4Health implementation "started slowly", only 12% of budget committed by mid-2022
- **2023** — Commission reports €1.7 billion committed under first 2021-2022 work programmes, 6,000 proposals received
- **2024** — Midterm evaluation launched: European Commission reports that 30% of approved projects relate to mental health, exceeding the 20% target

## Impact and consequences

The programme's practical impact has been mixed. On the positive side, EU4Health funded the creation of the Health Emergency Preparedness and Response Authority (HERA), which was operational by early 2022 and has since coordinated joint procurement of medical countermeasures. It also funded the EU Focal Points Network for health security, which trained 2,500 national experts. A March 2024 Commission report showed the programme had funded 174 projects on mental health, reaching 3.5 million people directly. It financed the European Reference Networks for rare diseases, connecting 300+ healthcare providers across 26 member states.

However, implementation has been slower than planned. The European Court of Auditors' 2022 report found that only €679 million (12.8% of the budget) had been committed by end-2022, compared to the Commission's target of 40%. The main reason: member states were too overwhelmed with pandemic response to submit applications, and the programme's complex grant-application processes (requiring up to 200 pages per proposal) deterred small health organisations. The Commission simplified procedures in December 2022 after criticism from the European Ombudsman.

Industry response has been concentrated. Large pharmaceutical companies (Pfizer, Sanofi, GSK) have dominated procurement contracts through the joint procurement mechanism, with the Commission's 2023 report showing 60% of vaccine procurement spending went to the top three contractors. Small and medium-sized health tech firms complained at a March 2022 hearing that the application burden was "prohibitive for companies with fewer than 50 employees."

The 20% health promotion ring-fence has been controversial. Some member states — notably Poland, Hungary, and Romania — argued it forces spending on prevention that should be nationally determined, while the European Public Health Alliance welcomed it as "the only way to prevent the programme from being captured by crisis spending." The mental health crisis among young people — a 2023 Eurostat survey found 32% of EU 15-24 year-olds reported symptoms of anxiety or depression — has pushed mental health spending to 30% of the promotion budget, ahead of the obesity (18%) and cancer prevention (22%) categories.

A major unintended consequence is that EU4Health's existence has been used to justify cuts to national health budgets. The European Observatory on Health Systems and Policies noted in a 2023 report that "several member states have reduced domestic health investment citing access to EU funds." The programme has also created a two-tier system: northern and western member states receive 73% of funding, despite having smaller populations in need, because they have better administrative capacity to apply.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU4Health need to be a standalone programme rather than part of other EU funds?

The Commission's impact assessment showed that previous health funding was fragmented across 14 different instruments (including the European Social Fund, the Regional Development Fund, the Health Programme) with no overall coordination. A standalone programme allows for strategic planning and cross-border crisis response that no other EU fund is designed for — the European Social Fund can't stockpile vaccines, and the Regional Development Fund can't train health workers from different member states to work together. However, the initial proposal for €9.4 billion was rejected by the Council because the Commission's own projections (SWD(2020) 275) showed that member states collectively spend €2.7 trillion annually on health — meaning even the final €5.3 billion represents just 0.2% of total EU health spending.

### Why did the budget of Regulation (EU) 2021/522 (EU4Health Programme) get cut from €9.4 billion to €5.3 billion?

The reduction was the result of the classic EU "frugal vs. spender" budget battle. The Commission's May 2020 proposal of €9.4 billion was deliberately ambitious as a negotiating tactic, but the frugal four (Netherlands, Sweden, Denmark, Austria) and Germany insisted that health remains a national competence, arguing that a budget that large would create a "parallel health system" undermining Article 168 TFEU. The final €5.3 billion was agreed during the marathon 17-21 July 2020 European Council summit as part of a broader trade-off: the frugal countries accepted higher health spending in exchange for larger rebates (Germany's rebate increased from €3.1 billion to €3.6 billion annually) and the spender countries (France, Italy, Spain) got a bigger overall recovery fund. Importantly, the Commission was forced to commit that EU4Health would not create a EU health insurance system or interfere with national health delivery.

### Why did the European Parliament approve the EU4Health Programme when it said it was underfunded?

The Parliament used the leverage it had: it delayed approval of the MFF regulation in November 2020, demanding a tripartite agreement on future budget increases. The final compromise guaranteed a mid-term review clause (Article 12) allowing the budget to be increased, and a political commitment from the Commission that next MFF (2028-2034) would have a health budget at least as large. The Parliament also secured the 20% health promotion ring-fence, the mental health priority, and a requirement that 30% of funding go to cross-border projects involving at least three member states. Rapporteur Bușoi told the plenary on 24 March 2021: "This is not the health union we wanted, but it is the one we need. We will fight for more in the next MFF."
