## What is it?

The Return Directive sets out the common rules across the EU for how Member States must handle the return of third-country nationals who are staying illegally. It establishes a procedure that includes a voluntary departure period (7 to 30 days), a formal return decision, an entry ban (up to five years), and a removal operation as a last resort. The directive also introduces safeguards: the right to appeal return decisions, a ban on removal when there is a serious risk of harm, and restrictions on the use of detention.

The directive applies to any non-EU national who does not, or no longer, meets the conditions for entry, stay, or residence in an EU Member State. It covers the entire return process from detection to removal, including legal remedies and detention conditions. It explicitly excludes those who have a right to EU free movement or who are refused entry at external borders.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to the chaotic and divergent national policies on return that emerged after the 2004 EU enlargement and the rising number of irregular migrants entering the bloc. The immediate trigger was a series of high-profile diplomatic crises, including the 2005 Ceuta and Melilla incidents where Spanish and Moroccan forces forcibly removed hundreds of sub-Saharan migrants, leading to at least 14 deaths and global condemnation. These events exposed the lack of common EU standards for return, allowing Member State practices to vary wildly from humane voluntary returns to summary expulsions.

The structural tension was between the free movement area (Schengen) and the absence of a common return policy. The 2005 Hague Programme, adopted under the Dutch Presidency, explicitly called for "common standards for the return of third-country nationals" to prevent "forum shopping" by irregular migrants and to reduce diplomatic fallout from unilateral deportations. The Commission, led by Commissioner Franco Frattini (Justice, Freedom and Security, 2004-2008), drafted the directive arguing that existing national laws created "legal uncertainty" for migrants and "inefficiency" for Member States.

The coalition that passed it was a centre-right majority in the European Parliament (EPP, ALDE) supported by the Council of interior ministers from the major destination countries (Germany, France, UK, Italy, Spain). The main opposition came from the Greens/EFA and the GUE/NGL groups in Parliament, who warned the directive would legitimise long-term detention and weaken legal protections. In the Council, Sweden and Finland raised concerns about detention limits, but the larger states prevailed. The directive passed the European Parliament on 18 June 2008 by a vote of 369-197 with 107 abstentions, a relatively narrow margin that reflected deep ideological divisions.

The timing mattered: the 2007-2008 migration surge, with Eurostat reporting 1.2 million irregular apprehensions in 2007, made the directive politically urgent. The European Council explicitly linked the directive to the EU's credibility in migration management. The alternative considered was no EU regulation at all, which would have left the 2003 Return Action Programme (a non-binding framework) in place, but the 2005 Ceuta deaths made voluntary coordination politically untenable.

## Timeline

- **2005** — The Hague Programme (European Council) calls for common return standards after Ceuta/Melilla deaths.
- **2006** — Commission publishes a Green Paper on a common return policy; Commissioner Frattini proposes a formal directive.
- **2007** — European Parliament and Council negotiate; major sticking points are detention limits (6 vs 18 months) and the length of entry bans.
- **2008** — Directive 2008/115/EC adopted on 16 December, with a transposition deadline of 24 December 2010.
- **2010** — All Member States except the UK (which opted out) and Ireland (which secured opt-out) transpose the directive; Italy and Greece are among the last to comply.
- **2011** — The Court of Justice of the EU issues its first ruling on the directive (Arslan case, C-534/10), confirming that detention must not be automatic.
- **2014** — The Commission publishes its first implementation report, finding major gaps in transposition, especially on detention conditions and legal remedies.
- **2020** — The Commission's 2020 Action Plan on Integration and Inclusion references the directive but does not amend it.

## Impact and consequences

The directive standardised procedures across all 25 participating Member States, but implementation has been deeply uneven. In Spain, the optional 72-hour detention limit for irregular migrants at borders (introduced in 2009) was upheld by the European Court of Human Rights in 2013, but NGOs like the Spanish Commission for Refugees (CEAR) documented cases of indefinite *de facto* detention. In Italy, the 2011-2012 "Bossi-Fini" law was partially overturned by the Constitutional Court for being inconsistent with the directive's safeguards.

The biggest consequence was the European Court of Human Rights' 2012 judgment in *Hirsi Jamaa v. Italy* (the "pushback" case), where Italy was found to have violated the directive (and the ECHR) by summarily returning migrants to Libya without return decisions or appeal rights. The ruling forced Italy to change its naval interception policy.

In 2019, Frontex reported that only 38 EU Member States (of 27) had fully transposed the directive's detention provisions, with Bulgaria, Greece, and Hungary still using unlimited administrative detention in practice. The Commission's 2020 evaluation found that only 29% of return decisions were actually enforced, a persistent gap that undermines the directive's core purpose.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the directive allow detention for up to 18 months?

The 18-month cap was a compromise in the 2007-2008 negotiations. The Commission initially proposed 12 months, but Germany and France demanded longer to handle cases where migrants intentionally obstruct removal (e.g., destroying travel documents). The final text sets a six-month initial period, extendable by an additional 12 months only when removal is "likely to last longer" due to a lack of cooperation from the third-country national or delays in obtaining documents from third countries. The European Parliament had fought for a 6-month maximum, but the interior ministers won the extension.

### Why is the voluntary departure period only 7 to 30 days?

The 7-30 day period in Article 7 was designed to balance the migrant's right to prepare for return against the state's interest in quick removal. The Commission's 2006 impact assessment found that longer voluntary periods (like 60 days in France) led to higher rates of absconding (up to 40%). However, the directive allows Member States to impose shorter periods (including zero) if there is a risk of absconding, a threat to public policy, or if the application for legal stay was found to be clearly abusive. This loophole has been heavily criticised by the UN High Commissioner for Refugees.

### Why does the UK and Ireland opt out of this directive?

The Return Directive was adopted under Title IV of the TFEU (Area of Freedom, Security and Justice), which at the time allowed the UK, Ireland, and Denmark to opt out of any Justice and Home Affairs legislation. The UK formally opted out in 2009 under the Treaty of Lisbon protocols, preferring to keep its own "hostile environment" detention powers (up to 28 days for administrative detention) and faster removal processes. Ireland also opted out, while Denmark has a separate opt-out protocol.

### What happens when a migrant appeals a return decision?

Article 13 guarantees the right to an effective remedy before a competent judicial or administrative authority. The appeal must have suspensive effect—meaning the return decision is suspended pending the outcome—only if the Member State so decides. Critics (including the Council of Europe's Commissioner for Human Rights in 2013) argue this lack of automatic suspension violates Article 13 of the ECHR. The Court of Justice confirmed in 2015 (*Abdida*, C-562/13) that appeals against removal decisions must have automatic suspensive effect if the return would expose the migrant to a serious risk of irreparable harm.
