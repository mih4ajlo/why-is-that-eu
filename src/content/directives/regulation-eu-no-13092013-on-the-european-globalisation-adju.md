## What is it?

The European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF) is an EU solidarity instrument that co-finances active labour market measures — such as retraining, job-search assistance, and entrepreneurship grants — for workers made redundant by major structural changes in global trade patterns, or by the global financial and economic crisis. It is not a social benefit nor a severance payment; it covers up to 60% of the costs of personalised services, with Member States and employers typically topping up the rest.

Originally created under the 2006-2013 Multiannual Financial Framework, the EGF was renewed for 2014-2020 under Regulation (EU) No 1309/2013, which extended its scope to cover redundancies caused by the global financial and economic crisis, lowered the threshold for triggering applications, and introduced a crisis derogation.

## Why was it introduced?

The European Globalisation Adjustment Fund began as a political bargain struck at the 2005 Brussels European Council — a response to the deep anxiety over jobs lost to offshoring and competition from China, triggered by the end of the Multi-Fibre Arrangement in 2005 and the rapid rise of Asian manufacturing.

The specific trigger: in 2004, the textile and clothing sector in several EU member states — particularly in France, Italy, and Poland — experienced massive job losses after the World Trade Organization's Agreement on Textiles and Clothing phased out quotas. The European Commission's 2005 impact assessment noted over 75,000 textile jobs lost in the EU between 2002 and 2004, concentrated in regions like Nord-Pas-de-Calais in France and Lombardy in Italy. Member state governments, led by then-French President Jacques Chirac, demanded EU-level action to complement national labour market policies.

The structural tension was clear: EU trade liberalisation had created winners through cheaper imports and export opportunities, but the costs were concentrated in specific regions and sectors. Without a visible safety net, political support for further trade agreements — including the Doha Round and bilateral deals with Mercosur and South Korea — risked collapsing. The European Parliament, under the 1999-2004 legislature, had previously supported similar ideas in resolutions, citing the US Trade Adjustment Assistance programme as a model.

The coalition that pushed the EGF through was unusual. It united centre-right trade liberalisation advocates (who needed a compensation mechanism to sell further trade deals), social democrats and Greens (who saw it as a social justice measure), and regional governments (who wanted direct funding for declining industrial areas). Opponents included budget hawks in the Council — led by the Netherlands and the UK — who argued it duplicated the European Social Fund and risked creating moral hazard, and some industry groups that feared higher social costs. The initial proposal in 2005 required a special legislative procedure, and the 2006-2013 fund was only approved after the European Council agreed to cap its annual budget at €500 million.

The financial and economic crisis of 2008-2009 broke the political deadlock over the EGF's scope. Under the original 2006 regulation, the fund could only be activated for redundancies caused by "major structural changes in world trade patterns" — a condition that excluded workers laid off due to the crisis itself. When crisis-driven redundancies in sectors such as automotive, construction, and retail vastly exceeded trade-related ones — Eurostat recorded 24 million job losses across the EU from 2008 to 2013 — the Commission, under President José Manuel Barroso, proposed broadening the EGF's eligibility criteria. The Parliament, led by rapporteur Marian Harkin (ALDE, Ireland), pushed for a crisis derogation. The result was Regulation No 1309/2013, which allowed the EGF to cover redundancies from "the global financial and economic crisis" as a transitional measure through 2017.

What changed decisively: the 2013 regulation lowered the triggering threshold from 1,000 redundancies over four months to 500 redundancies over four months, made it easier to apply in smaller member states (where 500 workers might be a significant share of the labour force), and introduced a simpler application procedure. The Commission’s own evaluation in 2017 found that, between 2007 and 2016, over 135,000 workers had been supported across 146 EGF applications, with a total EU contribution of €619.4 million.

## Timeline

- **2004** — End of Multi-Fibre Arrangement triggers sharp textile job losses in EU; French President Chirac proposes EU compensation fund at European Council.
- **2005, March** — European Council agrees to create European Globalisation Adjustment Fund with annual budget of up to €500 million.
- **2006, December** — Regulation (EC) No 1927/2006 establishing the EGF for 2007-2013 enters into force.
- **2008-2009** — Global financial crisis causes mass layoffs; EGF unable to cover crisis-related redundancies under original rules.
- **2010, November** — European Commission issues first evaluation report noting limited uptake (only 20 applications by 2010); Parliament calls for scope expansion.
- **2013, December** — Regulation (EU) No 1309/2013 adopted, extending EGF to 2020, lowering threshold to 500 redundancies, and adding crisis derogation.
- **2014-2020** — EGF applications shift from textiles to automotive, construction, and retail sectors due to crisis derogation.
- **2017** — Commission evaluation finds EGF supported 135,000+ workers with €619 million EU contribution.
- **2019** — Final crisis derogation expires; EGF returns to trade-only trigger.
- **2021** — Under 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework, EGF is replaced by the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund for Displaced Workers (EGF) under Regulation 2021/1057, with a reduced annual budget cap of €186 million and continued trade-only trigger.

## Impact and consequences

The EGF's practical impact has been modest relative to the scale of job losses. The Commission's 2017 evaluation reported that, by 2016, only 146 of a possible 803 eligible applications had been made — a take-up rate of roughly 18%. Member states hesitated to apply due to administrative complexity, the need for co-financing, and political reluctance to appear unable to manage labour transitions domestically. Germany, France, and Italy accounted for over 60% of all applications, while smaller member states like Estonia and Cyprus rarely used the fund.

The fund's design has been criticised for being too rigid: it covers only personalised services (training, counselling, job-search support), not income replacement, making it unattractive to workers who need immediate financial support. The European Court of Auditors' 2012 special report noted that EGF co-financing often replaced, rather than supplemented, national spending — a deadweight loss.

The most consequential effect may have been political. The EGF served as a visible EU response to globalisation anxiety, helping to maintain broad support for trade liberalisation in the European Parliament and Council. When the EU negotiated free trade agreements with South Korea (2011), Canada (2017), and Mercosur (2019), advocates could point to the EGF as a compensation mechanism.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EGF have such low uptake among Member States under Regulation (EU) No 1309/2013 on the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (2014-2020)?

According to the Commission’s 2017 ex-post evaluation, the administrative burden of preparing an application — which required detailed documentation of redundancies, cost estimates, and a justification linking layoffs to trade patterns — deterred many Member States, particularly those with smaller administrations. Additionally, the requirement for 50% co-financing from national or employer sources made the fund less attractive compared to the European Social Fund, which could finance similar measures with lower matching requirements.

### Why was the crisis derogation removed in 2021 from Regulation (EU) No 1309/2013 on the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (2014-2020)?

The European Council and Parliament, negotiating the 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework, concluded that the COVID-19 pandemic and its economic effects were handled by the temporary Support to mitigate Unemployment Risks in an Emergency (SURE) instrument, not by the EGF. The EGF was refocused on its original trade-specific purpose, with a reduced budget (€186 million annually) and stricter eligibility criteria. The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment for the new EGF regulation argued that broader economic crises required dedicated instrument, not a repurposed trade-adjustment fund.

### Did the EGF actually help workers find new jobs under Regulation (EU) No 1309/2013 on the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (2014-2020)?

The Commission's 2017 evaluation found re-employment rates varied widely by application and sector. In automotive sector applications (e.g., Opel in Belgium, Ford in Spain), re-employment rates exceeded 70% within 18 months of programme completion. In textile applications (e.g., Northern Greece), re-employment rates fell below 40%, reflecting the collapse of the local sector without replacement jobs. The evaluation noted that EGF support was most effective when combined with regional development funds and active labour market policies.
