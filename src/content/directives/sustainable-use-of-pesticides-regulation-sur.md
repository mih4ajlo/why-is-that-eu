## What is it?

The Sustainable Use of Pesticides Regulation (SUR) was a legislative proposal by the European Commission, published on 22 June 2022, to replace the existing 2009 Sustainable Use Directive (2009/128/EC). It aimed to set legally binding, EU-wide targets: halving both the use and risk of chemical pesticides, and halving the use of more hazardous pesticides, by 2030, measured against a 2015-2017 baseline.

The regulation would have required all member states to adopt national reduction targets (with flexibility but a minimum 35% reduction), banned pesticide use entirely in "sensitive areas" like urban green spaces and protected nature zones, and mandated integrated pest management (IPM) as the default farming practice. Farmers would have been required to keep electronic records of pesticide applications, and national advisory services would have been expanded.

## Why was it introduced?

The SUR was a direct response to a triple crisis that had become undeniable by the early 2020s: collapsing insect and bird populations across Europe, rising public concern about pesticide residues in food and water, and the failure of the 2009 directive to deliver meaningful reductions.

The trigger for the specific, hard-edged regulatory design was not a single scandal but a slow-motion ecological collapse documented by science. The European Environment Agency's 2020 State of Nature report showed that only 15% of EU habitats were in favourable conservation status, with agricultural intensification—including pesticide use—identified as the primary driver. A landmark 2017 study in *PLOS ONE* by German researchers, widely covered in EU media, found a 75% decline in flying insect biomass in German nature reserves since 1989. The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) repeatedly flagged that over 90% of food samples tested for pesticide residues contained multiple substances, with chronic risks to human health, particularly for children.

Politically, the SUR was a flagship of the European Green Deal, championed by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in her 2019 political guidelines. It sat at the intersection of the Farm to Fork Strategy (published May 2020) and the Biodiversity Strategy for 2030. The key commissioner driving it was Frans Timmermans, Executive Vice-President for the Green Deal, who consistently framed pesticide reduction as non-negotiable for meeting the EU's climate and biodiversity targets.

The coalition supporting the SUR included the Greens/EFA group in the European Parliament, the Socialists and Democrats (S&D), and a segment of the liberal Renew Europe group. The European Environmental Bureau (EEB) and PAN Europe (Pesticide Action Network) were the primary NGO campaigners. They argued the 2009 directive had been toothless—member states set their own targets, and most barely cut usage at all. According to a 2020 European Commission evaluation, only a handful of member states (France, Denmark, the Netherlands) had adopted national action plans with measurable reduction goals.

Opposition was fierce and well-organised. Copa-Cogeca, the main EU farming lobby, argued the targets were impossible without collapsing food production. The European Crop Protection Association (now CropLife Europe), representing companies like Bayer, BASF, Syngenta, and Corteva, warned of a 20-30% drop in cereal yields based on their own modelling. Several Central and Eastern European member states—especially Poland, Hungary, Romania, and Bulgaria—objected to the "sensitive areas" ban, which they said would wipe out farming on millions of hectares.

The timing was shaped by a specific political factor: the European Parliament elections of June 2024. As the legislative process dragged through 2023, it became clear the SUR would not pass before the elections. In February 2024, the European Parliament's ENVI Committee voted 47-37 to reject the entire proposal, forcing it to the full plenary. The centre-right European People's Party (EPP) had turned against it, with lead MEP Peter Liese (EPP/Germany) leading a campaign for a much more flexible, guidelines-based approach. On 6 February 2024, the Commission officially withdrew the proposal, acknowledging it lacked sufficient support in both the Parliament and the Council. A 2023 Commission impact assessment had already estimated that full implementation would cost farmers €2.5-3 billion annually in lost yield and additional labour, though it projected long-term savings of €4 billion through reduced input costs and environmental damage.

## Timeline

- **2009** — The Sustainable Use Directive (2009/128/EC) is adopted, establishing a framework for national pesticide reduction plans, but with non-binding targets.
- **2017** — The *PLOS ONE* insect decline study shocks policymakers; EFSA publishes a report showing 97% of food samples contain multiple pesticide residues.
- **2019** — Ursula von der Leyen's European Green Deal targets a 50% reduction in chemical pesticides by 2030.
- **May 2020** — The Farm to Fork Strategy is published, explicitly committing to a legislative proposal for binding reduction targets.
- **June 2022** — The Commission publishes the draft SUR, with a 50% reduction target and mandatory IPM.
- **October 2023** — The European Parliament's ENVI Committee deadlocks; a compromise text is rejected.
- **February 2024** — The ENVI Committee votes 47-37 to reject the entire SUR proposal; the Commission withdraws it on 6 February 2024.
- **2024** — No successor regulation is proposed; the 2009 directive remains in force, with the Commission promising a "more collaborative approach" in future legislative proposals.

## Impact and consequences

The SUR never entered into force, so its direct impact is zero. However, its political consequences were substantial. Its withdrawal marked the first major legislative failure of the European Green Deal, and a significant loss for Timmermans, who left the Commission in August 2023 to lead the Dutch Green/Socialist coalition in the Dutch general election.

The failure shifted the EU policy landscape on pesticides permanently. Member states like France and Germany—which had already committed to national reduction targets (France's Écophyto 2030, Germany's 50% cut)—continued their own plans, but without EU enforcement. The practical consequence is that pesticide use in the EU has not declined. According to a 2023 Eurostat report, total pesticide sales in the EU declined by only 6% between 2011 and 2021, with insecticide sales actually rising. The SUR's failure reinforced the view among environmental groups that voluntary national targets are insufficient—and among farming groups that binding EU targets are politically impossible.

The episode revealed a structural fracture between the Commission's Green Deal ambitions and the political reality in member states and the Parliament. It also demonstrated the strength of the agricultural lobby: Copa-Cogeca and national farming unions coordinated an aggressive campaign, including farmer protests across multiple member states in early 2023, which directly influenced MEPs ahead of the 2024 elections.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe's proposed pesticide reduction rules fail politically when the Green Deal climate policies succeeded?

The SUR targeted millions of individual farmers—unlike the EU's climate regulations, which mostly target large industrial emitters and energy companies. Each farmer would have faced immediate, visible changes to their operations: banned spraying in large areas, mandatory IPM, electronic record-keeping. This created a much broader and more politically vocal opposition base. In contrast, the "Fit for 55" climate package directly affected a much smaller number of corporate entities and could be mediated through emissions trading and carbon pricing.

### Why couldn't the 2009 pesticide rules be made stricter instead of writing a whole new law?

The Commission and environmental groups argued the 2009 directive was fundamentally flawed: it required member states to create "national action plans" but set no binding targets. A 2020 Commission evaluation concluded that only a handful of member states had achieved measurable reductions, and that the directive lacked enforcement mechanisms. The SUR was designed to replace a "soft law" approach with "hard law"—setting EU-level binding targets that member states would have to meet or face infringement proceedings. This was precisely why it met such intense opposition.

### Would Europe's proposed pesticide reduction rules have actually worked in practice?

The Commission's 2022 impact assessment projected a 50% reduction in use and risk by 2030, saving farmers €4 billion annually in input costs and generating €3.5 billion in environmental and health benefits. But the assessment acknowledged significant uncertainty about farmer behaviour, yield impacts, and the availability of alternative pest control methods. EFSA's 2023 review of IPM data found that adoption remained below 20% in most member states, suggesting a massive behavioural and economic barrier to compliance.

### What happens now with the plan to cut pesticide use in Europe?

With the SUR withdrawn, the 2009 directive continues. The Commission, under President von der Leyen's second term (2024-2029), has signalled a willingness to drop binding targets and instead push for "voluntary national targets with a robust monitoring framework." The 2024-2029 Commission's political guidelines, published in July 2024, mention pesticide reduction only in passing, and avoid the word "target."
