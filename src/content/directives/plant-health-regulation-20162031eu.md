## What is it?

The Plant Health Regulation (2016/2031/EU) is the EU's framework for protecting crops, forests, and natural plant life from harmful pests and diseases. It replaced the aging Plant Health Directive (2000/29/EC) with a more proactive, risk-based system. The regulation requires EU member states to conduct annual surveys for listed quarantine pests, to immediately eradicate any outbreak when technically feasible, and to issue a "plant passport" for all plants moved professionally within the EU – a traceability document similar to a phytosanitary certificate for internal trade.

It also overhauls the rules for imports: plants from non-EU countries can only enter through designated border control posts where mandatory checks are carried out. A new category of "priority pests" – those with the most severe economic, environmental, or social impacts – triggers stricter obligations, including contingency plans and simulation exercises by member states. The regulation was adopted in October 2016 and has applied since 14 December 2019.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven directly by the Xylella fastidiosa outbreak in the Italian region of Apulia, first officially detected in October 2013. The bacterium, transmitted by spittlebugs, began killing centuries-old olive trees in the Salento peninsula at an alarming rate. By late 2014, over 800,000 olive trees were affected, according to the Italian government. The existing directive, written in 2000, had no clear rules for rapid response to such a fast-spreading pathogen – it took the EU two years to designate a list of "regulated non-quarantine pests", and its legal tools for mandatory eradication were weak.

The crisis broke a decade-long deadlock over updating the plant health regime. The European Commission had already published an evaluation in 2010 finding the old directive "insufficient to cope with new global challenges" – but member states were split. Northern countries wanted stricter import controls to protect their agricultural sectors (e.g., potato growers fearing ring rot), while southern countries resisted high costs and feared trade disruption. The Xylella emergency in Italy, combined with repeated outbreaks of the pine wood nematode in Portugal (2008) and the citrus greening threat in Spain, created a coalition of affected member states – Italy, Portugal, Spain, Greece – who pushed for a tougher law. Latvia, holding the Council presidency in 2015, made plant health a priority.

The political trigger came when the European Parliament’s Agriculture Committee, led by Italian MEPs, threatened to block a weaker Commission proposal. The final text, agreed in trilogue in December 2015, introduced the "priority pest" mechanism and mandated eradication except in "containment areas" – a concession to Italy, which argued total eradication of Xylella from the entire Salento was impossible. Health Commissioner Vytenis Andriukaitis publicly called the regulation "the most ambitious plant health law in the EU's history."

Timing also mattered: the global trade in plants for planting had grown explosively – the Commission’s 2013 impact assessment noted that EU imports of plants and plant products had more than doubled since 1999. The old directive, designed when most trade was in commodities like grain, was unprepared for the billions of pots and cuttings crossing borders annually.

## Timeline

- **1993** — The single market removes internal border checks, increasing the risk of pest spread. The existing Plant Health Directive (77/93/EEC) is updated.
- **2000** — A new Plant Health Directive (2000/29/EC) consolidates rules but remains largely reactive.
- **2010** — European Commission evaluates the directive, concludes it is outdated. Calls for a "more risk-based approach."
- **October 2013** — Xylella fastidiosa is first confirmed in olive trees near Gallipoli, Apulia, Italy. The Commission begins emergency measures.
- **May 2013** — Commission publishes legislative proposal for a new Plant Health Regulation (COM(2013) 267).
- **2014–2015** — Negotiations in Council and Parliament. Italy demands flexibility for "containment" instead of mandatory eradication. Northern states push for strict import rules.
- **October 2016** — Regulation (EU) 2016/2031 is adopted by the European Parliament and Council.
- **14 December 2016** — Regulation enters into force.
- **14 December 2019** — Regulation becomes applicable (after a three-year transition for member states to set up systems).
- **2020** — The first "priority pest" list is adopted, including Xylella fastidiosa. Italy is fined by the Commission for failing to enforce eradication measures.
- **2022** — The Commission approves a derogation for Apulia allowing "containment zones", effectively admitting eradication failed there.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed plant health governance in the EU. Member states were forced to set up mandatory annual surveys for 20 quarantine pests; by 2020, the Commission reported that over 85% of member states had completed their first survey plans. The plant passport system – mandatory for all professional plant movements since 2019 – initially overwhelmed small nurseries and garden centres, with some reporting a 30% increase in administrative burdens. Italy faced the stiffest adjustment: the government had to allocate over €300 million in compensation to olive growers, according to Italian agricultural ministry figures. In 2021, the Commission launched infringement proceedings against Italy for failing to conduct timely surveys and for allowing infected nursery stock to circulate.

The Xylella crisis itself continued. By early 2023, the disease had killed over 21 million olive trees in Salento, according to the regional government. The new regulation’s "containment areas" in Apulia – a compromise that many scientists opposed – became permanent, essentially accepting that the bacterium could not be eradicated from the peninsula. This sparked a lawsuit from environmental NGOs, who argued the regulation’s escape clause was being misused.

For EU trade, the regulation tightened import controls: the number of "high-risk plants" subject to temporary ban or intensified checks increased from 24 to over 40 species. The Commission’s 2021 report on early implementation found that interceptions of prohibited pests at EU borders rose by 25% in the first year of application. However, smaller member states struggled with the cost of staffing border control posts – Malta and Cyprus requested additional support.

## Key questions answered

### What caused the olive tree die-off in southern Italy?

A bacterium called Xylella fastidiosa, native to the Americas, was introduced to the Salento region of Apulia probably through imported ornamental coffee plants. Spread by insect vectors – mainly the spittlebug Philaenus spumarius – it infects the xylem tissue, blocking water transport and causing the trees to die of drought. The outbreak was first identified in 2013, but by the time it was confirmed, the bacterium had already spread over thousands of hectares.

### Why did the EU need a new law on plant health?

The previous directive, from 2000, was designed for a world where most plant trade was in bulk commodities. It lacked strong tools to force governments to act quickly once a pest arrived, and it had no "plant passport" for internal EU movements – so infected nursery stock could cross borders undetected. The Xylella crisis showed that the old system was too slow: it took years to agree even basic containment measures. The new regulation mandates rapid eradication, annual surveys, and full traceability through passports.

### Do EU countries have to destroy all infected plants?

Not automatically. The regulation requires "immediate eradication" of quarantine pests – cutting down and destroying infected plants and host plants in a buffer zone. But if a pest is so widespread that eradication is "impossible," a member state can apply for a "containment" derogation. This is what Italy obtained for Xylella in Apulia, allowing it to manage the disease rather than try to wipe it out. Some scientists and environmental groups argue this loophole undermines the entire law.

### Does the regulation affect what plants I can bring into the EU?

Yes. The regulation strengthened controls at EU borders. Most plants for planting imported from outside the EU must be accompanied by a phytosanitary certificate and undergo mandatory checks at a border control post. A list of "high-risk plants" – currently including species such as olive, oak, and citrus trees – can be temporarily banned or subjected to stricter inspections. Travellers bringing plants for personal use also face restrictions; many species require a certificate.
