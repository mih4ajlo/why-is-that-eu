## What is it?

The 2017 Energy Labelling Regulation replaced the 2010 Energy Labelling Directive and overhauled how the EU labels the energy efficiency of household appliances. Its most visible change was the abolition of the A+, A++, and A+++ classes that had proliferated under the old system, and the resurrection of a simpler A-G scale — with A initially left empty to allow for future product improvements.

Beyond the label itself, the regulation created a centralised digital database called EPREL (European Product Registry for Energy Labelling). Manufacturers must register all energy-labelled products in EPREL before placing them on the EU market, including technical documentation and test reports. This database is public-facing, allowing consumers, retailers, and market surveillance authorities to compare products and verify performance claims.

## Why was it introduced?

The decision to scrap the A+++ scale was driven by a structural failure: the label had become a victim of its own success. Launched in 1995 with A-G classifications, the label drove manufacturers to improve energy efficiency so rapidly that by the early 2000s, most products clustered at A or A+. In 2010, the EU responded by adding A+, A++, and A+++, but this created a fragmented, confusing system. A 2014 test by consumer group BEUC found that 40% of consumers misinterpreted the A+++ to A-D scale. By 2017, over 90% of washing machines and dishwashers already carried A+++ labels, meaning the top tier had become a new baseline — the very problem A-G was originally designed to solve.

The political coalition for reform was unusual. The European Commission's Directorate-General for Energy (DG ENER), under Commissioner Miguel Arias Cañete (2014-2019), pushed for the rescaling. Environmental groups, led by the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) and Coolproducts campaign, argued the A+++ system was "giving consumers the illusion of green choices." Consumer organisations, including BEUC, documented widespread confusion. Industry, however, was divided. Major manufacturers like BSH Hausgeräte and Whirlpool initially opposed a full rescaling, warning of "market disruption" and "cost of relabelling" — estimates from the Commission's 2015 impact assessment put industry compliance costs at €300-500 million across the EU. The European Committee of Domestic Equipment Manufacturers (CECED) lobbied for keeping the A+ system and adding further pluses, a position that had prevailed in 2010.

The critical blockage was broken by timing and a trigger event. The European Parliament's Industry, Research and Energy (ITRE) committee, led by rapporteur Dario Tamburrano (EFDD, Italy), took a strong stance for the A-G return. The Juncker Commission's 2015 "Better Regulation" agenda gave political cover to simplify rules. But the decisive moment came in March 2016 when the Commission's own Joint Research Centre (JRC) published a technical analysis that showed maintaining A+++ would require adding not just A++++ but up to seven plus classes by 2030 for the label to remain meaningful. The absurdity of labels like "A++++++++" made headlines across Europe. Germany's Stiftung Warentest and France's UFC-Que Choisir published side-by-side comparisons showing consumers could not distinguish between products rated A+ and A+++.

The Parliament adopted its position in July 2016 with 575 votes in favour, 74 against, and 42 abstentions — a landslide that surprised even Commission officials. Council followed in March 2017. The regulation entered into force in August 2017, with the new A-G labels appearing on products from March 2021.

## Timeline

- **1995** — First EU energy label law (Directive 95/13/EC) introduces A-G scale for household appliances.
- **2004-2010** — Rapid efficiency improvements drive >90% of products to A or A+ classes under original label.
- **2010** — Directive 2010/30/EU adds A+, A++, A+++ classes; critics warn of confusion.
- **March 2016** — JRC technical report warns that maintaining A+++ would require up to seven plus-classes by 2030.
- **July 2016** — European Parliament adopts its position with 575 votes in favour; ITRE committee rapporteur Dario Tamburrano leads negotiations.
- **March 2017** — Council of the EU formally adopts the regulation; votes not recorded but qualified majority reached.
- **August 2017** — Regulation enters into force.
- **January 2019** — First EPREL database module open to market surveillance authorities.
- **March 2021** — First wave of new A-G labels appears on washing machines, dishwashers, refrigerators, TVs, and lamps. Old A+++ products allowed to remain in shops until stock exhausted.
- **December 2022** — Second wave of rescaling for light bulbs and luminaires.
- **March 2023** — Third wave for heating appliances (heat pumps, water heaters, space heaters).

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was a clean break in communication. For the first time in a decade, no products could claim A-class efficiency — the top tier sat empty by design, reserved for future innovation. The Commission's 2021 consumer survey found 85% of EU consumers could correctly identify the most efficient product on the new label, up from 60% under the A+++ system.

Industry faced significant relabelling costs. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment for the first product groups estimated €380 million in industry costs from printing new labels, updating packaging, and retraining sales staff. Larger manufacturers absorbed this more easily; smaller producers, particularly white-label manufacturers in Eastern Europe, reported higher relative costs.

EPREL proved transformative for market surveillance. By 2023, the database contained over 1.2 million product entries across 15 product groups. National authorities, such as the UK's Office for Product Safety and Standards and Germany's Marktüberwachung, reported that EPREL-enabled checks doubled their annual compliance verification rate compared to 2019. The database also gave consumer groups like BEUC and Clean Energy Wire direct access to product specs, enabling independent price-efficiency comparisons that had previously required data requests.

However, enforcement gaps remain. A 2022 study by the European Environmental Bureau found that 7% of products listed in EPREL had no online label visible at retail, and 3% of entries contained missing or false technical data. The Commission has not issued fines directly — enforcement falls to Member States — but Belgium, France, and Sweden have issued financial penalties to manufacturers for non-compliance.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe stop using A+++ for energy labels?

The A+++ scale had lost its meaning because nearly all products achieved the top rating by 2017, making it impossible for consumers to distinguish genuine efficiency leaders. The EU's Joint Research Centre calculated that continuing the plus system would require labels like "A++++++++" by 2030, which made the reform politically unstoppable.

### Can manufacturers still sell appliances with the old A+++ label?

Yes, but only until existing stock runs out. The regulation allowed a transition period: products already on the market with old labels could remain on shelves. However, new products placed on the EU market after the March 2021 deadline for each product group must carry the new A-G scale.

### What does the EPREL database actually do for consumers?

EPREL allows anyone to look up the exact energy consumption, noise level, water use, and technical specifications of any registered appliance by entering its model number. Before EPREL, consumers had to trust the label alone; now they can verify claims, compare models side-by-side, and check whether a product was tested under real-world conditions.

### Does the new label apply to all types of household appliances?

No — the rescaling was rolled out in waves. Washing machines, dishwashers, refrigerators, TVs, and lamps were first (March 2021). Light bulbs and luminaires followed (December 2022). Heating appliances like heat pumps, water heaters, and space heaters came next (March 2023). Older product groups like vacuum cleaners and ovens are scheduled for later phases.
