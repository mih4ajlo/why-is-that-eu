## What is it?

The Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) is an EU regulation that entered into force on 23 May 2024, setting binding benchmarks for the bloc's supply of 34 critical and 17 strategic raw materials. The Act demands that by 2030, the EU extract at least 10% of its annual consumption of strategic raw materials, process at least 40%, and recycle at least 25%. No single third country may supply more than 65% of any strategic raw material at any stage of processing.

The regulation also streamlines permitting for strategic projects, establishes a European Raw Materials Board, and requires member states to conduct national exploration programs. It covers materials essential for electric vehicle batteries, wind turbines, semiconductors, and defense systems — lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements, gallium, and germanium among them.

## Why was it introduced?

The CRMA was a direct response to a geopolitical shock: China's export controls on gallium and germanium, announced on 3 July 2023. China produces roughly 94% of the world's gallium — essential for semiconductor wafers and LEDs — and over 80% of germanium, used in fiber optics and infrared optics. The controls, which required exporters to obtain special licenses, sent EU alarm bells ringing. Brussels had long warned about raw material dependency, but this was the first time Beijing weaponized a supply chain the EU had no domestic source for.

The structural tension was decades in the making. The EU had outsourced not just extraction but most processing and refining capacity for critical minerals to China, which invested heavily after its 2014 "Made in China 2025" strategy. By 2020, the Commission's own 2020 "Critical Raw Materials Resilience" report found the EU was 98% dependent on China for rare earth elements essential to permanent magnets in wind turbines and electric vehicles. For lithium, the EU sourced 78% from Chile and 8% from the US, but China controlled about 60% of global lithium refining capacity. This created a classic vulnerability: the EU could dig up the rock but had no way to turn it into metal.

The coalition dynamics that passed the CRMA were unusual. The European Parliament's lead rapporteur, Nicola Danti (Renew Europe, Italy), assembled a broad cross-party coalition that included the center-right EPP, center-left S&D, and the Greens. They faced opposition from environmental NGOs and some Green MEPs who argued the extraction targets would accelerate mining in sensitive ecosystems — the EU's own 2023 "State of Nature" report had found that mining was a pressure on 8% of protected habitats. The mining industry, led by EuroLihitin, lobbied for faster permitting and lower environmental hurdles, while downstream users like the European Battery Alliance warned that without domestic supply, EV battery factories would simply move to the US under the Inflation Reduction Act.

Timing was everything. The European Commission's original proposal, unveiled on 16 March 2023 by Commissioners Thierry Breton (Internal Market) and Maroš Šefčovič (Green Deal), had been in preparation since the 2022 Versailles Declaration where EU leaders agreed to reduce strategic dependencies. But the proposal stalled in Council over how stringent recycling targets should be. China's gallium controls in July 2023 broke the deadlock — suddenly even skeptical member states like Finland (which has key mines but wanted weaker recycling quotas) and Portugal (which wanted to fast-track lithium projects) accepted stronger targets, including the 65% single-country cap proposed by MEPs like Hildegard Bentele (EPP, Germany).

The immediate trigger that made the 65% cap politically viable was the French-German agreement in September 2023. Berlin had initially resisted a blanket supplier cap, fearing it would complicate trade with Australia and Chile. But after Chinese export controls on germanium and gallium, German industry — especially Siemens and Volkswagen — warned the Chancellery that semiconductors and EV magnets were at risk. The German Chancellery shifted position, and on 26 September 2023, France and Germany jointly proposed the 65% cap. The final text, agreed between Council and Parliament on 13 November 2023, included the cap alongside expedited permitting — maximum 27 months for extraction projects, 15 months for processing, and 12 months for recycling.

What changed permanently was the EU's willingness to accept industrial policy over free trade orthodoxy. Previous attempts, like the 2020 European Raw Materials Alliance, had been voluntary and underfunded. The CRMA created legally binding targets plus a "strategic projects" status that overrides some environmental permitting procedures — a shift that the Commission's own impact assessment acknowledged could increase local environmental conflicts, but which the 2023 Strategic Foresight Report deemed necessary given that by 2030 the EU would need 18 times more lithium and 5 times more cobalt just for the EV transition.

## Timeline

- **2010** — The European Commission publishes the first list of 14 critical raw materials, based on economic importance and supply risk.
- **2014** — China's "Made in China 2025" strategy explicitly targets domination of rare earth processing.
- **2017** — The EU opens a formal complaint at the WTO against China's raw material export restrictions (including rare earths). WTO panel rules partially in EU's favor in 2019.
- **2020** — Commission publishes "Critical Raw Materials Resilience" report, finding 98% dependency on China for rare earths.
- **March 2022** — Versailles Declaration EU leaders commit to reducing strategic dependencies in raw materials.
- **March 2023** — Commission proposes the Critical Raw Materials Act (16 March 2023).
- **July 2023** — China imposes export controls on gallium and germanium (3 July 2023).
- **September 2023** — France and Germany agree on 65% single-country cap (26 September 2023).
- **November 2023** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council (13 November 2023).
- **December 2023** — European Parliament endorses the deal (12 December 2023, 549-64 with 26 abstentions).
- **March 2024** — Council formally adopts the regulation (18 March 2024).
- **May 2024** — Entry into force (23 May 2024).
- **August 2024** — Member states must designate national competent authorities for permitting.
- **2025** — First list of Strategic Projects expected to be approved by European Raw Materials Board.

## Impact and consequences

The CRMA's most immediate impact has been on permitting. Since entry into force, at least 12 mining projects have submitted applications for Strategic Project status, including lithium extraction in Portugal's Barroso region (Savannah Resources) and rare earth processing in Estonia (Neo Performance Materials). The Commission's 2024 Strategic Project guidance warns that member states must allocate sufficient resources to permitting authorities — a bottleneck the 2023 impact assessment identified as causing typical mine permitting times of 15-20 years in the EU versus 3-5 years in Australia.

Industry response has been mixed. The European Association of Mining Industries (Euromines) praised the expedited permitting but warned that binding recycling targets of 25% would be "impossible" without massive investment in recycling infrastructure — the EU currently recycles less than 1% of rare earth elements according to the 2024 EERA report. Environmental groups including ClientEarth and the European Environmental Bureau have announced legal challenges against fast-tracked projects, arguing the CRMA's forced prioritization violates the EU's own nature protection directives, specifically the Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) under which 18% of Natura 2000 sites overlap with known mineral deposits.

The geopolitical consequences have been immediate. China's Ministry of Commerce responded on 20 March 2024 by announcing expanded export control lists covering antimony, gallium, and germanium, while the China Nonferrous Metals Industry Association warned the CRMA could trigger "retaliatory measures." Meanwhile, the US passed the Inflation Reduction Act in 2022 with its own critical mineral provisions, and in March 2024 the EU-US Critical Minerals Agreement was signed to coordinate recycling standards — directly linked to the CRMA's definitions.

The first enforcement test will come in 2027, when the Commission must report on member states' national exploration programs. Countries that miss the 2025 deadline for designating permitting authorities risk infringement proceedings. The EU's 2024 budget allocated €1.2 billion for raw materials security under the Innovation Fund and EIB loans, but the 2023 impact assessment estimated total investment needs of €200-400 billion by 2030 — making the CRMA more a direction-setting instrument than a fully funded one.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the Act set a 65% single-country cap for processing?

The cap targets China's dominance in refining. China controls approximately 90% of rare earth processing, 60% of lithium refining, and 70% of cobalt refining. The 65% threshold was specifically chosen because no EU member state could realistically replace Chinese capacity alone, but the cap forces supply diversification to at least three sources. Without it, the Commission's 2023 impact assessment warned that by 2030, 80% of EU strategic raw material processing would remain in a single country.

### Why did the Act include recycling targets, not just extraction?

Historically, EU recycling rates for critical raw materials were negligible — under 1% for rare earths and under 5% for lithium. The 25% recycling target for 2030 was the most contentious figure in negotiations, with mining companies arguing it was unachievable and environmental groups pushing for 40%. The final compromise reflects the reality that urban mining (from e-waste and battery scrap) is slower to scale than virgin extraction, but it also sends a signal to the circular economy industry that public procurement and state aid will prioritize recycled content.

### Why did the Act pass when earlier raw materials strategies failed?

Earlier EU raw materials initiatives — the 2008 Raw Materials Initiative and the 2020 European Raw Materials Alliance — were non-binding and under-resourced. The CRMA became binding because three factors converged in 2023: first, China's gallium/germanium controls provided a concrete, undeniable crisis. Second, the Inflation Reduction Act in the US created competitive pressure — European battery manufacturers threatened to relocate. Third, the Versailles Declaration in March 2022 had already committed EU leaders to "reduce strategic dependencies," so the CRMA fulfilled a political promise. The critical mass came when Germany shifted position after Chinese export controls, breaking the previous deadlock where Berlin had favored voluntary measures.

### Why are environmental groups challenging a law aimed at the green transition?

The tension is between speed and ecological protection. The CRMA's fast-tracked permitting can override standard environmental impact assessments for "Strategic Projects" — the Commission's 2023 impact assessment acknowledged this could increase local habitat destruction. Environmental groups argue that mining for lithium and rare earths in Natura 2000 areas, which cover 18% of known EU mineral deposits, risks destroying the very ecosystems the green transition is supposed to protect. The European Environmental Bureau's 2024 legal analysis specifically targets the Regulation's Article 10, which allows Strategic Projects to bypass certain Habitats Directive procedures.
