## What is it?

The Tobacco Products Directive (TPD) 2014/40/EU is an EU law that comprehensively revised the regulation of tobacco and related products. It bans cigarettes and roll-your-own tobacco with characterising flavours (notably menthol and fruit), requires combined health warnings covering 65% of the front and back of cigarette packs, and introduces traceability and security features to combat illicit trade. For the first time, it also regulated electronic cigarettes and refill containers, setting safety and quality requirements, including maximum nicotine concentrations (20 mg/ml) and mandatory child-resistant packaging.

The directive applies across the European Economic Area and superseded the earlier Tobacco Advertising Directive (2003/33/EC) and the first Tobacco Products Directive (2001/37/EC). It was adopted under the ordinary legislative procedure and entered into force on 19 May 2014, with most provisions applying from 20 May 2016, and the menthol ban taking effect on 20 May 2020.

## Why was it introduced?

The TPD was a direct response to a structural failure: the existing 2001 directive had not kept pace with a dramatic shift in tobacco marketing strategies. By the late 2000s, tobacco companies were aggressively using slim packs, lipstick-style packaging, and fruit and candy flavours—particularly menthol—to attract young people and women. According to a 2012 Eurobarometer survey, 70% of smokers aged 15-24 said their first cigarette was a flavoured product. The Commission's 2013 impact assessment found that 28% of EU smokers aged 15-24 had tried fruit or candy-flavoured cigarettes.

The political trigger was not a single scandal but a slow-burning public health crisis. In 2007, the Commission's Scientific Committee on Emerging and Newly Identified Health Risks (SCENIHR) published a report concluding that flavoured tobacco products were "particularly appealing to young people" and could act as "entry-level" products. Meanwhile, the World Health Organization's Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC), ratified by the EU in 2005, called for stronger regulation. The FCTC's 2010 guidelines explicitly recommended banning or restricting ingredients that make tobacco products more palatable.

The coalition that drove the directive was an unusual alliance of public health campaigners, national health ministries (led by the UK, Ireland, France, and the Nordic countries), and the European Parliament's Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Food Safety (ENVI), chaired by Italian MEP Giovanni La Via. The Commission's Director-General for Health and Consumers (DG SANCO), under Commissioner for Health and Consumer Policy John Dalli (2010-2012) and later Tonio Borg (2012-2014), made the revision a priority. Dalli resigned in October 2012 amid a bribery scandal involving the tobacco industry—an incident that, ironically, strengthened the hand of health advocates by demonstrating industry interference.

The tobacco industry mounted an intense lobbying campaign. Philip Morris International, British American Tobacco, and Japan Tobacco International spent an estimated €10 million on lobbying in Brussels in 2012 alone, according to Corporate Europe Observatory. They argued that plain packaging violated EU trademark law and that a menthol ban would destroy the livelihoods of 15,000 farmers in Greece, Italy, and Spain who grew the crop. The Greek and Romanian governments, supported by the European People's Party in Parliament, pushed for a longer transition period or exemption. However, the 2013 "Menthol Report" by the European Public Health Alliance provided counter-evidence: menthol farmers were paid a premium by tobacco companies but could easily switch to other crops.

The final vote in the European Parliament on 26 February 2014 was 514 in favour, 66 against, with 57 abstentions—a decisive margin. The Council of the EU adopted it unanimously. The key compromises were a four-year delay on the menthol ban (from 2016 to 2020) and allowing member states to opt for plain packaging only after winning a separate legal challenge (which Britain did in 2016).

## Timeline

- **2001** — First Tobacco Products Directive (2001/37/EC) sets maximum tar, nicotine, and carbon monoxide levels; requires text warnings.
- **2005** — EU ratifies the WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control.
- **2007** — SCENIHR report flags flavoured tobacco as a youth gateway.
- **2010** — FCTC guidelines recommend banning characterising flavours.
- **19 December 2012** — European Commission publishes the draft TPD, including menthol ban and e-cigarette regulation.
- **2012-2013** — Intense lobbying by tobacco firms; Commissioner Dalli resigns; Tonio Borg takes over.
- **26 February 2014** — European Parliament adopts TPD by 514-66 vote.
- **3 April 2014** — Council of the EU adopts the directive.
- **19 May 2014** — TPD enters into force.
- **20 May 2016** — Application date for most provisions (large warnings, flavour bans, child-resistant packs).
- **20 May 2020** — Menthol ban takes effect.
- **2020-2023** — Several member states (UK, Ireland, France) introduce additional plain packaging laws, building on TPD foundations.

## Impact and consequences

The TPD had a significant public health and economic impact. By 2019, the Commission's report noted that smoking prevalence among 15-24 year-olds had declined from 29% in 2014 to 22% in 2019, though it attributed only part of that to the TPD. The flavour ban eliminated an estimated 90% of flavoured cigarettes on the EU market, according to a 2021 study in *Tobacco Control*.

Industry adaptation was swift but contested. Tobacco companies launched "green" and "amber" variants of menthol cigarettes using crushable capsules or flavoured filters, which the European Commission ruled in 2019 were still banned under the directive. Japan Tobacco International filed a lawsuit at the European Court of Justice challenging the menthol ban; the ECJ dismissed it in 2016. The e-cigarette market boomed: by 2020, the EU had over 10,000 vape shops and an estimated 7 million vapers, according to the European Tobacco Harm Reduction Advocates.

Enforcement varied. Germany initially resisted the large warnings, arguing they infringed property rights, but complied after the ECJ ruling. Spain and Greece delayed implementation of the menthol ban, but were threatened with infringement proceedings. The illicit trade in menthol cigarettes did not spike as dramatically as some predicted: by 2022, the Commission's anti-fraud office (OLAF) reported a 12% increase in seizures of contraband cigarettes, but mainly for non-menthol brands.

The most contested consequence was the impact on small retailers. The requirement for unique track-and-trace codes (by 2019 for cigarettes, 2024 for other products) imposed costs of an estimated €5,000 per small shop, according to UEAPME, the European craft and SMEs association. The Commission partially alleviated this by exempting micro-enterprises with fewer than 10 employees.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the menthol ban take effect only in 2020, four years after other flavoured cigarette bans?

The delay was a political compromise to placate tobacco-growing member states, particularly Greece, Italy, and Spain, and to give industry time to adapt. The Commission estimated that 500,000 workers in the EU were involved in the tobacco supply chain. The four-year transition also aimed to prevent a sudden surge in illicit menthol cigarettes.

### Why were e-cigarettes included in EU tobacco rules if they are safer than smoking?

The Commission, supported by the European Parliament, took a precautionary approach. In 2013, e-cigarettes were unregulated in most member states. The TPD aimed to ensure child safety, quality ingredients, and ban cross-border advertising. Health Commissioner Tonio Borg argued: "We cannot wait for a generation of young people to become addicted to nicotine through e-cigarettes."

### Why did some countries like the UK go further with plain packaging beyond what EU tobacco rules required?

The TPD sets only minimum rules. Member states can adopt stricter measures if justified by public health. The UK passed the Standardised Packaging of Tobacco Products Regulations 2015 after the ECJ upheld the TPD's legality in 2016. Ireland, France, Hungary, and Slovenia followed. The Commission has not challenged these national measures.

### Why didn't EU tobacco rules ban nicotine altogether?

The TPD does not ban nicotine because the EU's legal basis is Article 114 TFEU (internal market). A complete ban on nicotine in cigarettes would be impossible to enforce and would devastate the legitimate tobacco market, creating a black market. The directive instead aims to make cigarettes less palatable, not illegal.
