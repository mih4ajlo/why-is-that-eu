## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2016/429, known as the Animal Health Law (AHL), is the EU's central legal framework for preventing and controlling transmissible animal diseases. It replaced roughly 40 separate directives and regulations — some dating back to the 1960s — with a single, risk-based system that covers farmed animals, pets, wildlife, and animal products such as semen, ova, and embryos.

The law operates on a "prevention is better than cure" logic. It requires EU countries to establish mandatory disease surveillance plans, registers of animal holdings, and rapid notification procedures for "listed diseases" — a prioritised catalogue of around 100 pathogens grouped by their severity and transmission risk. It also introduced new rules for the safe movement of animals across borders, traceability requirements for certain species (not just livestock but also dogs, cats, and ferrets in commercial movements), and expanded biosecurity obligations on animal keepers. Crucially, it shifted the EU from reacting to outbreaks to proactively monitoring disease risks, using data-sharing and categorisation to allocate resources more effectively.

## Why was it introduced?

The single most dramatic trigger was the **2001 foot-and-mouth disease (FMD) epidemic in the United Kingdom**. That outbreak began at a pig farm in Heddon-on-the-Wall, Northumberland, in February 2001. By the time it was declared over in September, over 6 million animals had been culled across the UK and the Netherlands, costing the British economy an estimated £8 billion (approximately €12 billion at the time). The European Commission's Joint Research Centre later estimated that 70% of the animals killed were not actually infected — they were slaughtered as a precaution, in what critics called a "firebreak" policy that caused immense animal suffering and public outrage.

The political fallout was seismic. The UK government faced a crisis of confidence: Agriculture Minister Nick Brown was forced to defend the mass cull in Parliament, while the European Commissioner for Health and Consumer Protection at the time, **David Byrne**, an Irish lawyer, pushed for a root-and-branch review. The Commission's **2002 report on FMD control** concluded that the patchwork of national rules and pre-1990s EU directives had left Europe dangerously vulnerable. As Byrne later told the European Parliament, "the lesson of 2001 is that the weakest link in the chain breaks it."

But the FMD crisis was not the only warning shot. In **2005**, the EU faced outbreaks of **avian influenza (H5N1)** in wild birds and poultry in several member states, including France and Germany, leading to mass preventive culls in Italy's Po Valley poultry belt. In **2007**, an outbreak of **African swine fever** in Georgia swept into the EU via wild boar, reaching Poland and the Baltic states by 2014. By 2020, over 40,000 outbreaks had been reported in EU wild boar and domestic pigs, according to the European Food Safety Authority.

The structural tension was clear: the old system — built on Council Directives 64/432/EEC (cattle and swine) and 91/68/EEC (sheep and goats) — had been designed for a world of national borders, limited trade, and slower disease transmission. It allocated outbreaks to rigid categories (A, B, or C) with no flexibility for risk-based control. It required no central register of animal holdings, so when an outbreak occurred, tracing animal movements could take weeks. The 2013 Commission evaluation noted that "the current legislation is fragmented, inconsistent, and excessively prescriptive."

The coalition that pushed for the AHL was unusual. Veterinary authorities in northern Europe — particularly **Denmark**, **the Netherlands**, and **the UK** — had long called for modernisation, supported by the **COPA-COGECA** farming lobby, which wanted predictable, non-discriminatory trade rules that would replace member states' ad hoc border closures. A decisive ally was the **European Parliament's Committee on Agriculture**, whose rapporteur, **Michel Dantin** (EPP, France), argued in 2015 that "the livestock sector cannot afford a repeat of 2001." The primary opponents were southern member states — **Italy** and **Greece** — whose small-scale livestock holders feared the cost of new biosecurity requirements and electronic registration. The European Commission's **2013 impact assessment** estimated that compliance costs for EU livestock farms would be around €89 million in the first year but would save €1.2 billion over a decade through avoided outbreaks.

The specific event that broke the legislative deadlock was the **2013 adoption of the EU's Animal Health Strategy (2013–2020)**, which set a political commitment to "One Health" — linking animal, human, and environmental health. The Lisbon Treaty (2009) had already moved animal welfare into a higher legal status. But what sealed the deal was the **2014 outbreak of African swine fever in the EU**: by the time the Commission drafted the regulation in 2015, the disease had reached wild boar in Lithuania and Poland. The **Commissioner for Health and Food Safety**, **Vytenis Andriukaitis** (Lithuania), a former surgeon, told EU agriculture ministers in 2015: "If you think biosecurity is expensive, try a disease outbreak."

The Parliament adopted the regulation in April 2016 by a majority of **579 votes in favour, 41 against, and 23 abstentions**. The Council formally adopted it in May 2016.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Foot-and-mouth disease epidemic in the UK: 6 million+ animals culled, £8 billion economic cost.
- **2002** — Commission report on FMD control calls for overhaul of animal health legislation.
- **2005** — EU outbreaks of avian influenza H5N1 in wild birds and poultry.
- **2007** — African swine fever (ASF) detected in Georgia; begins spreading westward.
- **2013** — Commission evaluation finds current legislation "fragmented, inconsistent, and excessively prescriptive."
- **2014** — ASF reaches EU territory (Lithuania, Poland).
- **2015** — Commission tables draft Animal Health Law.
- **April 2016** — European Parliament adopts AHL (579-41-23).
- **May 2016** — Council formally adopts Regulation 2016/429.
- **21 April 2021** — AHL enters into force (application date, after a 5-year transition period).

## Impact and consequences

The Animal Health Law reshaped veterinary surveillance across the EU. By 2022, the Commission reported that 26 of 27 member states had established compulsory national animal disease surveillance programmes, up from 18 under the old regime. The law's "listed disease" system prompted **Austria**, **Germany**, and the **Netherlands** to overhaul their animal-holding registers, with the Netherlands alone adding 85,000 small-scale livestock keepers to its database who had previously been unregistered.

Industry response was mixed. The **European Livestock Association (COPA-COGECA)** praised the clarity of the single rulebook but warned in 2021 that "the cost of electronic identification for sheep and goats is a burden for family farms." Several member states, including **Poland** and **Romania**, sought derogations for sheep identification in 2019, which the Commission granted until 2024 for transhumance operations. The **Federation of Veterinarians of Europe** welcomed the focus on biosecurity, noting that the law for the first time required veterinarians to report "suspicious" cases even if not confirmed.

Enforcement has been uneven. In **2022**, the Commission opened infringement proceedings against **Greece** for failing to implement electronic identification of sheep by the deadline. **Spain** and **Portugal** were cited for incomplete wild boar surveillance for ASF. The European Court of Auditors' **2022 special report on animal health** found that "member states' compliance with the AHL's traceability requirements varies significantly, with eight member states lacking fully operational central databases."

The most significant consequence has been the law's role in **managing the ASF outbreak** in the EU. Between 2014 and 2023, over 50,000 ASF outbreaks were reported in wild boar and domestic pigs, but the AHL's risk categorisation (dividing the EU into "infected zones," "buffer zones," and "free zones") allowed pork trade to continue from disease-free regions. The Commission's **2024 assessment** estimated that the AHL prevented €2.5 billion in potential trade losses during that period.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the AHL take five years to apply?

The five-year transition period from adoption in 2016 to application in 2021 was designed to give member states time to overhaul their national animal health systems: establish central databases, train veterinarians in the new risk-based approach, and issue secondary legislation (delegated and implementing acts) for specific diseases. The Commission's 2016 impact assessment warned that "without a sufficient transition, many member states — particularly those with fragmented livestock sectors — would fail to comply."

### Does the AHL apply to pets as well as farm animals?

Yes, but with a narrower scope. The AHL covers **dogs, cats, and ferrets** only in the context of **commercial movements** (sale, trade, shows, or breeding establishments). For these animals, it mandates microchipping, rabies vaccination, and documentation. Non-commercial travel by pet owners (fewer than 5 animals) is still governed by the separate **EU Pet Travel Scheme (Regulation 576/2013)** . The AHL does not apply to hobby animals kept outside commercial trade.

### How does the AHL handle wildlife?

The AHL is the first EU animal health law to explicitly impose obligations regarding wildlife. It requires member states to **monitor listed diseases in wild animals**, particularly for pathogens with high spillover risk to livestock (e.g., ASF in wild boar, avian influenza in waterfowl). If a listed disease is detected in wildlife, the affected member state must establish a "restricted zone" and implement measures to reduce contact between wild and domestic animals. According to the European Environment Agency's **2022 State of Nature report**, 12 member states had reported active wildlife disease surveillance programmes under the AHL, compared to 5 under the previous regime.

### What happened to the old EU animal health rules?

They were **repealed** in full on 21 April 2021. The AHL replaced around 40 separate directives, including the 1964 livestock trade directive and the 1991 sheep and goat regulation. However, many **detailed technical provisions** (e.g., specific diagnostic methods, vaccination protocols) were not included in the AHL itself but were delegated to **secondary acts** — Commission Delegated Regulation 2020/689 (disease prevention and surveillance rules) and Commission Implementing Regulation 2020/687 (disease control measures). This "tiered" structure was intended to make the law more adaptable to new scientific evidence.
