## What is it?

The Dual-Use Export Controls Regulation establishes a common EU framework for authorising the export, transfer, brokering, and transit of items that can be used for both civilian and military purposes — such as advanced chemicals, precision machine tools, encryption software, and surveillance hardware. It replaced the 2009 regulation (428/2009/EU) to cover new technologies and address security and human rights concerns.

Exporters must apply for a license from their national authority before shipping listed dual-use items outside the EU. The regulation maintains an EU-wide common control list (updated periodically to align with international regimes like the Wassenaar Arrangement) but also introduces a catch-all clause allowing member states to control unlisted items when they have reason to believe they will be used in weapons of mass destruction or human rights violations. For the first time, it explicitly requires member states to consider the risk of an item being used for internal repression or serious human rights abuses when deciding on export licenses.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a structural tension: the existing 2009 rules, designed for traditional military dual-use goods, were failing to address the rapid proliferation of digital surveillance tools that authoritarian regimes used to suppress dissent. A pivotal incident was the 2019 revelation that an Italian company, Hacking Team, had exported its remote surveillance software "Galileo" to Sudan and other countries with poor human rights records while the EU had no specific controls on such tools. Export licenses were granted under the earlier regulation because spyware was not on the common control list, even though its intended use was clearly repressive.

The political coalition for reform united the European Parliament, led by rapporteur Klaus Buchner (Greens/EFA), with the European External Action Service and several member states (notably Germany, France, and the Netherlands) that had grown frustrated with the patchwork of national controls. The Parliament had passed a resolution in January 2017 calling for a human security dimension to export controls, and the Council under the Maltese presidency initially resisted, fearing that explicit human rights criteria would harm Europe's high-tech export competitiveness. The bloc delayed passage for nearly three years, until the European Council agreed in 2019 to include human rights considerations after a compromise that kept the human rights check as a discretionary factor, not a mandatory prohibition.

The timing was shaped by two events: the 2018 assassination of Jamal Khashoggi, which exposed how Saudi Arabia used Western surveillance technology, and the 2020 Commission consultation that showed over 70% of EU citizens favouring stronger controls on cyber-surveillance. Commissioner for Trade Phil Hogan (later succeeded by Valdis Dombrovskis) pushed the final text through. Earlier attempts to update the 2009 regulation had stalled over disagreement on whether to include a binding human rights clause. The 2021 text unblocked this by tying the human rights risk assessment to a new, non-binding “guidelines” mechanism — a political signal but not a rigid restriction.

## Timeline

- **2009** — The previous Dual-Use Regulation (428/2009/EU) enters into force, consolidating controls around international regime lists.
- **2014-2016** — Commission assessments show that spyware and surveillance exports are rising, with no unified EU approach; national controls diverge significantly.
- **18 December 2016** — The Commission proposes a new dual-use regulation, including a human rights clause, catch-all for cyber tools, and an EU coordination mechanism.
- **January 2017** — European Parliament passes a resolution supporting a stronger human rights dimension.
- **2018-2019** — Interinstitutional negotiations stall; the Council resists binding human rights criteria. The Khashoggi killing in October 2018 raises political pressure.
- **June 2019** — The European Council agrees to a compromise that includes a human rights risk factor but leaves member states discretion.
- **8 May 2021** — The final text of Regulation 2021/821/EU is published in the Official Journal.
- **9 September 2021** — The regulation enters into force and application begins, replacing the 2009 regulation.
- **2022-2023** — Member states begin requiring licenses for previously uncontrolled cyber-surveillance items (e.g., IP network surveillance systems, digital forensics tools) under the new catch-all and modified list.

## Impact and consequences

Exporters of high-tech goods — especially companies in cybersecurity, telecommunications, and digital forensics — now face additional licensing requirements for items like "zero-day" exploit tools and mobile phone interceptors. The European Commission’s 2021 impact assessment noted that the regulation would add about 300-400 new controlled items, covering areas such as quantum computing, semiconductor manufacturing equipment, and surveillance software. Industry associations, including DigitalEurope, warned that over-broad definitions could hurt European competitiveness, but the final text narrowed the scope by requiring a dual-use character (i.e., the item must have a legitimate civilian use in addition to potential military/repressive use). Small and medium-sized exporters face higher compliance costs, but the regulation introduces a new EU single license for low-risk exports to trusted destinations.

The most significant change is that member states now have a legal basis to deny licenses for cyber-surveillance tools heading to regimes where internal repression is documented. By early 2023, national authorities in the Netherlands and Germany had publicly cited human rights risks to block exports of mobile surveillance equipment to several Middle Eastern and Asian governments — a first under any EU dual-use regime.

## Key questions answered

### What kinds of products are considered "dual-use" and need an export license?

Dual-use items range from chemicals that can be used in nerve agents to high-performance computers that could process nuclear simulations. Under the 2021 regulation, the definition explicitly includes cyber-surveillance software — such as tools for intercepting mobile phone calls, extracting data from compromised devices, or exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities — if they are designed to enable intrusion without user consent. Exporters must check if their product is on the EU common list or if a catch-all applies.

### When does the EU restrict exports of surveillance technology to other countries?

The regulation does not create an automatic ban on all surveillance-tech exports. Instead, it requires national licensing authorities to assess each export against a set of criteria, including the risk of the item being used for internal repression or serious human rights violations. If an exporter knows or has been informed that a listed item is intended for such purposes, a license is required and can be refused. The regulation also empowers member states to impose controls on unlisted items if they have concrete reasons to suspect misuse.

### Do European companies need to apply for export licenses for every export?

No. The regulation uses a two-tier system: the common control list (Annex I) requires license applications for exports to all non-EU countries for listed items. For unlisted items, a catch-all clause applies only when the exporter is aware (or has been informed) that the item is intended for use in weapons of mass destruction, military end-uses in embargoed countries, or serious human rights abuses. Additionally, the regulation introduces a Global Export License for low-risk items sent to trusted destinations such as the United States, Japan, or Australia, reducing administrative burdens.
