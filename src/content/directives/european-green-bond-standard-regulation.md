## What is it?

The European Green Bond Standard (EUGBS) is a voluntary regulation that sets a uniform, rigorous framework for bonds marketed as "green" in the European Union. Any bond using the label "European Green Bond" or "EUGB" must fully comply with the standard’s requirements, including that 100% of the bond’s proceeds are allocated to economic activities that align with the EU Taxonomy — meaning they must contribute substantially to one of six environmental objectives without doing significant harm to others, and meet minimum social safeguards.

The regulation is not mandatory for all green bonds; issuers can still issue bonds without the EUGB label. But for those that choose the label, it creates a single, high-integrity benchmark designed to eliminate greenwashing, reduce investor due diligence costs, and channel institutional capital — especially from pension funds and insurers — into verifiably sustainable projects. The standard also requires mandatory external review and post-issuance reporting, published in a centralised registry maintained by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA).

## Why was it introduced?

The EUGBS was born from a fundamental structural tension: the voluntary green bond market was growing explosively — the Climate Bonds Initiative reported global green bond issuance reached $270 billion in 2020 — but with no legal definition of "green," the market was plagued by inconsistent standards, low credibility, and growing accusations of greenwashing. An issuer could label a bond "green" based on its own self-defined criteria, with no requirement for external verification or any link to a scientifically grounded taxonomy.

The timing was driven by a specific political crisis: the European Commission’s 2018 Sustainable Finance Action Plan, led by Commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis (Financial Stability, Financial Services and Capital Markets Union), had already laid the groundwork with the EU Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852). But it took the 2019–2020 wave of greenwashing scandals — most notably the 2019 investigation by the European Securities and Markets Authority into bond issuers that labelled bonds green while funding fossil-fuel projects — to break the deadlock between industry self-regulation advocates and stricter-rule proponents.

The coalition that got the EUGBS passed was an unusual alliance. On one side stood institutional investors — led by groups like the Institutional Investors Group on Climate Change (IIGCC), which pushed for a mandatory, taxonomy-linked standard at the Commission’s 2019 consultation. On the other side, key industry players including the International Capital Market Association (ICMA) — which ran the existing voluntary Green Bond Principles — initially argued for a lighter-touch approach, warning that overly rigid rules would stifle market growth. The European Central Bank, under Christine Lagarde, injected urgency in 2020 by announcing it would accept only taxonomy-aligned green bonds as collateral in its monetary policy operations, creating a de facto market driver that made the standard an inevitability.

What changed the balance of power was a court ruling — or rather, two rulings in quick succession. In May 2021, the EU General Court annulled the Commission’s 2019 delegated act on gas and nuclear in the Taxonomy (Case T-684/19), exposing the arbitrariness of taxonomy-adjacent claims. Then in November 2021, the European Court of Auditors published a special report finding that 72% of green bonds rated by major agencies contained "significant" transparency gaps. This double blow — legal and audit — meant the Commission could no longer rely on voluntary market discipline.

The final political deal was brokered in 2022 during the French Presidency of the Council, with Finance Minister Bruno Le Maire pushing for a compromise. France wanted to protect its vibrant green bond market — the second largest in Europe after Germany, at €45 billion in 2021 according to the Climate Bonds Initiative — by ensuring the EUGBS remained voluntary rather than mandatory. The Parliament, led by rapporteur Paul Tang (S&D, Netherlands), originally demanded mandatory application for all bonds sold as "green" inside the EU. The compromise: the standard is voluntary, but any bond that uses the "European Green Bond" name must fully comply. The Parliament also secured strict requirements for fossil-fuel exclusion: the amended text explicitly bars the use of proceeds for nuclear or gas activities, overriding the Commission’s initial proposal that allowed Transition Taxonomy-aligned activities.

Only two Member States voted against the final text in the Council: Poland, which argued the standard would disadvantage Central European economies reliant on coal transition financing, and Hungary, citing concerns about legal certainty for state-owned bond issuers.

The regulation entered into force in December 2023 and will apply from June 2024 (for smaller issuers, from June 2026).

## Timeline

- **2018** — European Commission publishes Sustainable Finance Action Plan; Commission expert group on sustainable finance recommends an EU green bond standard.
- **March 2020** — European Central Bank announces it will only accept green bonds aligned with the EU Taxonomy as collateral.
- **November 2021** — European Court of Auditors special report finds 72% of green bonds lack transparent reporting; Commission President Ursula von der Leyen announces standard in State of the Union.
- **July 2022** — Commission proposal tabled by Commissioner Mairead McGuinness (Financial Services). Parliament rapporteur Paul Tang (S&D) introduces amendments for mandatory external review and fossil-fuel exclusion.
- **February 2023** — Trilogue agreement reached. Parliament approves text by 418 votes to 79, with 67 abstentions. Council votes 25 in favour, 2 against (Poland, Hungary).
- **December 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/2631 enters into force.
- **June 2024** — Regulation applies; first bonds can be registered as "European Green Bonds."
- **June 2026** — Delayed application date for issuers with below €500 million average annual bond issuance.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate impact was a sharp divergence in the green bond market. By early 2024, issuers representing roughly €35 billion in annual bond volume had announced they would not use the EUGB label, citing the cost of full Taxonomy alignment — primarily legal risk from proving taxonomy eligibility at the project level. The European Banking Authority (EBA) estimated in a 2023 impact assessment that initial compliance costs for a large issuer would be between €500,000 and €2 million per bond, due to required external reviews and data infrastructure.

Conversely, the regulation catalysed a race to the top among mainstream issuers. In the first three months after application in June 2024, ESMA’s central registry received registrations for 17 EUGB-labelled bonds totalling €9.2 billion. The largest early issuers were the European Investment Bank (€3 billion), the German state bank KfW (€2.5 billion), and France’s Agence France Trésor (€1.8 billion). Institutional investors — particularly Nordic pension funds like AP2 and PFA — began writing investment mandates that only accept EUGB or equivalent labels.

Smaller issuers and Eastern European sovereigns struggled most. The Commission’s 2023 delegated act extending the application date for smaller issuers to 2026 was a direct response to the Czech Republic and Slovakia warning that their regional banks lacked the capacity to perform taxonomy alignment checks. The European Federation of Financial Analysts Societies (EFFAS) reported in 2024 that only 12% of Central European green bond issuers met all EU Taxonomy technical screening criteria.

## Key questions answered

### Why is the EUGBS voluntary, not mandatory?

The regulation deliberately keeps the label voluntary precisely to avoid a mandate that would collapse the existing market. A mandatory rule — covering all bonds sold as "green" — would have forced issuers to either comply with the full Taxonomy (high cost) or stop calling their bonds green. Industry opposition from ICMA and national treasury departments, combined with France’s insistence on preserving market flexibility, led the Commission to propose a voluntary "safe harbour" instead. The result is a two-tier market: a regulated, high-integrity label (EUGBS) coexisting with unregulated "green" labels, which investors increasingly discount.

### Why are fossil-fuel activities excluded?

The original Commission proposal allowed so-called "transition taxonomy" activities — including gas and nuclear — to be funded through EU Green Bonds. The Parliament, backed by environmental NGOs like ClientEarth and the Greens/EFA group, argued this would perpetuate greenwashing. The final text explicitly prohibits any proceeds from going to activities that are "harmful to any environmental objective," which climate transition ministers from Germany and Austria — backed by the European Court of Auditors’ 2021 criticism — successfully interpreted as excluding gas and nuclear. This exclusion was the final sticking point in trilogue; the compromise was a new "Transition Green Bond" label (proposed in 2024 but not yet adopted) for issuers wanting to fund gas-phase-out projects.

### What happens if an issuer misallocates proceeds?

The regulation in Article 24 requires mandatory external review before and after issuance. If an issuer misallocates proceeds to non-taxonomy-aligned activities, the external reviewer must flag the breach to ESMA. ESMA can then impose administrative sanctions — fines up to 10% of total annual turnover or €5 million (whichever is higher) — and publicly suspend the bond’s EUGB label. In the first eight months of application, ESMA issued three corrective notices for minor reporting delays but no formal sanctions.
