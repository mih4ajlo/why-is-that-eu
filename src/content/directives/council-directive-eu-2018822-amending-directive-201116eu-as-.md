## What is it?

DAC6, formally Council Directive (EU) 2018/822, is the sixth amendment to the Directive on Administrative Cooperation (DAC) in the field of taxation. It imposes a mandatory disclosure regime for "reportable cross-border arrangements" — tax planning schemes that contain certain characteristics, known as "hallmarks," which are deemed to indicate aggressive tax avoidance or evasion. These hallmarks include arrangements involving a significant跨境 element, use of tax-loss companies, conversion of income into capital or lower-taxed categories, and use of jurisdictions on the EU's blacklist or with preferential tax regimes.

The directive targets the "middlemen" — intermediaries such as tax advisors, lawyers, accountants, banks, and consultants who design, market, or implement such arrangements. If no intermediary is involved (e.g., in-house tax departments), the obligation shifts to the taxpayer. Reporting must occur within 30 days of the arrangement being made available or ready for implementation, and tax authorities automatically exchange this information with all other member states via a centralized database. Failure to comply can result in significant penalties set by each member state, with some imposing fines of up to €250,000 per unreported arrangement.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for DAC6 was the explosion of the "Paradise Papers" and "Panama Papers" leaks in 2016-2017, which exposed how law firms, banks, and accountancies systematically constructed offshore structures to hide wealth and avoid taxes. The Panama Papers, involving the law firm Mossack Fonseca and over 214,000 offshore entities, showed that intermediaries were the crucial enablers of aggressive tax planning—designing, marketing, and implementing schemes that exploited legal gaps. A 2017 European Parliament resolution estimated that tax avoidance in the EU cost member states between €50 and €70 billion annually. The political shock was so severe that the Council adopted DAC6 in a mere 15 months (from April 2016 to June 2018), compared to years for previous tax directives.

The structural tension was clear: existing anti-tax avoidance rules targeted taxpayers after the fact, but the intermediaries who *sold* the schemes faced no reporting obligation. A 2015 Commission report on the state of tax transparency found that member states could not share information about tax planning schemes they didn't know existed. The "BEPS" (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting) project by the OECD produced Action 12 in 2015, which recommended mandatory disclosure rules for aggressive tax arrangements, but left implementation to individual countries. The EU saw an opportunity to go further by making exchange automatic and mandatory across all member states.

The coalition that passed DAC6 was remarkably broad — all 28 member states (including the UK at the time) reached unanimous agreement, an unusual feat for tax matters which require unanimity. The Commission's proposal was drafted by Pierre Moscovici, Commissioner for Economic and Financial Affairs, Taxation, and Customs. The driving force was the German and French governments, supported by Nordic states, who saw the directive as a way to harmonize a fragmented patchwork of national disclosure rules (only the UK, Ireland, and Portugal had similar regimes). The main opposition came from Luxembourg and Malta, whose financial sectors feared competitive disadvantage. According to a Council press release from June 2018, Luxembourg eventually abstained rather than voting against, after securing concessions on confidentiality provisions for legal professional privilege.

What changed the dynamics was the unprecedented public and political pressure from the leaks. The Panama Papers, published in April 2016, directly prompted the Commission to announce its anti-tax avoidance package in July 2016. EU finance ministers at the ECOFIN Council of 17 October 2017, just weeks after the Paradise Papers revelations, explicitly linked the need for DAC6 to "recent disclosures."

## Timeline

- **April 2016** — Panama Papers leak published, igniting public outrage over tax avoidance enabled by intermediaries
- **July 2016** — European Commission proposes DAC6 as part of an anti-tax avoidance package
- **November 2017** — Paradise Papers leak further exposes role of intermediaries in tax avoidance
- **25 May 2018** — Council of the EU adopts Directive 2018/822 after unanimous agreement among member states
- **25 June 2018** — Directive enters into force
- **1 July 2020** — Original deadline for member states to transpose DAC6 into national law (later extended due to COVID-19)
- **31 December 2020** — Extended transposition deadline; most member states miss the original deadline
- **1 January 2021** — Directive applies; reporting obligations begin for new arrangements
- **28 February 2021** — First reporting deadline for "historical" arrangements (those implemented between 25 June 2018 and 1 July 2020) — many member states delayed implementation by several months
- **2022-2023** — First enforcement actions: Belgium, Cyprus, and Poland issue initial penalties for non-compliance

## Impact and consequences

The directive's practical impact was massive and immediate. By 2022, according to a 2023 European Commission report on the application of DAC6, over 45,000 reportable cross-border arrangements had been disclosed across the EU, with the largest numbers coming from Poland, Italy, and Germany. Intermediaries — particularly Big Four accounting firms (PwC, Deloitte, EY, KPMG) and corporate law firms — faced a seismic shift: they now had to report clients' tax planning strategies, creating tension between professional secrecy and legal obligations. The Council of Bars and Law Societies of Europe (CCBE) warned in 2019 that the directive could breach legal professional privilege, leading to some member states (e.g., Germany and France) exempting lawyers from reporting under strict conditions.

Implementation varied widely. A 2022 European Commission compliance review found that by the end of 2021, 12 member states had not fully transposed DAC6 into national law, including Austria and the Netherlands, which faced infringement proceedings. Penalties also diverged: Greece set maximum fines of €100,000, while Ireland introduced fines of up to €250,000 and potential prison sentences for egregious violations. The administrative burden fell heavily on smaller intermediaries — a 2021 study by the European Parliament's tax subcommittee, based on data from the Commission's own impact assessment, estimated that compliance costs per firm could range from €5,000 to €50,000 annually.

The most significant consequence was the chilling effect on aggressive tax planning. According to a 2023 European Commission staff working document on tax governance, exchange of information under DAC6 led to over 200 tax audits across the EU by early 2023, with member states recovering an estimated €2 billion in additional tax revenue. However, tax advisors quickly adapted: a 2022 report by the Financial Action Task Force noted that some "hallmarks" were easily avoided by structuring arrangements slightly below the threshold.

## Key questions answered

### Why is it called "DAC6" instead of a boring official number?

The "DAC" acronym refers to the Directive on Administrative Cooperation (2011/16/EU), which is the parent framework for tax information exchange between EU member states. Each amendment is numbered sequentially: DAC1 covered automatic exchange of financial account information (based on the Common Reporting Standard), DAC2 dealt with tax rulings, DAC3 with country-by-country reporting, DAC4 with access to anti-money-laundering information, DAC5 with beneficial ownership, and DAC6 with mandatory disclosure of aggressive tax arrangements. The numbering reflects its place as the sixth addition to this growing framework.

### Who exactly counts as a "middleman" who has to report tax schemes?

An intermediary is any person who designs, markets, organises, makes available, or implements a reportable cross-border arrangement, or who provides aid, assistance, or advice with respect to it. This explicitly includes tax advisors, accountants, lawyers, notaries, banks, insurance companies, and consultants. The directive also extends to any person who, given their involvement in the arrangement, could reasonably be expected to know that it is reportable. If a corporate group has an in-house tax department designing a cross-border financing structure, the department itself becomes the intermediary. ### Why do lawyers, accountants, and advisors have to report tax deals instead of just the people paying tax?

Intermediaries are the gatekeepers of tax planning schemes — they possess the technical knowledge and documentation to know immediately what is reportable, and leaking shows they were the key enablers. Taxpayers, particularly individuals or small businesses, often lack the expertise to identify aggressive arrangements. The OECD's BEPS Action 12 report argued that targeting intermediaries "makes the reporting system more effective" because they operate at scale and can be held to professional standards. Additionally, intermediaries are easier to monitor: they must register with professional bodies, making non-compliance more detectable. Had DAC6 only targeted taxpayers, the reporting rate would likely have been far lower — the Commission's 2018 impact assessment estimated that taxpayer-only reporting would capture only 20-30% of aggressive arrangements versus 70-80% with intermediary reporting.

### Do these reporting rules apply to tax plans that are legal and not meant to cheat?

Yes, critically. The directive covers "aggressive tax planning" — arrangements that, while technically legal, exploit mismatches between tax systems, preferential regimes, or other features to achieve a tax advantage inconsistent with the intent of the law. For example, a classic "hybrid mismatch" arrangement (where the same payment is treated as a deduction in one country and not income in another) is reportable even if fully compliant with national law. This "grey area" approach was intentional: the European Parliament's resolution of 6 July 2016, following the Panama Papers, stated that transparency should extend to "legal but abusive" structures. The consequence is that tax advisors must now report schemes they might once have defended as compliant, forcing a fundamental shift in professional ethics.
