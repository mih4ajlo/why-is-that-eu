## What is it?

AMLD6 is a directive that requires all EU member states to criminalise money laundering in a uniform way, setting minimum definitions for what constitutes a money laundering offence, what the penalties must be, and how far criminal liability can extend to legal persons—companies, trusts, and other entities—not just individuals. It replaced the patchwork of national criminal codes that had allowed money launderers to operate across borders by exploiting inconsistencies.

The directive defines money laundering as knowing possession, transfer, concealment, conversion, or use of property derived from criminal activity. It mandates that member states ensure these acts are punishable by a maximum prison term of at least four years, and it extends liability to legal persons—allowing fines of at least €5 million for companies, or up to 10% of annual turnover in serious cases.

Unlike its predecessors (AMLD1-5), which focused on preventive measures such as customer due diligence and suspicious transaction reporting, AMLD6 is a criminal law instrument. It operates alongside the 2015/849/EU directive (AMLD4) and its 2018 amendment (AMLD5), which deal with the regulatory framework. The directive was adopted as part of a broader EU response to the Panama Papers scandal and the growing recognition that financial crime networks easily outran national law enforcement.

## Why was it introduced?

The single most dramatic trigger for AMLD6 was the **Panama Papers leak in April 2016**, which exposed how law firms, banks, and shell companies across Europe systematically helped wealthy clients hide assets and launder money. The data, leaked from the Panamanian law firm Mossack Fonseca, implicated banks in over 200 countries and territories, including dozens of European financial institutions. The EU's response was swift and unusual: within weeks, the European Parliament established a special committee on tax rulings and other measures similar in nature or effect (TAXE2), and by July 2016, the Commission announced its intent to introduce criminal law harmonisation for money laundering.

Before AMLD6, the EU had relied on a patchwork of national criminal laws. While the 1990 Council of Europe Convention and the 2005 Third Anti-Money Laundering Directive (2005/60/EC) had set standards, they left significant room for interpretation. In Germany, money laundering was punishable by up to 10 years, in Malta by only 2 years. Some countries required proof of intent; others allowed conviction for negligence. This variation gave sophisticated launderers a menu of jurisdictions to target—a structural tension that the 2015 FATF mutual evaluation report on the EU had flagged as a vulnerability.

The coalition that pushed AMLD6 through was unusually broad. The European Parliament's **Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON)** and **Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs Committee (LIBE)** jointly championed the directive, with rapporteurs **Judith Sargentini (Greens/EFA, Netherlands)** and **Emil Radev (EPP, Bulgaria)** coordinating efforts. The Commission, under **First Vice-President Frans Timmermans** and **Commissioner for Justice, Consumers and Gender Equality Věra Jourová**, made anti-money laundering a flagship of the 2016 Action Plan to strengthen the fight against terrorist financing. The Council—particularly finance ministers from Germany, France, and the Netherlands—supported strong criminal penalties, while the UK, then still an EU member, pushed for provisions that protected its financial services sector.

Opposition came primarily from **the business lobby**, represented by **BusinessEurope** and the **European Banking Federation (EBF)** , which warned that extending criminal liability to companies could chill legitimate cross-border activity, especially for smaller firms. **The European Central Bank** expressed concern that excessive liability could destabilise banks caught up in regulatory disputes. The **European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee (JURI)** raised objections about legal certainty, arguing that the directive's broad definition of proceeds of crime could capture legitimate business income if a company accidentally violated a related regulation. However, the Panama Papers moment had created an unbreakable political momentum: only 37 MEPs voted against the final text in the European Parliament vote on **25 October 2018** (with 537 in favour and 14 abstentions).

Timing was critical. The directive was proposed in December 2016, just months after the **Terrorist Financing Action Plan** (February 2016) that followed the November 2015 Paris attacks. The attacks had shown that terrorist networks financed themselves through small-scale fraud and money laundering, not just large-scale organised crime. This dual focus—on high-end corruption (Panama Papers) and low-level terrorist finance (Paris attacks)—gave the Commission an irresistible argument: criminal law harmonisation was not just about tax evasion but about public security.

What changed fundamentally was that earlier attempts to harmonise criminal penalties had always been blocked by national sovereignty concerns. The **Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (1999)** and the **2001 Framework Decision on money laundering** had set mutual recognition standards but left penalising to member states. The Lisbon Treaty (2009) gave the EU explicit competence in criminal law for certain crimes, including money laundering, but member states resisted using it until the Panama Papers broke the political dam.

## Timeline

- **April 2016** — Panama Papers leak published by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists, exposing widespread use of offshore structures by European banks and corporations.
- **July 2016** — European Commission announces an Action Plan to strengthen the fight against terrorist financing, including a commitment to propose criminal law harmonisation for money laundering.
- **December 2016** — Commission proposal for AMLD6 is tabled by Commissioner Věra Jourová, alongside a revision of the preventive AMLD4 (which became AMLD5).
- **March 2017** — European Parliament's ECON and LIBE committees begin joint scrutiny of the proposal, with public hearings including testimony from **Mossack Fonseca whistleblower John Doe** (via video link).
- **October 2018** — European Parliament adopts the directive with 537 votes in favour, 37 against, 14 abstentions.
- **November 2018** — Council of the EU formally adopts the directive.
- **December 2018** — Directive enters into force on the twentieth day following publication in the Official Journal (2 December 2018).
- **3 December 2020** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2020–2023** — European Commission launches infringement proceedings against several member states, including **Cyprus, Malta, and Luxembourg**, for late or incomplete transposition.
- **February 2024** — The Commission reports that all member states have now transposed AMLD6, though implementation quality varies significantly.

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced all 27 member states to standardise their criminal penalties for money laundering. Countries that had previously treated money laundering as a minor offence—such as **Malta** (2 years maximum) and **Cyprus** (3 years)—raised their penalties to at least 4 years. **Germany** and **Austria** had to amend their codes to explicitly include self-laundering (where a criminal also launders their own proceeds), which some national courts had previously excluded.

Legal persons—companies, partnerships, trusts—became directly liable. Under AMLD6, a bank that fails to prevent money laundering by its employees can now face corporate criminal liability with fines of at least €5 million or 10% of global turnover. This provision hit **Danske Bank** particularly hard: in 2022, the Danish bank reported it had set aside **€8.5 billion** in provisions related to its Estonian branch's €200 billion money laundering scandal, with the EU's new liability framework making it harder to argue that only individual employees were responsible.

The directive also required member states to criminalise the receipt, possession, or use of proceeds from criminal activity, not just the act of laundering itself. This closed a loophole that had allowed individuals to argue they were merely "beneficiaries" of crime rather than participants. According to the **European Commission's 2020 impact assessment**, this expansion was expected to increase the number of money laundering convictions across the EU by an estimated 15–25%, though actual figures have not yet been systematically published.

Industry response was mixed. Large banks, represented by the **European Banking Federation**, invested heavily in compliance automation, but smaller financial institutions in **Eastern Europe** struggled with the new criminal liability. The **European Banking Authority (EBA)** , empowered by AMLD5 and AMLD6 to coordinate anti-money laundering supervision, issued in 2021 a set of guidelines on the treatment of criminal liability for financial institutions, which faced pushback from several national central banks concerned about disproportionate application.

Enforcement has been uneven. **Luxembourg** and **Cyprus**—both major financial centres—were notably slow to transpose the directive, with the Commission launching infringement cases in 2021. **Switzerland**, not an EU member, nonetheless amended its own criminal code in 2023 to align with AMLD6, citing pressure from the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) and market access concerns.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a criminal law directive when member states already had anti-money laundering laws?

Because member states' criminal laws varied so widely that sophisticated launderers could systematically exploit the differences. A criminal who could launder money in one country with a 2-year sentence, while another country required proof of intent where negligence sufficed, simply moved operations. The EU's 2015 FATF evaluation found that cross-border money laundering cases were almost never prosecuted because evidential standards could not be reconciled. AMLD6 solved this by defining the crime and the penalty floor uniformly.

### Why are legal persons (companies) held criminally liable instead of just individuals?

Because the Panama Papers and subsequent scandals—such as Danske Bank's €200 billion Estonian branch laundering scheme (2018–2022)—showed that money laundering often happens through institutional structures where no single individual can be proved to have criminal intent, but the organisation as a whole benefits. AMLD6 makes a company liable if a person acting on its behalf commits money laundering for the company's benefit. The Commission argued this was necessary because, in the words of its 2016 impact assessment, "corporate structures are the primary vehicle for large-scale money laundering, and individual accountability alone has proven insufficient."

### Why is the minimum penalty set at four years?

The four-year threshold was chosen to align with the **European Arrest Warrant** framework, which only applies to offences punishable by at least three years (for surrender) or four years (for other forms of mutual recognition). By setting the penalty at four years, the Commission ensured that money laundering would be automatically extraditable across all member states, eliminating the need for double criminality checks. This was a deliberate technical move to make cross-border prosecution seamless.

### Why was AMLD6 adopted so quickly after the Panama Papers when earlier attempts had stalled?

The Panama Papers created a rare moment of unified political will across the European Parliament, Commission, and most member states. The scandal directly implicated **banks in 19 EU member states**, including major institutions such as **Deutsche Bank, HSBC, and UBS**. Public outrage was intense, and the European Parliament's special TAXE committee had already documented how tax evasion and money laundering were intertwining. The **November 2015 Paris attacks** added the terrorism finance dimension, making opposition seem politically untenable. Together, these events broke the previous decade-long deadlock over criminal law sovereignty.
