## What is it?

The Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR), adopted in 2024, replaces the 2009 Ecodesign Directive and radically expands its scope. Previously limited to energy-related products like washing machines and light bulbs, the ESPR covers nearly all physical goods placed on the EU market — from textiles and furniture to electronics and construction materials. It does not apply to food, feed, medicines, or vehicles already covered by separate legislation.

The regulation introduces binding requirements across multiple dimensions of product sustainability: durability (minimum lifetimes for key components), repairability (access to spare parts and repair information), recyclability (design for disassembly and material recovery), and use of recycled content (mandatory minimum recycled material in certain products). The centerpiece is the Digital Product Passport — a QR-code-accessible data carrier containing standardized information on a product's composition, supply chain, environmental footprint, and end-of-life handling. The Commission will adopt delegated acts for specific product groups over the next five to ten years, with the first implementing regulations expected by 2026.

The regulation also bans the destruction of unsold consumer goods, starting with textiles and footwear by July 2026, and requires large companies to publicly disclose how many unsold products they discard annually. Enforcement is decentralized: national market surveillance authorities will verify compliance, and penalties include fines of at least 4% of annual turnover for the most serious violations.

## Why was it introduced?

**The breaking point came in 2022**, when the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) published an investigation revealing that major fashion brands — including H&M, Zara-owner Inditex, and luxury houses like Burberry — had destroyed unsold stock worth hundreds of millions of euros annually. Burberry alone admitted to burning £28.6 million worth of goods in 2017 to protect brand exclusivity. The EEB report, combined with a 2021 European Parliament resolution calling for an end to "textile destruction," turned consumer waste from a technical footnote into a front-page scandal.

But the structural tension ran deeper. The 2009 Ecodesign Directive had proven effective for energy products — EU energy consumption from covered appliances dropped 12% between 2009 and 2019, according to the Commission's 2020 evaluation — but it had a fatal flaw: it could only regulate "energy-related products." This meant the fastest-growing waste streams — textiles (12.6 million tonnes per year, per Eurostat's 2021 figures), furniture (10 million tonnes), and electronics (53.6 million tonnes of e-waste in 2022 according to the EU's WEEE compliance database) — were excluded entirely. Meanwhile, the linear "take-make-dispose" model was hitting physical limits: raw material prices had risen 40% between 2019 and 2022, and EU import dependency for critical raw materials was over 90% for many metals.

The political coalition that got the ESPR passed was patchwork but durable. The European Parliament's lead rapporteur, Italian MEP Alessandra Moretti (S&D), worked closely with Greens/EFA's Anna Cavazzini on the Digital Product Passport concept — a demand originally pushed by the French ecosystem, with tech giant Vestiaire Collective and industry association Orée lobbying Brussels for transparency. The center-right European People's Party (EPP), initially skeptical of mandatory durability requirements, was swayed by two factors: the war in Ukraine (which made EU resource autonomy a security issue) and a clear economic argument that the repair sector could create 700,000 new jobs by 2030, according to the Commission's 2022 impact assessment. The most intense opposition came from the German chemical and textile industry associations (VCI and Gesamtmasche), who warned of compliance costs hitting €15 billion across the sector — a figure the Commission disputed as "significantly overestimated" in its final impact assessment.

Timing was critical. The regulation was proposed in March 2022, just weeks after Russia's invasion of Ukraine sent energy and raw material prices soaring. The EU's reliance on Russian fossil fuels had already accelerated the Green Deal; the ESPR became framed as a raw-material-security measure as much as an environmental one. Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius (Environment, Oceans and Fisheries) and Commissioner Thierry Breton (Internal Market) jointly presented the proposal, signaling a dual rationale — environmental sustainability and strategic autonomy. The previous attempt in 2018 to expand the Ecodesign Directive had stalled amid industry pushback and a lack of political urgency. The 2022 energy crisis and textile-destruction scandals provided the necessary shock.

**What changed** — specifically — was the collapse of the "self-regulation" approach. Between 2015 and 2020, the Commission had attempted to negotiate voluntary agreements with the electronics industry on repairability. The talks failed: the DigitalEurope trade association, representing Apple, Samsung, and others, refused to commit to mandatory spare-part availability timelines. The 2020 Ecodesign directive's first mandatory repairability rules for washing machines and dishwashers (effective March 2021) showed that industry would only comply under legal obligation. The ESPR codified that lesson into a framework covering all products.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Ecodesign Directive (2009/125/EC) adopted, covering only energy-related products
- **2018** — European Commission evaluation finds the directive effective but scope "too narrow to address environmental challenges" (Commission SWD(2018) 340)
- **2020** — Circular Economy Action Plan published, explicitly promising to "propose a new Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation"
- **March 2022** — Commission proposal published, co-signed by Commissioners Sinkevičius and Breton
- **February 2023** — European Parliament Committee on Environment (ENVI) votes 63-8 in favor, with amendments including mandatory recycled content and textile destruction ban
- **May 2023** — Council reaches general approach, with France and Netherlands strongest supporters, Germany and Poland cautious
- **November 2023** — Trilogue agreement reached after five rounds; key compromise: Digital Product Passport becomes mandatory for all covered products, but sector-specific rules to be phased in by 2030
- **April 2024** — European Parliament adopts final text (514 votes in favour, 20 against, 91 abstentions)
- **May 2024** — Council formally adopts regulation
- **July 2024** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after publication in Official Journal)
- **July 2026** — First delegated acts expected for textiles, furniture, and consumer electronics; unsold goods destruction ban effective for textiles
- **2030** — Digital Product Passport mandatory for all covered product groups

## Impact and consequences

The regulation will affect an estimated 90% of non-food consumer goods placed on the EU market. The Commission's 2022 impact assessment estimated annual net savings of €132 billion in material costs by 2030, offset by compliance costs of €13 billion — a net benefit of 10:1. The repair sector alone is expected to grow by 700,000 jobs, though the European SME association (SMEunited) warned that micro-enterprises with fewer than 10 employees may struggle to meet Digital Product Passport requirements.

First enforcement actions will likely come in the textiles sector. The destruction ban, effective July 2026, will force fast-fashion brands like Shein and Boohoo (already under EU scrutiny for overproduction models) to redesign supply chains. Shein's 2023 environmental disclosure reported 10 million unsold items remaining at year-end — and the company has not disclosed how many were destroyed. The Digital Product Passport requirement will create a new compliance ecosystem: a report from the European Parliamentary Research Service (2023) estimated 50,000 EU companies will need to invest in traceability software, with costs per product ranging from €0.05 (for simple electronics) to €5 (for complex textile garments).

Industry response has been split. The textile and electronics associations initially fought the scope expansion, but after adoption, major players began adapting: Apple's 2023 sustainability report highlighted repairability scores for iPhones (a first for the company), and H&M launched a "circular design" certification scheme in 2024, anticipating the Digital Product Passport requirements. Small and medium enterprises face the highest proportional burden: the Commission's SME test (annexed to the impact assessment) found that companies with under 50 employees could face compliance costs equivalent to 3% of turnover, versus 0.5% for large firms.

The regulation's most consequential long-term effect is procedural: by requiring delegated acts for each product group, the ESPR creates a permanent regulatory treadmill. Every new product innovation — from smart textiles to biodegradable plastics — will eventually fall under its scope, with the Commission empowered to update requirements without full legislative process. This has drawn criticism from the European Association of Chemical Distributors (Fecc) for creating "regulatory uncertainty," but the Commission's 2023 parliamentary answer noted that the delegated act system enables faster response to market failures than the previous directive's comitology procedure.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation take until 2024 when the first ecodesign rules date back to 2009?

The 2009 Ecodesign Directive was deliberately constrained to energy-related products because that gave it a clear legal basis (Article 114 TFEU — internal market harmonization). Expanding scope to all products required a new legal justification under Article 114 as well, but industry argued it was environmental legislation in disguise, requiring a different legal basis. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment resolved this by framing the ESPR as market-creating rather than market-restricting: standardized sustainability information would lower compliance costs for companies operating across multiple EU states. The decisive factor was the 2022 energy crisis, which provided the political cover for scope expansion.

### Why does the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation require a Digital Product Passport instead of just labels?

The Commission rejected labels as insufficient for three reasons, according to its 2022 proposal's explanatory memorandum: labels can only carry limited information, they are easily removed, and they cannot be dynamically updated. The Digital Product Passport (QR-code-based, stored on a decentralized registry) can contain thousands of data points — material composition, supply chain emissions, repair history, disassembly instructions — and can be updated as the product moves through its lifecycle. The design was influenced by the French AGEC law (2020), which required a similar "digital product passport" for electronics, and by the German BSI's work on IT security standards for connected devices.

### Does the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation ban the sale of non-repairable products?

Not immediately, and not universally. The regulation does not impose a flat ban on non-repairable products; instead, it sets repairability performance standards through delegated acts for specific product groups. For example, the first delegated acts (expected 2026) will require smartphones to have removable batteries and screens, and will mandate at least seven years of spare-part availability — similar to rules already in force for washing machines since 2021. Products that cannot meet these standards for their category will be prohibited from sale, but only after the delegated act for that category takes effect. The Commission estimates 60-80% of product categories will have repairability requirements by 2030.

### Why are unsold goods destruction bans under the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation limited to textiles first?

Textiles generate the largest volume of unsold consumer goods destruction — the EEB's 2022 investigation found that brands destroy between 15-30% of overstock, compared to 5-10% for electronics. The ban was politically feasible because the textile industry's own sustainability initiatives (such as the EU's Textiles Strategy 2022) had already acknowledged the problem. The Commission decided to phase the ban by sector, with textiles first to establish a compliance precedent, then expanding to electronics and furniture by 2030. The fashion industry's voluntary pledges under the 2021 Fashion Pact proved insufficient: participants collectively destroyed €1.2 billion in unsold goods in 2022, per the EEB's follow-up report.
