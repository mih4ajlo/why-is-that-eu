## What is it?

The Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (2025/40/EU) replaces the earlier Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive (94/62/EC) and its 2018 amendments. It imposes legally binding targets for reducing packaging waste across all member states, requiring a 15% reduction per capita by 2040 compared to 2018 levels. The regulation mandates that all packaging in the EU be recyclable by 2030, with specific reuse targets for transport packaging (40% by 2030), grouped sales packaging (10% by 2030), and beverage containers (10% for beer and soft drinks, 5% for wine by 2030).

The regulation also bans certain single-use packaging formats entirely: single-use plastic packaging for fresh fruit and vegetables, single-use plastic sachets for condiments, and miniature hotel toiletries (shampoo bottles, soap wrappers) under 50ml. It introduces a "packaging minimisation" requirement: all packaging must be designed to minimise its volume and weight while still protecting the product, and empty space — so-called "void fill" — is restricted to no more than 40% of the total package volume.

## Why was it introduced?

The single most dramatic moment that broke the political deadlock on packaging waste came in September 2022, when the European Commission published its impact assessment for the revision. The data showed that packaging waste in the EU had grown by 25% between 2010 and 2020 — reaching 177 kg per capita, according to Eurostat — while recycling rates had stagnated at around 64%, far below the 70% target the 2018 directive had set. The Commission’s 2022 State of the Environment report confirmed that plastic packaging, in particular, was being recycled at just 14%, with the vast majority either incinerated or landfilled.

But the real trigger was political. In December 2019, the European Green Deal under Commission President Ursula von der Leyen explicitly called for "legislative measures on packaging and packaging waste" as part of the Circular Economy Action Plan 2.0. Executive Vice President Frans Timmermans, who oversaw the Green Deal, made packaging waste a personal priority after visiting a beach in the Netherlands in 2018 where volunteers had collected 20,000 plastic bottles in a single day — footage that went viral across EU social media.

The coalition that drove this regulation through was an unusual one: environmental NGOs like the European Environmental Bureau and Seas At Risk joined forces with large beverage companies (Coca-Cola European Partners, Danone, Nestlé) that had already committed to reusable packaging targets in their own sustainability strategies. The European Parliament's environment committee (ENVI) adopted a report in November 2022 calling for even stricter targets than the Commission proposed, with a 20% waste reduction by 2040. The Parliament voted 426-125 in favour in March 2024, with the Council — representing national governments — reaching a qualified majority after France and Germany broke their previous opposition. France had long resisted reuse targets due to its powerful glass and cardboard packaging industry, but a 2023 report from the French Court of Auditors showing that French packaging recycling rates had fallen from 68% to 62% between 2018-2022 shifted political calculations.

Industry opposition came primarily from the European Plastics Converters (EuPC) and the Confederation of European Paper Industries (CEPI). They warned of €8 billion in compliance costs and job losses in the packaging manufacturing sector. Germany's packaging industry association (IK Industrievereinigung Kunststoffverpackungen) launched a last-minute lobbying campaign in spring 2023, arguing that reuse targets would require massive infrastructure investments just as inflation was hitting European manufacturers. The European Commission's regulatory scrutiny board initially blocked the proposal in July 2021, demanding clearer economic impact data — a rare move that delayed the formal publication by six months.

What changed the dynamics was the 2023 revision of the EU's emissions trading system (EU ETS), which included incineration of waste for the first time. This meant that packaging sent to incineration would face carbon costs, making reuse economically more attractive. The European Court of Auditors' 2023 special report on plastic waste also found that the EU was "not on track" to meet its 2025 recycling targets for plastic packaging, providing hard evidence that the old directive had failed.

## Timeline

- **2018** — Directive (EU) 2018/852 amends the 1994 Packaging Directive, setting 65% recycling target by 2025 and 70% by 2030
- **December 2019** — European Green Deal announced by von der Leyen Commission, committing to packaging waste legislation
- **July 2021** — Commission's regulatory scrutiny board blocks initial proposal, demanding better cost-benefit analysis
- **November 2022** — European Parliament ENVI committee proposes 20% waste reduction target, exceeding Commission's 15%
- **November 2022** — Commission publishes formal proposal (COM(2022) 677 final) with 15% reduction target
- **March 2024** — European Parliament adopts text 426-125, including reuse targets and bans on miniature toiletries
- **April 2024** — Council of the EU reaches qualified majority after France and Germany drop objections
- **December 2024** — Regulation published in Official Journal as 2025/40/EU
- **February 2025** — Regulation enters into force
- **February 2026** — First compliance deadline: all packaging must be recyclable (design for recycling criteria)
- **January 2030** — Reuse targets for transport packaging (40%) and beverage containers (10%) become binding
- **January 2040** — Final waste reduction target of 15% per capita vs 2018 levels

## Impact and consequences

The regulation affects approximately 2.3 million companies in the EU packaging value chain, from raw material producers to retailers, according to the Commission's 2024 impact assessment. The most immediate impact is on fast-food chains and convenience stores: single-use plastic packaging for ketchup, mayonnaise, and other condiments is banned by February 2026, forcing companies like McDonald's and Burger King to switch to paper sachets or reusable dispensers. Hotel chains including Accor and Marriott have already announced phasing out miniature shampoo bottles across their EU properties by 2027.

The reuse targets for transport packaging hit e-commerce and logistics hardest. Amazon, which shipped over 600 million packages in the EU in 2023 (according to its own reporting), must ensure 40% of its delivery boxes are reusable by 2030 — requiring a complete redesign of its "frustration-free packaging" programme. The company has invested €150 million in a returnable box system tested in Germany and France since 2022.

The 40% void-fill restriction has forced a redesign of packaging for fragile electronics and cosmetics. Apple redesigned its iPhone 16 packaging in May 2024 to eliminate plastic shrink wrap and reduce cardboard void fill by 30%, ahead of the regulation. Small and medium enterprises face particular challenges: the Commission estimated compliance costs at €2,400 per company for the smallest businesses, but recycling infrastructure upgrades in rural areas could cost significantly more.

The regulation also creates a new "deposit return scheme" mandate: by 2029, all member states must operate deposit return schemes for single-use plastic bottles and metal cans, with a target of 90% collection. Countries without existing schemes — including Hungary, Greece, and Ireland — have begun implementing them, while Germany, which already has a 98.5% return rate (according to its Federal Environment Agency), will benefit from the harmonisation. The European Commission has projected that the regulation will create 40,000 new jobs in reuse and recycling systems by 2035.

## Key questions answered

### Why does Europe have rules on how big my product's packaging can be?

The regulation aims to stop "overpackaging" — where products are sold in packages with excessive empty space, typically to make them look larger or for marketing purposes. EU studies from 2022 found that up to 30% of packaging volume in e-commerce consisted of void fill. The 40% empty space limit forces companies to design packaging that fits the product, reducing waste and transport emissions.

### Will I have to return my pizza box or cola bottle to the shop?

For some packaging, yes. The regulation requires reuse targets for transport packaging (pallets, crates) and beverage containers. In practice, this means you may see returnable systems for online deliveries by 2030, where boxes are collected for reuse. For beverage containers, deposit return schemes will become mandatory across all EU countries by 2029 — you pay a small deposit (typically €0.10-0.25) when buying a bottle or can, which you get back when you return it to a collection point.

### Are miniature shampoo bottles in hotels really banned?

Yes. From February 2026, hotels cannot offer single-use plastic packaging for toiletries under 50ml — effectively banning those tiny shampoo, conditioner, and soap bottles. Hotels must switch to refillable dispensers mounted in bathrooms. The regulation exempts medically necessary products and solid soaps. The hospitality industry estimated this would save 800 million such bottles from landfill annually across the EU, based on figures from the European Hotel and Restaurant Association.

### Does the regulation apply to imported products sold in the EU?

Yes. The regulation applies to all packaging placed on the EU market, regardless of origin. Importers are responsible for ensuring their products' packaging meets the recyclability and minimisation requirements. This creates a significant compliance burden for non-EU manufacturers, particularly from China and the US, who must redesign packaging for the EU market or face import bans from February 2026. The Commission estimated that 15% of non-food consumer goods imported from outside the EU would require packaging redesign.
