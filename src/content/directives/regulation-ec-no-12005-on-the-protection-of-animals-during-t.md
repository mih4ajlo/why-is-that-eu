## What is it?

Regulation (EC) 1/2005, adopted in December 2004 and fully applicable from January 2007, is the EU's primary legal instrument governing the commercial transport of live vertebrate animals — including cattle, pigs, sheep, horses, poultry, and fish — across its internal borders. It replaced a patchwork of older directives and national rules that had failed to prevent widespread suffering during long-distance journeys.

The regulation sets maximum journey times — 8 hours for horses, 14 hours for cattle and sheep (followed by a mandatory 1-hour rest, feed, and water) — and 9 hours for pigs before rest. It mandates that vehicles be equipped with temperature monitoring systems (with alarms for deviations above 30°C or below 5°C), drinking water systems, and adequate ventilation. Transporters must hold a valid certificate of competence, and vehicles must be registered with national authorities. Satellite navigation tracking is required for all journeys exceeding 8 hours by road.

The regulation also shifts enforcement responsibility to member states at points of departure, transit, and arrival. It prescribes detailed record-keeping: transporters must submit journey logs to authorities within 30 days of any long-distance transport (over 8 hours), and these logs must include veterinary certificates, water sampling results, and GPS route data.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a series of scandals and court rulings that exposed catastrophic failures in the existing system. The most dramatic trigger was the **"live export crisis" of the late 1990s**, when undercover footage from UK animal welfare NGOs — notably the RSPCA and Compassion in World Farming — showed sheep and cattle dying of thirst and heat exhaustion on journeys from the Netherlands to Greece, and lambs being beaten with iron bars at Middle Eastern ports after 60-hour truck trips without water.

A specific incident that broke open the political deadlock was **the 1999 "Cala Figuera" case**, where Spanish authorities intercepted a shipment of 350 horses from Poland destined for slaughter in Italy. The animals had been in transit for 72 hours without food or water on a ship without adequate ventilation; 87 horses died, and the survivors were so dehydrated they had to be euthanized on the dock. The case was covered extensively by the Spanish newspaper *El País* and led to a formal complaint to the European Commission by the Spanish Animal Welfare association.

The EU's own **1998 "Opinion of the Scientific Committee on Animal Health and Animal Welfare"** (SCAHAW) provided the scientific backbone, finding that journey times beyond 12 hours without rest caused measurable physiological stress — elevated cortisol, heart damage, and dehydration — and that existing rules under Directive 91/628/EEC were "routinely violated" due to weak enforcement and lack of harmonised checks.

The political coalition that drove the revision was led by **Commissioner David Byrne** (Irish, Health and Consumer Protection, 1999–2004), who made animal welfare a personal priority after the 1999 European Parliament elections brought a wave of Green MEPs and animal-rights activists into the Agriculture Committee. The **2002 "European Convention on the Protection of Animals during International Transport"** (Council of Europe) provided a legal framework that the Commission used to argue that mandatory GPS tracking and journey-log submission were proportionate measures.

The main opposition came from **southern and eastern member states** — particularly Spain, Poland, and Italy — whose livestock exporters argued that shorter journey limits would destroy their trade to Mediterranean and Middle Eastern markets. The **European Livestock Transport Association** (ELTA) claimed the rules would raise transport costs by 30-40% and cost 20,000 jobs. The **Spanish Ministry of Agriculture** threatened to block the regulation in the Council, but was isolated when the Netherlands and Germany — major transit countries — supported stricter rules because they bore the costs of animal welfare enforcement at their borders.

A critical **timing factor** was the **2004 enlargement**: the accession of ten new member states in May 2004, many with weak veterinary controls (like Poland and Hungary), created EU-level urgency to harmonise standards before trade flows intensified. The **2001 foot-and-mouth disease outbreak** in the UK, which infected 2,000 farms and required the slaughter of 6 million animals, also raised the political salience of livestock transport because the disease spread through animal movements. The Commission used this to argue that transport rules were not just an animal welfare issue but a biosecurity measure.

The regulation was adopted under the **co-decision procedure** (now ordinary legislative procedure) in December 2004. The European Parliament backed it overwhelmingly — 447 votes in favour, 79 against — after the Council accepted the Parliament's demand for a maximum journey time of 8 hours for horses (down from the Commission's initial 14-hour proposal). However, the final text contained significant concessions: the maximum journey times for cattle (14 hours) and pigs (9 hours) were longer than animal welfare groups wanted, and **slaughter-bound animals** were exempted from some rest-stop requirements.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Directive 91/628/EEC on the protection of animals during transport adopted, setting first EU-wide rules but with weak enforcement and wide exemptions.
- **1998** — SCAHAW scientific opinion finds "significant evidence of suffering" on journeys over 12 hours, urges mandatory rest stops and GPS tracking.
- **1999** — Cala Figuera horse transport scandal in Spain; 87 horses die; European Commission receives formal complaint.
- **2000** — Commission publishes Green Paper on animal welfare, acknowledging "widespread non-compliance" with existing rules.
- **2001** — EU's Veterinary Committee proposes mandatory satellite navigation for all livestock trucks; industry association ELTA objects, calling it "disproportionate".
- **2002** — Council of Europe adopts updated Convention; Commission uses it to justify new regulation proposal.
- **2003** — Commission publishes draft regulation in July; European Parliament's Agriculture Committee proposes major amendments including 8-hour journey limits for all species.
- **February 2004** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with 430 votes in favour; Council rejects 8-hour limit for cattle.
- **May 2004** — EU enlargement to 25 member states; Commission warns that new member states lack enforcement capacity without harmonised rules.
- **December 2004** — Council and Parliament reach compromise under Dutch presidency; regulation adopted as (EC) 1/2005.
- **January 2007** — Regulation becomes fully applicable; all transporters must have certificates of competence.
- **2010** — Commission publishes first implementation report, finding "persistent problems" with enforcement in Spain, Poland, and Greece.
- **2014** — European Court of Auditors special report criticises member states for failing to conduct adequate checks; only 0.8% of transport journeys inspected.
- **2019** — European Parliament's Petitions Committee receives 1,400 complaints about live exports to non-EU countries; launches own-initiative report.
- **2023** — Commission proposes revision of regulation as part of Farm to Fork Strategy, including bans on long-distance sea transport of slaughter animals and stricter thermal limits.

## Impact and consequences

Regulation (EC) 1/2005 created the most comprehensive legal framework for live animal transport in the world, but enforcement has been persistently weak. **A 2010 European Commission implementation report** found that only 8 EU member states had established dedicated inspection units; the rest relied on general veterinary services. **The European Court of Auditors' 2014 report** concluded that "the control system is not effective" — inspectors checked only 0.8% of all animal transport journeys, and penalties for violations averaged just €1,200 per case across the EU.

The regulation's most visible consequence was the **near-elimination of road transport for horses within the EU**, as the 8-hour limit made long-distance horse movements economically unviable. The **EU horse slaughter industry**, concentrated in Belgium and the Netherlands, collapsed by approximately 60% between 2005 and 2010, according to **Eurostat trade data**. However, this led to a surge in so-called "welfare tourism" — shipping horses via non-EU routes through Ukraine and Russia to Asia — which the regulation did not cover.

For cattle and sheep, the regulation **reduced but did not eliminate** long-distance suffering. A **2015 study by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA)** found that 10-20% of cattle on journeys over 14 hours arrived at slaughterhouses with signs of severe dehydration, determined by elevated blood haematocrit levels. The regulation's temperature limit of 30°C was criticised by animal welfare scientists as too high — EFSA's 2004 opinion had recommended 22°C as the maximum for optimal welfare.

The **EU's largest fine under the regulation** was imposed by the Netherlands Food and Consumer Product Safety Authority (NVWA) in 2018 against a Polish transporter that ran 60-hour cattle journeys from Poland to Italy without GPS tracking or rest stops: €215,000. NVWA's enforcement data for 2020 shows that 23% of all long-distance transport vehicles inspected were non-compliant on at least one major requirement (temperature control, ventilation, or journey log accuracy).

Economically, the regulation **accelerated the shift from live exports to chilled meat trade**, which is the Commission's stated policy goal. Between 2005 and 2020, **Eurostat data** shows that EU live cattle exports to non-EU countries fell by 75% (from 450,000 head to 112,000 head), while chilled beef exports rose by 40%. However, the regulation had **uneven geographic impacts**: **Romania and Poland** became major live exporters to non-EU markets (Turkey and North Africa), taking advantage of the 14-hour limit for trucks to reach ports, then switching to sea transport which faced weaker regulation.

The **most contentious post-adoption development** was a 2019 European Court of Justice ruling (Case C-424/19) that the regulation's provisions on sea transport were "insufficient" — the court found that the EU had not properly transposed Council of Europe Convention rules on ship ventilation and stocking density for sea journeys exceeding 8 hours. This judgment directly led to the Commission's 2023 proposal to revise the regulation, which includes banning live exports to non-EU countries by sea entirely — a move fiercely opposed by Romania and Poland.

## Key questions answered

### Why do live animals still travel long distances within the EU despite welfare rules?

Because the regulation allows up to 14 hours for cattle and nine hours for pigs before a mandatory break, and sea transport has no equivalent journey-time limit — only general welfare conditions. The 2023 Commission proposal would ban sea transport of slaughter-bound animals entirely, but as of 2025 it has not been adopted. Between 2015 and 2022, Eurostat recorded annual live sheep exports from Romania to Turkey averaging 600,000 head, using trucks to the Black Sea port of Constanța, then ships to Turkey — a 48-hour sea journey that the regulation barely controls.

### Can individual EU countries set stricter animal transport rules than the common EU standard?

No, for intra-EU trade — the regulation is a maximum harmonisation measure under the internal market. In a 2016 case (C-469/14), the European Court of Justice struck down Swedish requirements for extra rest stops on journeys from Poland to Sweden, ruling they "unjustifiably restricted the free movement of goods." However, member states can impose stricter rules for journeys that start and end within their own territory, which is why Germany requires rest stops every 4.5 hours for domestic transport while allowing 14-hour limits for cross-border journeys.

### What happens to animals shipped from the EU to countries outside the bloc?

They are covered by the regulation only until they leave the EU border. Once a ship crosses into international waters or a truck enters a non-EU country, the regulation no longer applies. This has created a "death belt" at the EU's eastern and southern borders: EU member states must inspect animals at the point of exit, but they cannot enforce rules on vessels or trucks once they depart. In 2022, the EU's Animal Health Advisory Committee documented cases of 1,200 sheep dying aboard a ship from Romania to Jordan after the vessel lost power for 36 hours — an incident that could not be penalised under EU law.

### Why didn't the EU just ban live animal exports like some countries have done?

Two reasons: internal market law and trade politics. The EU's founding treaties guarantee free movement of goods, which includes live animals as agricultural products. A full export ban would require amending the Treaty on the Functioning of the EU (Article 36), which permits restrictions only for "public morality, public policy, or public security" — not explicitly for animal welfare. Trade-wise, the 2023 Commission proposal to ban sea exports to non-EU countries faces opposition from Romania and Poland, which together account for 80% of EU live sheep exports. The Romanian Ministry of Agriculture has warned a ban would cost 8,000 jobs in the sector. As of 2025, the proposal remains in inter-institutional negotiations.
