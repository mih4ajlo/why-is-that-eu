## What is it?

The Eurodac Regulation (EU No 603/2013) created a centralised EU database for storing and comparing fingerprints of asylum seekers and individuals apprehended for irregular border-crossing. Its primary purpose is to support the Dublin Regulation, which determines which EU Member State is responsible for processing an asylum application. By matching fingerprints against the database, authorities can identify if a person has already applied for asylum or crossed a border in another Member State, preventing multiple applications and enabling transfers back to the first country of entry.

The regulation mandates that Member States take fingerprints of all asylum seekers aged 14 or over and of anyone apprehended in connection with an irregular border crossing. These fingerprints are stored for 10 years for asylum seekers and 18 months for irregular crossers, after which they must be erased. The system is managed by the European Agency for the Operational Management of Large-Scale IT Systems (eu-LISA).

## Why was it introduced?

The Eurodac system was born from a political crisis that nearly tore apart the Schengen area in the late 1990s. When the Dublin Convention (1990) tried to fix responsibility for asylum claims on the first Member State of entry, national authorities had no practical way to verify whether an asylum seeker had already been fingerprinted elsewhere. The result was systematic abuse: according to a 1998 Commission report, some Member States reported that up to 30% of asylum applicants had already applied in another state, clogging national systems and fueling anti-immigrant backlash.

The immediate trigger was the breakdown of asylum cooperation during the Kosovo crisis in 1999, when Germany — then holding the EU presidency — warned that the Dublin system was collapsing under the weight of undocumented repeat applications. Interior Minister Otto Schily (Germany) and his French counterpart Jean-Pierre Chevallier pushed for a centralised fingerprint system at the Tampere European Council in October 1999. The UK, led by Prime Minister Tony Blair, was initially skeptical about data-sharing, but the 9/11 attacks in 2001 shifted the security calculus — the UK became a vocal supporter after the Commission proposed linking Eurodac to counter-terrorism efforts.

The structural tension was clear: the Dublin system required trust that Member States could enforce its rules, but without a common database, that trust was impossible. The coalition that passed the original Eurodac Regulation (EC No 2725/2000) in December 2000 was a classic "northern vs southern" deal — northern states (Germany, Sweden, Netherlands) wanted to close loopholes for multiple applications, while southern states (Italy, Greece, Spain) wanted burden-sharing guarantees they never got. The final vote in the Council was unanimous, but only after the Commission agreed to limit data retention to asylum seekers, not all irregular migrants.

The 2013 recast (Regulation 603/2013) was triggered by two events: the 2011 European Court of Justice ruling in *NS v Secretary of State for the Home Department* (C-411/10), which found that transferring asylum seekers back to Greece violated fundamental rights due to systemic deficiencies; and the 2012 Commission evaluation showing that Eurodac was catching only 30% of repeat applications because Member States were failing to enroll fingerprints at borders. Commissioner Cecilia Malmström (Home Affairs, 2010–2014) pushed through a reform that extended the database to include law enforcement access for counter-terrorism — a move opposed by the European Parliament's civil liberties committee, which voted 38-22 against the provision in 2012. The compromise, reached in June 2013, allowed limited police access only for serious crimes and under judicial oversight.

## Timeline

- **1990** — Dublin Convention signed, assigning asylum responsibility to first Member State of entry, but without verification tools
- **1999** — Tampere European Council calls for a "common European asylum system" including fingerprint database; Kosovo crisis exposes system failures
- **2000** — Original Eurodac Regulation (EC 2725/2000) adopted by Council, effective 2003
- **2003** — Eurodac begins operations; first year sees 140,000 fingerprint records
- **2008** — Commission evaluation finds only 30% of repeat applications detected due to non-enrollment at borders
- **2011** — ECJ ruling in *NS v Home Secretary* blocks transfers to Greece
- **2013** — Recast Regulation (EU 603/2013) adopted June 26, adding law enforcement access; Commissioner Malmström negotiates compromise with Parliament
- **2015** — Migration crisis overwhelms Eurodac; over 1.8 million fingerprints taken, exceeding capacity
- **2016** — eu-LISA takes over system management; Commission proposes further reform to expand biometric data (facial images)
- **2020** — Commission proposal for Eurodac reform (COM/2020/614) adds facial images, lowers age threshold to 6, extends data retention to 5 years for irregular migrants
- **2023** — Ongoing negotiations on reform stall amid migration policy deadlock

## Impact and consequences

Eurodac transformed European asylum enforcement. By 2022, the database held over 8 million fingerprint records, according to eu-LISA's 2022 annual report. In practice, however, the system's effectiveness has been limited by chronic non-compliance: a 2017 European Court of Auditors report found that Greece enrolled fingerprints for only 30% of irregular arrivals in 2015, and Italy missed 40% of cases in 2016. The consequence was that the Dublin system continued to fail — only 15% of pending transfers were actually executed in 2016, according to the Commission's 2017 report on the Dublin system.

The system faced significant privacy challenges. In 2014, the European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) Giovanni Buttarelli criticized the law enforcement access provisions, warning they shifted Eurodac from an asylum support tool to a "migration policing" instrument. The 2018 *Paposhvili v Belgium* ECJ ruling further constrained Dublin transfers by requiring individualized assessments of risks.

Industry impact was indirect but substantial — the system drove a €200 million market for biometric border technologies by 2020, according to a 2019 study by the European Parliament's research service, with companies like Idemia, NEC, and Thales dominating procurement. Smaller Member States struggled with implementation costs — a 2020 Commission impact assessment estimated that upgrading national systems to comply with proposed reforms would cost each member state between €2 million and €10 million.

## Key questions answered

### Why do asylum seekers' fingerprints go into a pan-European database under the Eurodac Regulation?

Because the Dublin Regulation requires determining which Member State is responsible for an application, and authorities cannot trace previous claims without a shared verification system. Eurodac is the technical enforcer of Dublin.

### Why was law enforcement access added in the 2013 Eurodac Regulation?

The 2012 Commission evaluation showed that terrorism suspects were applying for asylum to avoid surveillance; Commissioner Malmström argued police needed access to prevent "asylum shopping" by individuals posing security threats. The Parliament agreed only after limiting access to serious crimes and requiring judicial approval.

### Why doesn't the Eurodac Regulation stop secondary movements within the EU?

Because fingerprint enrollment at borders is inconsistent — the Commission found in 2017 that only 60% of irregular arrivals were fingerprinted, rendering the database only partially useful. Without universal enrollment, Eurodac cannot identify all secondary movers.

### Why is the age threshold for fingerprinting 14 in the Eurodac Regulation?

The age of 14 was chosen in 2000 to balance child protection against operational needs — the original regulation cited Council of Europe guidelines on juvenile justice. Critics argue this leaves many minors unenrolled; the 2020 reform proposal seeks to lower it to 6.
