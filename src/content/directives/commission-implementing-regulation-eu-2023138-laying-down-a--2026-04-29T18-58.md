## What is it?

The European Open Data Implementing Regulation (2023/138/EU) mandates that six categories of public-sector information—geospatial, Earth observation and environment, meteorological, statistics, company and company ownership, and mobility—be published as "high-value datasets" (HVDs) for free reuse across the EU. These datasets must be available in machine-readable formats via application programming interfaces (APIs) and, where feasible, as bulk downloads, with metadata compliant to a common standard (DCAT-AP). The regulation applies to all EU member states and their public-sector bodies, with partial exemptions for public undertakings and research institutions.

The regulation derives its legal force from Article 14(1) of the Open Data Directive (EU) 2019/1024, which required the European Commission to define a list of thematic datasets "of high socioeconomic value" that must be made available free of charge, beyond the standard cost-recovery rules for other public-sector information. It entered into force on January 23, 2023, with most provisions applying from June 9, 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation emerged from a structural failure: the 2003 Public Sector Information (PSI) Directive had allowed member states to charge for data reuse or restrict access, creating a fragmented landscape where valuable public data—satellite imagery, weather forecasts, cadastral maps—was either priced beyond commercial reach or unavailable in usable formats. By 2018, the European Commission's own impact assessment (SWD(2018) 145) estimated that the EU's data economy was worth €300 billion, but public-sector data reuse was stymied by legal uncertainty and inconsistent licensing. Companies like HERE Technologies (geospatial mapping), Copernicus data brokers, and precision agriculture firms complained publicly that they had to negotiate separate access terms in each of 27 member states, with some countries charging thousands of euros for national weather datasets.

The political breakthrough came during the Juncker Commission's Digital Single Market strategy. Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society Mariya Gabriel (2014–2019) pushed for an updated directive, arguing in a 2017 blog post that "open data is the raw material of the data economy." But the real catalyst was the Cambridge Analytica scandal in March 2018, which exposed how proprietary Facebook data could be weaponized for political manipulation—and contrasted starkly with the EU's underused public-sector data wealth. The scandal broke weeks before the Commission's legislative proposal for the revised Open Data Directive (April 25, 2018), giving Gabriel and her successor Thierry Breton leverage to argue that publicly funded data must be a common good.

Industry coalition dynamics split along predictable lines. European open-data advocates like Open Knowledge Foundation, the European Environmental Bureau, and Transparency International Europe formed a coalition—the "Open Data for All" campaign—calling for mandatory free access. On the other side, public-sector bodies in Germany, France, and the Netherlands warned of revenue loss: the Dutch Kadaster (land registry) earned €50 million annually from geospatial data sales, while Germany's weather service (DWD) charged €1.2 million per year for high-resolution data. The European Cities Open Data Network, representing 50+ municipalities, argued that free data would create more economic value through downstream innovation than direct sales.

The Parliament's Industry, Research and Energy Committee (ITRE) adopted its position in February 2019, with Rapporteur Neoklis Sylikiotis (GUE/NGL, Cyprus) proposing a mandatory HVD list—a controversial departure from the Commission's more flexible approach. The Council of the EU pushed back, with Germany's Bundesrat warning in November 2018 that "mandatory free-of-charge access to high-value datasets could lead to a loss of revenues for public bodies." The compromise, reached in December 2018 trilogues, was Article 14: the Commission would define the list via delegated acts, giving member states implementation flexibility but requiring free access.

High-value datasets themselves appeared earlier in EU law: the INSPIRE Directive (2007/2/EC) had already defined spatial data themes, and Regulation (EU) 377/2014 on the Copernicus programme mandated free access to Earth observation data. But these remained sectoral exceptions. The 2023 regulation universalized the principle.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation creates a mandatory minimum standard: all six thematic categories must be available free of charge, in machine-readable formats, with APIs supporting real-time access where technically feasible. For example, geospatial data from national mapping agencies (e.g., France's IGN, Germany's Landesvermessungsämter) that previously cost up to €5,000 per dataset must now be free. The European Commission's 2022 impact assessment estimated the economic impact at €1.5–3.0 billion annually through new services in logistics, insurance, agriculture, and oil-finding, versus a compliance cost of roughly €250 million.

Implementation challenges are significant. Several member states have missed the June 2024 deadline: as of September 2024, the European Data Portal's monitoring dashboard showed that only 19 of 27 countries had operational APIs for all six categories. Spain, Greece, and Romania have faced infringement risks, with the Commission opening dialogues. The German Federal Ministry of the Interior warned in a 2023 notice that "some federal states lack the technical infrastructure for bulk downloads."

Industry response has been mixed. Geospatial companies like Esri Germany and TomTom praised the move, while weather-data brokers like MeteoGroup (UK) warned that free public data could undermine their proprietary forecasting services. Startups and SMEs—particularly in precision agriculture (e.g., Viper Agri) and urban mobility (e.g., Citymapper)—stand to benefit most, as they gain equal access to data previously reserved for well-funded incumbents.

The regulation has also spurred ancillary innovation: the European Commission launched a "High-Value Dataset Navigator" tool in 2024 to help entrepreneurs find and combine datasets, and the European Data Portal has integrated APIs from 22 countries. The UK, post-Brexit, has not been affected, but Northern Ireland's public bodies must comply under the Protocol on Ireland/Northern Ireland.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU rules force governments to give away public data for free?

Because the 2019 Open Data Directive recognised that charging for public-sector data—like weather forecasts, maps, or company registers—creates a barrier to small businesses and startups that cannot afford thousands of euros per dataset. The European Commission's 2018 impact assessment estimated that free access could unlock up to €3 billion in economic value annually through new apps, logistics tools, and environmental services. The rule is mandatory because some countries (Germany, Netherlands) had lucrative data sales that they were reluctant to abandon, so the law sets a baseline: the six highest-value categories must be free; other data can still be sold at cost.

### Does this regulation apply to private companies like Google Maps or Meta?

No. The regulation applies only to "public sector bodies"—government agencies, municipalities, state-owned meteorological and mapping services, and agencies like the European Environment Agency. Private companies are not required to share their data unless they are public undertakings (e.g., state-owned railway data). This was a deliberate choice under the Open Data Directive to avoid imposing burdens on private-sector innovation.

### What makes a dataset "high-value" enough to be covered?

The regulation defines six categories based on three mandatory criteria from the 2019 Directive: (1) ability to generate significant socioeconomic or environmental benefits via new products and services; (2) high potential for reuse combined with economies of scale; and (3) ability to benefit from interoperability across member states. The Commission's 2021 consultation identified geospatial data (land registries, transport networks) and company registers (ownership structures) as top candidates—both critical for anti-money laundering and logistics. The final list excludes health data and social statistics, which were deemed too sensitive or complex to standardize immediately.

### Will citizens see any direct benefit from this regulation?

Yes, within months. For example, real-time traffic and mobility data (category 6) must become freely available, enabling apps like Google Maps or Citymapper to show public transport delays and bike availability across all EU cities without paying for data feeds. Environmental monitoring (category 2) is crucial for air quality apps, while statistical data (category 4) will fuel open-data platforms like Eurostat's dashboard. The European Data Portal already hosts 1.2 million datasets from member states, but the regulation forces consistent API access—meaning a developer in Warsaw can query Lisbon's land registry or Vienna's weather data with the same code.
