## What is it?

The Instant Payments Regulation, formally part of the broader 2024 payment package amending the SEPA Regulation, mandates that banks and other payment service providers in the euro area must offer instant credit transfers — payments that arrive within ten seconds, 24/7/365 — at no extra cost compared to standard transfers. For non-euro EU countries, the obligation applies with a later deadline.

The regulation overhauls the previous voluntary framework for instant payments, which had been in place since 2017 but achieved limited adoption. It also introduces mandatory user verification to reduce fraud risks associated with real-time transactions, including a requirement for providers to check that the payee's IBAN matches the account name.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the European Commission's 2022 retail payments strategy, which found that despite the technical infrastructure existing since 2017 under the SEPA Instant Credit Transfer (SCT Inst) scheme, only around 11% of all credit transfers in the EU were instant by late 2022, according to Commission data. The Netherlands and the Baltic states had over 90% adoption, while Germany and France — the bloc's two largest economies — lagged below 5%. This structural tension — a functioning standard that few used — created a clear failure of the voluntary approach.

The coalition driving the regulation was led by the European Payments Council, a body of 75 banks and payment associations, which had long advocated for making SCT Inst mandatory to achieve network effects. The European Central Bank (ECB) was equally vocal: ECB Executive Board member Fabio Panetta warned in a 2022 speech that the slow uptake "undermines the digitalisation of the European economy" and called for legislation. On the other side, German and French banking associations — the Bundesverband deutscher Banken and the Fédération Bancaire Française — opposed mandatory requirements, arguing that upgrading core banking systems for 24/7 processing would cost small banks between €1-5 million each, according to their 2022 impact estimates.

The parliamentary debate saw a cross-party coalition of the European People's Party, Socialists & Democrats, and Renew Europe push for the regulation, with Finnish MEP Ville Niinistö (Greens/EFA) serving as rapporteur for the file. The final text passed the European Parliament in February 2024 with 599 votes in favour, 49 against, and 57 abstentions — a comfortable majority that reflected broad political agreement on the need to end the voluntary deadlock.

The timing was driven by two external shocks. First, the COVID-19 pandemic had massively accelerated digital payments — according to the ECB, the share of contactless card payments in shops rose from 41% in 2019 to 61% by 2021 — yet instant payments remained stuck. Second, Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine highlighted the geopolitical vulnerability of the EU's reliance on the US-dominated Visa and Mastercard networks, which could be cut off; a homegrown instant payment infrastructure became a resilience priority. Internal Commission documents cited the 2022 blocking of SWIFT payments to Russian banks as a demonstration of how payment infrastructure is strategic.

Previous attempts to boost instant payments had failed. A 2019 voluntary commitment by 25 European banks to reach 30% market share by 2022 had fallen short, with actual adoption hovering around 10%. The EBA Clearing Company's RT1 system, launched in 2017, processed only 2.5 billion transactions by end-2022 — a fraction of total SEPA volumes. The breaking point came in December 2022, when the Commission's impact assessment concluded that leaving adoption to market forces would take until 2030 to reach 50% of euro-area transfers — an unacceptable timeline for a digital single market.

## Timeline

- **2017** — SEPA Instant Credit Transfer (SCT Inst) scheme launched; adoption voluntary.
- **2019** — European Payments Council pledges voluntary 30% market share by 2022; pledge fails.
- **2022** — ECB's Fabio Panetta calls for mandatory rules; Commission publishes retail payments strategy showing 11% adoption.
- **October 2023** — Commission proposes the Instant Payments Regulation.
- **February 2024** — European Parliament adopts regulation with 599 votes in favour.
- **March 2024** — Council of the EU formally adopts the regulation.
- **April 2024** — Regulation published in the Official Journal.
- **2025** — First compliance deadline for euro area: providers must offer instant credit transfers at same cost as standard transfers.
- **2027** — Full compliance deadline for non-euro EU countries.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation immediately shifts the business model for European banking. According to the Commission's 2023 impact assessment, the market for instant payments in the EU was estimated at €200 billion in transaction value by 2025, and mandatory adoption could push that to €1 trillion by 2030. Banks face estimated one-time compliance costs of €50,000 to €5 million depending on size, with the German banking industry warning that up to 200 small German savings banks might struggle to meet the 2025 deadline.

For consumers and businesses, the regulation eliminates premium pricing — previously, many banks charged €0.20-€1.00 per instant transfer compared to free standard transfers, according to ECB data. The fraud mitigation measures, particularly the IBAN-name matching requirement, are expected to reduce authorised push payment (APP) fraud, which the European Banking Authority estimated at €1.3 billion in losses in 2021 across the EU.

Non-compliance carries significant penalties: national competent authorities can impose fines of up to 10% of annual turnover for payment service providers that fail to offer instant transfers or charge discriminatory prices. The first enforcement actions are expected from national central banks and financial supervisors in 2026, after the initial compliance deadline.

A notable consequence is the impact on payment innovation: the regulation forces all banks to offer instant payments, creating a level playing field for fintech startups that previously had to rely on the voluntary scheme. Companies like Revolut and Wise publicly supported the regulation, as it removes the advantage of larger banks that could afford premium infrastructure. However, some smaller banks warned they may exit the credit transfer market entirely rather than comply.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU force banks to offer instant payments instead of letting the market decide?

Because the voluntary approach had demonstrably failed. By 2022, only 11% of credit transfers were instant, despite the SCT Inst scheme being technically available since 2017. The Commission's impact assessment found that without regulation, it would take until 2030 to reach 50% adoption — too slow for a digital single market where other regions like India (Upi) and Brazil (Pix) were already processing billions of instant payments.

### Why do the new EU rules for instant bank transfers also include measures to prevent fraud?

Because instant payments — irreversible within seconds — create new fraud opportunities. The European Banking Authority recorded €1.3 billion in APP fraud losses in 2021, and the UK's Payment Systems Regulator found that instant payment fraud rose 30% year-on-year in 2022. The IBAN-name matching requirement, originally proposed by the European Banking Authority in its 2022 guidelines, aims to ensure that the fastest payment method is also secure.

### Why did Germany and France oppose the EU's push for mandatory instant payments?

German and French banking associations argued that mandatory instant payments would impose disproportionate costs on small banks, particularly German Sparkassen (savings banks) and French Caisses d'Épargne. The Bundesverband deutscher Banken estimated total compliance costs for the German banking sector at €1-2 billion, and questioned whether small institutions had the technical capacity to process payments 24/7/365. However, a compromise allowed compliance deadlines to be extended by six months for micro-enterprises.

### How do the new EU rules for instant payments connect to the planned digital euro?

The regulation is a prerequisite for the ECB's digital euro project. The digital euro, if introduced, would be an instant payment settlement system — meaning all banks must first be capable of instant processing. The Commission's 2023 legislative proposal for a digital euro explicitly links to the Instant Payments Regulation, stating that "the legal framework for instant payments is a necessary condition for the technical feasibility of a digital euro."
