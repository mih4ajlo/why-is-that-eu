## What is it?

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) is a regulation that establishes a uniform legal framework for the development, marketing, and use of AI systems across the European Union. It applies a tiered, risk-based approach: some AI practices are prohibited entirely (such as social scoring by governments, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces with narrow exceptions, and emotion recognition in workplaces or schools); "high-risk" AI used in areas like hiring, credit scoring, education, medical devices, or law enforcement faces strict obligations on data quality, documentation, human oversight, and conformity assessment; "limited-risk" AI such as chatbots must meet transparency duties; and "minimal-risk" AI is largely unregulated.

The Act also introduces a separate regime for "general-purpose AI models" (GPAI) — the foundation models behind tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude — with additional obligations for models deemed to pose "systemic risk" (defined initially by a compute threshold of 10²⁵ floating-point operations used for training).

Like the GDPR, the AI Act has extraterritorial reach: it applies to any provider placing an AI system on the EU market or whose system's output is used in the EU, regardless of where the company is based. Fines can reach €35 million or 7% of global turnover for prohibited practices.

## Why was it introduced?

The AI Act has roots stretching back to the mid-2010s, when EU policymakers grew increasingly concerned that European citizens were being subjected to algorithmic decision-making — in lending, recruitment, policing, and welfare — without meaningful safeguards. The 2018 Cambridge Analytica scandal, the spread of deepfakes, and high-profile failures of facial recognition systems (notably ones with documented racial bias) sharpened public anxiety. At the same time, China's rollout of social credit systems and large-scale biometric surveillance crystallised fears about what an unregulated AI future could look like.

Politically, the project fit a broader European strategy. Margrethe Vestager, the Commission's powerful competition chief and digital lead from 2014, and Ursula von der Leyen, who took over the Commission in 2019 promising "a Europe fit for the digital age," wanted Europe to assert "digital sovereignty." Having effectively missed the consumer internet platform wave to the US, the EU's response was to lead in *regulation* — a strategy commentators dubbed the "Brussels Effect." The GDPR (2018) had shown that EU rules could become global de facto standards; the AI Act was conceived as the next pillar, alongside the Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act.

The Commission published a White Paper on AI in February 2020 and a formal proposal in April 2021. The original draft focused on a relatively stable set of high-risk use cases. Then ChatGPT launched in November 2022, triggering a global panic about generative AI. Negotiations, which had been quietly progressing, suddenly had to grapple with foundation models that didn't fit the original "intended purpose" framework. France, Germany, and Italy — home to Mistral, Aleph Alpha, and AI champions they hoped to nurture — pushed back hard against heavy regulation of GPAI, fearing it would kneecap European competitors before they could scale. Civil society groups, the European Parliament (led by co-rapporteurs Brando Benifei and Dragoş Tudorache), and several member states pushed in the opposite direction, demanding bans on biometric mass surveillance and tough rules for the largest models.

The final compromise, hammered out in a marathon 38-hour trilogue in December 2023, layered a separate GPAI regime on top of the original risk-based architecture. Law enforcement secured carve-outs for biometric identification under judicial authorisation; industry secured a tiered approach where only the most powerful "systemic risk" models face the heaviest obligations.

## Timeline

- **2018** — EU High-Level Expert Group on AI is established; GDPR enters into force, setting the template.
- **2019** — Expert Group publishes "Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI."
- **February 2020** — Commission publishes White Paper on Artificial Intelligence.
- **April 2021** — Commission proposes the AI Act.
- **November 2022** — ChatGPT launches, upending the negotiations and forcing the inclusion of general-purpose AI rules.
- **June 2023** — European Parliament adopts its negotiating position, including bans on real-time biometric identification.
- **December 2023** — Political agreement reached after a 38-hour trilogue.
- **March 2024** — European Parliament formally adopts the Act.
- **May 2024** — Council gives final approval.
- **1 August 2024** — Regulation enters into force.
- **2 February 2025** — Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI become applicable.
- **2 August 2025** — GPAI obligations and governance rules apply.
- **2 August 2026** — Most remaining provisions, including high-risk system rules, become applicable.
- **2 August 2027** — Rules for high-risk AI embedded in regulated products (e.g. medical devices, machinery) apply.

## Impact and consequences

The Act has reshaped corporate AI strategy worldwide. Major US developers — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta — have committed to compliance roadmaps and signed (or declined to sign) the Commission's GPAI Code of Practice. Meta refused to sign the Code in July 2025, citing legal uncertainty; Apple delayed the EU launch of Apple Intelligence; Meta withheld its multimodal Llama models from the EU, citing regulatory ambiguity. Some commentators see this as the "Brussels Effect" working; others see it as evidence that Europe is being cut off from frontier AI.

Within the EU, a new European AI Office was established inside DG CNECT to supervise GPAI providers, supported by a scientific panel of independent experts. National competent authorities handle high-risk system supervision, layered on top of existing sectoral regulators (medical devices, financial services, etc.).

Industry pushback has been intense. In summer 2025 dozens of major European companies (including Airbus, ASML, BNP Paribas, Mistral, and SAP) signed open letters asking the Commission to "stop the clock" on implementation, arguing that the standards and guidance were not yet ready. The Commission responded with a "Digital Omnibus" simplification package in late 2025 but held the line on the core deadlines. Critics from civil society, meanwhile, argue that the law's many exceptions — particularly for migration and law enforcement — leave significant gaps.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU pass AI rules before the US or China did?

Three reasons converged: a strategic doctrine of "digital sovereignty" that treats regulation as Europe's competitive lever; a strong fundamental rights tradition (Article 8 of the Charter, the GDPR legacy) that views algorithmic decisions about people as inherently rights-affecting; and the absence of dominant European AI firms whose lobbying might have slowed the process the way US tech lobbying has slowed federal AI legislation in Washington.

### Why does the EU regulate AI based on how risky it is, instead of having one rule for all AI?

A horizontal ban or a single set of rules for all AI would have been politically impossible and economically destructive — AI is too broad a category. Risk-tiering let negotiators concentrate restrictions on uses that affect safety or fundamental rights (recruitment, policing, credit) while leaving spam filters and video game NPCs alone. It also mirrors the EU's existing "New Legislative Framework" for product safety, making the law fit alongside CE-marking regimes for toys, medical devices, and machinery.

### Why were rules for general-purpose AI (like ChatGPT) added so late to Europe's AI law?

The original 2021 proposal was written before foundation models dominated the field. ChatGPT's release in late 2022 made it obvious that regulating AI by "intended purpose" failed for systems with no fixed purpose. Adding a separate GPAI tier was the price of keeping the law relevant — but it also opened a fight between France/Germany (protecting Mistral and Aleph Alpha) and the Parliament/civil society, resolved through a two-tier system where only the largest models face systemic-risk obligations.

### Why does Europe's AI law set the "systemic risk" threshold at a specific computing power level (10²⁵ FLOPs)?

It is a proxy chosen because compute is currently the most measurable correlate of capability. The threshold roughly captures GPT-4-class models and above. The Commission can revise it via delegated act as the field evolves — a deliberate flexibility designed to avoid the law freezing in place against fast-moving technology.
