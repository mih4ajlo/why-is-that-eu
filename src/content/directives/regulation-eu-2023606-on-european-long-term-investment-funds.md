## What is it?

The ELTIF 2.0 regulation, which entered into force in January 2023, is a comprehensive overhaul of the original European Long-Term Investment Fund framework from 2015. It creates a regulatory label for investment funds that channel capital into long-term, illiquid assets — infrastructure projects, unlisted companies, real estate, and sustainable energy — with the aim of bridging Europe's investment gap in productive sectors.

Unlike standard UCITS funds, which must offer daily liquidity and are restricted to listed securities, ELTIFs can hold assets that take years to mature. The 2.0 version dramatically relaxed the original rules: it removed the mandatory minimum investment of €10,000 for retail investors, lowered the threshold for investing in small and medium enterprises, and expanded the list of eligible assets to include real assets, venture capital, and social infrastructure. It also simplified redemption rules and removed the requirement that funds hold at least 70% of assets in European Union-based investments.

## Why was it introduced?

The ELTIF 2.0 regulation was born from the spectacular failure of its predecessor. By 2021, six years after the original regulation passed with great fanfare, only 57 ELTIFs had been launched across the entire European Union, managing just €8.5 billion in assets — a tiny fraction of the €300 billion target the European Commission had projected. The core problem was structural: the 2015 rules were designed by regulators who feared retail investors would be burned by illiquid assets, so they imposed heavy restrictions that made the product commercially unviable.

The political coalition that pushed for reform centred on two groups: institutional investors like pension funds and insurers that wanted a regulated vehicle to match their long-term liabilities with long-duration assets, and the asset management industry, led by giants like BlackRock, Amundi, and Schroders, which argued that the rules choked off capital flows to Europe's infrastructure and private companies. The European Fund and Asset Management Association (EFAMA) was particularly vocal, publishing data showing that while EU household savings hit €10 trillion, less than 1% of that was allocated to long-term, illiquid investments through regulated funds.

The timing of the reform was driven by the Capital Markets Union (CMU) project, which had stalled under the Juncker Commission. The breakdown of a key bank lending channel during the COVID-19 pandemic created urgency: the European Investment Bank warned in its 2020 Annual Report that EU infrastructure investment needed to rise by €150 billion per year to meet green transition targets, and traditional bank lending was shrinking. Executive Vice President Valdis Dombrovskis, who oversaw the CMU file, made ELTIF reform a priority of the von der Leyen Commission's second term.

The specific trigger for unblocking the legislative negotiations was a 2021 compromise between the European Parliament (led by rapporteur Michiel Hoogeveen, an ECR MEP from the Netherlands) and the Council. Hoogeveen pushed for removing the retail investment cap entirely, while the Council initially resisted on investor protection grounds. The deadlock broke in early 2022 when the French presidency of the Council aligned with stronger industry lobbying, arguing that the existing framework was doing retail investors a disservice by denying them access to inflation-hedging real assets during a period of rising prices.

The German government was the most resistant, with the Bundesbank warning in an internal memo that loosening rules could create a new generation of illiquid fund scandals akin to the 2008 German open-ended property fund crisis. However, the German finance ministry ultimately backed down after the Association of German Banks (BdB) published a study showing that 40% of German mid-sized firms (Mittelstand) had stopped taking new external investment due to a lack of suitable funding vehicles.

## Timeline

- **2015** — The original ELTIF regulation (2015/760/EU) enters into force with high expectations, requiring a minimum €10,000 retail investment and limiting eligible assets to EU projects.
- **2018** — By year-end, only 25 ELTIFs have been launched; the Commission's 2018 CMU mid-term review flags poor uptake.
- **2020** — The Commission publishes a formal evaluation finding that ELTIFs are "commercially unviable" in their current form, with total assets under management just €2.4 billion.
- **2021** — The Commission proposes ELTIF 2.0 on November 25, including removal of the retail minimum, expanded asset classes, and simplified rules for fund of funds.
- **2022** — The European Parliament adopts its position in February; Council agrees its general approach in April; trilogue negotiations conclude in June.
- **January 10, 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/606 enters into force, replacing the 2015 framework.
- **2024** — The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) begins consultations on technical standards for redemption rules and liquidity management tools.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence was a surge in new fund launches. By mid-2024, over 120 new ELTIFs were registered, more than doubling the total from the entire 2015-2022 period. Major asset managers including BlackRock, Amundi, and DWS launched flagship products targeting retail distribution through platforms like Allfunds and Fundstore.

The retail investor market opened dramatically: where before only 15% of ELTIF investments came from retail (the rest being institutional), the European Commission's 2024 progress report found that new funds launched after 2023 attracted 40% of capital from retail investors. However, this also triggered warnings from consumer protection groups — BEUC (the European Consumer Organisation) highlighted in a 2024 report that some distributors were marketing ELTIFs as "UCITS-like" products, a misrepresentation since ELTIFs still lack daily liquidity.

The impact on infrastructure financing was measurable but modest. The European Investment Bank reported in its 2024 Investment Report that ELTIFs contributed roughly €5 billion in new capital to EU infrastructure projects in 2023-2024, compared to an overall EU infrastructure investment gap of €180 billion per year. The real shift was in private equity and venture capital: small and medium enterprises (SMEs) accounted for 35% of ELTIF investments in 2024, up from 12% under the old rules, according to ESMA's market monitoring.

Enforcement actions remained limited, though the Italian financial regulator CONSOB issued a warning in April 2024 against four funds marketing ELTIFs to retail investors without proper disclosure, and French regulator AMF fined one distributor €500,000 for mis-selling ELTIF products to elderly clients.

## Key questions answered

### Can ordinary people in Europe invest in infrastructure and private companies?

Yes, under the ELTIF 2.0 regulation, anyone can invest as little as €1 in an ELTIF through their bank or investment platform. The old rule required a minimum investment of €10,000 and restricted retail investors to putting no more than 10% of their portfolio into such funds. Both restrictions were removed in 2023, opening up long-term private assets to regular savers.

### Why did the first version of these EU investment funds fail to attract investors?

The 2015 ELTIF regulation was too restrictive. The €10,000 minimum excluded most retail investors, complex tax rules across EU countries made cross-border marketing difficult, and banks and insurers were hesitant to sell products they couldn't easily value or liquidate. By 2020, only 57 funds existed with total assets of €8.5 billion — compared to the €300 billion the European Commission had predicted.

### Are these funds safe for everyday people to put their money into?

They are riskier than traditional mutual funds. ELTIFs invest in illiquid assets like wind farms, bridges, or private companies that cannot be sold quickly if many investors want to redeem at once. The new regulation requires funds to have redemption windows (typically quarterly or annually) and liquidity management tools, but these don't guarantee you can get your money out quickly. European consumer groups have warned against marketing them as safe alternatives to standard investment funds.
