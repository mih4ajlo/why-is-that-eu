## What is it?

The Rail Passenger Rights Regulation (Regulation 1371/2007/EC, revised by Regulation 2021/782/EU as of 2023) sets a legally binding floor for what rail passengers can expect when traveling within the EU. It covers all commercial passenger services, both domestic and international, though Member States may temporarily exempt domestic services. Core provisions include: automatic compensation for delays of 60–119 minutes (25% of ticket price) and delays of 120+ minutes (50%);

The regulation requires rail operators to offer a choice of either re-routing or reimbursement and compensation when delays exceed 60 minutes. It mandates that persons with disabilities or reduced mobility receive free assistance at stations and on trains, and sets liability rules for death, injury, or lost luggage — mirroring aviation rules in key respects. Enforcement is national: each Member State designates a body to handle complaints and fine non-compliant operators.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation’s origin lies in a structural tension: by the early 2000s, EU rail liberalisation had created competition — new entrants like NTV’s Italo in Italy, and France’s Ouigo — but passenger protections remained fragmented among national laws and voluntary industry codes. The European Commission’s 2004 White Paper on transport noted that passengers faced inconsistent compensation across borders; a London-Paris Eurostar delay might trigger a £150 payout, while a Berlin-Warsaw delay by PKP or DB might yield nothing.

The political coalition that passed it was a classic EU social-market alliance. Transport Commissioner Jacques Barrot (France, 2004–2008) championed it, framing rail rights as the land-based counterpart to the 2004 Air Passenger Rights Regulation. The European Parliament’s Transport Committee, led by rapporteur Paolo Costa (Italy, ALDE), pushed for stronger provisions — notably compulsory compensation for delays of just 30 minutes, and mandatory liability limits for lost luggage. The Council, representing transport ministries, resisted, with Germany and Austria arguing that rail was less monopolistic than aviation and that staggered compensation would be impractical.

The deadlock broke in late 2006 following a series of high-profile incidents. In July 2006, a Thalys train from Paris to Brussels was stranded for 12 hours due to a power failure, with no compensation offered under the then-voluntary industry code. The Belgian consumer organisation Test-Achats filed a complaint, and the Commission cited this case in its impact assessment. The UK’s Office of Rail Regulation, meanwhile, reported that in 2005 only 12% of delayed passengers on domestic services claimed compensation — a figure Barrot used to argue that mandatory, automatic compensation was needed.

A second factor was the political momentum from the 2006 European Year of Workers’ Mobility, which spotlighted cross-border commuting. The Commission’s 2006 proposal explicitly linked rail rights to labour mobility: a worker commuting from Lille to Brussels could not afford to lose a day’s wage due to a delay with no recourse.

The final regulation passed in October 2007 with 529 votes in favour to 47 against in the European Parliament. The biggest opposition came from UK Conservative MEPs, who argued that automatic compensation would raise ticket prices. The regulation entered force in December 2009, with a two-year transition for domestic services. Italy, France, and Germany used the domestic-exemption clause heavily — France waited until 2023 to fully apply it to SNCF’s TER and Intercité services, citing cost.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Commission White Paper on transport identifies gaps in rail passenger rights, proposes a legislative framework
- **July 2006** — Thalys power failure leaves 700 passengers stranded for 12 hours, triggered national complaints and media coverage
- **December 2006** — Commission publishes impact assessment, citing Test-Achats complaint and UK OPR data on low claim rates
- **December 2006** — Transport Commissioner Barrot formally proposes Regulation 1371/2007
- **October 2007** — European Parliament adopts regulation (529 votes for, 47 against)
- **December 2009** — Regulation enters into force for international services
- **December 2011** — Domestic services must comply (with opt-out allowed for 5-year periods)
- **2013** — Commission publishes first implementation report; finds widespread non-compliance and domestic exemptions
- **2017** — Commission proposes revision to close loopholes and strengthen enforcement
- **November 2021** — Revised Regulation (2021/782/EU) adopted, removing most domestic opt-outs
- **June 2023** — Revised regulation applies fully across all EU Member States

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed the rail industry’s approach to passenger care. Eurostar, Thalys, and international operators quickly adopted automatic compensation — Eurostar paid out €4.7 million in 2012 alone for delays. DB’s rail+air service, the ICE-Airport connection, had to redesign its booking system to comply. By contrast, domestic services in France, Germany, and Italy remained partially exempt until the 2021 revision closed the loophole.

The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) reported in 2018 that compliance varied wildly: in Sweden, compensation was paid on 95% of eligible domestic claims, but in Poland it was under 20%. The 2023 revision eliminated the domestic exemption entirely for all new services; however, existing domestic services with long-term contracts (e.g., regional French TER lines) had until 2025.

The regulation’s accessibility provisions had a significant effect: by 2015, 78% of EU mainline stations had accessibility ramps or lifts, up from 45% in 2008, according to the European Disability Forum. Rail operators invested a combined €1.2 billion in accessible rolling stock between 2010 and 2020, per the European Railway Agency.

Enforcement, however, remains patchy. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against seven Member States between 2012 and 2018 for failing to designate enforcement bodies. In 2020, Italy’s enforcement body fined Trenitalia €4.3 million for failing to compensate passengers on delayed regional trains — a rare high-profile enforcement action.

## Key questions answered

### Why did domestic exemptions last so long?

Germany and France argued that domestic rail networks — where delays are more frequent than on high-speed services — would face prohibitive costs. Deutsche Bahn claimed in 2011 that full compliance would cost €200 million annually. The Commission’s 2013 report found these claims overblown, but the domestic exemption became a political trade-off: Germany demanded it in exchange for supporting aviation rights. The 2021 revision finally removed it because intra-EU rail travel had grown to 87% domestic, making the fragmented system unsustainable for single-market passenger mobility.

### Why does aviation have stronger passenger rights than rail?

The 2004 Air Passenger Rights Regulation (261/2004) lifted a higher threshold — aviation had already built a unified, pan-European compensation system, whereas rail was national and fragmented. The aviation lobby was also weaker: airlines operating across Europe had already adopted unified cancellation policies for commercial reasons. Rail operators, by contrast, are often state-owned monopolies (SNCF, DB, FS) that had no commercial incentive to offer compensation. The 2021 revision narrowed the gap by requiring rail to offer re-routing (via train or alternative mode) — a right aviation has had since 2004 — but rail still lacks aviation’s right to care (meals, hotels) for delays under 60 minutes.

### What changed in the 2021 revision?

The revised regulation (2021/782/EU) eliminated the domestic exemption for all services after a 2-year transition, meaning every EU train must now offer compensation for delays. It also introduced new provisions: mandatory through-ticketing for journeys involving transfers between different operators (a key demand of the European Passengers’ Federation); strengthened liability for lost or damaged mobility equipment (wheelchairs, walkers) with a minimum compensation of €1,300; and a requirement for real-time delay information in accessible formats. The Parliament’s rapporteur, Karima Delli (France, Greens), secured the through-ticketing provision — a major win that had been blocked for years by state-owned operators.

### Who opposed the regulation most aggressively?

National rail operators were the principal opponents. In 2007, SNCF’s CEO Anne-Marie Idrac sent a letter to Commissioner Barrot warning that mandatory compensation would raise ticket prices by 5–8%. Deutsche Bahn’s CEO Hartmut Mehdorn argued similar lines. In the Council, Germany, Austria, and France formed a blocking minority that secured the domestic-exemption clause. The European Rail Infrastructure Managers (EIM) lobbied for — and obtained — a clause exempting delays caused by infrastructure failures (e.g., signal faults) from compensation, arguing that operators should not pay for network faults.
