## What is it?

The Low Voltage Directive (LVD) 2014/35/EU sets essential safety requirements for electrical equipment designed to operate with a voltage rating between 50 and 1000 volts for alternating current (AC) and between 75 and 1500 volts for direct current (DC). It covers virtually all consumer and industrial electrical products sold in the EU — from household appliances and lighting to wiring and power tools. Equipment outside these voltage ranges falls under other directives, such as the Radio Equipment Directive or the Medical Devices Regulation.

Products covered must meet the LVD's essential safety objectives, which include protecting against electric shock, mechanical hazards, fire, and electromagnetic interference. Manufacturers must perform a conformity assessment, usually self-declaration, and affix the CE marking before placing products on the market. The directive follows the "New Approach" principle: it lays down broad safety goals, while detailed technical specifications are provided by harmonised European standards (like EN 60335 for household appliances).

## Why was it introduced?

The story begins not with a single scandal but with a structural failure that nearly paralysed the European single market for electrical goods in the early 1970s. Before the original 1973 Low Voltage Directive, each Member State had its own national safety standards and mandatory certification schemes. A German toaster might require VDE approval, a French one NF certification, and a British one BS marking. Getting a product approved in all nine Member States of the then-European Economic Community could take two years and cost more than developing the product itself.

The breakthrough came from an unlikely coalition: the European Commission's internal market directorate under Commissioner for Industrial Affairs Altiero Spinelli (1970-1976) and the European Committee for Electrotechnical Standardisation (CENELEC), founded in 1973. CENELEC, a private industry body representing national electrotechnical committees, pushed for a system where harmonised European standards could replace the patchwork of national ones. The Commission saw this as a tool to dismantle technical barriers to trade, a core EEC objective.

The 1973 directive (73/23/EEC) was a radical innovation: it introduced the "low voltage presumption" — that equipment meeting harmonised standards is deemed compliant with safety requirements. This was the first time the New Approach to technical harmonisation was used, two decades before it became formal EU policy in the 1985 "New Approach to Technical Harmonisation and Standards" White Paper. The political bargain was clear: industry got a single market, and consumers got a baseline of safety without a centralised EU bureaucracy testing every product.

The 2014 recast was not prompted by any specific accident or crisis, but by the broader 2008 New Legislative Framework (NLF) overhaul. The NLF, championed by Commission President José Manuel Barroso and Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy, aimed to strengthen market surveillance and clarify obligations of economic operators. The LVD was updated to align with NLF definitions on manufacturer, importer, and distributor responsibilities, and to reference the decision on a common framework for the marketing of products (768/2008/EC). The recast also clarified scope exclusions, such as equipment for explosive atmospheres and medical devices, which are covered by more specific sectoral legislation.

## Timeline

- **1973** — Council adopts first Low Voltage Directive 73/23/EEC. It is the first New Approach directive, introducing the presumption of conformity with harmonised standards.
- **1985** — Commission publishes its "New Approach to Technical Harmonisation and Standards" White Paper, formally codifying the approach pioneered by the LVD.
- **2008** — New Legislative Framework adopted, including Decision 768/2008/EC on a common framework for marketing of products. Commission begins review of all sectoral product legislation.
- **2014** — Directive 2014/35/EU adopted by the European Parliament and Council, replacing 73/23/EEC. Main changes: alignment with NLF definitions, clearer obligations for importers and distributors, mandatory CE marking affixing rules.
- **2016** — Application date: 20 April 2016. National transposition deadlines for Member States (due by 19 April 2016).
- **2020** — European Commission publishes evaluation report noting high compliance rates but identifying weaknesses in market surveillance of low-cost imports, particularly from third countries.

## Impact and consequences

The LVD is one of the most successful pieces of EU product safety legislation. The 2014 evaluation found that it covers an estimated 80% of all electrical products sold in the EU market, representing hundreds of billions of euros in trade annually. The harmonised standards system reduced manufacturers' testing costs by an estimated 40-60% compared to the pre-1973 national certification regime, according to Commission impact assessments.

However, the self-declaration system has a known vulnerability: it relies on manufacturers' honesty and competent market surveillance. The 2020 Commission evaluation highlighted that online marketplaces selling low-cost electrical goods from outside the EU — particularly from sellers in China, Vietnam, and Turkey — frequently placed non-compliant products on the market. A 2019 RAPEX (EU rapid alert system) report showed that electrical products were the second most commonly notified dangerous product category after toys, accounting for 26% of all alerts.

Industry response has been broadly positive, with European associations like the European Committee of Electrical Installation Equipment Manufacturers and LightingEurope actively participating in the development of harmonised standards. However, small and medium-sized enterprises have occasionally raised concerns about the complexity of the standards ecosystem, which now comprises over 600 harmonised standards references.

## Key questions answered

### Why do electrical safety rules only cover devices running on 50 to 1000 volts of alternating current?

The voltage range was chosen as a practical compromise. Below 50V AC, equipment is considered intrinsically safe — even if a person touches both conductors, the voltage is low enough that no dangerous current flows through the body. Above 1000V AC, equipment requires specialised engineering and professional installation, and is covered by different legislation (such as the ATEX directive for explosive atmospheres or national regulations for high-voltage infrastructure). The 50V threshold is actually a standard recognised internationally by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) as the boundary between extra-low voltage and low voltage.

### Why are companies allowed to test and certify their own electrical products for safety instead of using an independent lab?

This was the political bargain that made the single market possible. Requiring every product to be tested by a notifed body would have created a costly bottleneck and kept small manufacturers from competing. The self-declaration system, combined with the "presumption of conformity" for products meeting harmonised standards, was designed to be fast, cheap, and trust-based. The risk is managed through post-market surveillance — Member States' authorities can test products at any time and remove non-compliant ones from the market. The 2014 recast strengthened market surveillance obligations precisely because the self-declaration system had been exploited by some unscrupulous importers.

### What are the consequences if an electrical product fails to meet basic safety requirements?

The consequences can be severe. If a Member State's market surveillance authority identifies a non-compliant product, it can demand the manufacturer withdraw it from the market, recall it from consumers, or impose fines. Persistent or dangerous non-compliance can trigger a "safeguard clause" procedure, where the Commission notifies all Member States, and the product is effectively banned from the entire EU market. The 2019 RAPEX statistics showed that China was the country of origin for over 60% of LVD-related notifications. Individual manufacturers can face reputational damage, loss of CE marking privileges, and liability claims under national product liability laws.
