## What is it?

The Vehicle General Safety Regulation (Regulation 2019/2144/EU), often abbreviated as the GSR, is a sweeping update to the EU's type-approval rules for motor vehicles. It replaced four earlier directives—including the original General Safety Regulation from 2009—and imposes a comprehensive set of mandatory safety features on all new cars, vans, trucks, and buses sold in the EU. The regulation came into force in 2022 for new vehicle types and will apply to all new vehicles from 2024 and 2029, depending on the specific technology.

The GSR covers two broad areas. First, it mandates a suite of advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS) that were previously optional or absent: intelligent speed assistance, emergency lane-keeping systems, driver drowsiness and distraction detection, event data recorders ("black boxes"), and automated emergency braking for detecting pedestrians and cyclists. Second, it establishes a formal regulatory framework for automated and fully driverless vehicles—the first of its kind in the world—allowing the EU to approve vehicles that can operate without a human driver, under certain conditions.

## Why was it introduced?

The GSR was a direct response to the failure of the existing regulatory system to keep pace with technological change, and to a specific political crisis: the European Parliament's refusal to accept the Commission's earlier, weaker approach to safety mandates, combined with a mounting death toll on EU roads that had stalled at around 25,000 fatalities per year for most of the 2010s.

The structural tension was clear. The 2009 General Safety Directive required only basic features like seatbelts and airbags, and left advanced safety technologies as optional—or in some cases, as cost-options that manufacturers rarely fitted. Meanwhile, the European Commission's own impact assessment, published in 2018, found that fitting mandatory intelligent speed assistance and emergency braking could reduce fatalities by up to 20,000 over a decade. Industry groups, led by the European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA), opposed mandatory mandates, arguing that the market should decide and that costs to consumers would rise by hundreds of euros per vehicle. The Commission's initial proposal in 2018, under Commissioner for Internal Market and Industry Elżbieta Bieńkowska, was itself a compromise—it mandated fewer systems than Parliament wanted, and set later deadlines than safety advocates demanded.

The coalition that broke this deadlock was unusual. The European Transport Safety Council (ETSC), consumer groups like BEUC, and the Greens in the European Parliament formed a unified front, demanding a more ambitious mandate. The Parliament's rapporteur, German MEP Róża Thun (EPP), drove the negotiations with a specific focus on intelligent speed assistance—a technology that had been fiercely opposed by some car clubs and drivers' associations. The key moment came in the spring of 2019, when the Parliament voted 432 to 203 to add lane-keeping systems and advanced emergency braking to the mandatory list, overriding the Commission's proposed timeline. The Council of member states, initially reluctant, was swayed by data from EuroNCAP showing that these systems already cut crash rates by 30-50% in voluntary fits, and by a campaign from the Dutch and Swedish governments, both of whom had been pushing for mandatory ADAS since 2016.

The timing was driven by two factors. First, the European Parliament elections were scheduled for May 2019, giving the outgoing Parliament a short window to finalize the law before the political composition shifted. Second, a series of high-profile crashes involving automated features—notably a 2018 fatal accident in the US involving a Tesla on Autopilot—had raised public awareness and created pressure for clear rules. The Commission had initially planned to regulate automated driving through a separate "automated vehicle" directive, but the Parliament demanded it be folded into the GSR, arguing that safety and automation were inseparable.

The alternative considered by the Commission was a voluntary system, where manufacturers could opt in to safety features and market them as selling points. This approach was rejected when the impact assessment showed that only 10% of vehicles sold in the EU in 2016 had intelligent speed assistance as standard, and that voluntary deployment had stalled since 2012.

## Timeline

- **2014** — European Parliament passes non-binding resolution calling for mandatory autonomous emergency braking and lane-keeping systems; Commission does not act.
- **2017** — EuroNCAP begins including ADAS features in its safety ratings; vehicles without them face lower scores.
- **May 2018** — Commission publishes proposal for a new General Safety Regulation, mandating fewer systems than Parliament demands.
- **April 2019** — European Parliament votes 432-203 to expand the mandate to include lane-keeping, advanced emergency braking, and driver monitoring.
- **November 2019** — Council of the EU formally adopts the regulation; text published in the Official Journal.
- **July 2022** — GSR applies to all new vehicle types (entry into force for type-approval).
- **July 2024** — GSR applies to all new vehicles sold in the EU for most ADAS features.
- **January 2029** — Remaining features (e.g., direct driver monitoring, advanced alcohol interlocks) become mandatory for all new vehicles.

## Impact and consequences

The GSR's most immediate impact was on the automotive supply chain. Suppliers like Bosch, Continental, and Valeo rapidly scaled up production of cameras, radar sensors, and driver-monitoring systems. By 2023, Bosch reported that its sales of ADAS components had doubled since 2019, driven largely by the EU regulation. Smaller manufacturers—especially low-volume producers like sports-car makers and specialist van converters—sought exemptions, and the Commission granted derogations for up to 1,500 vehicles per year per manufacturer.

Consumer costs were lower than industry had predicted. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment estimated a price increase of €200–300 per vehicle; by 2023, the European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA) acknowledged that costs were closer to €150–250, and that some systems, like intelligent speed assistance, had already been fitted voluntarily on 60% of new cars in the EU by 2022.

Implementation varied by member state. Germany, with its large luxury car market, had the highest rate of early compliance; Bulgaria and Romania, where average vehicle age was over 14 years and new-car sales were low, saw limited impact until the 2024 deadline. A 2023 European Commission report found that 12 member states had fully transposed the GSR into national law by the deadline, while 15 were still in the process of updating their market-surveillance frameworks.

The GSR's framework for automated driving has had a slower start. The Commission adopted the first implementing rules for fully automated vehicles in 2022 (Regulation 2022/1426/EU), but as of 2024, no manufacturer had applied for type-approval of a level 4 or level 5 vehicle under the new system. Industry observers cited the complexity of liability rules and the absence of harmonized traffic laws as the main barriers.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU mandate intelligent speed assistance (ISA) against industry opposition under the Vehicle General Safety Regulation?

The Commission's 2018 impact assessment showed that ISA could reduce collisions by up to 30% and fatalities by 20%, saving an estimated 25,000 lives over 10 years. Industry opposition—led by ACEA, which argued that ISA would annoy drivers and increase costs—was met with data from several studies, including a 2017 Swedish trial that found 85% of drivers accepted the system after using it. The Parliament insisted on ISA being mandatory, not just informative, after the ETSC provided evidence that voluntary systems had a compliance rate of only 10%.

### Why does the Vehicle General Safety Regulation (GSR) require event data recorders ("black boxes") in all new vehicles?

The requirement, effective from July 2022, was pushed by accident investigation agencies and the European Court of Auditors, who argued that a lack of standardized crash data had hampered safety analysis. The European Road Safety Observatory had shown in 2016 that only 7 EU countries collected detailed event data, and that comparisons between member states were impossible. The GSR mandates that data recorders capture 11 parameters (speed, braking, steering angle, seatbelt status) for 5 seconds before and after a crash, and that the data remain anonymous unless a court orders its release.

### Why did the Vehicle General Safety Regulation (GSR) also cover automated and driverless vehicles?

The inclusion of automated driving was a late addition, driven by the Parliament's insistence that the same regulation cover both legacy safety features and future technologies. The Commission had originally planned a separate directive, but the 2018 fatal Tesla crash in California—where the driver was using Autopilot—created a political consensus that the EU needed a single, coherent legal framework. The GSR's implementing rules, adopted in 2022, define three categories of automation: driver-only (level 0-2), assisted (level 3), and fully automated (level 4-5), with different approval requirements for each.
