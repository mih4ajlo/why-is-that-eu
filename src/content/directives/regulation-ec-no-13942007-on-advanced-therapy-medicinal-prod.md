## What is it?

The Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMP) Regulation created a single, EU-wide framework for authorising gene therapies, somatic cell therapies, and tissue-engineered products. Before 2007, these products fell into a regulatory void: they were neither fully drugs nor fully medical devices, and national authorities applied inconsistent rules. The regulation classifies ATMPs as medicinal products, making them subject to the centralised marketing authorisation procedure through the European Medicines Agency (EMA), with a dedicated Committee for Advanced Therapies (CAT) providing scientific expertise.

The regulation defined three categories: gene therapy medicinal products (GTMPs), somatic cell therapy medicinal products (sCTMPs), and tissue-engineered products (TEPs). Combined ATMPs (containing a medical device) and hospital-exemption pathways were also created. It amended Directive 2001/83/EC and Regulation 726/2004/EC to integrate ATMPs into existing pharmaceutical law.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a specific market failure and regulatory crisis: by the early 2000s, European biotechnology companies had developed promising gene and cell therapies but faced a fragmented approval landscape. Germany, France, and the UK each applied different rules, creating a "patchwork" that the European Commission's 2004 assessment of the pharmaceutical acquis identified as a barrier to innovation and patient access. The pivotal incident was the 1999 death of Jesse Gelsinger in a US gene therapy trial — although not an EU case, it shattered public confidence and prompted regulators worldwide to demand stricter oversight, making the lack of a harmonised EU framework politically untenable.

The structural tension was between two competing logics: national health authorities wanted to control advanced therapies as biologicals or medical devices under existing rules, while industry argued that the high development costs (€500 million to €1 billion per therapy, according to industry estimates cited by the Commission) required a single EU market to achieve economies of scale. The orphan drug regulation (141/2000) had already created incentives for rare-disease treatments, but ATMPs — often targeting small patient populations — fell through gaps.

The coalition that pushed the regulation through was unusual: patient advocacy groups (like the European Organisation for Rare Diseases, EURORDIS) aligned with innovative biotech firms (such as Amsterdam Molecular Therapeutics, now uniQure, and Oxford BioMedica) and the European Commission's DG Enterprise (led by Commissioner Günter Verheugen, 2004-2010). Opponents included some national medicines agencies (notably Germany's BfArM) that feared losing control over hospital-based therapies, and the medical devices industry, which worried that tissue-engineered products (like skin substitutes) would be reclassified as high-risk drugs. The European Parliament's Environment and Health Committee voted 45-12 in favour in July 2007, with the final plenary vote in September 2007 passing by a large majority (546 in favour, 60 against, 23 abstentions). The key compromise was the hospital-exemption clause (Article 28), which allowed non-routine use of ATMPs prepared on a non-industrial basis in hospitals under national law — a concession to Germany and France.

Timing was decisive: the regulation succeeded under the Barroso I Commission (2004-2009), when health and innovation agendas converged. The 2005 EU Competitiveness Council had already called for a "European strategy for advanced therapies," and the 2006 EMEA (now EMA) report on gene therapy clinical trials showed that only 4 of 47 applications had received harmonised EU-level opinions. The final push came in 2007, when the UK's House of Lords Science and Technology Committee published a searing report warning that Europe was "falling behind the US and Japan" in cell therapy regulation.

What changed from earlier failed attempts: a 2003 Commission consultation had proposed treating advanced therapies under the existing medical devices directive (93/42/EEC), but this was rejected by biotech companies who argued that the device regime lacked adequate clinical-trial oversight and post-market surveillance. The 2005 proposal shifted to a medicinal-product framework, which required negotiation of the hospital-exemption clause and the CAT committee structure. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Austrian MEP Karin Scheele (PES), brokered the final deal in trilogue negotiations in June 2007.

## Timeline

- **2003** — European Commission launches a public consultation on advanced therapies, proposing classification under medical devices directive; industry rejects this
- **2005** — Commission presents a formal proposal (COM(2005) 567 final) treating ATMPs as medicinal products, with a dedicated committee at EMEA
- **2006** — EMEA report shows only 4 of 47 gene therapy applications received harmonised EU opinions; UK House of Lords warns Europe is falling behind
- **2007** — European Parliament votes 546-60 in favour (September); Council adopts regulation as a "first reading agreement" (October)
- **2008** — Regulation enters into force (December 30)
- **2009** — Committee for Advanced Therapies (CAT) holds first meeting (January); EMA issues first ATMP classification guidelines
- **2011** — First ATMP receives EU marketing authorisation: ChondroCelect (tissue-engineered cartilage), withdrawn in 2016 for commercial reasons
- **2013** — Glybera (gene therapy for lipoprotein lipase deficiency) becomes first gene therapy approved in the West; priced at €1.1 million per treatment
- **2014** — EMA publishes first ATMP post-marketing safety report; only 2 ATMPs currently authorised
- **2020** — Kymriah and Yescarta (CAR-T cell therapies) receive approval, marking commercial breakthrough
- **2022** — Commission reports that 23 ATMPs have been authorised, but only 11 remain on the market; hospital-exemption pathways are used in Germany for over 200 individual treatments annually

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most significant impact has been on industry investment and patient access. Between 2008 and 2020, the EMA received 387 ATMP classification requests, but only 23 received marketing authorisation — a 6% approval rate that reflects both scientific complexity and the stringency of the evidence requirements. The European Commission's 2022 evaluation found that the regulation "has successfully created a harmonised EU framework" but that "only 11 of 23 authorised ATMPs are commercially available," with the rest withdrawn due to pricing disputes or lack of reimbursement.

Germany has become the dominant player: of the 200+ hospital-exemption treatments administered annually, 80% occur in German university hospitals, according to the 2022 Commission report. This has created a two-tier system where well-funded German patients access therapies that remain unavailable in Bulgaria, Romania, or Greece. The average time from ATMP classification request to EU authorisation is 5.4 years, compared to 3.2 years for standard biologics.

Industry response has been mixed. Large pharmaceutical companies (Novartis, Gilead, Roche) have entered the field through acquisitions: Novartis paid $475 million for Kymriah's developer, while Gilead acquired Kite Pharma for $11.9 billion in 2017 — the latter after the regulation had created a predictable approval pathway. But small biotechs complain that the centralised procedure's costs (€200,000-€400,000 for an application fee, plus €2-5 million for clinical trial setup) are prohibitive. The EMA's 2020 stakeholder survey found that 62% of SME developers cited "regulatory complexity and cost" as the main barrier to applying for centralised authorisation.

The regulation's most controversial provision — the hospital-exemption clause — has been used strategically by national health systems. Germany's Arzneimittelgesetz (AMG) allows hospitals to manufacture ATMPs for individual patients without EMA approval, leading to what the Commission's 2022 report called "divergent safety standards across Member States." In 2021, the German Federal Institute for Drugs (BfArM) reported 247 hospital-exemption treatments, including 19 serious adverse events — but because these treatments are exempt from EU pharmacovigilance rules, reporting is voluntary.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU set up a special committee just for advanced gene and cell therapies?

The Committee for Advanced Therapies was created because ATMPs require specialised scientific expertise that the standard EMA committees lacked — particularly in gene editing, cell culture, and tissue engineering. The 2006 impact assessment noted that existing experts in the Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use (CHMP) had "limited experience with non-viral gene therapy vectors and tissue scaffolds." The CAT, with 20 members including patient representatives and clinicians, provides 72-day accelerated assessments for SME applicants.

### Why are so few approved gene and cell therapies actually available to buy in Europe?

The fundamental challenge is that ATMPs are typically one-time, patient-specific treatments costing €300,000 to €2 million, but health technology assessment (HTA) bodies in most Member States lack frameworks for valuing curative therapies. The 2022 Commission evaluation found that only 11 of 23 authorised ATMPs have concluded reimbursement agreements in at least one Member State. Glybera was withdrawn in 2017 after only 31 patients were treated globally — largely because Germany's IQWiG refused to accept the cost-effectiveness model. The regulation mandates EU-level safety and efficacy approval but leaves pricing and reimbursement entirely to Member States.

### How did the death of a teenager in a gene therapy trial lead to stricter EU rules for these treatments?

Although the 1999 death of 18-year-old Jesse Gelsinger in a US gene therapy trial at the University of Pennsylvania occurred outside Europe, it fundamentally altered regulatory expectations worldwide. The US FDA halted all gene therapy trials for 18 months; the EU's EMEA responded by issuing stricter trial oversight guidelines in 2000. When the ATMP regulation was drafted, the spectre of Gelsinger's death — caused by a massive immune response to an adenoviral vector — drove the requirement for "comprehensive risk management plans" in ATMP applications (Article 14). Commissioner Verheugen explicitly referenced the case in his 2005 legislative proposal, stating that "public confidence in gene therapy requires the highest standards of safety."

### Why can hospitals still make their own gene and cell therapies without getting approval from the European Medicines Agency?

The hospital-exemption clause (Article 28) was a political compromise demanded by Germany and France, whose university hospitals had decades of experience manufacturing cellular therapies for individual patients. Without it, these countries threatened to block the regulation entirely. The exemption applies only to ATMPs prepared on a "non-routine basis" and used within the same Member State under a physician's prescription. The Commission's 2020 report acknowledged that "the definition of 'non-routine basis' has proven difficult to interpret," leading to Germany's extensive use while Greece and Portugal have not granted any hospital exemptions at all.
