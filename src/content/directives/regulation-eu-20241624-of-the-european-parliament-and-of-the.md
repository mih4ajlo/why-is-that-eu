## What is it?

The Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR) replaces the patchwork of national implementations of previous EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD4, AMLD5, AMLD6) with directly applicable rules. This means EU member states cannot gold-plate or water down the requirements — banks, crypto-asset service providers, real estate agents, and high-value goods dealers across the bloc now face identical obligations for customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.

Crucially, the regulation establishes the **Anti-Money Laundering Authority (AMLA)** — a new EU-level supervisor headquartered in Frankfurt — which will directly oversee the riskiest financial institutions and coordinate national supervisors. It also creates a centralised **Beneficial Ownership Register** accessible to obliged entities and, with legitimate interest, to journalists and civil society organisations.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation is a direct response to a series of spectacular money laundering scandals that exposed the failure of fragmented national supervision. The most dramatic trigger was the **Danske Bank scandal** in 2018-2019, where it emerged that €200 billion in suspicious transactions from non-resident customers in Estonia flowed through the bank's tiny Tallinn branch between 2007 and 2015. The Commission's 2019 impact assessment noted that around €1.3 billion of these flows were directly linked to Russian and other former Soviet oligarchs. Danish and Estonian supervisors blamed each other; no single authority had the power to intervene.

The **FinCEN Files** leak in 2020 by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ) revealed that major global banks continued to move billions in suspicious funds despite internal warnings, and that EU national supervisors rarely shared information. The leak directly implicated Deutsche Bank, HSBC, and ING in processing transactions linked to organised crime. EU Commissioner for Financial Services Mairead McGuinness, who took office in October 2020, told the European Parliament in 2021 that "the status quo is not an option — we need a single rulebook and a single supervisor."

The **political coalition** that pushed this through was unusually broad. The European Parliament's lead rapporteur, Eero Heinäluoma (S&D, Finland), built a cross-party alliance including Renew Europe, the Greens, and parts of the centre-right EPP. The Council, divided between northern member states with strong supervision (Netherlands, Sweden, Estonia) and southern states concerned about costs (Malta, Cyprus, Luxembourg), was eventually brought on board by the promise of AMLA's direct oversight reducing the "race to the bottom" in supervision.

The **timing** was decisive. The COVID-19 pandemic's massive increase in digital financial transactions, combined with sanctions against Russian oligarchs following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, created an unprecedented sense of urgency. The Commission's February 2022 proposal noted that "the capacity to quickly and effectively trace assets of sanctioned persons has been severely hampered by the lack of a centralised EU beneficial ownership register."

**What changed** that made this possible after years of stalled reform? The revelations from the **Pandora Papers** (October 2021) and the **Mahmoud el-Ismaili** case — where a Maltese journalist was murdered in 2017 while investigating links between politicians and money laundering — cemented political will. When the European Parliament voted on the final text on 24 April 2024, 555 MEPs supported it, with only 76 against — an overwhelming majority.

## Timeline

- **2007** — Third Money Laundering Directive (2005/60/EC) takes effect; national implementation variations begin to emerge
- **2015** — Fourth AML Directive (2015/849/EU) adopted, introducing beneficial ownership registers for the first time
- **2018** — Danske Bank whistleblower Howard Wilkinson alerts supervisors; €200 billion suspicious flow estimates published
- **2018** — Fifth AML Directive (2018/843/EU) adopted to extend rules to virtual currencies and prepaid cards
- **2020** — FinCEN Files published; Commissioner McGuinness announces structural review of AML framework
- **July 2021** — Commission presents legislative package including AMLR, AMLD6, and proposal to establish AMLA
- **December 2022** — Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement on AMLA seat (Frankfurt) and supervisory structure
- **April 2024** — European Parliament adopts regulation (555-76); Council follows on 15 May 2024
- **July 2024** — Regulation published in Official Journal as (EU) 2024/1624
- **January 2026** — Full application date for most provisions; AMLA begins direct supervision of selected entities
- **2028** — AMLA to assume direct supervision of up to 40 of the EU's riskiest financial institutions

## Impact and consequences

The regulation represents the most significant hardening of EU anti-money laundering rules in a generation. Banks face uniform due diligence requirements with no derogations for low-risk jurisdictions. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated the harmonised rules could reduce suspicious transaction reporting inconsistencies by up to 30% — in 2021, national Financial Intelligence Units received over 1.2 million suspicious transaction reports in total, but only 3% were cross-referenced between countries.

Crypto-asset service providers are now fully within scope, requiring them to collect and verify beneficial ownership information for all transfers over €1,000 — a direct response to the 2022 report by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) that found over 60% of member jurisdictions had weak or no regulation of virtual assets in the EU.

The Beneficial Ownership Register is already facing legal challenges. In November 2022, the Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU) struck down public access to beneficial ownership data in the *WM v Sovim* case (C-37/20), ruling it violated privacy rights. The AMLR attempts to balance this by requiring "legitimate interest" for journalists and NGOs while giving obliged entities automatic access.

Countries with historically weak AML enforcement — Malta, Cyprus, Luxembourg, and Estonia — face the greatest pressure. The Commission's 2022 country reports identified Malta as having "structural deficiencies" in its AML framework, with only 12 criminal convictions for money laundering between 2015 and 2021 despite hundreds of suspicious transaction reports. Cyprus reported zero convictions for tax-related money laundering in the same period.

## Key questions answered

### Can EU residents still open anonymous bank accounts in the EU?

No. The regulation bans all anonymous accounts, safe-deposit boxes, and bearer shares. Banks must identify the beneficial owner — the real person ultimately controlling an account — for all accounts opened after July 2024. For existing accounts, institutions have until July 2027 to verify ownership. Any account where the beneficial owner cannot be identified must be closed.

### Who can look up who really owns a company in the EU's central database?

Obliged entities like banks and lawyers have automatic access. Journalists and civil society organisations can request access if they demonstrate "legitimate interest" — typically investigative reporting or anti-corruption work. The general public does not have automatic access, following the CJEU's *Sovim* ruling. All access is logged and monitored to prevent "fishing expeditions."

### Why did the EU create a new watchdog instead of making national authorities cooperate better?

The Danske Bank scandal showed national supervisors blame each other when a problem crosses borders. The Finnish supervisor (FSA) and Estonian supervisor (EFSA) each claimed the other was responsible for monitoring the Estonia branch. AMLA has binding powers to investigate and coordinate, can order a national supervisor to open a case, and directly oversees the 30-40 riskiest financial institutions in the EU. The Commission estimated that 75% of cross-border AML cases in 2021 involved three or more member states.

### Do crypto companies have to follow EU anti-money-laundering rules?

Yes. Crypto-asset service providers — exchanges, wallet providers, and firms involved in transfers — must conduct full customer due diligence on transactions over €1,000, report suspicious activity, and verify beneficial ownership of corporate clients. Self-hosted wallets (wallets not on an exchange) can still receive small transfers, but any transaction over €1,000 triggers reporting. This harmonises rules across the EU, ending the previous patchwork where Malta licensed lax crypto firms while Germany had stricter rules.
