## What is it?

The Flavourings Regulation (1334/2008/EC) sets out which substances can be used to give taste and smell to food across the European Union, how they must be labelled, and what safety data manufacturers must provide. It created a single EU-wide list of approved flavourings — everything from vanilla extract to synthetic strawberry flavouring — that replaced the previous system where each member state maintained its own approval regime.

The regulation distinguishes several categories: natural flavourings (derived from plants or animals), nature-identical flavourings (chemically synthesised but identical to those found in nature), and artificial flavourings (substances not found in nature). It banned the use of certain naturally occurring toxic compounds — like coumarin from cassia cinnamon — unless their concentration stays below strict limits. All new flavourings must undergo a safety evaluation by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) before they can be marketed.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by two structural failures: bitter national disputes over the safety of certain flavourings and the rise of "natural" labelling claims that consumers could not verify.

The immediate trigger was a 2004 rebellion by Denmark and Sweden. These countries had long maintained stricter national limits on coumarin, a flavouring compound found in cassia cinnamon that can cause liver damage in high doses. When the European Commission attempted to harmonise coumarin limits upward to facilitate trade in traditional cinnamon-heavy pastries like Swedish *kanelbullar*, Copenhagen refused to comply, citing its own toxicological assessments. The resulting deadlock exposed the weakness of the existing system — a 1996 Commission Directive on flavourings (96/21/EC) that set generic categories but left national authorities to make their own safety determinations.

Simultaneously, major food companies were exploiting the labelling gap. In 2005, the UK consumer watchdog *Which?* tested 30 "all-natural" flavoured products and found that 12 contained artificial flavourings not declared on the label. German consumer group *Stiftung Warentest* published similar findings in 2006, reporting that "natural vanilla flavour" in many ice creams was actually derived from synthetic vanillin. The European Consumers' Organisation (BEUC) formally complained to the Commission in 2007, alleging systematic mislabelling that undermined consumer trust in the single market.

The political coalition that passed the regulation was unusual: consumer groups and Nordic health authorities allied with export-oriented food producers from France and Italy, who wanted a single, clear standard to avoid having to reformulate products for each member state. The main opposition came from German and Austrian bakers' associations, who argued that strict coumarin limits would destroy traditional recipes. The European Parliament's Environment Committee voted 47-12 in favour of the Commission's proposal in 2008, with the strongest support from Socialist and Green MEPs from Scandinavia and the Netherlands.

The timing was also shaped by two broader developments. First, the General Food Law Regulation (178/2002/EC) had just established EFSA in 2002, creating a central scientific body that could perform the risk assessments the old system lacked. Second, the 2008 Chinese milk scandal — in which melamine-tainted infant formula killed six babies — had made European politicians acutely sensitive to food safety issues. Commissioner for Health Markos Kyprianou explicitly linked the flavourings regulation to the need for "a robust, science-based system that prevents another melamine disaster" during the parliamentary debate in April 2008.

What changed from earlier attempts was the mandatory pre-market approval system. Earlier drafts from 2002 had proposed voluntary industry self-regulation, but the coumarin dispute and the mislabelling scandals had demonstrated that voluntary approaches could not guarantee either safety or truthfulness. The Commission's 2008 impact assessment calculated that the regulation would cost industry roughly €80 million in compliance costs but save consumers €200 million annually through reduced confusion and fewer safety incidents.

## Timeline

- **1996** — Commission Directive 96/21/EC sets broad categories for flavourings but leaves safety decisions to national authorities
- **2004** — Denmark refuses to implement EU coumarin limits, triggering infringement proceedings and exposing regulatory gaps
- **2005-2006** — UK and German consumer groups publish investigations revealing widespread mislabelling of "natural" flavourings
- **2007** — European Commission publishes proposal for Regulation 1334/2008, introducing mandatory pre-market approval
- **2008, April** — European Parliament Committee on Environment votes 47-12 in favour of the proposal
- **2008, December 16** — Regulation adopted by Council and Parliament
- **2009, January 20** — Regulation enters into force
- **2011, January 22** — Application date: all flavourings on the market must comply; a transition period allowed existing products to be sold until 2013
- **2012** — EFSA publishes the first EU-wide list of approved flavourings, containing approximately 2,100 substances
- **2014** — First major enforcement: Danish authorities seize and destroy shipments of cassia cinnamon exceeding coumarin limits, with two Swedish bakeries fined a combined €45,000
- **2017** — Commission revises the list to include 14 new synthetic flavourings after EFSA safety reviews

## Impact and consequences

The regulation fundamentally restructured the flavourings market. Before 2008, an estimated 3,500 flavourings were used across EU member states, with widely varying national approvals. By 2012, EFSA had whittled this to around 2,100 substances, eliminating approximately 1,400 compounds that lacked sufficient safety data or had been banned in at least one member state.

Small and medium-sized flavour houses were hit hardest. The German flavourings association *Deutscher Verband der Aromenindustrie* reported in 2010 that 14% of its member companies had stopped producing certain flavourings because the cost of compiling EFSA safety dossiers — estimated at €50,000 to €150,000 per substance — exceeded projected sales revenue. This concentration effect disproportionately benefited multinationals like Givaudan (Switzerland), Firmenich (Switzerland), and IFF (US), which together controlled roughly 50% of the global flavour market.

Consumer labelling changed visibly. A 2015 Commission survey found that "natural flavouring" claims decreased by 30% on supermarket shelves after the regulation's stricter definitions took effect. Instead, products increasingly used terms like "natural vanilla flavouring with other natural flavourings" — the legal formula for when a flavouring is mostly natural but contains components from other sources.

Enforcement varied sharply. Nordic countries actively tested products; Finland fined a bakery €5,000 in 2016 for using unapproved synthetic ethyl vanillin in organic biscuits. Southern European countries historically enforced less aggressively — a 2018 Commission audit found that Greek authorities had tested only 12 flavouring samples in two years, compared to 340 in Sweden.

## Key questions answered

### Can I tell from the label whether a flavouring is artificial or natural?

EU law requires the label to say "flavouring" for artificial compounds and "natural flavouring" only when the flavouring substance is physically derived from a plant or animal source — not chemically synthesised. If a product says "natural strawberry flavouring," the taste must come from actual strawberries. If it says just "strawberry flavouring," it could be chemically identical but synthetic. The 2008 regulation made these categories legally enforceable, closing loopholes that had allowed companies to call synthetic compounds "natural" based on minimal amounts of real plant material.

### Why can't I buy cinnamon powder in Sweden that tastes as strong as in the US?

Because the regulation caps coumarin — a liver-toxic compound naturally present in cassia cinnamon — at 15 mg/kg for everyday baked goods and 50 mg/kg for traditional seasonal products. Sweden and Denmark pushed for these limits after their own toxicological assessments in the early 2000s showed that heavy cinnamon consumers (often children eating *kanelbullar* daily) could exceed safe intake levels. The regulation allows higher levels for traditional products but requires reformulation of mass-market cinnamon goods. US regulations have no coumarin limit for cinnamon.

### Does every new flavouring need to be approved before it can be sold in the EU?

Yes. Since 2009, any flavouring not on the EU-wide positive list must undergo a safety evaluation by EFSA before it can be marketed. This pre-market approval system — similar to the one used for food additives — replaced the old "negative list" approach where authorities had to prove a substance was dangerous before banning it. The regulation also grandfathered in existing flavourings that had been used in at least one member state before 2009, but required manufacturers to submit safety dossiers for those substances within three years to keep them on the list.

### What happened to flavourings that were taken off the approved list?

The regulation gave manufacturers a transition period to sell products already on the market containing now-banned flavourings, but required those products to be reformulated or withdrawn by 2013. The most significant category affected was synthetic coumarin, which was removed entirely because it offered no functional benefit over natural coumarin and raised the same liver toxicity concerns. Some specialty flavourings — like the compound used for "green apple" in sugar-free chewing gum — were removed after EFSA found insufficient genotoxicity data, leading to reformulation costs estimated at €15 million for the confectionery industry.
