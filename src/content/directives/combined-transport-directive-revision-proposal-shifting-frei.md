## What is it?

The Combined Transport Directive (originally 92/106/EEC) is the EU’s main legal tool for encouraging freight to use a mix of transport modes — road, rail, inland waterways, and short sea shipping — rather than road-only journeys. The 2023 revision proposal from the European Commission, tabled under the Green Deal’s "Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy," updates definitions, strengthens economic incentives (like reduced road tolls and lower taxes on short road legs), and introduces a stronger enforcement mechanism.

The core idea is simple: if a truck carries a container or trailer by train or barge for the majority of its journey, then the road portion that connects to the rail terminal gets preferential treatment — lower tolls, fewer restrictions, and simpler paperwork. The proposal seeks to replace the current, fragmented national approaches with a common EU framework that makes combined transport genuinely cheaper and faster than road-only alternatives.

## Why was it introduced?

The revision’s opening drama is not a scandal but a failure of ambition: the original 1992 directive had become a dead letter. By 2021, according to the European Court of Auditors (ECA), combined transport accounted for barely 5% of EU freight, and the Commission’s own impact assessment found that the original directive’s market share had actually *declined* since the early 2000s. The policy had been overtaken by reality — loopholes multiplied, national implementation diverged wildly, and industry simply defaulted to road.

The structural tension was fundamental: the single market’s liberalisation of road haulage (starting in the 1990s) made truck-only transport artificially cheap, because road infrastructure costs were largely socialised while rail and barge operators faced high fixed costs for terminals, tracks, and vessels. The 1992 directive tried to correct this by allowing member states to offer subsidies (e.g., reduced fuel taxes, lower road tolls) for combined transport. But it left so much discretion to national governments that the incentives became a patchwork: Germany offered generous toll exemptions; France applied different thresholds; Spain barely used the directive at all. The result was that the directive became a competitive distortion rather than a driver of modal shift.

The coalition that pushed for revision was a rare alliance: the European Parliament’s centre-right and centre-left, the Commission's transport and climate directorates, and a surprising bloc of industry groups. The European Rail Freight Association (ERFA) and the International Union of Railways (UIC) lobbied hard for tighter definitions — they argued that the old directive’s vagueness allowed operators to claim combined transport status for journeys where the rail leg was trivial (e.g., just 20 km). Meanwhile, the European Sea Ports Organisation (ESPO) and short-sea shipping operators demanded inclusion of short sea legs, which the original directive had mostly ignored.

The timing was decisive: the 2019 European Green Deal set a target of reducing transport emissions by 90% by 2050, and the "Fit for 55" package in 2021 made clear that road-to-rail shift was essential. The 2021 State of the Union speech by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen explicitly mentioned revising the Combined Transport Directive. The European Parliament’s 2021 own-initiative report on sustainable mobility, led by MEP Karima Delli (Greens/EFA), called for a "complete overhaul" of the directive, arguing that "the current rules are not fit for the green transition."

The main opposition came from road haulage associations — particularly the International Road Transport Union (IRU) — which warned that stricter combined transport rules would raise costs for small transporters and lead to "competitiveness losses." Some member states, notably Poland and the Baltic countries (whose economies rely heavily on road freight), expressed concerns in inter-institutional negotiations about imposing additional administrative burdens on truck operators who occasionally use rail.

The specific event that unblocked the prolonged stalemate? The combination of three factors: the 2021 Energy Price Crisis (which made diesel very expensive and suddenly made combined transport look cost-competitive), the Russian invasion of Ukraine (which disrupted traditional transit routes and pushed the EU to strengthen its own transport resilience), and the publication of the European Court of Auditors' June 2022 report "EU support for intermodal freight transport: more targeted and simpler rules needed", which documented that the 1992 directive "had not achieved its objectives" and that "varied national implementation" had rendered it "largely ineffective".

## Timeline

- **1992** — Original Combined Transport Directive (92/106/EEC) adopted, establishing the "combined transport" definition and allowing member states to offer reduced road tolls and tax incentives for multimodal freight.
- **2005-2010** — Evaluations by the Commission show minimal modal shift; combined transport share stagnates at ~5-7% of EU freight; road freight continues to grow.
- **2015** — The Commission publishes "Freight Transport Logistics in the EU", a communication that identifies the Combined Transport Directive as "in need of modernisation".
- **2016** — European Parliament resolution on sustainable mobility calls for revision; Commission launches a public consultation.
- **2019** — European Green Deal published, committing to a 90% reduction in transport emissions by 2050. Combined transport revision included in the Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy.
- **2021** — "Fit for 55" legislative package proposed; Commission impact assessment on the directive revision estimates potential for 25-30% reduction in CO2 from road freight if combined transport share doubles.
- **June 2022** — European Court of Auditors report finds the original directive "ineffective" and calls for "simpler, more targetted rules".
- **November 2022** — Commission publishes its revision proposal; introduces a new definition of "combined transport" that requires the non-road leg to cover at least 60% of the total distance (up from the previous 100 km rule).
- **2023** — European Parliament and Council begin negotiations; Parliament's TRAN committee pushes for mandatory inclusion of inland waterways and short sea shipping; Council (under Swedish Presidency) resists binding national targets.

## Impact and consequences

If adopted, the revision would directly affect an estimated 1.2 million trucks that currently make cross-border journeys annually within the EU — roughly 15% of all international road freight — by making combined transport financially viable for a much larger share. The Commission’s impact assessment projected that the proposal could increase combined transport’s market share from ~5% to 15-20% by 2035, saving an estimated 10-15 million tonnes of CO2 per year.

The road haulage industry responded with a mix of caution and opportunism: the IRU initially opposed mandatory national targets, but proposed an alternative "Green Deal for Road Transport" that would allow voluntary carbon offsetting. Rail freight operators welcomed the proposal: DB Cargo and Lineas publicly endorsed the 60% distance threshold, calling it "long overdue." Inland waterway operators in the Netherlands and Belgium — who had seen stagnant cargo volumes — said the inclusion of short sea shipping would create new competition for barge capacity.

Countries that rely heavily on road transit — especially Poland (home to ~30% of EU long-haul truck fleets), Romania, and Bulgaria — argued that the 60% threshold would disadvantage their drivers, who often cannot access rail terminals within a reasonable distance. The Commission countered with a transition period: for the first five years, the threshold would be 40%, giving member states time to build infrastructure.

A notable early test came in Germany: as early as 2022, the German Ministry for Digital and Transport (led by FDP minister Volker Wissing) piloted a "Truck Toll Discount for Combined Transport" on the A1 and A7 motorways, offering 75% reduction on tolls for trucks that could prove rail or barge usage. This pilot, which covered 5,000 trucks in the first six months, demonstrated both the potential and the administrative challenge — enforcement required a digital system for tracking cargo across modes.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU want to move freight off the road?

The EU’s transport sector accounts for roughly 25% of its total greenhouse gas emissions, with road freight alone contributing about 6%. The Green Deal’s 90% transport emissions reduction target cannot be met through vehicle electrification alone — trucks are heavier, less efficient, and slower to electrify than cars. Moving freight to rail (which emits 75% less CO2 per tonne-km than road) and inland waterways (even lower) is the most immediate policy lever available.

### Does this affect local deliveries or only long-haul trucks?

The revision targets the "first and last mile" problem: it only applies to combined transport operations where the road leg is the *shortest* part of the journey (connecting to a rail or barge terminal). For a package delivered directly by truck from warehouse to store — the vast majority of intra-urban deliveries — the directive does not apply. But for long-distance cross-border freight (above 300 km), the directive is intended to push operators to use rail or barges for the middle portion, with trucks handling only the final 20-50 km.

### Will this raise prices for consumers?

Potentially, in the short term. The Commission’s impact assessment estimated that implementation costs for operators could increase freight rates by 2-4% during the transition period, as companies invest in new intermodal terminals and adapt logistics chains. However, the same assessment projected that within 5-10 years, the modal shift would reduce overall logistics costs by 8-12% due to lower fuel use, reduced tolls, and fewer driver shortages — ultimately lowering the price per shipped unit.

### What happens if a country doesn't meet the targets?

The proposal includes a "compliance mechanism" that is still being negotiated. Under the Commission’s original text, member states that fail to meet their national combined transport growth targets (set at 10% increase in tonne-km of combined transport by 2035 relative to 2022) would face infringement proceedings — but with no specific financial penalties. The European Parliament has pushed for binding targets with automatic financial corrections, while the Council prefers a "monitoring-only" approach. The final compromise is expected in 2024-2025.
