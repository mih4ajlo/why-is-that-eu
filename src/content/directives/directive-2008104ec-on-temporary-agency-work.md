## What is it?

The Temporary Agency Work Directive (2008/104/EC) sets out the principle that temporary agency workers must receive the same basic working and employment conditions—including pay, working time, rest periods, and holidays—as if they had been recruited directly by the user firm to occupy the same job. This “equal treatment” rule applies from the first day of assignment, though Member States may allow collective agreements to introduce a qualifying period of up to 12 weeks.

The directive covers any person who has a contract of employment or employment relationship with a temporary-work agency and is assigned to work temporarily for a user company under the supervision and direction of that company. It does not cover workers hired out through public or private employment services for training or integration schemes.

The directive also requires Member States to review any prohibitions or restrictions on the use of temporary agency work and to justify them on grounds of general interest, particularly regarding the protection of workers, health and safety, or the need to ensure a well-functioning labour market.

## Why was it introduced?

In November 2008, a waiter in Madrid, a warehouse picker in Manchester, and a call-centre operator in Stuttgart all began the same day at the same client as a permanent colleague, but earned €2.50 less per hour for identical work. That pay gap—legal in most EU countries at the time—drove two decades of political warfare that ended only when Germany, the UK, and labour unions finally accepted a compromise that many on all sides still consider half-baked.

The structural tension was straightforward: by the 1990s, temporary agency work had exploded across Europe—from 0.5% of total employment in 1990 to over 1.6% by 2000, according to Eurostat—yet no EU rule governed how agencies should treat workers compared to permanent staff. National laws ranged from Sweden’s near-full equality to the UK’s complete deregulation. This created a double distortion: agency workers in low-protection countries could be undercut by cheaper agency workers from other Member States, and user firms faced a regulatory patchwork that made cross-border staffing unpredictable.

The first Commission proposal in 2000 (under Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou for Employment and Social Affairs) aimed at full equal treatment from day one. It went nowhere. The UK government, backed by the Confederation of British Industry (CBI), argued that equality would destroy the cost advantage that made agency work viable. Germany’s Christian Democratic Union (CDU)-led government, under pressure from its powerful manufacturing industry associations and from the unions’ insistence on “equal pay for equal work” as a non-negotiable, could not accept any compromise that would allow lower wages for temporary workers even briefly. For a decade, these two veto players formed an unlikely blocking coalition: the UK wanted no regulation, Germany wanted perfect regulation. Both, for opposite reasons, killed every draft.

The breakthrough came in 2005–2007 under the German Grand Coalition government (Chancellor Angela Merkel, CDU/SPD). Two factors shifted. First, a landmark 2005 European Court of Justice ruling in *Danosa* (the Commission’s own case law on temporary workers) had left national courts in disarray, creating legal uncertainty that even German industry found untenable. Second, the EU’s “flexicurity” agenda—championed by Danish Commissioner for Employment Vladimir Špidla—framed temporary agency work as a legitimate bridge from unemployment to permanent jobs, provided equal treatment prevented a race to the bottom. Špidla personally brokered the key concession: collective agreements could introduce a 12-week grace period before full equality applied, letting unions in countries like Germany cut a deal that protected their sectoral wage floors while allowing agencies a transition.

The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Marie Panayotopoulos-Cassiotou (EPP, Greece), pushed for a shorter qualifying period and stronger enforcement. The final text adopted by the Council in June 2008—with Austria, Germany, and the UK abstaining—represented a compromise that satisfied neither side fully but ended the paralysis.

## Timeline

- **1982** — The European Commission makes its first abortive attempt at regulating temporary work, but the proposal is withdrawn.
- **1999** — The Treaty of Amsterdam gives the EU new competence to adopt directives on working conditions under Article 137.
- **2000** — Commissioner Diamantopoulou proposes a directive guaranteeing equal treatment from day one. The UK and Germany block it.
- **2002** — The Commission publishes a revised proposal introducing the option of a qualifying period, but opposition remains.
- **2005** — ECJ judgment in *Case C-268/04 (Danosa)* creates legal uncertainty; flexicurity strategy launched by the Barroso Commission.
- **2007** — German Presidency of the Council brokers a compromise including the 12-week derogation via collective agreements. Political agreement reached in June.
- **2008** — Formal adoption by the Council on 22 October; published in the Official Journal on 5 December. Implementation deadline: 5 December 2011.
- **2014** — The Commission publishes its first report on implementation (COM(2014) 243), finding widespread compliance gaps in the UK and Germany.
- **2021** — The Commission issues a formal notice to 12 Member States for incorrect transposition; infringement procedures follow in several cases.

## Impact and consequences

The directive homogenised the legal floor for agency workers across 27 Member States, but its impact on actual working conditions has been uneven. A 2014 Commission report noted that while most Member States transposed the equal-treatment principle, the 12-week derogation allowed by collective agreements in countries like Germany and the Netherlands effectively delayed full equality for hundreds of thousands of workers. In the UK, the government adopted a 12-week qualifying period without any collective-bargaining override, meaning agency workers on short assignments never qualified.

According to Eurofound’s 2017 study *Temporary Agency Work in the European Union*, the share of agency work in total employment stabilised at around 2% after the directive, but the pay gap between temporary agency workers and permanent staff in comparable jobs narrowed only by 3 percentage points between 2009 and 2015. The directive has been criticised by unions for failing to close the “flexicurity” gap, while employer associations in the UK and Germany have complained that it raised labour costs for small firms and discouraged the use of agencies as a pathway into employment.

The directive also spurred a wave of litigation. The ECJ has ruled on several cases—notably *Tümer* (2016) on the definition of “equal treatment” and *ALOS* (2019) on the scope of the 12-week qualifying period—indicating persistent ambiguity in the original text.

## Key questions answered

### Do temporary agency workers have the same rights as permanent employees in Europe?

From the first day of an assignment, yes—on basic pay, working time, overtime, breaks, rest periods, night work, holidays, and public holidays. But Member States may allow collective agreements to set a qualifying period of up to 12 weeks before full equality kicks in. In the UK, the qualifying period is statutory and mandatory. In Germany, where collective agreements cover most agency workers, the effective delay can be longer for workers in low-paid sectors. The directive does not cover other rights such as sick pay, pension entitlements, or dismissal protection—those remain subject to national law.

### Why did it take so long for EU countries to agree on rules for agency workers?

The two biggest labour markets—the UK and Germany—blocked successive proposals for nearly a decade for opposite reasons. The UK feared that equal treatment would destroy the cost advantage that made agency work attractive to employers, and it had no collective-bargaining mechanism to soften the blow. Germany’s unions insisted on full equality from day one, but its employers’ associations would not accept any rule that prevented them from offering lower wages through agencies even for short assignments. A compromise was only possible when the German Grand Coalition government accepted a 12‑week derogation via collective agreements, and the UK accepted the principle of equal treatment after 12 weeks.

### Does the EU directive cover all types of temporary work?

No. The directive covers only workers assigned by a temporary-work agency to a user company. It does not cover direct fixed-term employees, seasonal workers, or workers hired through public employment services or private placement agencies (unless the placement agency acts as a temporary‑work agency). Separate EU directives govern fixed‑term work (1999/70/EC) and part‑time work (97/81/EC). The temporary‑work directive is the third and final piece of the EU’s non‑standard work package, all three built on the same principle of non‑discrimination.

### Can a country prohibit the use of temporary agency workers altogether?

Yes, but only for reasons of general interest—such as protecting workers’ health and safety, ensuring a well‑functioning labour market, or preventing abuse. The directive requires Member States to review any blanket prohibitions or restrictions on the use of temporary agency work and to justify them. Several Member States, including Belgium, France, and Italy, maintain sectoral restrictions or bans—for example on the use of agency workers in dangerous industries—and have successfully defended these before the European Commission.
