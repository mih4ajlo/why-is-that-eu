## What is it?

The European Company Statute, formally Council Regulation (EC) No 2157/2001, creates a new legal form — the *Societas Europaea* (SE) — that allows corporations to operate across the European Union under a single set of rules instead of being subject to the company laws of each member state where they have operations. An SE can be formed by merging two or more companies from different EU countries, creating a holding company, or converting an existing national company, provided it has a cross-border element.

To form an SE, a company must have a registered office and a head office in the same EU country, but it can then transfer its registered office anywhere within the EU without dissolving or reincorporating — a major practical advantage. The regulation is explicitly complemented by Directive 2001/86/EC, which supplements the SE Statute with specific rules on worker involvement, ensuring employees retain rights to information, consultation, and participation.

## Why was it introduced?

The SE Statute’s driving force was not a single scandal but a 30-year structural failure in the single market: companies could not easily restructure or merge across borders without incurring high legal costs, dual incorporation requirements, and complex negotiations with up to 15 national company law regimes. By the 1990s, major European firms like Daimler-Benz (Germany) and Chrysler (USA) had chosen US corporate forms — DaimlerChrysler was incorporated in Delaware, not in any EU state — because Delaware law offered a predictable, unified framework. European business federations, notably the Union of Industrial and Employers' Confederations of Europe (UNICE, now BusinessEurope), lobbied Brussels for a European legal form they argued would save an estimated 30-50% of cross-border restructuring costs, according to a 1998 Commission impact assessment.

The political breakthrough came in 2001 after decades of deadlock over worker participation rights. Previous proposals, from the first draft in 1970 under Commissioner for the Internal Market Wilhelm Haferkamp, had been blocked primarily by Germany, where the *Mitbestimmung* (co-determination) system gives workers half the seats on supervisory boards of large firms. German unions and the Social Democratic government feared an SE form would let companies escape these obligations. The compromise, brokered by Internal Market Commissioner Frits Bolkestein (Netherlands, 1999-2004) and Social Affairs Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (Greece, 1999-2004), was Directive 2001/86/EC: before forming an SE, management and workers must negotiate a participation agreement; if negotiations fail, a set of "standard principles" — including national-level default rules — apply. This "negotiated solution" unblocked the Council in late 2001, with a qualified majority including Germany, France, and the UK; the European Parliament voted 342-149 in favour in 2001.

Timing mattered: the 1999 resignation of the Santer Commission had slowed work, but the post-Nice Treaty political climate (December 2000) revived cross-border initiatives, and the arrival of the euro (1999-2002) intensified pressure for a harmonised business framework. The alternative — a full harmonisation of national company laws — was rejected as politically unfeasible and intrusive, so the SE Statute remained optional.

## Timeline

- **1970** — First Commission proposal for a European Company Statute submitted by Commissioner Haferkamp, but abandoned due to disagreements over worker participation.
- **1989** — Commission’s amended proposal fails again, as the Single European Act’s momentum could not overcome German veto threats over co-determination.
- **2000, February** — Commission Vice-President Bolkestein presents a revised proposal, tying it to a separate directive on worker involvement.
- **2001, October 8** — Council of the European Union formally adopts Regulation 2157/2001 and Directive 2001/86/EC.
- **2004, October 8** — The SE Regulation enters into force across the EU, with a three-year transition for member states to adapt national laws.
- **2004, December** — The first SE is registered: MAN Diesel (Germany-UK), though a simpler SE was formed earlier by a Swiss holding company.
- **2008** — The number of SEs worldwide reaches 470, according to the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC), with most "brass plate" SEs registered in Germany.
- **2019, June** — The Commission’s ex-post evaluation, REFIT, finds only 3,300 SEs registered, of which up to 85% are "shell" companies with no employees, according to the Commission’s report.
- **2021** — The EU’s Sustainable Corporate Governance initiative revisits aspects of SE rules, but no major revision is proposed.

## Impact and consequences

The SE Statute has had a modest impact on legal practice but a significant one on political debate. As of 2021, only around 3,000 SEs were registered across the EU — far fewer than the tens of thousands of national public limited companies. According to the Commission's 2019 REFIT evaluation, the majority of SEs were "cost-saving" vehicles: small firms using the SE label for marketing or for a one-time cross-border restructuring, without hiring any new employees in a different member state. The evaluation also found that only 30% of SEs had chosen to transfer their registered office from one country to another — the regulation's flagship flexibility.

The biggest unintended consequence was the "race to the bottom" feared by unions: Germany, which became the most popular registration country for SEs (over half of all registrations by 2019, per ETUC data), saw some companies like Allianz SE and BASF SE choose the legal form specifically to avoid German co-determination rules. The 2001 compromise allowed workers to negotiate participation, but in practice, many German SEs used the "before and after" loophole: if no agreement was reached and the standard principles applied, the size of the supervisory board could shrink, reducing worker seats from 50% to as low as a third. In 2015, the European Court of Justice ruled in *Erzberger* (Case C-566/15) that SEs cannot pick which national rules on worker participation apply — they must follow the rules of the member state where the registered office is located, a small check on forum-shopping.

Industry response was mixed: large multinationals praised the SE's flexibility for tax planning and group simplification, but small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) found the regulation too complex and costly — a point raised by the European SME Envoy's 2016 report. The Commission's 2019 evaluation concluded the SE Statute had not achieved its goal of fostering a genuine European corporate culture and noted that national company law reforms (e.g., the UK's 2006 Companies Act, Germany's 2008 *Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung* reform) had made cross-border operations easier without an SE form.

## Key questions answered

### Why didn't a European Company have to be publicly traded on a stock exchange?

The regulation deliberately allowed both listed and unlisted companies to form an SE, because the original aim was to facilitate cross-border operations for all types of firms, not just those raising capital on public markets. However, in practice, unlisted SEs became the dominant form (over 90% by 2019, per Commission data), as many were small entities using the SE label for internal restructuring or for a single cross-border merger, rather than for public trading. The lack of a listing requirement also reduced scrutiny of these entities, contributing to the "brass plate" problem.

### Why did EU rules for creating a European company fail to stop companies from shopping for the most lenient national laws?

The Delaware phenomenon in the US — where companies incorporate in a single state with favourable laws — did not replicate in the EU because SE registration remained tied to the national law of the member state where the SE's registered office and head office are located. This "real seat" requirement, upheld in the 2007 *Cartesio* ruling (Case C-210/06), prevented SEs from being registered in a low-regulation state without transferring their management. The Commission’s 2019 evaluation noted that this limitation, combined with harmonised company law in many areas, meant the SE never offered the same flexibility as Delaware. Firms seeking light regulation simply converted to national forms in jurisdictions like Luxembourg or the UK.

### Who fought hardest against the creation of a European company law and why?

German trade unions and the German government under Chancellor Gerhard Schröder (SPD) were the most powerful opponents during the 1990s, because they feared the SE form would allow large corporations like Volkswagen or Siemens to bypass the 1976 *Mitbestimmungsgesetz* (Co-determination Act), which mandates a 50% worker representation on supervisory boards for companies with over 2,000 employees. The compromise — Directive 2001/86/EC requiring negotiated worker participation — was the price Germany demanded. French government negotiators, led by Finance Minister Laurent Fabius, also demanded safeguards to prevent SEs from evading French social law, such as the *Code du Travail*’s works councils. The eventual vote in favour was brokered by the rotating Portuguese Council Presidency in late 2001, with Germany voting in favour only after the directive was secured.
