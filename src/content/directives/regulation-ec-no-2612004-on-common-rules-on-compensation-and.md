## What is it?

Regulation 261/2004 gives air passengers departing from or arriving at an EU airport a legally enforceable right to compensation — cash payments of €250 to €600 — when their flight is denied boarding against their will, cancelled without sufficient notice, or delayed by three hours or more at arrival. The regulation also mandates airlines to provide care in the form of meals, hotel accommodation, and communication during disruptions.

The rules apply to all flights within the EU, and flights into the EU operated by EU-based carriers. The compensation is fixed, not proportional to ticket price, and is payable regardless of whether the ticket was refunded. Airlines can avoid paying only if they prove the disruption was caused by "extraordinary circumstances" — extreme weather, security risks, or air traffic control strikes — that could not have been avoided.

## Why was it introduced?

The deregulation of European air transport in the 1990s, culminating in the 1997 full liberalisation of the internal aviation market, created a boom in low-cost carriers (Ryanair, easyJet, Wizz Air) that competed aggressively on price. As ticket prices fell, airlines increasingly relied on overbooking and flight cancellations as cost management tools — gambling that enough passengers would not show up, and compensating those bumped with vouchers or nothing.

By the late 1990s, the pattern was systemic. Passenger complaints to national enforcement bodies surged, particularly in the UK and Belgium, where airlines routinely denied boarding with no compensation and blamed cancellations on "operational reasons" — a catch-all that shielded them from liability under existing IATA rules. A 1998 study by the European Commission found that between 1992 and 1997, denied boarding affected an estimated 200,000 passengers annually on intra-EU flights alone, with compensation rates below 10%.

The legislative trigger came from two directions. First, a 1999 judgment of the Court of Justice of the EU (C-249/98, *van der Wal*) clarified that passengers were entitled to compensation under the Montreal Convention when denied boarding — but the amounts were low and the process required individual litigation. Second, the European Parliament’s Committee on Transport and Tourism, led by rapporteur Giorgos Dimou (EPP, Greece), pushed for a binding EU regime after a 2002 incident at Brussels Airport: a Sabena flight to Athens was repeatedly overbooked, resulting in elderly passengers being stranded for 18 hours without food or water, prompting a formal complaint to the Ombudsman.

The Commission’s 2001 proposal initially sought compensation of €150-€300, but the European Parliament, under rapporteur Dieter-Lebrecht Koch (EPP, Germany), successfully raised the amounts and extended coverage to delays of three hours or more. The Council of Ministers, dominated by member states with major flag carriers (France, Germany, UK), resisted — arguing the costs would harm legacy airlines. The breakthrough came in late 2003, when the Parliament’s transport committee, backed by consumer and tourism associations, threatened to block the full internal aviation market package unless compensation rules were strengthened. The final compromise, adopted in February 2004, reflected the Parliament’s core demands: fixed compensation scales, mandatory care obligations, and a shared burden of proof shifting to airlines.

## Timeline

- **1998** — Commission study finds that over 200,000 passengers annually denied boarding on EU flights, majority receiving no compensation.
- **2001-12-20** — European Commission publishes proposal for a regulation on air passenger rights, with compensation of €150-€300 for denied boarding.
- **2003-10** — European Parliament votes to extend compensation to delays of 3+ hours, increase amounts to €250-€600, and require airlines to prove "extraordinary circumstances".
- **2004-02-11** — Council of the EU formally adopts Regulation 261/2004 after conciliation with Parliament.
- **2004-02-17** — Regulation published in Official Journal of the EU.
- **2005-02-17** — Regulation becomes applicable across all EU member states.
- **2006** — First infringement proceedings launched against six member states (including Italy and Greece) for failing to designate national enforcement bodies.
- **2008** — Court of Justice confirms in *C-402/07 (Sturgeon)* that passengers delayed by 3+ hours are entitled to compensation comparable to cancelled flights, closing a loophole.
- **2013** — Commission proposes revision (COM/2013/130), but negotiations stall due to industry pushback over "extraordinary circumstances" definition.
- **2023** — Commission launches formal evaluation of the regulation, citing over 160,000 unresolved passenger complaints annually across the EU.

## Impact and consequences

Airlines initially resisted sharply. Ryanair, in a 2005 statement, called the regulation "a charter for fraud" and warned it would raise ticket prices by 10-15%. Instead, compensation claims became a secondary revenue stream for law firms and online claim aggregators (AirHelp, Flightright), which by 2020 processed over one million claims annually. The industry response was largely cost absorption: Ryanair reported in 2019 that compensation payouts under EC 261 accounted for just 1.2% of operating costs.

The regulation created a genuine enforcement problem. National enforcement bodies — each member state’s civil aviation authority — varied wildly in competence. A 2019 EU Ombudsman inquiry found that Spain's AESA and Italy's ENAC handled fewer than 5% of complaints within the legal timeline, while Germany's Luftfahrt-Bundesamt resolved over 80% within three months. The Commission’s 2021 report estimated that only 35% of eligible passengers actually file claims, with the rest deterred by procedural complexity and language barriers.

The biggest legal battle remains the "extraordinary circumstances" defence. Airline associations repeatedly challenged the regulation in national courts, arguing technical faults (bird strikes, engine failures) should exempt them. In a landmark 2016 ruling (*C-257/14, van der Lans*), the CJEU narrowed the defence considerably: airlines must prove that the technical fault was "inherent to the air carrier's normal exercise of its activity" — effectively rejecting routine maintenance issues as extraordinary. British Airways, after the 2018 engine fire on a Boeing 777 in Las Vegas, initially invoked "extraordinary circumstances"; the UK Supreme Court rejected this in 2022, ordering compensation for 109 passengers.

## Key questions answered

### How much money can an airline passenger get for a cancelled or delayed flight in Europe?

For cancellations with less than 14 days' notice, passengers are entitled to €250 for flights under 1,500 km, €400 for intra-EU flights over 1,500 km, and €600 for non-EU flights over 3,500 km. For delays of three hours or more at arrival, the same amounts apply. The airline can deduct these amounts from any re-routing cost, but the cash compensation stands regardless of whether the ticket was refunded.

### Can every EU airline passenger get compensation for a weather delay?

No. Weather is considered an "extraordinary circumstance" that exempts airlines from paying cash compensation. However, the airline must still provide care — meals, hotel accommodation, and two free phone calls — even for weather-related delays. The line between weather and operational planning is often disputed: if a forecast was available 48 hours in advance and the airline still scheduled the flight, courts have sometimes awarded compensation despite weather.

### Why do some countries enforce EU flight compensation rules better than others?

The regulation gave enforcement to each member state's own civil aviation authority, without a central EU enforcer. Germany, Austria, and the Netherlands have proactive enforcement bodies that fine airlines and process complaints quickly. Spain, Italy, and Greece have chronically under-resourced national enforcement bodies — Spain's AESA had a backlog of 40,000 complaints in 2022. The European Commission has started infringement proceedings against Spain and Italy for systemic enforcement failures, but has not stripped them of national competence.

### Is there any way to avoid the compensation limit set in 2004?

No. The €600 cap on compensation has never been raised since the regulation took effect in 2005, despite inflation reducing its real value by nearly 70%. Airline associations have successfully blocked all Commission attempts to revise the amounts upward — most recently in 2017, when EU Transport Commissioner Violeta Bulc (2014-2019) proposed a revision that was abandoned after eight major airlines threatened to sue. As a result, a €600 claim from 2005 is worth roughly €430 in 2024 purchasing power, according to Eurostat’s inflation series.
