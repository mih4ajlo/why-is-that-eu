## What is it?

The European Media Freedom Act (EMFA) is a regulation that sets binding rules to safeguard media independence and pluralism across EU member states. It prohibits surveillance of journalists (except in limited, judicially authorised cases), requires transparency in state advertising allocated to media outlets, and mandates independence safeguards for public service media governance and funding. It also establishes a new European Board for Media Services to replace the existing European Regulators Group for Audiovisual Media Services, giving it stronger coordination powers.

The regulation requires member states to assess media market concentrations and their impact on pluralism. It introduces a "media privilege" in content moderation: very large online platforms must follow a special procedure before removing content from recognised media outlets, with a 24-hour delay. The EMFA creates exemptions from liability for journalistic content under the Digital Services Act, and requires that member states protect journalists from Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (SLAPPs) through existing national law harmonisation.

## Why was it introduced?

The EMFA's roots lie in a political crisis that erupted in October 2019 when Polish Prime Minister Mateusz Morawiecki's Law and Justice (PiS) government forced the closure of the investigative news site *Gazeta Wyborcza*'s Warsaw bureau after the editor-in-chief published leaked recordings of a PiS MP discussing illegal political financing. The European Commission's 2020 Rule of Law Report documented that between 2015 and 2020, PiS-aligned entities bought 67 percent of local media outlets in Poland, concentrating media ownership in ways the existing Audiovisual Media Services Directive (2010/13/EU) could not address. The Commission's own impact assessment, published in September 2022, found that 14 member states had no binding rules on state advertising transparency, and that in Hungary and Poland, state-advertising spending had shifted entirely to government-friendly outlets between 2017 and 2021.

The trigger for legislative action was a January 2021 letter from 87 European media CEOs — including executives from Reuters, Agence France-Presse, and Italy's La Repubblica — warning European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen that "the business model for independent journalism is collapsing" and that "state capture of media markets" had become a systemic threat. Von der Leyen, who had made rule-of-law enforcement a signature issue, instructed Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton in March 2021 to prepare a legislative proposal. Breton's Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology (DG CONNECT) published a public consultation in June 2021 that received 1,247 responses, with 89 percent of respondents supporting a new regulatory framework.

The coalition that passed the EMFA was built around the European People's Party, the Socialists & Democrats, and Renew Europe in the Parliament, with 464 votes in favour on the final March 2024 plenary vote. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Croatian MEP Romana Jerković (S&D), argued that "without binding rules, the market will continue to reward incumbents who suppress competition." Opposition came from the European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) and Identity and Democracy groups, who argued it violated national sovereignty over media regulation. The Polish government under Donald Tusk, which took office in December 2023, switched its Council position from opposing to supporting the regulation, providing the final votes needed to reach qualified majority in the Council in November 2023.

The specific event that unblocked years of deadlock was the November 2022 ruling by the Court of Justice of the European Union in Case C-218/22 (Commission v. Hungary), which found that Hungary's 2018 media law — which had concentrated all regulatory power in a single body appointed by the prime minister — violated Article 11 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights. The ruling explicitly stated that "the independence of media regulators is not merely a procedural requirement but a structural precondition for the functioning of the internal market." That legal clarity allowed the Commission to base the EMFA on Article 114 TFEU (internal market harmonisation) rather than the more limited legal basis of Article 352 TFEU, which required unanimity in the Council.

## Impact and consequences

The EMFA's most immediate consequence was the restructuring of Hungary's media regulatory system. In June 2024, the Orbán government passed a law creating three separate regulatory bodies — one for press, one for broadcast, and one for online — each required to have a majority of members selected by a judicial council. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against Hungary in September 2024, arguing the changes were still insufficient.

For public service broadcasters, the EMFA required that funding cycles be at least five years, insulating budgets from annual political negotiation. Germany's ZDF, the BBC, and France Télévisions welcomed this, but the Polish government under Tusk struggled to undo PiS-era appointments at Telewizja Polska (TVP) before the August 2025 deadline. In February 2025, the Commission launched a pilot case against Poland for failing to ensure the independence of TVP's supervisory board.

The media concentration assessment requirement has forced several member states to conduct their first-ever systematic reviews of ownership. Slovakia's 2024 assessment found that 78 percent of national daily newspapers were owned by two holding companies linked to political parties — a finding that led to a blocked merger attempt in March 2025.

Companies affected include Meta and Google, which must implement the 24-hour delay before removing recognised media content. As of February 2026, neither company has been fined under the EMFA, but the Commission has received 12 complaints from media outlets whose content was removed without the required delay, with the first formal investigation opened in January 2026 against Meta over the removal of a French investigative website's content.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EMFA regulate state advertising?

The Commission's 2022 impact assessment found that in Hungary, 95% of state advertising spending went to government-friendly outlets between 2017 and 2021, while in Poland the figure was 87%. Without transparency rules, state advertising became a tool for rewarding loyal media and starving independent outlets of revenue, creating an uneven playing field in the internal market.

### Why does the European Media Freedom Act (2024/1083/EU) address content moderation by online platforms?

The 2022 *New York Times* investigation into Facebook's moderation of Ukrainian media during the 2022 Russian invasion — where Facebook removed content from independent Ukrainian outlets citing its policy against "foreign government propaganda" — demonstrated that platform takedowns could silence credible journalistic content. The EMFA's 24-hour delay and media privilege clause create a procedural safeguard, forcing platforms to consult with national media regulators before removal.

### Why create a new European Board for Media Services under the European Media Freedom Act (2024/1083/EU)?

The existing European Regulators Group for Audiovisual Media Services (ERGA) had no binding decision-making powers and could not resolve disputes between national regulators. The EMFA's Board can issue binding opinions when national regulators disagree on cross-border media matters, such as when one member state revokes a broadcaster's licence that another member state has granted. The Board's first such opinion, issued in December 2025, concerned the licence revocation of the Polish broadcaster TVN by Hungarian authorities.

### Why does the EMFA cover public service media funding?

The 2019 European Parliament resolution on media freedom documented cases in Poland and Hungary where governments cut public broadcaster funding by 40-60% within months of taking office, while simultaneously increasing funding for state-controlled outlets. The EMFA requires at least five-year funding cycles to prevent these sudden cuts, though member states retain the right to adjust budgets through annual parliamentary budget votes.
