## What is it?

The AI Liability Directive, proposed by the European Commission on 28 September 2022, creates a targeted harmonisation of national civil liability rules for harms caused by AI systems. It does not set up a new regulatory authority or fund — it changes what a claimant must prove in court when seeking damages for injury, property loss, or data damage involving an AI system.

The directive tackles the "black box" problem: when an AI makes a decision or takes an action that causes harm, its inner workings are often opaque even to the developer. A victim trying to sue faces an impossible task — they must explain exactly what went wrong inside the system. The directive introduces a rebuttable presumption of causality: if the claimant can show that (a) the defendant was at fault (e.g., for failing to comply with a safety requirement under the AI Act), and (b) it is reasonably likely the AI system caused the harm, the court presumes the causal link. The defendant can rebut this presumption by proving someone else caused the harm or that it was unavoidable.

The directive applies only to non-contractual civil law claims for damages — it does not affect contractual liability, nor does it create criminal liability. Claims must arise from AI systems that are "high-risk" as defined in the AI Act, or from systems that were materially modified after being placed on the market.

## Why was it introduced?

The spark for this directive was not a single catastrophic AI accident — it was the accumulating evidence that existing product liability law, written in 1985, was structurally incapable of handling algorithmic harms.

The original Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC) had been designed for physical products. A defective car brake, a faulty pacemaker. But AI systems operate differently: they are probabilistic, not deterministic; they evolve through post-market learning; and their "defect" may be invisible to both developer and user. The European Parliament, in a 2020 resolution, described the problem starkly: "existing liability frameworks are ill-adapted to the specific features of AI systems, in particular their opacity, complexity, autonomy, and data-dependency".

The structural tension was this: the EU wanted to build an "ecosystem of trust" for AI, essential for its broader digital strategy, but that trust would be hollow if citizens could not seek redress for harm. Commissioner for Justice Didier Reynders, who steered the file, said in a 2021 conference that "without clear liability rules, victims would be left without compensation, and innovation would be held back by uncertainty". The Commission's 2020 impact assessment accompanying the AI White Paper had already flagged that 14 Member States had started crafting their own AI liability rules, threatening the single market.

The coalition that got this through the Commission was an unusual one: consumer protection advocates (BEUC, the European Consumer Organisation) and industry groups like DigitalEurope both supported reform, but for opposing reasons. Consumers wanted easier access to justice; industry wanted legal certainty to avoid being sued in 27 different national regimes. The opposition came primarily from tech companies developing the most opaque AI systems — deep learning platforms where explainability tools remain primitive — and from some Member States concerned about gold-plating their existing civil procedure codes.

Timing was critical. The AI Act proposal, published in April 2021, had already defined "high-risk" AI systems. The liability directive was deliberately paired with it — you cannot regulate AI safety without also regulating what happens when safety fails. The Commission's 2022 work programme explicitly linked the two, calling the liability directive "the necessary complement to the AI Act". The push was accelerated by two concrete events: in 2020, the German Federal Ministry of Justice published a study finding that most automated decision-making claims failed precisely because of the proof problem; and in 2021, a European Parliament study counted at least 35 ongoing civil cases across Member States where claimants had abandoned AI-liability suits due to inability to prove causation.

What changed from earlier failed attempts? The 2020 Commission white paper on AI had considered a strict liability regime for all AI systems — which would have made developers liable regardless of fault. Industry vehemently opposed this as a "tax on innovation" that would crush European AI startups. The compromise, announced by Reynders in early 2022, was a fault-based system with a rebuttable presumption — less dramatic than strict liability but far more ambitious than the existing "prove everything" standard. This compromise passed the Commission's internal vote without the split that had blocked earlier digital liability reforms.

## Timeline

- **July 2020** — European Parliament adopts resolution calling for an EU framework for civil liability for AI systems
- **October 2020** — European Parliament adopts further resolution with recommendations for a regulation on civil liability for AI
- **February 2021** — Commission publishes evaluation of the Product Liability Directive, finding it insufficient for AI systems
- **April 2021** — Commission proposes the AI Act, defining high-risk systems; announces parallel work on liability rules
- **2021** — German Federal Ministry of Justice publishes study finding most automated decision-making claims fail on proof of causation
- **September 2022** — Commission adopts the AI Liability Directive proposal (COM/2022/496) alongside a revision of the Product Liability Directive
- **March 2023** — European Parliament's Committee on Legal Affairs (JURI) begins work on opinion
- **November 2023** — European Economic and Social Committee adopts opinion supporting the directive with amendments
- **July 2024** — AI Act enters into force; AI Liability Directive still under negotiation
- **Ongoing** — Trilogue negotiations

## Impact and consequences

The proposal's main practical impact, even before adoption, has been on the design of AI compliance programmes. The Centre for Information Policy Leadership (CIPL), an industry group backed by Microsoft, Google, and Meta, warned in January 2023 that the rebuttable presumption "could lead to a flood of frivolous claims" and that firms would need to invest substantially in explainability documentation to rebut it. The Commission's own impact assessment estimated the compliance cost for companies at €4,000–€15,000 per AI system for audit trail documentation — a figure industry groups call "significantly underestimated".

Consumer groups welcomed the shift. BEUC, the European Consumer Organisation, noted in its February 2023 response that "for the first time, victims of AI harm will not have to reverse-engineer a black box to get justice". The main opposition in the Parliament came from the EPP group, which proposed amendments in June 2023 narrowing the definition of "high-risk" systems and introducing a cap on damages — neither of which was in the Commission proposal.

The proposal has already had extraterritorial effects. The UK's Law Commission, in a March 2023 consultation paper on AI liability, explicitly cited the EU directive as a benchmark "worth watching". Japan's Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry, in an April 2023 report on AI governance, similarly acknowledged the EU approach.

Five provisions remain highly contested as of early 2025: (1) whether the defence of "state of the art" — a developer cannot be liable for risks unknown at the time — should apply to AI where post-market learning can discover new risks; (2) whether small companies should have a reduced standard of proof (the Commission said no, but the Parliament's JURI committee proposed a rebuttable presumption in favour of SMEs); (3) how to handle "distributed harm" where an AI system harms many individuals with small losses each (class actions are not uniformly available across Member States); (4) whether claims for pure data loss (e.g., corrupted data) should be covered; and (5) the interplay with GDPR's fault-based liability for data processing.

## Key questions answered

### Can someone sue an AI company for damages in the EU?

Yes, but only if the AI system is "high-risk" under the AI Act or was modified after deployment. The victim must show the developer or deployer violated a safety requirement and that this violation likely caused the harm; the court then presumes causality, which the defendant can rebut.

### What counts as "harm" from an AI system that can lead to a lawsuit?

The directive covers death, personal injury, damage to property, and loss or corruption of data. It excludes pure economic loss and privacy violations (which remain under GDPR). The harm must be caused by a "high-risk" AI system or one materially modified post-market.

### Do AI developers have to keep records to defend themselves in court?

Yes, but indirectly. The directive does not impose new documentation obligations in itself — those are in the AI Act. However, a developer that cannot produce audit trail documentation (required under the AI Act for high-risk systems) will find it very difficult to rebut the presumption of causality in court.

### Does this affect developers of simple chatbots or spreadsheet-based decision tools?

Only if those systems are classified as "high-risk" under the AI Act. The current version of the AI Act defines high-risk to include AI used in products subject to EU safety legislation (medical devices, cars, lifts) and in specific use cases like critical infrastructure, law enforcement, migration, and employment. A simple chatbot for customer complaints would not be covered.
