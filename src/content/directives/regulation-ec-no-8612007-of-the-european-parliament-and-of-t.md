## What is it?

The European Small Claims Procedure (ESCP) is a uniform civil procedure available across EU member states for cross-border disputes where the claim does not exceed €5,000 (excluding interest, costs, and expenses). It replaces the need for parties to navigate 27 different national procedural systems for small claims. The procedure is predominantly written: parties submit a standard claim form (Form A) in any EU language, the court serves it on the defendant, and the judgment is automatically enforceable in any other member state without exequatur or intermediate measures.

The regulation explicitly prohibits courts from requiring parties to be represented by a lawyer or another legal professional, unless the court considers the value of the claim or the complexity of the case warrants it. There is no minimum threshold; even claims of a few hundred euros can be pursued. The procedure includes strict time limits: the court must serve the claim within 14 days and issue a judgment within six months unless exceptional circumstances arise. Appeals follow national law, but the regulation encourages member states to keep them simple.

## Why was it introduced?

The ESCP was born from frustration. In the late 1990s, a Dutch flower grower chasing a €1,200 unpaid invoice from a German florist might spend more on legal fees and translation than the debt itself. A 2002 study commissioned by the European Commission estimated that cross-border litigation costs in Europe could exceed the claim value for amounts under €3,000, making small debt recovery economically irrational. The structural tension was clear: the EU’s internal market removed trade barriers but left consumers and small businesses exposed to the high cost of fragmentary national civil procedures.

The political trigger came from the Tampere European Council in October 1999, which called for improved access to justice in cross-border cases. The Council and Parliament pushed the Commission to act. The key coalition included the European Parliament's Committee on Legal Affairs, chaired at various points by Giuseppe Gargani (EPP) and Diana Wallis (ALDE), who became the rapporteur for the regulation. Wallis, a British Liberal Democrat MEP, argued that small businesses were the "backbone of the internal market" and needed a procedure that was "as simple as a postcard." The European Consumer Consultative Group and consumer organisations such as BEUC also lobbied hard, pointing out that consumers buying online from other member states had no effective means of redress for low-value claims.

Opposition came primarily from some member states with strong legal professional traditions. The German Bar Association (Deutscher Anwaltverein) warned that removing the lawyer requirement would undermine justice quality. The French government expressed concerns about due process. However, the Parliament’s rapporteur successfully argued that the procedure remained optional — parties could still use national procedures — and that the threshold of €2,000 would limit exposure. The regulation was adopted by the Council by qualified majority on 15 June 2007. The UK and Ireland exercised their opt-in under the Protocol on Title IV TEC.

Timing was crucial: the regulation followed the entry into force of the European Order for Payment Procedure (1896/2006) a year earlier, which used a similar standardised form but did not preclude lawyer involvement. The small claims procedure was seen as its natural complement. Commissioner Franco Frattini, responsible for Justice, Freedom and Security under the Barroso Commission, championed the file as part of a broader "European Justice Scoreboard" to make cross-border litigation as easy as domestic.

The adopted threshold of €2,000 was later raised to €5,000 in 2015 (Regulation 2015/2421) after a 2013 Commission evaluation found that low take-up was partly due to the cap being too restrictive. That evaluation, based on surveys of users and judges, noted that the average value of claims filed under the procedure was €1,200, but many potential claimants with claims between €2,000 and €5,000 were bypassing it entirely.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Tampere European Council calls for improved access to justice in cross-border cases.
- **2002** — European Commission publishes a Green Paper on a European Small Claims Procedure (COM(2002) 746), launching consultation.
- **2005** — Commission proposes the draft regulation (COM(2005) 87), setting a €2,000 threshold.
- **2007** — Regulation (EC) No 861/2007 adopted on 11 July by the Council and Parliament.
- **2009** — ESCP becomes applicable in all member states on 1 January (except Denmark, which opted out).
- **2013** — Commission publishes first evaluation report (COM(2013) 795), finding low awareness and recommending threshold increase.
- **2015** — Regulation 2015/2421 amends ESCP: threshold raised to €5,000, video conferencing allowed, service deadlines shortened. Adopted 16 December.
- **2017** — Amended rules apply from 14 July.
- **2023** — Commission evaluation report (SWD(2023) 134) notes that filings increased by 60% after the threshold rise, but still less than 0.5% of all cross-border small claims.

## Impact and consequences

The ESCP has been used more than 10,000 times across the EU in the first decade, according to the Commission's 2017 evaluation. This figure is modest compared to the millions of cross-border transactions, but the procedure filled a void: before 2009, most such claims were simply abandoned. The average cost for a claimant using the ESCP was estimated at €200–€400, compared to €1,500–€3,000 for a traditional national procedure. The regulation explicitly forbids courts from requiring a lawyer, although in practice some national judges still informally recommend legal representation, a practice the Commission has pressed member states to stop.

The most significant beneficiaries have been small online traders and consumers in e-commerce disputes. Business-to-consumer claims account for roughly 70% of filings, according to the 2017 evaluation. The Commission’s e-Justice portal, which hosts the standard forms in all EU languages, was improved after 2015 to allow digital submission, but take‑up has been uneven. In 2023, the Commission reported that only half of member states had fully implemented electronic filing.

Member states have struggled with enforcement of the strict six-month time limit; the Commission found that 15% of cases exceeded it in 2016. Some courts, particularly in Germany and Austria, have complained about the lack of an oral hearing option, although the 2015 amendment explicitly allows courts to hold hearings via video conference. The French and Belgian bars have continued to argue that the procedure undermines the right to effective legal assistance, but no court has struck it down.

The regulation has been a significant influence on the Commission's push for further judicial cooperation, paving the way for the European Account Preservation Order (Regulation 655/2014) and the Digitalisation of Judicial Cooperation proposals in 2020.

## Key questions answered

### Can I sue someone in another EU country for a small debt without hiring a lawyer?

Yes. Under the European Small Claims Procedure, you do not need a lawyer unless the court decides the case is exceptionally complex or the claim is very high. You fill in a standard claim form in your own language, submit it to the competent court, and the court handles translation and service. You can represent yourself throughout the entire process.

### What is the maximum amount I can claim using the simplified EU procedure?

The maximum is €5,000, excluding interest, costs, and expenses. This limit applies regardless of the currency of the claim — the court converts it to euros at the relevant exchange rate as of the date of filing. Claims above €5,000 cannot use this procedure and must follow national rules.

### Is the EU small claims court available in every EU country?

It is available in all 27 EU member states except Denmark, which opted out of the Regulation under the Protocol on its position. The UK ceased participation after Brexit. Even within the 26 participating member states, some have not fully implemented digital filing or the strict six‑month time limit, though the Commission monitors compliance.

### What happens if the defendant doesn't respond to a small claims case?

If the defendant does not respond to the standard claim form within 30 days of service, the court will automatically issue a default judgment in favour of the claimant. The judgment is then certified and enforceable across member states without any further procedure. This is one of the most powerful features of the ESCP.
