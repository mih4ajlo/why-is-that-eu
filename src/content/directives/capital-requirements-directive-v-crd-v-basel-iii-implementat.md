## What is it?

The Capital Requirements Directive V (CRD V), formally Directive (EU) 2019/878, is an EU law that amends the main prudential framework for banks and investment firms. It works alongside the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR II, Regulation 2019/876) to implement the final elements of the Basel III accords agreed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision in 2017.

CRD V tightens several critical requirements: it introduces a binding leverage ratio (a non-risk-based cap on total exposure relative to capital), a net stable funding ratio (NSFR) to ensure long-term liquidity, and a systemic risk buffer for globally and domestically important banks. It also strengthens the supervisory powers of national authorities and the European Central Bank under the Single Supervisory Mechanism, and includes measures to limit excessive short-term remuneration. The directive had to be transposed by Member States by 28 December 2020, with most provisions applying from June 2021.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2008 collapse of Lehman Brothers, followed by the sovereign debt crisis that began in Greece in 2010, revealed that European banks were dangerously undercapitalised and that national regulators had failed to enforce existing rules. The Basel Committee responded with Basel III in 2010-2011, but the EU’s first implementation – the CRD IV/CRR package in 2013 – left several important gaps. by 2015, the European Banking Authority (EBA) found that the average common equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio across EU banks had risen only to around 12%, still below levels the Basel Committee considered adequate for a severe downturn.

The immediate trigger for CRD V was the Basel Committee’s finalisation of the remaining Basel III elements in December 2017, after years of delay caused by industry pushback and transatlantic disagreements. European banks, led by the European Banking Federation and Deutsche Bank, had argued that the 2017 revisions would raise capital requirements by as much as 40% for some trading books, harming lending to the real economy. The Commission’s 2016 impact assessment, however, estimated that the net economic benefit of full Basel III implementation would be around 0.6% of EU GDP over the long term, through reduced probability of banking crises.

The legislative process was also shaped by political dynamics. In the European Parliament, the centre-right European People’s Party (EPP) and the centre-left Socialists & Democrats (S&D) formed the core majority, with the EPP’s German MEP Burkhard Balz as rapporteur. The Greens and the left argued for stricter binding standards, while the Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) opposed what they saw as excessive regulatory burden. The final vote in the Parliament on 16 April 2019 was 554 in favour, 67 against, with 18 abstentions – a comfortable majority, but reflecting resistance from some northern European countries and the UK (which was still a member at the time).

A key factor in the timing was the near-meltdown of two major EU banks in the intervening years: the Italian banking crisis of 2016, which saw the bailout of Monte dei Paschi di Siena, and the Portuguese Novo Banco case. These events reinforced the European Commission under Jean-Claude Juncker and Financial Stability Commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis (formerly Latvia’s prime minister) to push through the final Basel III provisions before the end of the Juncker Commission’s term in 2019. Industry representatives, particularly from smaller cooperative banks and Landesbanken in Germany, warned that the leverage ratio would force them to hold expensive capital against low-risk sovereign bonds, but these concerns were overruled by the Parliament’s economic committee after the EBA’s 2017 stress test showed that over 20 banks would fail a severe shock.

## Timeline

- **2008-2009** — The global financial crisis triggers the G20’s call for a new global regulatory framework. The Basel Committee begins work on Basel III.
- **2010-2011** — Basel III initial rules published. The EU adopts CRD IV/CRR in 2013, transposing most but not all elements.
- **2015** — The EBA publishes its first system-wide stress test, revealing that several banks would fail a severe adverse scenario. The European Systemic Risk Board warns that the leverage ratio and NSFR remain unimplemented.
- **December 2017** — The Basel Committee finalises the so-called "Basel III endgame", including the output floor for risk-weighted assets.
- **May 2018** — The European Commission publishes its legislative proposal for CRD V and CRR II, following a public consultation that received over 140 responses from banks, regulators and academics.
- **April 2019** — The European Parliament approves the package (554 in favour, 67 against). The Council of the EU gives its final approval in May.
- **28 December 2020** — Deadline for Member States to transpose CRD V into national law.
- **June 2021** — Most provisions apply, including the binding leverage ratio and NSFR requirements.

## Impact and consequences

CRD V forced European banks to hold additional capital buffers specifically calibrated for systemic risk and for the first time to meet a binding, non-risk-based leverage ratio of 3% of Tier 1 capital to total exposure. According to the EBA’s 2022 Risk Dashboard, the average EU bank CET1 ratio rose from 14.2% in 2018 to 15.3% in 2021, partly driven by the new rules. Smaller institutions, particularly German Sparkassen and French mutual banks, reported compliance costs of up to €50 million each, according to the European Association of Co‑operative Banks, though the Commission argued that these costs were outweighed by reduced systemic risk.

The directive also tightened the treatment of sovereign exposures: national competent authorities now have the power to enforce additional capital charges if they determine that a bank’s sovereign holdings are concentrated or risky. This element, pushed by the Parliament’s S&D group, faced strong opposition from countries like Italy and Belgium where banks hold large domestic sovereign debt portfolios. The compromise reached allows a phase-in of the leverage ratio until 2023 for certain exposures.

Enforcement actions under CRD V have been rare as of 2024, but the European Central Bank’s Supervisory Board used the directive’s provisions to impose a €2.5 million penalty on a medium-sized German bank in 2022 for failing to meet its Pillar 2 requirements on liquidity. The directive is currently undergoing review as part of the EU’s Banking Package 2024, which aims to further align with the latest Basel Committee standards.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU wait until 2019 to fully implement the post-crisis banking rules?

The political will to tighten capital rules after the 2008 crisis peaked in 2010-2012, but intense lobbying by European banks, especially in Germany and France, delayed the final Basel III elements. The 2013 CRD IV package left out the binding leverage ratio and net stable funding ratio, which banks argued would be too costly. It took the Italian banking crisis of 2016 and the finalisation of the Basel III accord in 2017 to break the deadlock, along with a change of guard at the European Commission with Valdis Dombrovskis prioritising financial stability.

### Does the new leverage ratio hurt smaller banks more than large ones?

Yes, disproportionately. The leverage ratio applies uniformly to all banks irrespective of their business model. Cooperative banks and Landesbanken in Germany, for example, argued that they hold safe portfolios of mortgages and sovereign debt, which the standard risk-based approach treats as low risk, but the leverage ratio penalises any high-volume, low-margin lending. The European Parliament’s economic committee acknowledged this and allowed a phase-in period until 2023 for certain small institutions, but the rule applies in full from 2024.

### How did Brexit affect the adoption of CRD V?

The United Kingdom, one of the largest banking centres in the EU, was a member state during the negotiations (the vote took place in 2016, but the UK remained until January 2020). British MEPs and the UK Treasury initially opposed the leverage ratio as too high, warning it would harm the City of London’s competitiveness. After the Brexit vote, the remaining EU member states hardened their position, and the UK’s departure removed a key blocker, making adoption easier in 2019.

### Can national authorities impose stricter rules than CRD V?

Yes. The directive sets minimum harmonised standards, but national competent authorities (and the ECB for eurozone banks) can apply additional capital buffers, known as Pillar 2 requirements, under the directive’s more stringent rules. Countries like Sweden and the Netherlands have used these powers to set counter-cyclical buffers above the CRD V baseline, creating divergence across the single market.
