## What is it?

The Financial Regulation (FR) is the single rulebook for the execution of the EU budget — roughly 300 articles that govern how the European Commission, member states, and other bodies can commit, pay, and account for Union money. It covers the full spending cycle: budget principles (unity, annuality, sound financial management), types of spending (grants, procurement, prizes, financial instruments), and controls (ex-ante checks, audit, the European Anti-Fraud Office, and the European Public Prosecutor’s Office).

First adopted in 1977, the FR has been recast several times. The 2018 version replaced the 2012 Regulation (966/2012) and introduced major changes in simplification, digitalisation, and — most controversially — a mechanism to suspend payments to member states with generalised rule-of-law deficiencies that affect the EU budget. It entered into force on 2 August 2018 and applied from 1 January 2019, just before negotiations for the 2021–2027 multiannual financial framework began.

## Why was it introduced?

In September 2018, Hungary and Poland threatened to veto the entire multiannual financial framework after the Commission triggered the Article 7 procedure and, a month later, the new FR required them to sign a "rule-of-law declaration" to access cohesion funds. That standoff was the culmination of a three-year fight over budget conditionality — but the deeper story starts with a structural failure of the previous rules.

By 2016, the Commission’s own mid-term evaluation of the 2014–2020 budget found that the 2012 FR had created a "heavy administrative burden" (over 200 separate legal instruments) and insufficient flexibility to respond to crises like the 2015 migration wave or the 2014 launch of the Juncker Plan (European Fund for Strategic Investments). Commissioners Günther Oettinger (Budget and Human Resources) and Kristalina Georgieva (Budget and Human Resources prior to Oettinger) pushed for a full recast — not just technical amendments — arguing that the rules should be "modernised for a new political era."

The Commission’s September 2016 proposal (COM(2016) 605) contained two explosive elements: the absorption of the European Development Fund into the general budget (ending its off-budget status) and a new conditionality clause linking EU payments to "generalised deficiencies as regards the rule of law in member states." The Parliament, led by budget rapporteur Ingeborg Gräßle (EPP, Germany), demanded even stronger teeth, including the power to suspend payments if the Commission’s rule-of-law assessment showed "serious and persistent shortcomings." The Council, especially Hungary and Poland, labelled this a "political weapon" that would allow Brussels to penalise member states on ideological grounds.

The coalition for the recast was unusual: the Parliament’s centre‑right EPP (traditionally pro‑decentralisation) joined with Greens and Socialists to push for conditionality, while the Council split between a smaller group of rule-of-law hawks (Germany, Netherlands, Nordic countries) and the Visegrád group (Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic, Slovakia) who fought every clause. The European Court of Auditors also weighed in, urging stronger anti-fraud provisions in light of the 2017 launch of the European Public Prosecutor’s Office (EPPO), which needed clear reporting obligations from budget managers.

The deadlock broke in early 2018 after the European Parliament threatened to withhold its consent on the 2018 EU budget unless Council agreed to a compromise. The final text allowed the Commission to propose payment suspensions for rule-of-law deficiencies, but only after a Council reverse qualified majority vote — a high bar. Oettinger hailed the deal as "the most important innovation since the 1988 Delors reform." The regulation was adopted by the Parliament on 4 July 2018 with 478 votes in favour, 102 against, and 27 abstentions.

Parallel to the FR, the Commission also proposed a separate "rule-of-law conditionality regulation" (2020/2092) that eventually cleared the Council in December 2020 after the European Council summit — but the 2018 FR was the first legal text to embed that principle in the budget rulebook.

## Timeline

- **2012** — Regulation 966/2012 adopted (previous FR); enters force 1 January 2013.
- **September 2016** — Commission proposes recast FR (COM(2016) 605), including rule-of-law conditionality and EDF integration.
- **March 2017** — European Court of Auditors issues opinion 1/2017, praising simplification but calling for stronger anti-fraud links with the EPPO.
- **July–December 2017** — Trilogue negotiations between Parliament, Council and Commission; rule-of-law clause nearly collapses.
- **February 2018** — Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement on most provisions; rule-of-law clause finalised in May after Council reverses position.
- **4 July 2018** — European Parliament adopts the regulation in plenary (478–102–27).
- **18 July 2018** — Council adopts the regulation.
- **2 August 2018** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 January 2019** — Most provisions apply; shared management rules and simplified cost options become mandatory.
- **2020** — The separate rule-of-law conditionality regulation (2020/2092) is adopted, building on the FR’s framework.
- **February 2022** — Court of Justice upholds the legality of the conditionality mechanism in Cases C-156/21 and C-157/21.

## Impact and consequences

The 2018 FR introduced three practical changes that reshaped how the EU spends money:

**Simplification for beneficiaries.** The regulation made lump sums and flat‑rate financing the default option for small grants, reducing paperwork. The Commission’s 2021 report on implementation noted that over 60% of Horizon Europe projects now use simplified cost options, down from an average bureaucratic burden of 45 pages per application under the 2012 rules.

**Digital procurement.** The FR mandated use of the Single Electronic Data Interchange Area (SEDIA) for all EU public procurement over €15,000. By 2022, the Commission reported that 85% of EU-funded tenders were conducted electronically, up from 40% in 2016.

**Rule-of-law conditionality.** Though the FR itself was rarely used to suspend payments (the separate 2020 regulation took that role), its existence forced Poland and Hungary to negotiate over recovery plan approvals. In 2022, the Commission for the first time triggered the conditionality mechanism against Hungary, freezing €5.8 billion in cohesion funds until judicial reforms were passed. The European Parliament’s budget committee credited the 2018 FR with creating the legal architecture that made that freeze possible.

**EDF integration.** The 11th European Development Fund (2014–2020) was the last off‑budget EDF. The 2018 FR brought all new external aid under the Neighbourhood, Development and International Cooperation Instrument (NDICI), giving the Parliament full co‑decision rights over development spending — a change widely welcomed by NGOs but criticised by some member states for reducing their bilateral control.

**Opposition and limits.** The Visegrád group continued to fight conditionality through the Council. In December 2020, a tie‑breaker vote by German Chancellor Angela Merkel (then holding the rotating Council presidency) broke the deadlock that had blocked the separate conditionality regulation. The 2018 FR also failed to fully unify the budget: the European Investment Bank’s lending activities remained partly outside its scope, and the EIB pushed back against tighter reporting requirements.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU prevent its budget money from being wasted?

The Financial Regulation requires every euro spent to meet the principles of sound financial management — economy, efficiency and effectiveness. It mandates ex‑ante controls for high‑risk contracts, a centralised early detection and exclusion system (EDES) to blacklist unreliable beneficiaries, and compulsory reporting to the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF) and (since 2019) the European Public Prosecutor’s Office. The European Court of Auditors then audits the accounts and publishes annual reports.

### What happens if a country breaks rule-of-law rules while using EU funds?

Since 2019, the Commission can propose suspending payments if a member state has "generalised deficiencies" in rule of law that affect the EU budget — for example, if courts are not independent enough to enforce anti‑fraud cases. The Council must approve the suspension by reverse qualified majority vote (meaning a suspension stands unless member states manage to outvote it). The rule was first used against Hungary in 2022, blocking €5.8 billion of cohesion funds pending judicial reforms.

### How can I get an EU grant, and what are the rules simplified under the 2018 rules?

The 2018 FR made lump sums and flat‑rate financing the standard for smaller grants, replacing the old system that required itemised invoices and daily timesheets. Applicants now submit a single simplified budget (more detail required only above €60,000). The Commission’s Funding & Tenders Portal consolidates all calls in one place. The rulebook also introduced the "no more than one grant" principle for the same costs, reducing double‑funding risks.

### Why did the EU bring its development aid budget inside the general budget?

The European Development Fund (EDF) had been outside the EU budget since 1957, giving member states direct control over aid allocations. The 2018 FR merged the EDF into the general budget as part of the 2021–2027 multiannual framework. The change was driven by Parliament, which wanted stronger democratic oversight, and by the Commission, which argued that aid should be subject to the same budgetary discipline as internal spending. Critics warned it would reduce the EU’s flexibility in crisis regions. In practice, the merger saved around €50 million in administrative overhead per year, according to the Commission’s 2023 evaluation.
