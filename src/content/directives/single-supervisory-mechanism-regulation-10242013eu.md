## What is it?

The Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM) Regulation, formally adopted in October 2013, gives the European Central Bank (ECB) direct supervisory authority over all "significant" banks in the eurozone — roughly 120 of the region's largest lenders, accounting for approximately 82% of total eurozone banking assets according to the ECB's 2023 annual report. It also establishes a framework for the ECB to oversee national supervisors' handling of smaller banks.

Under the SSM, the ECB conducts stress tests, grants or withdraws banking licences, assesses acquisitions of significant holdings, and enforces capital requirements. It operates through Joint Supervisory Teams (JSTs) that combine ECB and national authority staff for each significant bank. The regulation created the first truly pan-European banking supervisor, transferring powers that had been exclusively national for over a century.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the sovereign debt crisis of 2010-2012, but the deeper problem was structural: the eurozone had a single currency without a single banking supervisor. When the financial crisis hit in 2008, national supervisors had both the incentive and the ability to hide the true state of their banks' balance sheets. Ireland's blanket guarantee of its six biggest banks in September 2008 — worth €485 billion, roughly twice the country's GDP — was approved without any cross-border coordination. When losses materialised, Ireland's sovereign debt was contaminated, requiring an €85 billion EU-IMF bailout in November 2010.

The specific event that broke the political deadlock was the collapse of the Spanish banking system in 2012. Bankia, Spain's fourth-largest lender, was nationalised in May 2012 after revealing a €4.3 billion loss — but Spanish supervisors had certified its accounts as sound just months earlier. When Spain requested up to €100 billion in EU bailout funds for its banks in June 2012, European leaders realised the existing system of "national supervision, European resolution" had failed. The formal decision came at the Eurozone summit of 29 June 2012, where leaders committed to "breaking the vicious circle between banks and sovereigns" by creating a single supervisory mechanism.

The political coalition driving the SSM was unusual. The European Commission under José Manuel Barroso, particularly Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier, pushed the proposal hard. They were joined by the ECB under Mario Draghi, who had signalled in July 2012 that the ECB would do "whatever it takes" to save the euro but needed a proper banking union. Germany initially resisted, with Finance Minister Wolfgang Schäuble arguing that supervision should remain national. However, German Chancellor Angela Merkel shifted position after Spanish banks collapsed, fearing contagion to Italy and France. The European Parliament's ECON committee, led by rapporteur Marianne Thyssen, supported the proposal but insisted on democratic accountability and a separation of monetary policy from supervision — leading to the creation of the ECB's independent Supervisory Board.

The opposition came from several directions. The UK, outside the eurozone, threatened legal action unless it received guarantees that non-eurozone countries would not be disadvantaged — leading to a 2013 Council decision specifying "close cooperation" arrangements for non-euro EU members. Nordic countries like Sweden and Denmark, outside the euro, also expressed scepticism. Within the Parliament, liberal MEPs led by Sharon Bowles worried about the ECB's concentration of power, while left-wing groups feared austerity being baked into supervision. The final vote in the European Parliament on 12 September 2013 was 463 in favour, 145 against, with 18 abstentions — a comfortable majority but with significant opposition.

The timing succeeded because the Spanish banking crisis created a window where national sovereignty over banking was seen as the problem, not the solution. Previous attempts at harmonising supervision — like the 2010 European Banking Authority regulation — had kept ultimate authority with national supervisors. The SSM's "direct supervision" model was a leap designed explicitly to prevent national supervisors from hiding bad loans or favouring "national champions." The alternative considered in Commission impact assessments was a "network model" strengthening national supervisors, but the crisis had discredited this approach.

## Timeline

- **November 2008** — European Commission launches the de Larosière report, leading to the creation of the European Banking Authority in 2010, but with no direct supervisory powers
- **29 June 2012** — Eurozone summit commits to "breaking the vicious circle between banks and sovereigns" and creating a single supervisory mechanism
- **12 September 2012** — Commission President Barroso presents the SSM legislative package in his State of the Union address
- **March 2013** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement after controversially separating the SSM from the proposed Single Resolution Mechanism
- **12 September 2013** — European Parliament votes 463-145 in favour
- **15 October 2013** — Council formally adopts Regulation 1024/2013
- **3 November 2013** — SSM Regulation enters into force
- **4 November 2014** — ECB assumes direct supervisory responsibility for 120 significant banks, following a comprehensive assessment of 130 banks' balance sheets
- **2016** — ECB first uses direct powers to revoke a banking licence (Latvijas Krājbanka) after money-laundering concerns
- **2017** — ECB imposes €40 million in fines on three banks for breaching leverage ratio requirements
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic sees ECB relax capital requirements for all SSM-supervised banks, demonstrating flexibility
- **2023** — ECB's 2023 annual report shows 112 significant banks directly supervised, with total assets of €23.7 trillion

## Impact and consequences

The SSM fundamentally changed European banking. The comprehensive assessment preceding the 2014 handover found combined capital shortfalls of €25 billion across 25 of 130 banks tested — information national supervisors had not disclosed. Three Italian banks (Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena, Banca Carige, and Veneto Banca) were forced to raise capital publicly.

The shift of power from national supervisors to the ECB was dramatic. National supervisors lost their ability to go easy on "their" banks; the ECB's Supervisory Board, composed of representatives from all eurozone countries plus four ECB appointees, makes decisions by majority vote. This has led to public conflicts, notably when German supervisor Sabine Lautenschläger resigned in 2019, criticising the ECB's approach to non-performing loans.

Industry initially resisted the extra layer of bureaucracy. The German Banking Association (BdB) warned in 2013 that SSM would "create new risks" by over-centralising supervision. By 2018, however, a survey by the European Banking Federation found 78% of SSM-supervised banks accepted the system as a permanent feature, though many complained about the "gold-plating" of international standards.

The SSM's biggest test came in 2022-2023 with the collapse of Credit Suisse and Silicon Valley Bank. The ECB's stress tests found eurozone banks were less exposed than feared. However, critics argued the SSM had failed to prevent problems at Deutsche Bank, which faced repeated money-laundering scandals and was fined €16 million by the ECB in 2020 for risk-management failures.

Non-eurozone countries have been slow to join. Only Bulgaria and Croatia have entered "close cooperation" (Croatia joining the euro in 2023); others like Denmark and Sweden have stayed out. The SSM contributed to a perception of a two-tier EU where eurozone countries are supervised by Frankfurt while others remain under national control.

## Key questions answered

### Does the ECB now directly manage all banks in Europe?

No. The ECB directly supervises only "significant" banks in the eurozone — those with assets over €30 billion, or representing 20% of their home country's GDP, or that have requested or received public bailout funds. As of 2023, this covered 112 banks. Smaller banks remain under national supervisors, though the ECB oversees the system. Banks outside the eurozone are not covered, though non-euro countries can opt into "close cooperation."

### Why did the financial crisis make a single banking supervisor necessary?

Before the SSM, national supervisors hid their banks' problems for fear of triggering a sovereign debt crisis. When Ireland guaranteed its banks in 2008, the losses became the state's problem because no European authority could force the banks to recognise bad loans early. The SSM breaks this link by making a European institution responsible, meaning banks cannot hide losses from their home country and then expect the country's taxpayers to bail them out.

### Can the ECB force a bank out of business?

The ECB can withdraw a bank's licence — the ultimate sanction — which effectively forces it to close. It has done so in several cases, including Latvia's Latvijas Krājbanka in 2016 for money laundering. However, actual bank resolution (deciding whether a bank is wound down or sold) is handled by a separate body, the Single Resolution Board, under the Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (2014/59/EU). The SSM identifies the problem; the SRB decides the outcome.

### Are non-eurozone EU countries forced to participate?

No. Non-eurozone EU countries can choose to enter "close cooperation" with the SSM, meaning their banks become subject to ECB supervision. Only Bulgaria (which joined before adopting the euro) and Croatia (which joined before the euro in 2023) have done so. Countries like Denmark and Sweden have deliberately stayed out despite being in the EU, arguing that their national supervisory systems are sufficient and that ceding control over their largest banks to Frankfurt would reduce democratic accountability.
