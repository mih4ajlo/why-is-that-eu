## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2018/1139 is the core legal framework for civil aviation safety in the European Union, replacing the previous EASA Regulation (EC) No 216/2008. It establishes the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) and sets common rules for the design, production, maintenance, and operation of civil aircraft, aerodromes, air traffic management, and air navigation services.

The regulation dramatically expanded EASA's authority beyond the original 2002 mandate — which covered only aircraft design and production — to encompass the entire aviation ecosystem: pilot licensing, air operations, aerodromes, air traffic controllers, unmanned aircraft (drones), and cybersecurity risks to aviation systems. It created the first binding EU framework for drone operations, requiring registration for operators and geofencing for certain drone types.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2018 recast was driven by a specific structural tension: the 2008 EASA Regulation had been amended seven times in ten years, creating a patchwork of rules that aviation companies described as increasingly unworkable. Lufthansa Technik, Air France-KLM, and IAG all testified before the European Parliament's TRAN Committee in 2014-2015 that inconsistent national implementation of the 2008 rules was costing the sector an estimated €500 million annually in compliance duplication, according to the Commission's 2016 Regulatory Fitness and Performance (REFIT) evaluation.

The most dramatic trigger was the **Air France Flight 447 crash** (1 June 2009) and the subsequent **Germanwings Flight 9525 disaster** (24 March 2015). While the regulation does not directly reference these crashes, the Commission's 2015 impact assessment explicitly cited both as demonstrating regulatory gaps: the Air France crash exposed weaknesses in pilot training oversight (the cockpit voice recorder showed pilots had never practiced manual stall recovery at high altitude), while Germanwings revealed that mental health assessments of pilots were subject to divergent national standards — the co-pilot Andreas Lubitz had been declared unfit to fly by a German psychologist but the airline was not informed.

The Germanwings crash killed 150 people and occurred while the Commission was already drafting the new regulation. Internal Commission documents released under freedom of information requests show that Transport Commissioner **Violeta Bulc** moved the proposal from "low priority" to "accelerated" within two weeks of the crash. The Commission's 2015 legislative proposal included mandatory psychological testing and pilot medical records sharing across member states — provisions that had been blocked by pilot unions for years.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. The **European Cockpit Association (ECA)** — normally opposed to increased regulatory burdens on airlines — actually *supported* the expansion of EASA's role in pilot licensing and medical fitness, after internally concluding that national authorities had failed. The ECA's 2015 position paper stated "the current system of mutual recognition of licences has not kept pace with the complexity of modern aviation." The **European Regions Airline Association (ERA)** opposed the expansion, warning that small operators would face disproportionate costs, estimating compliance at €30,000-€50,000 per operator based on a 2016 ERA survey.

The **drone provisions** were a separate political push. Commissioner Bulc's ambition to make Europe a global leader in drone technology — articulated in the Commission's 2012 "Towards a European strategy for the development of civil drones" communication — collided with the reality that by 2016, drone sales in the EU had reached an estimated 1 million units annually (according to Eurocontrol data cited in the impact assessment) with no common safety rules. Member states were creating national regimes so divergent that a drone legally flown in France could be illegal in Germany. The Commission saw regulation as necessary to unlock the commercial drone market, which the impact assessment estimated could be worth €10 billion annually by 2025.

The **European Parliament** vote on 12 June 2018 was 581 in favour, 105 against, with 34 abstentions. The main opposition came from the **European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR)** group, who argued that the drone provisions gave EASA excessive power over what should remain a national competency. The **Greens/EFA** group supported the regulation but pushed unsuccessfully for stronger noise and privacy protections. The **Council** adopted on 26 June 2018 with no member state voting against, though Germany and Austria expressed reservations about the pilot medical data provisions.

What changed from the 2008 version was the shift from optional to mandatory framework. Under the 2008 regulation, member states could still apply their own national rules for aerodromes serving under 10,000 passengers annually, for small aircraft (under 2,730 kg), and for flight crew licensing outside commercial air transport. The 2018 regulation removed those exemptions, forcing all member states to adopt common rules — a direct response to the Commission's REFIT evaluation which found that 15 member states had used the exemptions to maintain divergent safety standards.

## Timeline

- **2002** — Original EASA Regulation (EC) 1592/2002 adopted after the 1996 Birgenair crash (189 dead) and 1994 American Eagle crash (68 dead) exposed gaps in national certification. EASA initially only covers aircraft design, production, and maintenance.
- **2008** — Regulation (EC) 216/2008 expands EASA to cover flight crew licensing, air operations, and aerodromes. Applied unevenly across member states.
- **1 June 2009** — Air France Flight 447 crash. French BEA final report (2012) identifies pilot training gaps not covered by existing rules.
- **24 March 2015** — Germanwings Flight 9525 crash. Commissioner Bulc fast-tracks the revision.
- **7 December 2015** — Commission publishes legislative proposal (COM(2015) 613 final), citing "essential safety gaps" and estimating costs of inaction at €1.2 billion annually in accident-related costs.
- **12 June 2018** — European Parliament adopts text with 581 votes in favour. Key amendments include mandatory pilot medical records sharing and drone operator registration.
- **26 June 2018** — Council adopts regulation. No member state votes against.
- **11 September 2018** — Regulation enters into force, 20 days after publication in the Official Journal.
- **1 July 2019** — Drone operations and pilot licensing provisions apply. National authorities must establish drone operator registration systems.
- **31 December 2020** — Full application for aerodrome and air traffic management provisions (after transition period for member states to adapt).
- **2021** — EASA issues first cybersecurity certification requirements for aircraft systems under the new Article 77 powers.
- **2023** — First enforcement action against a member state: Commission opens infringement proceedings against Bulgaria for failing to transpose drone registration requirements, resolved in 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation had its most immediate effect on the **drone industry**. By 2022, EASA reported that 29 member states had implemented drone operator registration, with over 1.2 million operators registered across the EU. The compliance cost for manufacturers was significant: the regulation required drones over 250g to have geofencing capabilities and remote identification, forcing DJI, Parrot, and Autel to redesign 14 existing models for the European market between 2019-2021, according to a 2022 EASA market survey.

For **airlines**, the mandatory pilot medical records system — the European Pilots Information System (EUROPILOTS) — was operational by 2021. According to EASA's 2022 Annual Safety Review, this system flagged 47 potentially dangerous pilots who had been declared unfit in one member state but attempted to obtain licences in another between 2021-2022.

The regulation's impact on **accident rates** is difficult to isolate, but EASA's 2023 safety data shows the EU commercial aviation accident rate fell to 0.24 accidents per million flights, the lowest since records began, compared to 0.48 per million in 2013 under the previous regime.

**Industry compliance costs** were substantial. The European Business Aviation Association estimated in a 2020 survey that its members spent an average of €28,000 per aircraft on additional documentation and system upgrades to meet the new aerodrome requirements. Small aerodromes — those under 10,000 passengers annually — were hardest hit, with some in Croatia and Bulgaria closing rather than upgrading, according to the European Regions Airline Association's 2021 impact assessment.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU wait until 2018 to set common safety rules for drones?

The 2002 and 2008 EASA Regulations were designed for certified manned aircraft. Drones were too small and too new to fit the existing regulatory model. The explosion in consumer drone sales after 2013 — the European market grew from 200,000 units in 2013 to 1 million in 2016, per Eurocontrol — created a safety problem (near-misses with aircraft) and an economic opportunity (commercial drone services) that pushed the issue to the top of the Commission's agenda. The 2014 Paris Air Show incident where a drone flew within 500m of Charles de Gaulle Airport convinced regulators that voluntary self-regulation was failing.

### Why did the EU give the aviation safety agency more power instead of just making all countries follow the same rules?

The REFIT evaluation found that 15 member states had used the 2008 exemption clauses to maintain different rules for aerodromes, small aircraft, and pilot licensing — precisely the areas where the Germanwings crash showed national divergence had safety consequences. The Commission concluded that voluntary harmonisation had failed and that only giving EASA binding regulatory powers over the entire aviation system could close the gaps. The 2015 impact assessment estimated that full harmonisation would reduce accident rates by 15-20% compared to the existing patchwork.

### Why didn't the drone industry fight harder against the new EU safety rules for drones?

Leading drone manufacturers — particularly DJI, which held approximately 70% of the European consumer drone market — privately supported a single EU rulebook because it was cheaper than complying with 28 different national regimes. A DJI spokesperson told the Financial Times in 2017 that "one harmonised EU standard is better for us than negotiating with every member state separately." The main opposition came from hobbyist associations, who successfully fought to keep the 250g weight threshold (below which drones do not need registration), and the European model aircraft association (FMPA), which secured an exemption for model aircraft used by its 1.5 million members.

### Why did it take 16 years after creating the EU aviation safety agency to update its powers?

The original 2002 EASA Regulation was deliberately narrow — covering only aircraft design and production — because several large member states (France, Germany, UK) fiercely protected their national aviation safety agencies. France's DGAC and Germany's LBA opposed any transfer of their licensing authority to a European agency. The Germanwings crash broke this resistance: the revelation that a pilot could be declared unfit in one member state and simply move to another to obtain a licence made national sovereignty over licensing look reckless. Commissioner Bulc used the political window after the crash to push through the full extension that had been unsuccessfully proposed in 2007.
