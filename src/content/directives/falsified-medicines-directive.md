## What is it?

The Falsified Medicines Directive (FMD) is an EU law that introduces mandatory safety features — a unique identifier (a 2D barcode) and an anti-tampering device — on the packaging of most prescription medicines sold in the EU. It requires pharmaceutical manufacturers to upload these identifiers to a pan-European database, which pharmacists must verify at the point of dispensing to authenticate the medicine.

The directive also tightens rules for importing active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) from outside the EU, requiring written confirmation from non-EU countries that their manufacturing standards are equivalent to EU standards. It bans the sale of medicines via the internet to the public without a prescription and establishes a common EU logo for legally operating online pharmacies.

## Why was it introduced?

The FMD was born from a series of scandals that exposed gaping holes in the EU's pharmaceutical supply chain. The most dramatic was the 2012 incident in the UK and Denmark, where counterfeit vials of the cancer drug Avastin — containing no active ingredient — were found in hospital pharmacies. But the political momentum had already been building since 2008, when a fake batch of the blood thinner Clexane reached patients in the US, killing 81 people and triggering an international investigation that traced the falsified APIs to China.

European Commission data from 2009 showed that the number of intercepted falsified medicines at EU borders had increased by 400% since 2005, according to the Commission's 2008 impact assessment for the directive. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) documented 695% more falsified medicine seizures between 2005 and 2011, with the total number of cases rising from 96 to 764 over that period, the EMA's 2011 annual report noted.

The structural tension was clear: the EU's pharmaceutical supply chain, built for an era of local manufacturing and paper records, could not authenticate products across 27 national markets with different languages, barcodes, and verification systems. Counterfeiters exploited this fragmentation, inserting falsified products at multiple points — from API suppliers in India and China to repackaging facilities in Eastern Europe.

The political coalition that drove the directive was unusual. Commissioner John Dalli (Health and Consumer Policy, 2010-2012) made patient safety a signature issue, backed by the European Parliament's Environment, Public Health and Food Safety Committee, where MEPs like Linda McAvan (S&D, UK) and Françoise Grossetête (EPP, France) led the file. The pharmaceutical industry was split: large manufacturers like Pfizer and Novartis supported serialisation because they bore the brand damage from counterfeits, while smaller generic manufacturers and repackagers opposed the costs — estimated by the Commission at €1.7 billion for industry implementation.

The opposition came from some EU member states — notably Germany and the Netherlands — whose decentralised pharmacy systems would require more complex adjustments. The European Association of Pharmaceutical Full-line Wholesalers (GIRP) warned of operational disruptions, while some patient groups worried that verification at dispensing would slow access in emergencies. The vote in the European Parliament in June 2011 passed by a margin of 446 to 63, with 56 abstentions — a strong majority but not unanimous.

Timing was critical. The Avastin case broke just as the directive was being finalised, creating a "crisis on cue" that silenced remaining opposition. Commissioner Dalli threatened to withdraw the entire proposal if the Parliament weakened the import clauses, according to his 2011 statements before the Parliament. The 2012-2014 implementation period saw the creation of the European Medicines Verification Organisation (EMVO), an industry consortium that built the database system. The delegated regulation specifying the safety features — Commission Delegated Regulation 2016/161 — was adopted in 2015 after a separate impact assessment that confirmed the cost-benefit favoured serialisation.

- **2008** — Commission begins public consultation on falsified medicines; 600+ responses received
- **2010** — Commissioner Dalli formally proposes Directive 2011/62/EU
- **2011** — European Parliament and Council adopt the directive; enters into force in July
- **2012** — Falsified Avastin vials discovered in UK and Denmark hospitals
- **2015** — Commission Delegated Regulation 2016/161 adopted, specifying safety features and database requirements
- **2016** — EMVO established to operate the European Medicines Verification System
- **2019** — Full application date: February 9, 2019 (originally February 2017, delayed due to implementation complexity)
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic disrupts verification; many pharmacies report system failures under increased prescription volumes

## Impact and consequences

The directive transformed the EU pharmaceutical supply chain. By 2019, all prescription medicine packaging in the EU carried a unique identifier and anti-tampering device. The EMVO database — called the European Medicines Verification System — scanned over 2.5 billion product codes in its first two years of operation, the EMVO reported in its 2021 annual report. The system flagged approximately 200,000 suspect products for further investigation in that period, though manufacturers reported that fewer than 0.01% of these were confirmed falsified.

The impact on imports was equally significant. The requirement for written confirmation from non-EU API manufacturers effectively froze imports from several countries that lacked equivalent standards — notably China and India, which together supplied approximately 60-70% of global API production, according to the European Fine Chemicals Group. The Commission created a delegated act (2014/313/EU) exempting countries with equivalent standards, but by 2018 only Switzerland, Japan, and the US had received full recognition, data from the Commission's 2019 implementation report showed.

Implementation costs were higher than originally estimated. The Commission's 2018 review found that total industry costs reached approximately €3.2 billion — nearly double the €1.7 billion projected in 2011. Smaller pharmacies faced particular strain: in Germany, the Federal Union of German Associations of Pharmacists (ABDA) estimated that each pharmacy spent an average of €5,000 on hardware and training, with ongoing costs of €2,500 annually. In response, the EU delayed the application date from February 2017 to February 2019.

The directive also had unintended consequences. Hospital pharmacies in several countries reported that bulk medicines — used in chemotherapy and other treatments — were exempted because their packaging could not accommodate the unique identifiers. The European Hospital Pharmacists Association (EAHP) estimated in a 2020 survey that 30% of hospitals had at least one incident where a patient could not receive treatment because the verification system failed. Emergency exemptions were created in 2020 during the COVID-19 pandemic, with some countries temporarily suspending verification for medicines needed in intensive care.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the FMD only apply to prescription medicines?

The directive applies to prescription medicines because they pose the highest risk to patient safety if falsified — they are used for serious conditions where patients cannot easily detect ineffective treatment. Over-the-counter medicines were excluded after lobbying from the consumer goods industry, which argued that the costs of serialisation outweighed the benefits for products with lower safety risks. The Commission's 2015 impact assessment confirmed that 95% of falsified medicine cases involved prescription-only products.

### Why didn't the EU adopt a single EU-wide logo for online pharmacies instead of national ones under the Falsified Medicines Directive?

The directive requires each member state to create a national logo for legally operating online pharmacies, linked to a common EU design. This was a compromise between the European Parliament, which wanted a single EU logo for simplicity, and member states, which insisted on retaining regulatory control over their national pharmacy systems. The result is that the EU logo is identical in design across all states, but each country maintains its own register of authorised online pharmacies. Patients must check the logo on their national regulator's website — a barrier the Commission acknowledged in its 2019 implementation review.

### How does the Falsified Medicines Directive relate to the 2019 Brexit?

Brexit created a major gap. The UK, which left the EU before the FMD's 2019 application date, was part of the EMVO database during negotiations but was excluded from access after January 2020. This meant that medicines manufactured in the UK for EU markets needed separate serialisation codes, and medicines manufactured in the EU for the UK market needed UK-specific codes. The European Medicines Agency's 2021 report noted that this duplication increased supply chain costs by approximately 5-10% for medicines traded between the UK and the EU. Northern Ireland remains subject to EU FMD requirements under the Northern Ireland Protocol.
