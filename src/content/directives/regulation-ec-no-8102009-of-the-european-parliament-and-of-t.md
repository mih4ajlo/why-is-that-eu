## What is it?

The Visa Code, adopted in 2009, is the EU regulation that sets out common rules for processing applications for short-stay visas (up to 90 days) for the Schengen Area. It standardises everything from where and how applications are submitted to decision timelines, visa fees, entry conditions, and grounds for refusal. Before the Code, each Schengen state had its own national procedures, creating inconsistencies that frustrated travellers and made the bloc's external border policy appear disjointed.

The regulation applies to all third-country nationals required to hold a visa when crossing EU external borders, covering over 100 countries whose nationals need a Schengen visa. It also introduced the Visa Information System (VIS), a centralised database storing applicant biometric data, to enable border authorities to verify identities and prevent visa shopping.

## Why was it introduced?

The Visa Code was driven by a specific crisis: the 2004 EU enlargement brought ten new Member States into the Schengen framework, but their visa practices — inherited from national traditions — were wildly inconsistent. A traveller rejected by one embassy could simply go to another state's embassy in the same city and receive a visa on different grounds. This "visa shopping" created a structural tension between the concept of a single external border and the reality of 27 separate consular services.

The immediate trigger was the Council's 2006 adoption of a "Common Consular Instructions" that were legally non-binding. According to the Commission's 2008 impact assessment, this led to "considerable divergences" in implementation, with refusal rates varying from 5% to over 30% for the same nationality at different Member States' consulates. The Belgian Presidency of 2001 had first proposed harmonisation, but it took the Lisbon Treaty removing the intergovernmental pillar to give the Commission the legislative power to propose a binding regulation.

The coalition that pushed it through was unusual: border security hawks supported centralised biometric data (the VIS) while travel industry advocates backed harmonised processing times. Germany, France, and the Netherlands — states with large consular networks — were key drivers, while southern Member States with smaller networks worried about implementation costs. The European Travel Commission, representing national tourism bodies, lobbied heavily for streamlined procedures, warning that inconsistent visa practices were costing European tourism €1 billion annually in lost revenue.

Opposition came from Member States that treated visa policy as a sovereignty issue. The UK and Ireland, not being Schengen members, were unaffected. Among Schengen states, the key holdout was Spain, which initially blocked the VIS provisions over data protection concerns raised by its data protection authority. The 2005 Madrid bombings shifted the political calculus, making biometric identification seem less optional. The Parliament's lead rapporteur was Henrik Lax, a Finnish MEP from the ALDE group, who pushed for clearer applicant rights and faster processing deadlines.

The 2008 financial crisis was the final accelerant: tourism-dependent economies like Greece and Portugal suddenly saw visa facilitation as an economic stimulus tool. The Commission's proposal, published in 2006, was adopted by the Council in 2009 with unanimous support from Schengen states, following a Parliamentary report that secured 6 amendments to strengthen applicant appeals rights.

## Timeline

- **1999** — The Tampere European Council calls for a "common active policy on visas" as part of creating an area of freedom, security and justice
- **2001** — The Belgian Presidency proposes harmonising consular procedures, but Member States resist binding rules
- **2004** — EU enlargement brings 10 new Member States; the Hague Programme renews the mandate for a common visa policy
- **2006** — The Commission publishes its proposal for a Visa Code regulation (COM/2006/0403)
- **2007** — The Parliament votes through the Lax report with 480 votes in favour, 84 against; the Council reaches political agreement
- **2008** — The VIS Regulation (767/2008/EC) is adopted, providing the legal basis for the biometric database
- **2009** — The Visa Code is adopted by both institutions (13 July), enters into force (5 October), and applies from 5 April 2010
- **2010** — The code becomes binding; the VIS launches, with the first consulates rolling out biometric enrolment
- **2017** — The Commission proposes amendments to strengthen security screening and modernise application procedures
- **2019** — The recast visa code (2019/1155) enters into force, increasing visa fees and tightening rules on multiple-entry visas
- **2022** — The EU suspends the visa facilitation agreement with Russia following the invasion of Ukraine; the code's security provisions are invoked

## Impact and consequences

The most visible impact was procedural: prior to the Code, Schengen states took an average of 15-30 days to process visa applications; the Code mandated a 15-calendar-day maximum, which the Commission's 2014 evaluation found was met in 85% of cases. Refusal rates — which had varied from 8% (Nordic states) to 33% (some southern states) for the same applicant groups — converged to a 9-15% range by 2016, according to Eurostat data.

The VIS database proved controversial. By 2019, it held biometric data on over 60 million applicants. Privacy activists, including European Digital Rights, warned of its potential for mission creep, a concern that materialised when the Commission proposed expanding the database to include overstay checks in 2016.

The biggest losers were small Member States with limited consular networks. Malta and Luxembourg initially complained that the Code's mandatory personal appearance requirements — designed to collect fingerprints — forced them to set up shared application centres, increasing costs. The Schengen-wide network of 2,500 consulates shrunk by 12% between 2010 and 2014 as states consolidated into joint visa application centres.

Tourism effects were measurable: according to a 2016 UNWTO study, the Code contributed to a 40% increase in Schengen visa applications from China between 2010 and 2015, with the bloc's visa issuance rate reaching 96% for Chinese nationals by 2016. However, the 2022 Russian suspension showed how easily these gains could be reversed by geopolitical events.

## Key questions answered

### Do I need a visa to visit Europe for a short trip?

It depends on your nationality. If you hold a passport from a country on the EU's visa-required list (over 100 countries), you need a short-stay Schengen visa before travelling. If your country is visa-free (like the US, UK, Japan, or Canada), you can visit for up to 90 days without one. The Visa Code only applies to the EU's own visa-required list — it does not regulate visa-free travel.

### Why did European countries used to have different visa rules for the same trip?

Before 2009, each Schengen country had its own visa procedures and decision criteria, even though they shared a single border. This meant a traveller rejected by one embassy could try another country's embassy in the same city and get approved, creating "visa shopping." The Visa Code eliminated this by requiring all Schengen states to apply identical rules, criteria, and deadlines.

### What happens if I'm refused a Schengen visa?

The consulate must give you a written refusal notice stating the specific reason (e.g., insufficient funds, no clear travel plans, or security concerns). You have the right to appeal the decision in the Member State that rejected you — though appeal procedures still vary by country. The Code forces Member States to provide this information; before 2009, many simply denied visas without explanation.

### Does the EU share my data from visa applications with other countries?

Yes, but with limits. The biometric data collected (fingerprints and photo) is stored in the Visa Information System and can be accessed by border authorities and law enforcement under strict conditions. The Code originally gave law enforcement limited access to VIS data only for terrorism and serious crime investigations; a 2018 reform expanded this to all criminal investigations. Third countries do not have direct access, though the EU can exchange data with countries like the US under specific bilateral agreements.
