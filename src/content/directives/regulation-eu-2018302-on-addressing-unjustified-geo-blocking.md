## What is it?

Regulation 2018/302/EU, known as the Geo-blocking Regulation, makes it illegal for online traders to block or limit access to their websites based on a customer's country of residence or nationality, or to automatically redirect customers to a different version of their site without explicit consent. It also bans discrimination in terms of access to goods and services that are sold through those websites — so a French customer cannot be denied the ability to buy a German product at the German price, for example.

The regulation covers cross-border purchases of physical goods (like furniture or electronics), electronically supplied services (such as cloud storage or website hosting), and certain services provided in a specific location (like hotel bookings or car rental). It does **not** cover audiovisual content protected by copyright (such as streaming sports or movies), where territorial licensing is still permitted. The regulation entered into force on 22 December 2018 and has applied since 3 December 2018, with a review clause requiring the Commission to report on its effectiveness by 2020.

## Why was it introduced?

The Geo-blocking Regulation was born from a decade of rising frustration among European consumers, who increasingly found themselves treated as second-class customers in their own single market. The most dramatic single incident came in 2016, when the European Commission ran a "mystery shopping" survey across 283 e-commerce websites. The results, published in September 2016, were shocking: 63% of attempts to buy from a website in another EU country were blocked or redirected. A German consumer trying to buy a pair of jeans from a French website, for example, would be told "this item is not available in your country" or be silently redirected to a German-language site with different prices.

This was not a technical problem — it was an economic one with immediate political consequences. The Commission estimated cross-border e-commerce at just 3% of all EU retail trade in 2015, a figure that had barely budged despite a decade of digital market-building. The structural tension was clear: the EU had dismantled physical borders for goods in 1993, but companies had rebuilt them in the digital realm through contractual clauses, payment system restrictions, and website redirects. German retailer Otto Group, French home improvement chain Leroy Merlin, and Dutch electronics giant Coolblue were among the many that actively geo-blocked, arguing they needed to do so to comply with national laws, manage local tax rules, or maintain separate pricing strategies in different markets.

The coalition that drove the regulation through was unusual. On one side, consumer organisations (BEUC, the European Consumer Organisation) and cross-border shopping platforms (like Amazon, which supported ending geo-blocking to expand its merchant network) pushed hard. The European Parliament's Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO), led by rapporteur MEP Anneleen Van Bossuyt (ECR, Belgium), became a powerful driver. On the other side, a coalition of media companies — led by Rupert Murdoch's News Corp, the European Publishers Council, and major football leagues (FIFA, the English Premier League, Spain's La Liga) — fought fiercely to exclude copyright-protected content. They argued that territorial licensing was the only way smaller broadcasters and sports leagues could survive against global streaming giants like Netflix and Amazon Prime.

The timing was critical. The regulation was a flagship initiative of Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker's Digital Single Market strategy, announced in May 2015. Juncker made it a personal priority, telling the European Parliament in his 2016 State of the Union speech: "I want to put an end to the scandal of geo-blocking." The political moment was ripe: Brexit had just happened (the UK voted to leave in June 2016), and the EU needed a visible win for the single market that delivered something tangible to ordinary citizens. The Commission's own impact assessment, published in May 2016, found that eliminating unjustified geo-blocking could boost cross-border e-commerce by €4 billion annually by 2020.

What changed that broke the earlier deadlock? Three things. First, the May 2016 mystery shopping survey provided irrefutable evidence that geo-blocking was widespread and systematic — not just a niche complaint. Second, the European Court of Justice's 2014 ruling in the *UPC Telekabel Wien* case (C-314/12) had already weakened absolute territorial exclusivity in copyright law, suggesting the legal terrain was shifting. Third, the media industry's lobbying was so intense that the Commission made a deliberate tactical choice: exempt copyright-protected digital content entirely, securing the regulation's passage while promising to revisit the issue in a later review. The final Regulation passed the Council in November 2018 with only Austria and the Czech Republic abstaining — a rare show of near-unanimous support.

## Timeline

- **1993** — The EU abolishes physical internal borders for goods, but no equivalent rule exists for digital commerce.
- **2012** — The Commission's "European Consumer Agenda" flags geo-blocking as a growing barrier to single market completion.
- **May 2015** — Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker announces the Digital Single Market strategy, listing geo-blocking as a priority.
- **2014** — ECJ ruling in *UPC Telekabel Wien* (C-314/12) weakens absolute territorial exclusivity in copyright, signalling legal change.
- **September 2016** — Commission publishes mystery shopping survey showing 63% of cross-border purchase attempts are blocked, sparking media coverage.
- **May 2016** — Commission publishes impact assessment estimating €4 billion annual benefit from ending geo-blocking.
- **25 May 2016** — Commission tables proposed Geo-blocking Regulation (alongside other Digital Single Market measures including the Portability Regulation).
- **14 February 2017** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with 526 votes in favour, 81 against.
- **27 November 2018** — Council formally adopts the Regulation (only Austria and Czech Republic abstain).
- **12 December 2018** — Regulation published in Official Journal.
- **3 December 2018** — Regulation enters into force and becomes directly applicable across all EU Member States.
- **2020** — First Commission review report finds the regulation has been broadly effective but "limited by the exclusion of copyright-protected content."
- **March 2022** — Commission publishes second review report, noting that geo-blocking has significantly declined but remains an issue for audiovisual services.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation had an immediate and visible effect. By 2020, the Commission's first review report found that the number of websites blocking or redirecting cross-border customers had fallen to around 20-30% from 63% in 2016, though compliance was uneven. German, French, and Austrian traders were the most likely to still geo-block, often citing confusion about VAT rules or national labelling requirements. The Commission launched infringement proceedings against Poland in 2022 for failing to properly enforce the regulation domestically, and several consumer protection authorities issued fines: the Italian competition authority (AGCM) fined Booking.com €10 million in 2019 for geo-blocking hotel bookings — linked to both the Geo-blocking Regulation and the Platform-to-Business Regulation — though this was later partly overturned on appeal.

The most significant unintended consequence was the exemption for copyright-protected digital content. Streaming services like Netflix, Disney+, and Sky simply shifted their business models: they still geo-block movies and TV series based on territorial licensing contracts, and consumers remain frustrated that they cannot access their home-country content when travelling (the Portability Regulation solved this for short-term travel, but not for longer periods). The music streaming industry partly adapted by offering Europe-wide licences (Universal Music, Sony Music, and Warner Music now often negotiate pan-EU deals), but video streaming remains fragmented. The Commission's 2022 review recommended extending the regulation to audiovisual services, but as of 2024, no proposal has been tabled.

Economically, the regulation had a limited impact on cross-border e-commerce growth. Eurostat data shows that by 2022, 56% of EU internet users had bought goods online cross-border — up from 38% in 2015 — but the increase was driven more by platformisation (Amazon, AliExpress, Wish) than by small retailers opening their sites to foreign buyers. The European Parliament's 2020 own-initiative report found that many small retailers still refused to ship across borders due to logistics costs, return complexities, and language barriers — suggesting geo-blocking was only one barrier of many.

## Key questions answered

### Can I be blocked from buying from another EU country's website?

No, in most cases. The Geo-blocking Regulation makes it illegal for online traders to block you from accessing their website or to automatically redirect you to a country-specific version based on your location, without your explicit consent. However, this applies only to physical goods and certain non-copyrighted services — streaming services, e-books, and online games protected by territorial copyright are exempted, so Netflix can still block you from a different country's catalogue.

### Why are some EU websites still blocked if this regulation exists?

Because the regulation exempts audiovisual content (films, sports, music) protected by copyright. Subscription services like Netflix, Sky, Spotify, and sports streaming platforms negotiate territorial licences that allow them to restrict access based on your IP address. The other main reason is non-compliance: some traders still geo-block due to complexity in paying VAT or fulfilling returns across borders, though the Commission has been actively enforcing the rules.

### How do I know if a company is violating the regulation?

If you are in one EU country and a trader blocks you from their website in another EU country (e.g., you're in Italy and a German site tells you "not available in your country"), or if you are automatically redirected to a national version without being asked for consent, and the product is a physical good or a non-copyrighted electronic service (like cloud storage or website hosting), the company is likely in violation. The easiest check is to try visiting the website via a direct URL and seeing if you can browse and complete a checkout. If you cannot, file a complaint with your national consumer protection authority.

### Does the regulation force companies to set the same price for everyone?

No. The regulation prohibits *discriminating* against customers based on nationality or location — meaning a French customer must be allowed to buy a German product at the German price. But the trader is free to set different prices for different EU countries, so long as those prices are applied uniformly within each country. The regulation also does not prevent traders from deciding where to ship; they are not required to deliver to every EU country, only to offer the same terms to anyone who can arrange delivery themselves (e.g., pick-up from a German address).
