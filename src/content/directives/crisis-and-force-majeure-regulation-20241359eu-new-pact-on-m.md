## What is it?

The Crisis and Force Majeure Regulation (EU 2024/1359) is one of ten legislative acts that make up the EU’s New Pact on Migration and Asylum, adopted in May 2024. It allows member states to introduce **temporary derogations** from standard asylum procedures during declared crises — when large numbers of people arrive suddenly, or when a third country deliberately pushes migrants towards an EU border to destabilise a member state (instrumentalisation).

Under the regulation, a member state hit by a crisis can extend border asylum processing times from the usual 12 weeks to up to **six months**, and in exceptional cases to **eight weeks for an initial screening** followed by a longer procedure. The border procedure can be applied to broader categories of applicants, including families with children (except unaccompanied minors) and people from countries with lower recognition rates. The regulation also allows **faster returns** of rejected applicants, with lower procedural safeguards, and gives member states the option to **register asylum applications up to four weeks** after they are lodged (instead of immediately) during a declared crisis.

The regulation is a “last resort” tool: a member state can trigger it only after notifying the European Commission and receiving a favourable opinion from the Council acting by qualified majority. The activation lasts for up to six months, renewable once for a further six months. The Commission can also propose a crisis to be declared at EU level, but only with the consent of the Council.

## Why was it introduced?

The entry point is a **smouldering political crisis** that broke open in September 2020 when the Moria migrant camp on Lesbos burned to the ground — the largest in Europe, housing over 12,000 people in squalid conditions. The fire came a week after the European Commission had already announced, on 23 September 2020, its long-awaited New Pact on Migration and Asylum. But Moria, a symbol of the system’s collapse, gave the crisis mechanism an immediate, visceral urgency.

**Structural tension.** The pre‑2024 asylum system, governed by the Dublin III Regulation (2013/604/EU) and the Asylum Procedures Directive (2013/32/EU), assumed that member states could process applications and return rejected migrants within a predictable timeframe. The 2015–2016 migration crisis — when EU countries received over 1.3 million first‑time asylum applications, according to Eurostat — blew those assumptions apart. Greece, Italy, and later Spain were overwhelmed. The Dublin system’s rule that the first entry country is responsible for processing an application collapsed in practice: Greece processed only a fraction of applications; tens of thousands moved onward irregularly. The EU’s response — a forced relocation scheme (Council Decision 2015/1601) — triggered a revolt by Hungary, Poland, and the Czech Republic, who refused to participate, leading to a prolonged legal battle at the Court of Justice (joined Cases C-643/15 and C-647/15, decided in 2017 but ignored).

**Coalition dynamics.** The 2024 package was the result of an unlikely coalition: Mediterranean frontline states (Greece, Italy, Malta, Cyprus) and the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen, who made migration reform a flagship. They were opposed by a bloc of “anti‑relocation” states led by Hungary, Poland, and Slovakia, who demanded maximum national control and minimal mandatory solidarity. The breakthrough came in June 2022, when the Council reached a “general approach” on the Crisis Regulation and the rest of the Pact. The key concession was **no mandatory quotas** — instead, a “flexible solidarity” system of relocations, financial contributions, or operational support. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Tomas Tobé (EPP, Sweden) in 2020 and then by Juan Fernando López Aguilar (S&D, Spain) in 2023, pushed for stronger safeguards — including a requirement that children under 12 are exempt from border procedures. The final text, adopted by the Parliament on 10 April 2024 (322 votes in favour, 266 against, 32 abstentions) and by the Council on 14 May 2024, watered down many of the Parliament’s protections.

**Timing.** The New Pact succeeded in 2024 because three things broke the previous deadlock. First, the **Belarus border crisis of 2021** — when the Lukashenko regime flew migrants from the Middle East to Minsk and pushed them towards Lithuania, Poland, and Latvia — gave the instrumentalisation concept real, operational meaning. Poland, previously a hard‑line opponent of EU migration rules, suddenly found itself on the front line and demanded EU action. Second, a **European Court of Human Rights judgment** in 2021 (N.D. and N.T. v. Spain) limited the use of summary pushbacks, raising legal uncertainty for member states who wanted rapid border controls. Third, the **2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine** triggered the largest displacement of people in Europe since 1945 — over 8 million Ukrainians fled to the EU, according to UNHCR, but they were granted temporary protection (Directive 2001/55/EC) without asylum procedures. This demonstrated that the EU could act fast when it wanted to, but it also exposed the gap: the standard asylum system remained dysfunctional.

**What changed.** The precise trigger was the **June 2022 Council general approach** on the Crisis Regulation. Until then, the Parliament and Council had been stuck on mandatory relocation. The Council’s internal shift — driven by a German‑French‑Italian compromise hatched in early 2022 — embraced flexible solidarity and made the package acceptable to both frontline and northern states. The Parliament, fearing a total collapse of the reform, accepted the compromise in December 2023.

## Timeline

- **September 2015** — More than 1.3 million asylum applications filed in the EU (Eurostat). The Dublin system is effectively suspended.
- **September 2020** — European Commission presents the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, including a proposal for a Crisis and Force Majeure Regulation (COM/2020/613 final). Moria camp fire occurs days later.
- **June 2022** — Council of the EU reaches a “general approach” on the Crisis Regulation, abandoning mandatory relocation in favour of flexible solidarity.
- **December 2023** — European Parliament and Council reach a provisional political agreement on the Crisis Regulation and the rest of the Pact.
- **10 April 2024** — European Parliament approves the Crisis Regulation (322-266-32) as part of the broader Pact vote.
- **14 May 2024** — Council of the EU formally adopts the regulation.
- **11 June 2024** — Regulation published in the Official Journal (L 2024/1359).
- **11 July 2024** — Regulation enters into force, 20 days after publication.
- **11 June 2026** — Deadline for member states to implement the revised Asylum Procedures Regulation and other Pact instruments; the Crisis Regulation remains immediately applicable.

## Impact and consequences

The Crisis Regulation fundamentally shifts the balance between individual rights and state control during emergencies. Early reactions from NGOs (including ECRE, Amnesty International) and the UNHCR warned that the six‑month border procedures, limited access to legal aid, and expedited returns could create “legal black holes” at the EU’s external borders. The regulation’s defenders, including the European Commission’s Home Affairs Commissioner Ylva Johansson, argued that only a workable crisis mechanism could prevent the complete breakdown of the Schengen Area and the return of internal border checks — which by 2024 had become permanent in nine EU states.

The regulation does not stand alone. It is tied to the **Asylum Procedures Regulation** and **the Returns Regulation** (also 2024), which mandate a mandatory border procedure for certain applicants (e.g., from low‑recognition-rate countries). The Crisis Regulation extends those procedures further. Member states like **Greece** and **Italy**, which already operate de‑facto crisis regimes (in hotspots and reception centres), now have a legal framework; they will be first to activate it. The **Commission** has already signalled that it will scrutinise notifications carefully, but the first activation is expected from Greece regarding the islands of Lesbos and Chios.

One concrete consequence: the regulation includes a **novel procedural right for migrants to challenge the declaration of a crisis** before national courts. If a member state declares a crisis without the required Council opinion, individuals could sue. The first such cases are likely to test the boundaries of the regulation.

The **instrumentalisation** clause also creates a new category: a member state can now claim that a third country is “directing migratory flows” toward its border, triggering crisis procedures. This was a direct response to the Belarus 2021 crisis and to the **2023–2024 pressure on Spain’s Ceuta and Melilla enclaves from Morocco** (after the Polisario‑Sahara dispute). Poland has already indicated it may invoke the provision if Belarus resumes migrant‑pushing.

## Key questions answered

### When can an EU country break normal asylum rules during a migrant crisis?

A member state can declare a crisis and use faster, less protective asylum procedures only if a sudden, very large influx (or instrumentalisation by a third country) makes normal processing impossible. The country must first notify the European Commission and get a green light from the EU Council (by qualified majority). The crisis mode lasts up to six months, renewable once. During that time, the country can hold people in border zones for up to six months before granting a decision, apply stricter rules to families, and fast‑track deportations.

### Does the new EU migration system force countries to take in migrants from frontline states?

No. The 2024 reform replaced mandatory relocation quotas with “flexible solidarity”. A member state hit by a crisis can request help, and other EU countries can choose among three options: relocate some asylum seekers, pay a financial contribution into an EU fund, or provide operational support (police, interpreters, equipment). The Council can later decide to make a specific form of solidarity mandatory (e.g., for minors), but only with a qualified majority vote. This compromise was the key that got the whole package through.

### What happened to the old Dublin system?

The Dublin III Regulation (2013/604/EU) was effectively replaced by a new **Asylum and Migration Management Regulation (2024/1358/EU)**. The old rule that the first entry country handles the asylum application is preserved in principle, but a new “solidarity pool” mechanism allows the Commission to decide, after a crisis, that some applicants should be relocated to other member states even if not requested. The Crisis Regulation creates an even stronger override: during a crisis, the Commission can redistribute applicants without the member state’s consent if the Council agrees.

### Why did the Crisis Regulation take four years to pass?

The main obstacle was mandatory relocation: frontline states wanted it; Visegrád states (Hungary, Poland, Czech Republic, Slovakia) blocked it. The breakthrough came in 2022 when Germany and France brokered a “flexible solidarity” alternative, and the Belarus border crisis showed that even anti‑relocation states could need EU help. The 2023 European Parliament elections also created a deadline: the rapporteurs knew that if the Pact was not voted on by April 2024, the entire reform would die and have to restart under a new Parliament. That urgency forced the final deal.
