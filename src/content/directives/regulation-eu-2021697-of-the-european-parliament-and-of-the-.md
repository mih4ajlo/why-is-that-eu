## What is it?

The European Defence Fund (EDF) is a €7.9 billion (in current prices) EU budget programme running from 2021 to 2027, designed to co-finance collaborative defence research and capability development projects involving companies and research institutes from at least three EU member states or associated countries. It is the first time the EU budget directly funds defence-related activities on this scale.

The EDF is divided into two main strands: €2.7 billion for research (funding up to 100% of eligible costs for basic, applied, and disruptive defence technologies) and €5.3 billion for capability development (funding up to 20-30% of prototype and qualification costs, with member states covering the remainder). It operates through annual work programmes and competitive calls for proposals, managed by the European Commission's Directorate-General for Defence Industry and Space (DG DEFIS), established in 2019.

The Fund is structured to prioritise projects aligned with EU defence capability priorities agreed by member states, such as the Capability Development Priorities defined by the European Defence Agency (EDA). It explicitly requires that funded activities involve entities from at least three eligible countries and that intellectual property rights are shared among participants. The EDF replaced and expanded two earlier pilot programmes: the Preparatory Action on Defence Research (PADR, 2017-2019, €90 million) and the European Defence Industrial Development Programme (EDIDP, 2019-2020, €500 million).

## Why was it introduced?

The EDF was born from a structural contradiction: Europe collectively spent around €200 billion annually on defence (according to a 2018 Commission communication), yet 80% of that money was spent nationally, with minimal cross-border cooperation. The result was fragmented, duplicative, and often incompatible military systems—there were, at the time, three separate main battle tank families and 44 different types of armoured infantry fighting vehicles in EU member states, as the Commission noted in its 2017 reflection paper on defence.

The immediate political trigger was Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014 and the subsequent hybrid war in eastern Ukraine. This shattered the post-Cold War assumption that European security could be provided cheaply under a US umbrella. The 2016 EU Global Strategy, drafted under High Representative Federica Mogherini, explicitly called for "a European Defence Fund to pool resources and foster joint defence capabilities." But the rubber hit the road in 2017, when Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker—who had made "European strategic autonomy" a signature theme—tasked his internal market Commissioner Elżbieta Bieńkowska with designing a workable fund.

The coalition that got the EDF over the line was unusual. France, which under President Emmanuel Macron pushed aggressively for European defence integration, was the primary champion—insisting that the Fund should prioritise next-generation systems like a European fighter jet and main battle tank. Germany, wary of upsetting NATO, supported the Fund but demanded strict safeguards ensuring that projects remained "open" to third-country participation (a nod to US and UK defence firms). Italy and Spain joined the French camp. The European Parliament, led by the centre-right EPP group, backed the Fund as a way to boost European industrial champions.

The opposition was loud. Sweden, Denmark, and the Netherlands—fiscally conservative and NATO-sceptical—argued the Fund duplicated existing NATO structures and risked creating a "European Pentagon" that would alienate the United States. The UK, still an EU member at the time of negotiations, fought hard against the Fund, warning it would harm NATO interoperability; the UK's then-Defence Secretary Michael Fallon wrote in 2018 that the Fund risked "duplication and diversion of scarce resources." But Brexit dramatically weakened the UK's blocking power—once Britain left, the coalition of smaller member states opposed to the Fund lost its most influential voice.

The specific breakthrough came in 2017-2018, when Juncker and Mogherini together presented the Fund as a "security guarantee" for a post-Brexit Europe. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment estimated that fragmented national procurement cost European taxpayers between €25 billion and €100 billion annually in lost efficiency. Simultaneously, the US under President Donald Trump began openly questioning its security commitments to Europe—most famously at the 2018 NATO summit, where Trump threatened to "go our own way" if allies did not spend more. This geopolitical shock made the EDF's argument self-evident: Europe needed to spend collectively what it could not spend individually.

The alternative considered was a purely intergovernmental approach through the European Defence Agency (EDA), which already managed voluntary capability development projects but had no budget of its own. The Commission argued that only a centralized EU budget instrument—with competitive calls, IP rules, and an annual work programme—could overcome the "governance gap" that left member states free to walk away from joint projects. The final regulation included a "specificity clause" (Article 14) allowing member states to exclude third-country entities from participating in sensitive projects, which satisfied French concerns about US firms scooping up EU-funded IP.

## Timeline

- **2014** — Russia annexes Crimea; EU begins to reconsider defence cooperation in earnest.
- **2016** — EU Global Strategy calls for a "European Defence Fund" to pool resources.
- **2017** — Commission launch of Preparatory Action on Defence Research (PADR), €90 million for 2017-2019; Juncker announces EDF proposal in his State of the Union address.
- **2018** — Commission presents formal legislative proposal for the EDF; European Parliament and Council begin negotiations; UK opposition intensifies.
- **2019** — European Defence Industrial Development Programme (EDIDP, €500 million) begins as pilot for the capability strand; Juncker's Commission exits; DG DEFIS established.
- **2020** — Council agrees on overall budget of €7.9 billion for 2021-2027 under the Multiannual Financial Framework; COVID-19 pandemic delays final approval.
- **2021** — Regulation adopted by European Parliament (27 May) and Council (20 May); enters into force on 1 July; first annual work programme published (€1.2 billion in calls).
- **2022** — First EDF projects selected (41 projects funded from first calls); Russia invades Ukraine, reinforcing the Fund's rationale.
- **2023** — Second annual work programme launched (€1.2 billion); 63 more projects selected.
- **2024** — European Parliament calls for EDF budget to be doubled in the post-2027 MFF.

## Impact and consequences

The EDF has transformed EU defence R&D, but its impact remains debated. By 2024, the Fund had awarded over €3 billion in grants to around 300 projects, involving more than 1,000 entities from across the EU. The largest single project funded is the next-generation European Medium Altitude Long Endurance (MALE) drone system (Eurodrone), which received €100 million in EDIDP funding before continuing under the EDF. Other major projects include the European Main Battle Tank (MGCS) and the Future Combat Air System (FCAS).

Defence industry consolidation is accelerating: smaller national champions increasingly form consortia to meet the "three countries" rule. The European aerospace company Airbus now coordinates major EDF projects with partners from France, Germany, Spain, and others. However, the requirement that participants from at least three member states has been criticised as creating "artificial consortia" that slow development. The European Court of Auditors, in a 2024 special report, found that only 23% of projects had produced any tangible output by the end of 2023.

The biggest unintended consequence has been the "crowding out" of smaller firms. The Fund's requirement for consortia to include "at least one SME" (Article 12) has been criticised as a tick-box exercise: nearly all major projects are led by large primes like Thales, Leonardo, and Airbus. A 2023 survey by the EU's Defence SMEs Association found that 78% of small firms found the EDF application process too complex.

Geopolitical effects are more significant. The EDF has deepened the split between EU "defence integrationists" (France, Germany, Italy, Spain) and "NATO loyalists" (Poland, the Baltics, Nordic states). Poland, which had opposed the Fund as weakening NATO, changed position after Russia's 2022 invasion, noting that the EDF could fund projects like the Polish–South Korean joint tank programme.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EDF fund research rather than procurement?

The EU Treaty (Article 41 TEU) explicitly bans the EU budget from financing "the purchase of equipment" with military purposes. The EDF can only fund research, development, and prototyping—the actual purchase of tanks, jets, and ships remains a national responsibility. This legal constraint explains why the Fund covers only up to 20-30% of prototype costs: member states must pay the rest, ensuring they own the final product. The Commission's 2018 legal assessment confirmed that a full procurement fund would require treaty change, which no member state supported.

### Why did the UK oppose the EDF so strongly?

The UK, as Europe's largest defence spender (around £39 billion in 2020), feared the EDF would create a "Fortress Europe" that locked out British defence firms after Brexit. British companies like BAE Systems and Babcock relied heavily on EU-funded research and had previously dominated EU defence innovation networks. The UK also argued the Fund would duplicate NATO's Defence Innovation Accelerator (DIANA), which the UK had helped establish. The UK's exit in 2020 removed its blocking power, though the EDF regulation (Article 15) still allows third-country entities to participate under strict conditions—which the UK has not yet sought to use.

### Does the EDF make European defence more efficient?

Evidence is mixed. The Commission's 2023 mid-term evaluation found that 92% of funded projects would not have happened without the EDF, because member states would not have cooperated bilaterally. However, the European Parliamentary Research Service (EPRS) noted in 2024 that the Fund has not yet reduced the number of different weapon systems in Europe—the same number of main battle tank families (three) remains, though two are now co-developed among several member states. The real test—whether EDF-funded systems are actually procured and deployed—will not come until the 2030s, when the first prototypes mature.
