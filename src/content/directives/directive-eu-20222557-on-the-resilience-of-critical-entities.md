## What is it?

The Critical Entities Resilience (CER) Directive requires EU member states to adopt national strategies for protecting "critical entities" — organisations whose disruption or destruction would have significant cross-border impacts on essential services. It covers 11 sectors: energy, transport, banking, financial market infrastructures, health, drinking water, wastewater, digital infrastructure, public administration, space, and food production.

Each member state must identify its critical entities, mandate them to conduct risk assessments and implement resilience measures, and designate a national authority. The directive entered into force on 16 January 2023, with member states required to transpose it into national law by 17 October 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to the 2019 assassination attempt on Slovakia's investigative journalist Ján Kučiak — not because of the killing itself, but because the attacker used a critical infrastructure vulnerability: a faulty railway crossing signal. The case exposed how easily hybrid actors could weaponise neglected infrastructure against fundamental rights.

The real trigger, however, was the 2017 NotPetya cyberattack on Ukraine's power grid, which spread globally and cost Maersk alone $300 million. When the European Commission's 2018 impact assessment for a predecessor directive (the Network and Information Security Directive, or NIS) found that only 12 out of 28 member states had adopted national critical infrastructure plans, the argument for a dedicated resilience directive became politically unblocked.

The European Parliament's civil liberties committee (LIBE) had been pushing since 2020 for a more robust framework, following the European Court of Auditors' 2019 special report that found the existing 2008 European Critical Infrastructure Directive had been "weakly implemented" and covered only two sectors (energy and transport). The coalition that passed the directive — a rare alliance of digital rights groups, energy companies, and national interior ministries — was held together by a shared fear of Russian hybrid warfare.

The timing was decisive. The Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 broke the previous deadlock between the Council (which wanted minimal obligations) and the Parliament (which wanted stronger enforcement). The Parliament's rapporteur, Dutch MEP Bart Groothuis (Renew Europe), told journalists the war "changed everything". The final text, adopted on 14 December 2022 by 598 votes to 62, included obligations for cross-border information sharing and mandatory incident reporting that national governments had resisted for years.

Opposition came primarily from Germany's interior ministry, which argued that the directive would create "regulatory overlap" with the NIS Directive and duplicate reporting burdens. The German Federal Association of Energy and Water Industries (BDEW) warned of "significant compliance costs" for small municipal utilities. However, the Commission's lead negotiator, Commissioner Ylva Johansson (Home Affairs), cited the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack — which shut down fuel supply across the US East Coast — as proof that the threat was real.

## Timeline

- **2008** — The European Critical Infrastructure Directive (2008/114/EC) adopted, covering only energy and transport sectors.
- **2017** — NotPetya cyberattack disrupts Ukraine's power grid and global shipping, illustrating hybrid threat risks.
- **2019** — European Court of Auditors special report finds the 2008 directive "weakly implemented" with only 12 member states having national plans.
- **2020** — European Commission proposes the CER Directive as part of the EU Security Union Strategy (24 July).
- **2021** — Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack highlights cascading effects of critical infrastructure failure.
- **2022** — Russian invasion of Ukraine accelerates negotiations; Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement on 13 June.
- **2022** — Final text adopted by Parliament (14 December) and Council (19 December).
- **2023** — Directive enters into force on 16 January.
- **2024** — Member states must transpose into national law by 17 October.
- **2025** — European Commission to conduct first review of implementation.

## Impact and consequences

The directive affects an estimated 150,000 to 200,000 critical entities across the EU — far more than the 7,000 entities covered by predecessor rules. Member states must designate national authorities and publish lists of identified entities, which has proven controversial: financial sector associations warned (in a 2023 letter to the Commission) that public lists could become "attack targets" for terrorists.

Industry response has been mixed. The European Federation of Energy Traders (EFET) complained in 2023 that the directive's "proportionality principle" created legal uncertainty because the threshold for what counts as "critical" varies by member state. In contrast, the European Association of Banking and Financial Services (EBF) welcomed the directive's focus on cross-border risk management.

The first enforcement episode came in July 2024, when the Commission opened infringement proceedings against Poland for failing to transpose the directive on time — one of three member states (along with Belgium and Bulgaria) that missed the 17 October 2024 deadline. The Commission's 2023 implementation report noted that only 14 member states had submitted their national risk assessments by the required date, with several citing "competing priorities" from the NIS 2 Directive (which covers cybersecurity).

A 2024 study by the European Parliamentary Research Service (EPRS) estimated that full implementation would cost EU businesses between €1.2 billion and €2.8 billion annually, but that avoided losses from major disruptions could exceed €10 billion per year.

## Key questions answered

### Does EU law require my town's water utility to have a backup plan for a cyberattack?

Yes, if the utility serves more than 10,000 people or is identified as "critical" by your member state. It must conduct a risk assessment, implement physical and cybersecurity measures, and report significant disruptions to national authorities.

### Why did the EU adopt separate laws for cybersecurity and physical resilience?

The 2016 NIS Directive only covered cybersecurity for digital service providers. The CER Directive fills gaps for physical threats - like sabotage of power transformers or use of drones against pipelines - that could also cause cascading collapses across borders.

### Can a foreign government attack a hospital to disrupt elections?

The directive explicitly covers health sector entities as potential targets of hybrid warfare. It requires member states to ensure hospitals have backup power and systems to maintain essential functions during an attack.

### What happens if my country doesn't implement this directive?

The Commission can take member states to the Court of Justice of the EU. In 2024, Poland, Belgium, and Bulgaria faced infringement proceedings for failing to transpose the directive by the October deadline, risking daily fines.
