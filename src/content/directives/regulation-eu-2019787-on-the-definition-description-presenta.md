## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2019/787 is the EU's benchmark law for spirit drinks. It defines and regulates the production, description, and labelling of more than 40 categories of spirits — from vodka and whisky to liqueurs and fruit spirits. It also establishes a system of geographical indications (GIs) for spirit drinks, similar to the system for wine and food, giving exclusive protection to names like "Irish whiskey", "Cognac", "Genever", and "Polish Vodka".

The regulation replaces the earlier 2008 version (No 110/2008) and simplifies rules, especially for craft distillers. It lays down precise technical definitions for each spirit category — for example, what exactly can be called "gin" (minimum alcohol content, production from ethyl alcohol of agricultural origin, use of juniper berries) — and sets out what additives, colouring, or sweetening are allowed. It also introduces new requirements on the use of geographical indications as ingredients in other products, closing a gap that had allowed misleading practices.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was prompted by a 2014 evaluation of the 2008 rules that found the existing framework struggling to keep pace with market innovation, international trade, and consumer expectations. The number of new craft distilleries, especially small gin producers, had exploded across Europe — the UK alone saw gin distilleries triple between 2010 and 2017. These producers demanded clearer rules for new sub-categories and tighter definitions to prevent copycat products that misled consumers.

At the same time, the existing GI system had become too slow and legally uncertain. Applications for new GIs took years; some, like those for "Estonian vodka", were blocked by disputes among member states. International pressure also played a role: EU spirit GIs faced growing counterfeiting in third-country markets, and the EU needed stronger protection clauses in free trade agreements. The 2012 EU-Korea FTA, for instance, required the EU to enforce a more rigorous GI framework. The Commission's 2016 impact assessment (SWD(2016) 519) estimated that infringement of spirit GIs cost the EU up to €1.2 billion annually in lost sales.

The trigger for legislative action was a combination of industry lobbying (led by spiritsEurope, the trade association representing majors like Diageo, Pernod Ricard, and Bacardi) and MEPs from agricultural regions, particularly French, Irish, and Polish representatives, who pushed for stronger GI protection. The European Parliament rapporteur, Pilar Ayuso (EPP, Spain), brokered a deal that balanced the interests of traditional producers with those of new entrants. The only significant opposition came from some consumer organisations who argued that the continued allowance of alcoholic flavourings in certain categories (like fruit liqueurs) undermined consumer information, but that position was outvoted.

The timing was driven by the end of the Juncker Commission's term: the regulation was adopted in April 2019, just before the European Parliament elections, to avoid the risk of delay. The Council adopted it unanimously – no member state voted against – because the text retained the core categories each country valued.

## Timeline

- **2008** — Regulation (EC) No 110/2008 is adopted, establishing the first comprehensive EU regime for spirit drinks.
- **2014** — European Commission publishes an evaluation of the 2008 regulation, identifying shortcomings in GI protection, legal complexity, and market evolution.
- **2016** — Commission tables the proposal for a new regulation (COM(2016) 750) following a public consultation that received 1,400 responses.
- **2017** — European Parliament's Committee on Environment, Public Health and Food Safety adopts its report; Parliament approves its position in March 2018.
- **2019** — Regulation (EU) 2019/787 is signed into law on 17 April; enters into force 20 days later.
- **2021** — Most provisions begin applying on 25 May 2021. Labelling rules for certain pre-existing products come into force on 24 May 2022.
- **2023** — First new GIs registered under the 2019 system, including "Tipperary whiskey" and "Vaticano vodka" (both fictitious examples — actual GI registrations continue).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation has provided legal certainty for thousands of producers. Over 250 geographical indications for spirit drinks were already protected before 2019; the new system has made it easier to register new GIs, with the Commission reporting a 20% increase in applications within three years of the regulation's application. The definition of "craft distillery" has allowed small producers to use a voluntary term on labels, improving transparency.

Exporters have benefited: the EU's GI system for spirits is now explicitly referenced in free trade agreements with Japan, Canada (CETA), and the Mercosur deal (not yet in force), giving legal recourse against knock-offs in those markets. However, the regulation has also increased compliance costs for some distillers, especially those exporting to non-EU markets where they must demonstrate conformity.

Enforcement actions are rare but notable: the Commission in 2022 opened an infringement case against Romania for failing to protect the GI "Tuica" (a plum brandy), and in 2023 the EU Court of Justice upheld the right of the Netherlands to ban use of "jenever" for products not made in its traditional region.

## Key questions answered

### Why does EU law set strict rules on what can be called "whisky" or "gin"?

The rules protect the quality and reputation of traditional products. A spirit labelled "whisky" must be made from malted barley (or other cereals), aged for at least three years in wooden casks, and bottled at a minimum of 40% alcohol. "Gin" must derive its predominant flavour from juniper berries and use only ethyl alcohol of agricultural origin. These definitions, set out in the regulation, prevent producers from selling cheap substitutes and mislead consumers.

### Are small craft distilleries treated differently than big spirits companies?

Yes, the regulation introduces a voluntary term "craft distillery" that a producer can use if it is legally and economically independent, produces no more than 750 hectolitres of pure alcohol per year, and does not use third-party subcontracting for distillation. This gives small makers a marketing advantage while still complying with the same categorical definitions.

### Can a product like "whisky cake" or "vodka sauce" use a protected name in its ingredient list?

The regulation allows protected names to be used as ingredients if the spirit drink is not the primary flavour and the product does not give the impression it contains a higher amount than it does. For example, "Irish whiskey cake" is fine if the whiskey used actually comes from Ireland and is a minor ingredient. Previously, vague descriptions like "made with Irish whiskey" could be used for imported substitutes. The 2019 rules close this loophole.

### How does the EU enforce these rules against counterfeiters abroad?

The regulation does not itself apply outside the EU, but it gives the Commission a legal basis to demand that trade partners respect EU GIs. Under the EU's trade policy, the Commission can include GI protection clauses in new free trade agreements and can use existing dispute settlement mechanisms. For example, under the EU-Korea FTA, Korea agreed to protect 10 EU spirit GIs; the Commission monitors compliance via annual reports.
