## What is it?

Euro 7 is the latest EU regulation setting emission limits for new motor vehicles, covering cars, vans, trucks, and buses. It replaces the Euro 6 standards for cars (in place since 2014) and Euro VI for heavy-duty vehicles (since 2013). Unlike previous Euro standards that focused almost exclusively on tailpipe pollutants — nitrogen oxides (NOx), carbon monoxide (CO), hydrocarbons, and particulates — Euro 7 broadens the scope to include non-exhaust emissions from brakes and tyres, and mandates longer durability requirements for emission control systems.

The regulation targets a wider range of driving conditions than its predecessors. Euro 6 standards were criticised for testing vehicles in ideal lab conditions that poorly matched real-world driving. Euro 7 introduces stricter on-road compliance tests, including short trips, cold starts, and driving in urban conditions with heavy stop-start traffic. For heavy-duty vehicles, the NOx limit is reduced to a fraction of the current level. The regulation also sets a minimum battery durability of 80% after five years or 100,000 kilometres for hybrid and electric vehicles, and requires that emissions remain below the stated limits for 200,000 kilometres for light-duty vehicles and up to 375,000 kilometres for heavy-duty vehicles, up from 100,000 kilometres under Euro 6.

## Why was it introduced?

The path to Euro 7 was shaped by a central irony: the European Commission proposed the toughest-ever exhaust standards just as it was legislating to ban the sale of new combustion-engine cars entirely by 2035. This opened a political and industrial fault line that delayed the regulation by nearly two years and produced a final text far weaker than the original 2022 proposal.

The structural tension lies in the failure of Euro 6 to meaningfully improve real-world air quality in European cities. According to the European Environment Agency's 2022 State of the Air report, air pollution from road transport still caused approximately 53,000 premature deaths annually in the EU. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment concluded that Euro 6 trucks emitted NOx at levels several times the legal limit under real-world driving conditions — a direct consequence of weak on-road testing requirements introduced only in 2017 after the Dieselgate scandal. Volkswagen's 2015 admission that it had installed defeat devices in 11 million diesel vehicles globally had shattered trust in the regulatory framework. The European Parliament's inquiry committee on emission measurements in the automotive sector (EMIS) concluded in 2017 that "the scandal was not an isolated case" and that the existing type-approval system was "structurally incapable of detecting cheating."

The original political coalition that drove Euro 7 ambitions was fragile. Frans Timmermans, then Executive Vice-President of the European Commission for the European Green Deal, pushed for a broad and ambitious regulation as a complement to the 2035 combustion-engine phase-out. The initial 2022 proposal set limits nearly as stringent as those requiring electric vehicles, and specifically banned the use of maximum allowed limits as design targets — a practice that auto-makers had used to push emissions just below regulatory thresholds. The proposal enjoyed support from urbanist MEPs, health campaigners like the European Public Health Alliance, and the Dutch and Scandinavian governments.

Opposition was ferocious and well-coordinated. The European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA), representing 14 major manufacturers including Volkswagen, Stellantis, and Renault, argued that the technology required to meet Euro 7 — advanced catalytic converters and particulate filters for petrol engines — would add between €1,000 and €3,000 to the cost of a small car, pricing European manufacturers out of the affordable segment and accelerating the shift to Chinese-made electric vehicles. In February 2023, Stellantis CEO Carlos Tavares warned publicly that Euro 7 could force the closure of factories in Italy and France. Italy's far-right government under Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, backed by the Czech Republic, Hungary, and Poland, threatened to veto the regulation in the Council.

The timing was critical. The original proposal landed in November 2022, six months after the political deal on banning new combustion-engine cars by 2035. That deal had already taken the industry's most vocal opposition and redirected it: if the end of the internal combustion engine was inevitable, why impose costly new compliance requirements on a sunset technology? The European Parliament's centre-right European People's Party (EPP), the largest political group, shifted position. In May 2023, the EPP's environment spokesperson, Jens Gieseke, introduced amendments that effectively gutted the proposal: removing new NOx limits for cars, maintaining existing Euro 6 test cycles, and raising durability requirements only modestly. The Parliament's final position, adopted in November 2023, was a stripped-down version that industry called workable and environmental groups labelled "Euro 6.5."

What changed between the 2022 proposal and the 2024 final text was this political reversal. The Commission, aware it lacked the votes in Council, negotiated a compromise through the rotating Spanish Presidency that adopted the weaker Parliament text. The final regulation, published in July 2024, dropped the most stringent tailpipe limits for cars, watered down the durability requirements for heavy-duty vehicles compared to the original Commission proposal, and exempted small-volume manufacturers producing fewer than 10,000 vehicles per year — a cap that included most luxury and boutique carmakers. Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius described the outcome as "realistic," while Transport & Environment, the Brussels-based clean transport NGO, called it a "missed opportunity" that "kicks the can down the road on brake and tyre particles."

## Impact and consequences

The impact of Euro 7 is principally felt in the durability and non-exhaust requirements rather than in tightening tailpipe limits for cars, which remain largely unchanged from Euro 6 levels. The European Commission's 2021 impact assessment estimated that, before the weakening, Euro 7 would reduce NOx emissions from heavy-duty vehicles by 80% compared to Euro VI levels. The final text, according to a Commission analysis shared with member states in 2023, achieves roughly a 50% reduction for trucks and approximately a 30% reduction for cars relative to Euro 6, largely from improved cold-start performance and stricter on-road testing.

The regulation affects all new vehicles sold in the EU from the respective application dates. The battery durability requirement — 80% capacity after five years or 100,000 km — applies to all hybrid and battery electric vehicles, covering the rapidly growing electric-vehicle market. The brake particle limits, set to 3 mg/km for pure electric vehicles and 7 mg/km for combustion-engine vehicles, represent the first-ever EU standard for non-exhaust emissions. The European Commission's Joint Research Centre estimated in a 2020 study that brake and tyre particles account for up to 50% of road-transport particulate matter emissions by mass in urban areas, a share that grows as tailpipe emissions decrease.

Industry compliance costs were heavily disputed. The original Commission proposal included an impact assessment estimating an additional cost of approximately €90–150 per vehicle for carmakers and €2,500 for heavy-duty vehicles. The European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA) countered that its members' estimates ranged from €1,000 to over €3,000 per car. The final text, by preserving Euro 6 tailpipe limits and focusing on durability and non-exhaust requirements, reduced the cost increase to roughly €100–200 per vehicle, according to an analysis by the Commission's Joint Research Centre conducted during the trilogue negotiations.

The countries most affected are those with large automotive manufacturing sectors that produce affordable combustion-engine cars: Italy, France, the Czech Republic, Romania, and Spain. Italy's government, during the 2023 Council negotiations, argued that stricter limits would raise the price of the Fiat Panda, a model that retailed for under €15,000, by an estimated 5–10%, pricing out lower-income buyers. The final regulation's exemption for manufacturers producing fewer than 10,000 vehicles per year protects boutique manufacturers like Ferrari, Lamborghini, and Bugatti — the Italian government's specific concern — but does not apply to mass-market producers.

## Key questions answered

### What does Europe's latest car pollution law actually regulate?

Euro 7 broadens the scope of EU vehicle emission rules beyond just tailpipe exhaust. It sets the first limits on particles from brakes and tyres, mandates that cars keep their emission control systems running for 200,000 kilometres (up from 100,000 km), and requires electric and hybrid car batteries to retain at least 80% capacity after five years. For heavy trucks, however, NOx limits are only moderately tightened from the previous Euro VI standard after a major industry backlash.

### Why did European carmakers fight so hard against new EU pollution limits?

Carmakers argued the original 2022 Commission proposal would add €1,000–€3,000 to the cost of small cars, making European models uncompetitive against cheaper Chinese electric vehicles. Behind this was a strategic conflict: industry invested heavily in electric vehicles to meet the 2035 ban on combustion engines, and saw costly new Euro 7 standards as unnecessary regulatory burden on a sunset technology. Seven EU countries — led by Italy, the Czech Republic, and Hungary — backed the industry's position, warning of factory closures and job losses.

### Do the new EU pollution limits apply to cars already on the road?

No. Euro 7 applies only to new vehicles sold from the application dates — July 2030 for cars and vans, July 2031 for trucks and buses. Cars already on the road are not retroactively required to meet these standards, though EU countries may introduce low-emission zones restricting older vehicles, as London, Paris, and Berlin have already done with Euro 4 and Euro 5 restrictions.

### Can European cities still ban older diesel and petrol cars under the new EU air pollution rules?

Yes. Euro 7 updates the technical standard for type-approval — what a manufacturer must meet to sell a new vehicle — and does not prevent cities from setting their own low-emission zones or congestion charge policies that restrict older vehicles. Euro 7 does set a common EU framework, however, which means a car approved under Euro 7 cannot be barred from sale in any member state on emission grounds alone.
