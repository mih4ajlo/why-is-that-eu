## What is it?

The Generalised Scheme of Preferences (GSP) is the European Union’s long-standing programme that allows developing countries to export goods to the EU at reduced or zero tariffs, promoting economic growth and poverty reduction. The 2023 recast (2023/1010/EU) updates and replaces the previous 2012 regulation, tightening the conditions under which these trade benefits can be granted, suspended, or withdrawn.

The new regulation introduces three main changes: first, it accelerates the procedure for temporarily withdrawing preferences in cases of serious and systematic violations of human rights, labour rights, or environmental conventions. Second, it adds new sustainability criteria for the special incentive arrangement for sustainable development and good governance (GSP+), including climate change commitments. Third, it strengthens monitoring and reporting obligations, and allows the EU to act more swiftly when violations occur.

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst for the 2023 revision was a series of high-profile human rights crises in GSP beneficiary countries that exposed the shortcomings of the 2012 regulation. In February 2020, the EU launched a formal investigation into Cambodia under the previous GSP after a violent crackdown on opposition politicians, journalists, and trade unions. The investigation, which led to a partial withdrawal of preferences in August 2020 for the first time in the scheme’s history, took over a year to complete from start to final decision — a timeline many in the European Parliament and civil society considered far too slow.

The same structural tension appeared during the 2018 evaluation of the GSP, which the European Commission published in January 2018. The evaluation found that the existing withdrawal procedure was cumbersome, requiring multiple formal notices, hearings, and a three-step consultation process that could take 18 to 24 months. Meanwhile, the European Parliament’s Committee on International Trade (INTA) had repeatedly called for faster enforcement, particularly after the 2017 Rohingya crisis in Myanmar — another GSP beneficiary. In September 2018, the Parliament adopted a resolution urging the Commission to “make full use of the existing GSP withdrawal mechanisms” and to propose a revision that would shorten procedures.

The coalition that drove the recast was unusual: it united centre-right MEPs who wanted stricter conditionality for trade benefits, left-wing MEPs focused on labour rights, and the Greens who pushed for mandatory climate benchmarks. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) and NGOs like Human Rights Watch lobbied actively. The main opposition came from business associations such as BusinessEurope, which warned that faster withdrawal would create uncertainty for importers, and from several Member States that wanted to maintain diplomatic flexibility. The Council initially resisted shortening the timeline too much, but the Parliament’s negotiating team, led by rapporteur Bernd Lange (S&D, Germany), secured a compromise that reduced the total time from launch of an investigation to final decision to around six to nine months.

The timing was strategic. The Commission’s proposal was published in September 2021, halfway through the von der Leyen Commission’s mandate, at a moment when the EU’s trade policy was being reshaped by the “Open Strategic Autonomy” agenda. The 2020 Cambodia investigation had demonstrated that the tool existed but was too slow. Moreover, the COVID-19 pandemic had increased the EU’s focus on supply chain resilience and due diligence, making stricter conditionality politically palatable. The trilogue negotiations concluded in March 2023, and the regulation was adopted in May 2023, entering into force on 1 January 2024.

What changed from earlier failed attempts? Previous calls for reform had stalled because Member States feared alienating developing country partners or losing commercial access to raw materials. The specific event that unblocked the recast was the Commission’s own 2020 Cambodia withdrawal decision — the first ever — which proved the mechanism could work but needed to be faster. A second unblocking factor was the 2021 adoption of the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), which created political pressure to align GSP conditionality with climate goals.

## Timeline

- **2012** — Regulation (EU) No 978/2012 establishes the current three-tier GSP system (standard, GSP+, Everything But Arms for LDCs).  
- **January 2018** — European Commission publishes evaluation of GSP, finding withdrawal procedures too slow.  
- **September 2018** — European Parliament adopts resolution calling for faster withdrawal and stronger conditionality.  
- **February 2020** — Commission launches GSP withdrawal investigation into Cambodia over human rights violations.  
- **August 2020** — Commission partially withdraws Cambodia’s GSP preferences for certain garments and footwear, effective February 2021. This is the first withdrawal in GSP history.  
- **September 2021** — Commission proposes recast of the GSP Regulation, including faster withdrawal procedures.  
- **March 2023** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council in trilogue.  
- **19 May 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/1010 adopted by the Council.  
- **1 January 2024** — New regulation enters into force and applies.

## Impact and consequences

The 2023 GSP recast directly affects 21 standard GSP beneficiary countries and 8 GSP+ countries (as of 2024, according to the Commission’s list). The most immediate consequence is that the EU can now withdraw preferences for serious human rights violations within approximately six months instead of up to two years. This gives the Commission a more credible deterrent.

Industry sectors most exposed are textiles, footwear, and agriculture, which rely heavily on GSP preferences for imports from countries like Cambodia, Bangladesh (standard GSP), and Pakistan (GSP+). The European Apparel and Textile Confederation (EURATEX) expressed concern in consultations that faster withdrawal could disrupt supply chains, while importers of finished goods from Sri Lanka and the Philippines warned of sudden tariff spikes.

Since the regulation’s entry into force, no new withdrawal investigations have been launched as of mid-2025, but the framework has already shaped diplomatic behaviour. According to the Commission’s 2024 GSP report, several beneficiary countries initiated domestic labour law reforms after the regulation’s adoption, though the Commission could not attribute causality. The new climate conditionality initially faced resistance from GSP+ applicants such as Mongolia and Bolivia, who argued that their emissions per capita were already low.

The regulation also strengthens parliamentary oversight: the European Parliament now formally receives quarterly reports on GSP implementation, and can invite the Commissioner for Trade — currently Maroš Šefčovič — to discuss ongoing withdrawal procedures.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU cut trade preferences for a country that violates human rights?

Yes. The 2023 GSP regulation allows the European Commission to investigate serious and systematic violations of human or labour rights in any beneficiary country. If confirmed, the Commission can partially or fully withdraw tariff preferences. The procedure now takes about six to nine months from launch to final decision, faster than under the previous rules. The EU first exercised this power in 2020 against Cambodia, and the new regulation codifies and accelerates that process.

### What must a country do to get better trade terms under GSP+?

Countries with a lower per capita income can apply for the special incentive arrangement (GSP+). They must ratify and effectively implement 27 international conventions covering human rights, labour rights, environmental protection, and good governance. The 2023 recast added climate-related conventions, such as the Paris Agreement and biodiversity treaties. Applicants also need to accept a monitoring mission by the Commission and submit regular reports. Once granted, GSP+ provides duty-free access for about 66% of all tariff lines.

### Does the new regulation affect trade with China or other large exporters?

No. The GSP only applies to low- and lower-middle-income countries as classified by the World Bank. China was a GSP beneficiary until 2015 but has since graduated because its GDP per capita exceeded the threshold. The 2023 recast did not change graduation rules. However, the regulation indirectly affects trade dynamics because it strengthens conditionality for developing countries that compete with Chinese exports in sectors like textiles and electronics.

### Why did the EU make climate a condition for trade preferences?

The 2023 recast aligned the GSP with the European Green Deal and the EU’s goal of climate neutrality by 2050. The previous regulation required only ratification of environmental conventions, but the new version demands “effective implementation” and includes climate-specific benchmarks. This change was pushed by the European Parliament’s Greens/EFA group, who argued that trade preferences should not reward countries free-riding on climate action. The Council accepted it after the Commission demonstrated that virtually all GSP+ applicants already ratified the Paris Agreement, making the requirement low-cost diplomatically.
