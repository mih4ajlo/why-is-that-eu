## What is it?

In April 2023, the European Commission proposed the most ambitious overhaul of EU pharmaceutical legislation in over two decades. The reform package consists of a new directive and a new regulation replacing the foundational texts — Directive 2001/83/EC and Regulation (EC) No 726/2004 — that have governed how medicines are authorised, marketed, and monitored across the EU.

The core objective is to create a single, future-proof regulatory framework that balances four competing goals: ensuring timely patient access to innovative therapies, addressing unmet medical needs, tackling antimicrobial resistance (AMR), and maintaining Europe’s competitiveness in global pharmaceutical R&D. The package introduces a variable data protection and market exclusivity system — rewarding companies that develop therapies for unmet needs or launch medicines in all 27 member states, while reducing incentives for less innovative products.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform was triggered by a stark failure of the existing system that had become undeniable by the early 2020s: patients in smaller or wealthier EU countries waited years for the same innovative medicines available in Germany or France. In 2020, the European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA) acknowledged that the average time between an EU-wide marketing authorisation and a medicine’s national launch was between 2 and 3 years in some member states, and in several Central and Eastern European countries, novel therapies were never launched at all. The European Commission’s 2020 Pharmaceutical Strategy — launched under Commissioners Stella Kyriakides (Health) and Thierry Breton (Internal Market) — explicitly cited this as a structural failure.

The immediate political driver was the COVID-19 pandemic, which exposed both the strengths and weaknesses of EU pharmaceutical governance. The rapid development of mRNA vaccines showed what could be achieved with coordinated investment, but the chaotic roll-out of treatments and vaccines — with member states hoarding doses and negotiating separate contracts — revealed the absence of a genuinely integrated market. The 2021 EU health emergency preparedness authority (HERA) proposal and the 2019 failed attempt at health technology assessment reform underscored the deep divisions between member states on pharmaceutical pricing and access.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. A broad majority of the European Parliament supported stronger access obligations, including the Socialists & Democrats (S&D) and the Greens, who had long argued that the existing system created windfall profits for pharmaceutical companies. The European Commission’s 2021 public consultation received over 1,500 responses, with patient organisations and health insurers overwhelmingly backing mandatory launch obligations. Industry opposition was led by EFPIA and the European Confederation of Pharmaceutical Entrepreneurs (EUCOPE), warning that shorter regulatory data protection would reduce R&D investment. A 2022 study by the European Medicines Agency (EMA) found that the top 20 pharmaceutical companies had invested €58 billion in R&D in 2021, and EFPIA’s then-Director General Nathalie Moll publicly cautioned that the reforms risked “moving Europe from a global leader to a second-tier market.”

The key moment that broke the deadlock was the 2022 European Parliament resolution on the European Health Union, which — with 546 votes in favour, 85 against — explicitly called for a legislation that “ensures timely and equitable access to medicines in all member states.” The Russian invasion of Ukraine further concentrated minds: it underscored the strategic vulnerability of supply chains for active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), 80% of which came from China and India, according to EMA data.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Directive 2001/83/EC comes into force, codifying 30 years of case law and national rules; sets the framework for medicine authorisation across the EU
- **2004** — Regulation (EC) No 726/2004 centralises marketing authorisation for certain medicines via the newly created European Medicines Agency (EMA)
- **2017** — The European Commission launches a public consultation on the future of EU pharmaceutical regulation, highlighting delays in patient access — the average time to national launch is 2-3 years in some states
- **2020** — The Commission publishes the Pharmaceutical Strategy for Europe, announcing intent to revise legislation; COVID-19 accelerates the debate
- **2021** — Commission launches an open public consultation on the revision; over 1,500 responses submitted
- **2022** — European Parliament resolution on the European Health Union calls for binding access obligations; Russian invasion of Ukraine highlights API supply chain vulnerabilities
- **April 2023** — Commission formally adopts the proposed directive and regulation; they enter the legislative process in the Council and Parliament
- **2024** — Ongoing negotiations in the Council; health ministers from Central and Eastern European states push for stronger launch obligations, while Germany and France seek to preserve industry flexibility

## Impact and consequences

If adopted, the reform will fundamentally restructure the incentives for pharmaceutical companies in the EU. The most consequential change is the variable data protection period: the standard 8 years of data exclusivity can be extended by up to 12 months for therapies addressing unmet medical needs (defined as serious diseases with no existing treatment), and by another 12 months if the medicine is launched in all 27 member states. Conversely, companies that delay launches in smaller markets would lose up to 2 years of data protection — a powerful financial penalty.

The European Commission’s 2023 impact assessment estimated that the reforms could improve patient access in low-launch-rate member states by 30-50% within 10 years. However, industry analysts at Evaluate Pharma warned in 2023 that the variable data protection could reduce EU pharmaceutical R&D investment by 2-3% over the same period, as companies might prioritise launch in the US or Japan.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is addressed through a novel transferable exclusivity voucher: companies that develop a new antibiotic can receive an extra year of data protection for a different medicine of their choice. The EMA estimated in its 2022 AMR progress report that only 2 of the 32 antibiotics in late-stage development target the most critical WHO priority pathogens.

The reforms also introduce mandatory environmental risk assessments for all new medicines — a response to growing evidence of pharmaceutical pollution in European rivers, with the European Environment Agency (EEA) reporting in 2020 that traces of 17 different active substances were found in 37% of EU water bodies.

## Key questions answered

### Why do some EU countries get new drugs years later than others?

Because pharmaceutical companies — particularly after securing an EU-wide marketing authorisation from the EMA — have no legal obligation to launch a medicine in every member state. In practice, they prioritise larger, wealthier markets like Germany, France, and the UK, where national health systems pay higher prices, and often skip or substantially delay launch in smaller markets like the Baltic states, Croatia, or Slovakia. The 2020 Pharmaceutical Strategy cited an average 2-3 year delay for novel therapies in Eastern Europe, with some cancer drugs never receiving national pricing negotiations at all. The 2023 reforms aim to fix this by making data protection periods contingent on launching across all 27 member states.

### How does the EU encourage companies to develop new antibiotics?

Antibiotic development has been commercially unattractive for decades because new drugs are used sparingly to preserve their efficacy, limiting sales. The 2023 reform introduces a transferable exclusivity voucher: a company that obtains a marketing authorisation for a new antibiotic addressing a priority pathogen receives an extra year of data protection for a different, commercially successful medicine of their choice. This voucher can also be sold to another company. The EMA estimates that humanity at large stands to lose the effectiveness of most antibiotics by 2030, and that only 2 of the 32 late-stage antibiotic candidates target the most critical WHO priority pathogens — making this aggressive incentive politically justifiable.

### What happens to EU pharmaceutical regulation after the UK left?

The UK's departure from the EU in 2020 removed one of the largest pharmaceutical markets from the single market and one of the most influential voices in regulatory negotiations. The UK had often supported a more flexible, industry-friendly framework. With the UK absent, the coalition of member states pushing for stronger access obligations and shorter data protection — led by Southern and Eastern European countries — gained relative weight, according to a 2022 analysis by the European Policy Centre. The EMA relocated from London to Amsterdam in 2019, and the reform package now proceeds without British influence, although the UK's Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) continues to work closely with the EMA on scientific standards.

### Will the reforms raise medicine prices in the EU?

The European Commission’s 2023 impact assessment predicts that the mandatory launch obligations — requiring companies to bring medicines to all 27 member states within 2 years — will not significantly increase overall prices because larger markets already pay more, and the spread of data protection incentives is designed to be cost-neutral overall. However, independent analysts from the European Public Health Alliance (EPHA) warned in 2022 that the transferable exclusivity voucher for antibiotics could be gamed: a large pharmaceutical company could buy a small biotech’s voucher and extend exclusivity on a blockbuster drug, postponing generic competition and raising public health system costs by an estimated €2-4 billion over the voucher’s lifetime.
