## What is it?

The Eurojust Regulation (2018/1727/EU) establishes the European Union Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation—commonly called Eurojust—as a formal EU agency based in The Hague. Its job is to help national prosecutors and judges work together when a crime involves more than one EU country. That includes terrorism, drug trafficking, cybercrime, human trafficking, money laundering, and fraud against the EU budget.

Eurojust does not investigate or prosecute cases itself. Instead, it acts as a matchmaker and coordinator: it can convene national authorities, help set up joint investigation teams, store and share evidence, and issue written opinions or requests to start an investigation or accept a prosecution. Member states are not legally obliged to follow those requests, but they must give reasons if they refuse. The regulation replaced a looser intergovernmental structure created by a Council Decision in 2002, giving Eurojust a proper legal basis under the Lisbon Treaty and subjecting it to the full accountability rules of an EU agency.

## Why was it introduced?

The original Eurojust was created by Council Decision 2002/187/JHA in the aftermath of the 9/11 attacks, when EU leaders realised that no single body existed to help prosecutors coordinate across borders. It was a small unit housed within the Council, not an agency, and its members had limited powers and weak data-sharing rules. The Lisbon Treaty, which entered into force in December 2009, changed the game: it mandated that Eurojust be established by a regulation adopted through the ordinary legislative procedure, giving the European Parliament equal say for the first time. The Treaty also required that the agency's structure, tasks, and data protection rules be fully spelled out in law.

But the political push for a stronger Eurojust collided with member state reluctance to cede sovereignty. For years, national justice ministries—especially from bigger states like Germany and France—resisted giving Eurojust any binding authority to overrule national prosecutors. Meanwhile, the European Parliament demanded stronger parliamentary oversight, stricter data protection, and clearer rules on cooperation with third countries like the United States, which had been a flashpoint since the EU-U.S. Mutual Legal Assistance Agreement in 2003.

What broke the logjam was a double trigger: the growing volume and complexity of cross-border crime, and the adoption of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in 2016. The Commission's 2013 evaluation of Eurojust found that its legal framework was "fragmented, outdated, and insufficient to meet operational needs." Specifically, the lack of a common data protection regime for judicial cooperation meant that exchanges of sensitive evidence were often slowed by incompatible national rules. The GDPR's framework for law enforcement data processing (Directive 2016/680) provided a template. The Parliament's rapporteur, Dutch MEP Judith Sargentini (Greens/EFA), pushed hard for strong data protection and democratic accountability. The final compromise, reached under the Juncker Commission with Commissioner for Justice Vera Jourová steering the file, gave Eurojust an independent data protection officer and a clear hierarchy with the European Parliament's oversight.

Three years of negotiations produced a text that passed the European Parliament on 4 July 2018 with 587 votes in favour, 17 against, and 36 abstentions. The Council adopted it on 14 November 2018. The regulation took effect on 12 December 2019, fully replacing the old Council Decision.

## Timeline

- **2002** — Council Decision 2002/187/JHA establishes Eurojust as an intergovernmental unit.
- **2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force, requiring a new legal basis for Eurojust and giving the Parliament co-decision powers.
- **2013** — The European Commission publishes an evaluation identifying shortcomings in Eurojust's legal framework and proposes a new regulation.
- **2016** — Adoption of GDPR and the Law Enforcement Directive (2016/680) sets the stage for updated data protection rules in judicial cooperation.
- **4 July 2018** — European Parliament adopts the Eurojust Regulation (587 votes in favour).
- **14 November 2018** — Council adopts the regulation.
- **12 December 2019** — Regulation applies, replacing the old Council Decision. Eurojust becomes a formal EU agency.
- **2020–2022** — Eurojust expands its third-country liaison network: agreements with the United States, Switzerland, Ukraine, and others are updated to match the new framework.
- **2022** — Eurojust sets up a joint investigation team (JIT) on war crimes in Ukraine within weeks of the Russian invasion, demonstrating the new procedural speed.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation gave Eurojust a stable budget, a clearer mandate, and stronger data protection rules. It also settled a long-running dispute over the role of national members: they are now full employees of the agency, not simply seconded national officials. This reduced conflicts of interest and improved internal coherence.

Operationally, the biggest change was the power to ask a member state to open an investigation or accept a prosecution when another country wants to hand over a case. In 2022, Eurojust reported that it mediated 62 jurisdictional conflicts and issued 12 written requests to start investigations. All were followed. That is a qualitative improvement over the old system where such requests carried little weight.

For data sharing, the regulation required Eurojust to comply with the GDPR-level rules in the Law Enforcement Directive. This allowed the agency to process larger volumes of evidence while maintaining privacy safeguards. It also unlocked cooperation with non-EU countries: the US, Norway, and Switzerland have all updated their liaison agreements since 2019.

The main challenge has been staffing. Eurojust had 236 staff in 2015; by 2023 it had grown to over 400, but member states have been slow to second prosecutors to the agency, especially from smaller countries. The regulation's requirement that each member state post at least one national member is being complied with, but some capitals still treat the post as a junior assignment.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU stop criminals from evading justice by moving between countries?

When a cross-border crime is detected—say, a drug trafficking network operating in Spain and the Netherlands—national prosecutors can ask Eurojust to coordinate. Eurojust can convene a coordination meeting, set up a joint investigation team, and help resolve conflicts over which country should prosecute. If prosecutors in one member state refuse to act on another's request, Eurojust can issue a formal opinion, and while it cannot force action, the refusal must be explained. This creates political and legal pressure to cooperate.

### Can Eurojust force a national prosecutor to start an investigation?

No. The regulation gives Eurojust the power to *request* that a member state start an investigation, accept a prosecution, or set up a joint team. If the member state refuses, it must give reasons. This is less than what the European Parliament originally wanted (a binding power to order investigations), but member states insisted on preserving sovereignty over criminal procedure. In practice, Eurojust's requests are almost always followed because refusing without good cause damages trust and can jeopardise future cooperation from other countries.

### How does Eurojust share evidence with non-EU countries like the United States?

The regulation allows Eurojust to exchange personal data with third countries if they provide an adequate level of data protection—similar to the GDPR adequacy mechanism. The agency has signed cooperation agreements with the US, Norway, Switzerland, Ukraine, and others. These agreements set limits: for example, data transferred to the US cannot be used for national security purposes unrelated to the original investigation. Eurojust also hosts liaison prosecutors from the US (since 2006) and from other partner states, which streamlines evidence sharing.

### Why did it take nine years to replace the original Eurojust after the Lisbon Treaty?

The Lisbon Treaty required a regulation, but the Parliament and Council spent years haggling over three core issues: the extent of Eurojust's powers (binding vs. non-binding requests), data protection rules, and third-country cooperation. The European Parliament wanted strong oversight and explicit bans on transferring data to countries with poor human rights records. Some member states, especially Germany and the UK (before Brexit), feared that a stronger Eurojust would encroach on national judicial independence. Only after the GDPR created a shared data protection standard and after several high-profile terrorist attacks in 2015–2016 demonstrated the need for faster cross-border coordination did the political will solidify.
