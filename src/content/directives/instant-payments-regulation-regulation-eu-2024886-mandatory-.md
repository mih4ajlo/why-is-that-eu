## What is it?

The Instant Payments Regulation (IPR, 2024/886/EU) mandates that all payment service providers in the euro area — banks, fintechs, and other authorised firms — must offer instant euro credit transfers 24/7, 365 days a year, with funds arriving in the recipient's account within ten seconds. The regulation also caps processing fees at the same level as standard credit transfers, ending years of premium pricing for speed.

The regulation builds on the Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) Instant Credit Transfer scheme from 2017, which was voluntary. By 9 January 2025, euro-area banks must be able to receive instant payments; by 9 October 2025, they must be able to send them. Non-euro EU countries (e.g., Sweden, Poland, Hungary) have tighter deadlines and need not join the scheme if their domestic currency is not covered, though most are expected to opt in.

For consumers, this means that a transfer from a Greek bank account to a German one should arrive in seconds, not one or two business days. For businesses, it enables instant settlement of invoices, payroll, and e-commerce transactions. The regulation also requires payment confirmation — verifying that the recipient's IBAN and name match — reducing fraud risk.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation's immediate trigger was a fundamental failure in the EU's payment infrastructure: even seven years after the launch of the voluntary SEPA Instant Credit Transfer (SCT Inst) scheme in November 2017, only about 70 percent of euro-area payment service providers had joined it, and real-time payments accounted for just 2 percent of all euro transfers according to the European Commission's 2022 impact assessment. The voluntary system had left huge gaps — Deutsche Bank in Germany did not offer outgoing instant payments until late 2023, and many smaller banks in Southern and Eastern Europe never signed up. The structural tension: a single market for payments existed on paper but not in practice, with cross-border transfers still taking days in an era of 4G internet.

The political coalition that pushed this through was a classic "consumer and pro-business" alliance: the European Parliament's leading rapporteurs — German MEP **Stefan Berger** (EPP, centre-right) and Spanish MEP **Ondřej Kovařík** (Renew, centrist) — framed the legislation as both protecting consumers from slow payment fees and helping EU companies compete with American fintechs like Stripe and PayPal. The European Banking Authority (EBA) had long signalled support, while the European Payments Council (the industry body operating SEPA) was internally divided: large banks in France and Germany saw instant processing as a cost burden, while Nordic and Baltic banks, which already offered near-instant payments, pushed for mandatory adoption.

The opposition came primarily from the **German Savings Banks Association (DSGV)** and the **French Banking Federation (FBF)**. They argued that mandatory instant payments would force small and regional banks to invest heavily in 24/7 IT infrastructure and anti-fraud systems without clear demand from customers. The German banking lobby specifically warned of "operational risks" and job losses among back-office staff who manually process slow transfers. In the Council, Germany and Austria raised last-minute objections in early 2023, demanding longer transition periods for non-euro states. The **European Council's final vote in December 2023** was not close — the regulation passed with 27 member states in favour, with Germany and Austria ultimately abstaining rather than blocking — reflecting a consensus that the single market's payment fragmentation had become economically indefensible.

The timing was critical. The regulation was proposed by the **von der Leyen Commission** in October 2022, less than six months after the **European Central Bank (ECB)** warned publicly that the slow adoption of instant payments was "undermining the euro's role as a global payment currency" — a direct reference to risks of currency substitution and loss of monetary sovereignty in a digital age. The ECB's **Fabio Panetta**, then executive board member, had explicitly linked instant payments to the success of the digital euro project, noting that without a viable instant payment layer, the digital euro's offline functionality would be meaningless. The political salience was further heightened by the **energy crisis of 2022**: the Commission argued that instant payments could accelerate supply chain payments for businesses squeezed by delayed bank transfers during the crisis.

What changed the dynamic from earlier failed attempts was a **concrete scandal and competitive shock**. In September 2020, **Revolut**, the UK-based fintech with 15 million European users, announced it would no longer accept incoming SEPA transfers from German banks because Deutsche Bank's slow processing caused "operational chaos". The incident, widely reported in German media (Handelsblatt, 16 September 2020), embarrassed Berlin and demonstrated that voluntary schemes were insufficient. Simultaneously, **Stripe** and **PayPal** were expanding instant payout services in the US, while EU-based e-commerce platforms like **Zalando** complained that slow bank transfers cost them sales — Zalando specifically estimated that 5 percent of checkout abandonments were due to payment delays. The Commission's 2022 impact assessment quantified the broader cost: EU businesses lose €12 billion annually from late payments, a significant fraction of which relates to slow cross-border transfers.

## Timeline

- **2008** — The Eurosystem (ECB and national central banks) launches TARGET2, the real-time gross settlement system for large-value euro payments, but retail payments remain slow.
- **2017** — The European Payments Council launches the voluntary SEPA Instant Credit Transfer (SCT Inst) scheme on 21 November; only a handful of banks join immediately.
- **2020** — Revolut blocks incoming German transfers, publicly blaming Deutsche Bank's slow processing. EU e-commerce complaints about slow payments intensify.
- **2022, 12 July** — The European Commission publishes its Retail Payments Strategy evaluation, finding that only 2% of euro transfers are instant; the Commission pledges to make instant payments mandatory.
- **2022, 26 October** — The von der Leyen Commission formally proposes the Instant Payments Regulation, including mandatory requirements for receiving (Q4 2024) and sending instant payments (Q4 2025).
- **2023, February** — The European Parliament's ECON Committee votes 38-6 in favour with 2 abstentions; amendments include capping fees at standard transfer levels.
- **2023, 25 October** — The Parliament adopts its position in plenary by a large majority (546-45, with 31 abstentions).
- **2023, 18 December** — The Council of the EU adopts the regulation without amendment; Germany and Austria abstain, but no member state votes against.
- **2024, 19 March** — The Instant Payments Regulation is published in the Official Journal as Regulation (EU) 2024/886.
- **2024, 8 April** — The regulation enters into force.
- **2025, 9 January** — Deadline for euro-area banks to be able to receive instant payments.
- **2025, 9 October** — Deadline for euro-area banks to be able to send instant payments.
- **2025, 9 July** — Deadline for non-euro EU countries to implement receiving capability for euro instant transfers (if they opt in).
- **2027, 9 July** — Full compliance for non-euro EU countries (sending capability).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects approximately **3,000 payment service providers** in the euro area — from massive institutions like BNP Paribas and Santander to tiny local savings banks. The ECB estimated in a 2023 report that compliance costs would range from €50,000 to €500,000 per institution, depending on existing infrastructure. The **German Banking Industry Committee** (consisting of the DSGV, BdB, and BVR) projected total costs of €2.5 billion across all German banks, though the Commission disputes this figure, citing a 2022 impact assessment showing payback within four years from fee reduction.

Industry response has been mixed. **Nordic banks** like Swedbank and SEB, which already offered near-instant payments, welcomed the regulation as levelling the playing field. **Southern European banks** — particularly Italian and Greek institutions where instant penetration was below 30% — complained about tight deadlines. Deutsche Bank announced in December 2023 it would upgrade its core banking system by mid-2025 to comply.

The first enforcement actions are expected in early 2025. The **EBA** will monitor compliance, and national competent authorities (e.g., BaFin in Germany, the Bank of Italy, Banque de France) can impose penalties up to 10% of annual turnover for non-compliance. However, the EBA has signalled it will allow a phase-in period for smaller institutions.

Countries most likely to struggle with compliance are those with fragmented banking sectors and lower digital infrastructure: Germany (with over 1,000 Volksbanken and Sparkassen), Italy (with hundreds of cooperative banks), and Greece. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment specifically warned that "member states with high numbers of small regional financial institutions face the greatest implementation challenge." The regulation does allow member states to temporarily exempt micro-enterprises (fewer than 10 employees, balance sheet under €2 million) for up to 18 months after the main deadlines.

A significant unintended consequence may emerge for **payment fraud**. While the regulation requires payment confirmation (name/IBAN matching) to reduce fraud, it does not change the liability rules: unlike card payments, instant transfers have no chargeback mechanism. Consumer groups, notably BEUC (the European Consumer Organisation), have warned that this could shift fraud losses to consumers, particularly in cases where scammers trick victims into sending instant transfers to accounts that appear legitimate. The Commission promised to revisit fraud liability rules by 2026.

## Key questions answered

### Can a bank charge me extra for sending money instantly?

No. The regulation bans any fee for instant payments that exceeds the fee for a standard credit transfer. If your bank charges €0 for a standard transfer, instant transfers must also be free. If they charge €1 for standard transfers, instant transfers cannot cost more than €1. This ended the practice of many banks charging €5-10 for real-time transfers, a premium of up to 500%.

### Does every EU country have to allow instant transfers from my bank account?

Yes, but with a timeline. All euro-area member states (20 countries) must comply by October 2025. Non-euro EU countries (Denmark, Sweden, Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic, Bulgaria, Romania) have until July 2027 to offer outgoing euro instant payments, and they can choose not to join the euro instant scheme if their domestic currency is not covered. But if you hold a euro account in, say, Sweden, it must be reachable for instant payments from Germany by July 2026.

### What happens if my bank doesn't offer instant payments by the deadline?

Your bank will be in violation of EU law and can face fines from its national regulator — in Germany, BaFin can impose penalties up to 10% of the bank's annual turnover. More practically, the bank will be forced to stop processing your standard euro transfers until it upgrades its systems, because the regulation prohibits offering only slow transfers after the deadline. This effectively compels compliance within the transition period.

### Is my money safer with instant payments than with a regular bank transfer?

Not automatically. The regulation requires a new "confirmation of payee" check — your bank must verify that the IBAN and recipient name match before the transfer is sent, reducing the risk of misdirected payments. This is similar to the UK's Confirmation of Payee system, which has reduced fraud by about 30%. However, instant transfers remain irreversible: if you send money to a scammer, your bank cannot reverse it. Unlike credit card transactions (which have chargeback rights under the Payment Services Directive), instant transfers have no statutory fraud reimbursement. Consumer groups are pressing the Commission to address this gap.
