## What is it?

eIDAS (Electronic Identification and Trust Services) is an EU Regulation that sets uniform rules for electronic transactions. Its core achievement is to make a qualified electronic signature legally equivalent to a handwritten signature in every member state, and to create a single market for trust services — including e‑signatures, e‑seals, e‑time stamps, registered electronic delivery, and website authentication certificates.

The Regulation also establishes a framework for mutual recognition of national electronic identification (eID) schemes. Any eID scheme notified by a member state under eIDAS must be accepted by all other member states for accessing public services online, provided it meets defined assurance levels (low, substantial, high).

## Why was it introduced?

The eIDAS Regulation was a direct response to the failure of the earlier Electronic Signatures Directive (1999/93/EC). That directive had aimed to enable cross‑border e‑signatures but left so much discretion to national laws that fragmentation persisted. A 2012 European Commission impact assessment noted that cross‑border use of electronic signatures remained negligible — business‑to‑business, citizen‑to‑government, and even intra‑company transactions were still routinely handled on paper when they crossed borders.

The structural tension was clear: the existing patchwork of national e‑signature laws contradicted the core promise of the Digital Single Market. A company that invested in an e‑signature solution compliant with German law could not use it in France or Poland without additional legal risk. This uncertainty hurt Europe’s digital competitiveness. According to the Commission’s 2011 legislative proposal, the lack of interoperability and legal certainty was costing the EU economy billions of euros annually in lost efficiency.

Political momentum built during the Presidency of José Manuel Barroso, with Neelie Kroes, the Digital Agenda Commissioner, making eIDAS a flagship initiative. The 2010 Digital Agenda for Europe had already identified the creation of a "trusted digital environment" as a priority. The 2013 EU Cybersecurity Strategy reinforced the need for trustworthy authentication services. But the real breakthrough in timing came from two factors: first, the economic crisis pushed governments to seek cost‑savings via digital public services; second, the Lisbon Treaty (in force 2009) gave the EU stronger competence to harmonise the single market, allowing a Regulation (directly binding) instead of a Directive.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. Large technology vendors (e.g., Microsoft, SAP, Siemens) and telecom operators lobbied for a rule that would standardise trust services and open up national markets. They were joined by the European Banking Federation, which saw e‑signatures as essential for digital banking across borders. The opposition came mostly from member states that had already developed national eID systems (e.g., Belgium, Estonia, Austria) and feared losing autonomy over their standards. Estonia, in particular, worried that a uniform EU rule could weaken its world‑leading e‑residency programme. The compromise was the “notified scheme” model: each member state can keep its own eID system, but if it chooses to notify it under eIDAS, all others must accept it for public services.

The Parliament’s rapporteur, Romanian MEP Silvia‑Adriana Țicău, pushed for strong mutual recognition and for making qualified trust services the default legal standard. The final Regulation was adopted in July 2014, replacing the 1999 Directive from July 2016. What changed the deadlock was the combination of the economic crisis and the Commission’s ability to frame eIDAS as a jobs‑and‑growth measure. By 2013, national governments had realised that harmonised electronic identification could unlock €50 billion in e‑government savings and cut administrative burdens for businesses, according to the Commission’s 2012 impact assessment.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Electronic Signatures Directive (1999/93/EC) adopted. It leaves significant flexibility to member states, leading to fragmented implementation.
- **2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force, strengthening EU competence for internal market harmonisation via regulations.
- **2010** — Digital Agenda for Europe published, calling for a “trusted digital environment” across the EU.
- **2011** — Commission departs from a revision of the 1999 Directive and instead proposes a full Regulation covering both e‑identification and trust services.
- **2013** — EU Cybersecurity Strategy published, endorsing the proposed eIDAS framework.
- **2014** — Regulation (EU) No 910/2014 adopted by the European Parliament and Council (vote in Parliament: 423‑111 with 95 abstentions).
- **2016** — eIDAS enters into force (July 1), fully replacing the 1999 Directive. Trust services provisions become directly applicable.
- **2016–2018** — Member states begin notifying their eID schemes. The first notifications are approved (e.g., Estonia, Belgium, Germany).
- **2018** — The eIDAS Cooperation Network, an intergovernmental body, is established to coordinate implementation.
- **2020** — By year‑end, only 14 member states have fully notified at least one eID scheme, according to an EU Commission progress report.
- **2021** — The Commission launches a review of eIDAS, aiming to expand its scope to cover identity wallets and private‑sector use (leading to the eIDAS 2.0 proposal).
- **2023** — The European Commission proposes the revised eIDAS Regulation (eIDAS 2), including the European Digital Identity Wallet.

## Impact and consequences

eIDAS has been a modest but real enabler of the Digital Single Market. By 2020, 14 member states had notified 23 eID schemes — making it possible for citizens from, say, Estonia to access Portuguese public services online using their national eID card. The Regulation also created a single EU trust mark for qualified trust services, reducing consumer uncertainty.

Industry response was mixed. Large enterprises — particularly in banking, insurance, and logistics — invested heavily in compliant e‑signature solutions, often using the qualified mark for contractual documentation. Small and medium‑sized enterprises (SMEs) initially found the rules complex and costly. According to a 2019 study by the European Commission’s Joint Research Centre, the cost of implementing eIDAS compliance for SMEs could be on the order of €10,000–€50,000, depending on the type of trust service.

The biggest criticism has been the slow pace of eID notification. As of early 2023, only 14 member states had notified eID schemes; the rest relied on trust services alone. This has limited the cross‑border use of national eIDs for private‑sector transactions (e.g., opening a bank account in another country). The EU’s own e‑Government Benchmark report for 2022 found that only about 60% of cross‑border e‑government services are fully available online — a figure that has stagnated.

## Key questions answered

### Can I use my national electronic ID to log into government websites in other EU countries?

Yes, but only if your country has notified its eID scheme under eIDAS and the website you are trying to access accepts eIDAS‑notified identities. By mid‑2023, about 14 member states had notified one or more eID systems, so the coverage is not yet universal. For example, an Estonian identity card works for many e‑government services in Belgium, Germany, and Italy, but a Spanish electronic ID may not be accepted in all other countries.

### Are electronic signatures legally valid across the entire EU?

A qualified electronic signature (the highest level under eIDAS) is automatically recognised as legally equivalent to a handwritten signature in every member state. This means a contract signed with a qualified e‑signature in France is enforceable in Romania without further evidence. Advanced and simple electronic signatures are also valid, but their legal effect may still be challenged depending on national law.

### What is the difference between a simple, advanced, and qualified electronic signature?

A simple signature is any data in electronic form attached to other data (e.g., typing a name at the bottom of an email). An advanced signature is uniquely linked to the signatory, capable of identifying the signer, and created with data under the signatory’s sole control (e.g., using a secure private key). A qualified signature is an advanced signature created by a qualified signature creation device and based on a qualified certificate issued by a qualified trust service provider (who is audited and supervised by a national supervisory body). Only the qualified level enjoys automatic cross‑border legal equivalence.

### Why did it take so long to get a European eID system in place?

The core obstacle was national reluctance to cede control over identity systems, which many member states see as sensitive for security and public administration. The 1999 Directive failed because it only recommended mutual recognition. eIDAS succeeded because it offered a “notified scheme” model — member states could keep their own national eID but had to make it interoperable if they wanted mutual recognition. Even so, implementation has been slow, with only 14 notifications by 2020, largely because many countries lacked technical interoperability standards or were still modernising their identity infrastructure.
