## What is it?

The New Genomic Techniques (NGT) Regulation creates a two-tier system for plants developed using modern gene-editing tools like CRISPR-Cas9. Under the new rules, plants produced through targeted mutagenesis (making small, directed changes to an organism's own DNA) or cisgenesis (inserting DNA from a sexually compatible species) can be classified as "Category 1 NGT plants" if they could theoretically have been produced through conventional breeding or occur naturally. These Category 1 plants are exempt from the European Union's long-standing GMO labelling and traceability requirements that apply to "Category 2 NGT plants" and all transgenic GMOs.

Category 1 NGT plants are treated essentially the same as conventionally bred plants. They do not require risk assessment, labelling, or traceability obligations. Category 2 NGT plants, which involve more complex genetic modifications, remain subject to the existing GMO Directive 2001/18/EC, including mandatory risk assessment, labelling, and traceability. The regulation allows for an "adaptive" process to move plants from Category 2 to Category 1 as scientific understanding evolves. Seeds and propagating material for Category 1 NGT plants must be registered, but the final food product does not require GMO labelling. Member states are allowed to adopt national measures restricting or prohibiting the cultivation of Category 2 NGT plants on their territory.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a fundamental structural tension: the EU's GMO laws, written in 2001, predated the modern gene-editing revolution. The 2018 ruling by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) in *Confédération Paysanne* (Case C-528/16) had classified all organisms produced by mutagenesis as GMOs, regardless of whether they involved old techniques (like chemical mutagenesis, long used in organic breeding) or new targeted genomic techniques. This created an absurd outcome: plants with thousands of random mutations from chemical mutagenesis remained unregulated, while a plant with a single precise, predictable edit using CRISPR fell under the full, costly GMO authorization process — which cost between €10-20 million and took 5-10 years per product, according to a 2021 Commission study.

The coalition that pushed for the new regulation was unusual. It included large plant-breeding companies like Corteva, BASF, Bayer, and Syngenta, which had invested billions in CRISPR technology and faced a regulatory dead end. They were joined by the European Seed Association (Euroseeds) and the Commission's own Directorate-General for Health and Food Safety (DG SANTE), which had concluded by 2021 that the current regime was "unsustainable" for innovation. Notably, many smaller biotech companies and public research institutes — like the Max Planck Society and INRAE — also argued that the CJEU ruling was blocking climate-resilient crop research. The European Parliament's 2022 own-initiative report (adopted 307-263) called for a regulatory overhaul.

The opposition was equally determined. Environmental groups including Greenpeace, Friends of the Earth Europe, and Testbiotech, along with the organic farming movement (IFOAM Organics Europe), argued that any relaxation of GMO rules would destroy the organic sector's "GMO-free" label and lead to patenting of natural genes. They pointed to a 2023 Eurobarometer survey showing 74% of EU citizens concerned about gene-edited foods. The European Parliament's Greens/EFA group, along with some left-wing and conservative MEPs, attempted to block the regulation, nearly succeeding in the Environment Committee (ENVI) vote in January 2024, where the majority was just 4 votes.

The timing was decisive. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment had concluded that the EU could lose €1.5-3 billion in potential agricultural benefits over the next decade if the rules were not changed. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 had exposed the EU's vulnerability to fertilizer and grain supply disruptions, creating a new food security argument. The French government, historically aligned with the organic lobby, shifted position under the Macron administration to support "innovation-friendly" regulation. The Belgian EU Presidency pushed the file through in 2024, and the final Council vote in December 2024 saw 24 member states in favour, with only Poland, Austria, and Luxembourg opposed.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Directive 2001/18/EC on deliberate release of GMOs is adopted, unable to foresee modern gene-editing techniques.
- **2018** — CJEU *Confédération Paysanne* ruling (C-528/16) classifies all mutagenesis-derived plants as GMOs, creating a regulatory gap.
- **2021** — European Commission publishes a study concluding that NGT products could contribute to sustainability goals, while the current rules block innovation.
- **2022** — European Parliament adopts own-initiative report (307-263) calling for a new legal framework for NGTs.
- **July 2023** — Commission tables the NGT Regulation proposal.
- **January 2024** — ENVI Committee of the European Parliament votes narrowly (45-41) to maintain the exemption for Category 1 plants.
- **April 2024** — European Parliament adopts its negotiating mandate with 307 votes in favour.
- **December 2024** — Council formally adopts the regulation, with 24 member states voting in favour.
- **2025** — Regulation enters into force. First Category 1 NGT applications expected.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation is estimated to affect over 100,000 seed varieties in the EU according to Euroseeds. The first wave of Category 1 NGT plants likely includes disease-resistant potatoes, drought-tolerant wheat, and high-lysine maize. The European Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated the regulation could reduce pesticide use by 10-15% by enabling fungal- and virus-resistant varieties.

The organic sector faces an existential challenge: IFOAM Organics Europe argues that co-existence of organic and Category 1 NGT fields is impossible without 100% traceability, which the regulation does not provide. Several organic certification bodies have threatened to lobby member states to use the new opt-out clause for Category 2 plants more aggressively.

The seed industry is reconfiguring: Bayer and Corteva have already announced accelerated CRISPR breeding programmes. Small biotech startups, previously relocating to the United Kingdom or Brazil, have announced plans to return to the EU. The regulation also includes a patent ban on NGT use for breeders — but only for Category 1 plants — which has already sparked legal debate, as companies like BASF hold hundreds of CRISPR patents.

## Key questions answered

### Are gene-edited foods labelled differently from genetically modified foods in the EU?

No — if a gene-edited plant qualifies as Category 1, meaning its genetic changes could theoretically occur naturally or through conventional breeding, its food products do not require GMO labels. This includes many traits like disease resistance or improved nutrition. Only Category 2 NGT plants, which involve more complex edits, remain subject to full GMO labelling, risk assessment, and traceability requirements.

### Why did the EU create separate rules for gene-edited plants instead of treating them like GMOs?

Because the European Court of Justice's 2018 ruling classified all gene-edited plants as GMOs, which created a situation where a modern, precise technique faced the same extremely costly and slow authorization process as older, less predictable genetic modification. The Commission concluded this was blocking research into crops that could help meet climate and food security goals, and a new legal category was needed to separate "safe" targeted edits from more complex GMOs.

### Can individual EU countries stop farmers from growing gene-edited crops?

Yes, but only for Category 2 NGT plants — those with more complex genetic modifications. Member states can adopt national measures to restrict or prohibit their cultivation on their territory, similar to the existing opt-out rules for GMOs. For Category 1 NGT plants — the simpler gene-edited crops — member states cannot introduce national bans, as these plants are treated as equivalent to conventional varieties.

### How do these new rules affect organic farming and labels that say "GMO-free"?

The organic sector is deeply concerned. Since Category 1 NGT plants do not require labelling, organic farmers argue they cannot guarantee their produce is free from gene-edited seeds. The regulation does not set mandatory segregation thresholds for NGT plants. IFOAM Organics Europe has warned that this could undermine the entire "GMO-free" certification system, which the regulation's supporters say is a calculated risk to enable faster adoption of sustainable agricultural technologies.
