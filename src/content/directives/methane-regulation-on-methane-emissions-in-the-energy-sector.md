## What is it?

The Methane Regulation (2024/1787/EU) is the EU’s first standalone law targeting methane emissions from the energy sector. It requires oil, gas, and coal companies to measure, report, and verify their methane leaks, repair them within set deadlines, and bans routine flaring and venting of natural gas by default. Importers of fossil fuels into the EU will also face equivalent monitoring obligations from 2027 — a first-of-its-kind global supply-chain requirement.

The law applies to upstream and midstream oil and gas operations (from extraction to pipelines), active and abandoned coal mines, and methane leaks from closed mines. It sets leak detection and repair (LDAR) frequencies based on leak size — large leaks must be found and fixed within days. Companies must submit annual reports to independent verifiers, and the European Commission will maintain a public transparency database. Non-compliance can lead to penalties set by member states, and imports from countries without equivalent methane rules will be restricted from 2030.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation’s deepest trigger was a 2021 scientific revelation that shook EU climate policy. The International Energy Agency (IEA) estimated in its 2021 Methane Tracker that reducing oil and gas sector methane leaks by 75% by 2030 could deliver the equivalent of eliminating all emissions from Russia’s entire energy sector — at near-zero cost. Methane, though short-lived in the atmosphere, traps about 80 times more heat than CO₂ over 20 years. The EU’s own 2020 emissions inventory showed that the energy sector accounted for roughly 19% of EU methane emissions, but actual leaks were known to be vastly underreported due to weak measurement rules.

The political breakthrough came in 2021–2022, driven by three coinciding forces. First, the EU’s new methane strategy, published by Commissioner for Energy Kadri Simson in October 2020, acknowledged that voluntary industry initiatives — such as the World Bank’s Zero Routine Flaring by 2030 initiative — had failed. A 2021 European Commission impact assessment found that only 12% of global flaring was covered by those pledges, and actual flaring volumes had risen in Kazakhstan and the U.S. during the 2010s. Second, the Glasgow Climate Pact at COP26 in November 2021 saw the launch of the Global Methane Pledge, with the EU and U.S. promising to cut global methane emissions by 30% by 2030 — creating international political momentum.

Third, and most decisively, Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 exposed the EU’s dependence on Russian natural gas. The Commission’s REPowerEU plan of May 2022 explicitly tied methane regulation to energy security: reducing leaks from domestic and imported gas would keep more gas in EU grids without new extraction. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, MEP Jutta Paulus (Greens/EFA, Germany), repeatedly cited satellite data from the Copernicus Sentinel-5P mission showing massive methane plumes over Russian gas fields and the Mediterranean — leaks that had gone unpunished under existing emission rules.

Industry opposition was fierce. The International Association of Oil & Gas Producers (IOGP), representing companies like Shell, TotalEnergies, and Equinor, argued in a June 2021 position paper that import obligations would be "impractical and legally unworkable," and that leak detection rules would impose costs of €100 million annually per operator. The European coal industry, represented by EURACOAL, warned that obligations on abandoned mines would be "technically impossible" to enforce. In the Council, Poland and Czechia — with large coal-mining histories — fought to exempt closed mines and delay import rules. The final compromise, agreed in trilogues in November 2023, included a phased timeline: coal mines get until 2031 for full compliance, while importers face obligations only from 2027, with a "methane performance standard" from 2030.

The vote in the European Parliament on 10 April 2024 saw 530 votes in favour, 63 against, and 36 abstentions — a strong cross-party majority, though hardline conservative MEPs from the European Conservatives and Reformists group voted no, citing energy security risks during the Ukraine war.

- **2020** — European Commission launches EU Methane Strategy (October), announcing intent to legislate for energy sector
- **2021** — IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (August) confirms methane as the second-biggest driver of warming; Global Methane Pledge launched at COP26 (November)
- **2022** — Russia invades Ukraine (February); REPowerEU plan (May) links methane cuts to energy independence; Commission proposes Methane Regulation (December 15)
- **2023** — European Parliament adopts position (May 8, 534-89-20); Council general approach (June); trilogue agreement reached (November 15)
- **2024** — European Parliament votes final approval (April 10, 530-63-36); Council adopts (May 24); regulation published in Official Journal (July 15) and enters force 20 days later
- **2025** — First reporting deadlines for operators (January); member states must establish penalties framework (by mid-year)
- **2027** — Importers must show equivalent measurement and reporting rules in source countries
- **2030** — Imported fossil fuels must meet a methane performance intensity standard
- **2031** — Full compliance for coal mines (active and abandoned)

## Impact and consequences

The regulation marks the first time the EU has imposed binding rules on methane beyond agriculture (which remains exempt, causing criticism from green groups). The European Commission’s 2023 impact assessment estimated that full implementation would cut EU energy methane emissions by 58% by 2030 compared to 2020 levels — avoiding the equivalent of 9 million tonnes of CO₂e per year. For imported gas, the Commission projected that applying the same standards to major suppliers like Russia, Algeria, and Nigeria could reduce global energy methane emissions by 15%.

The most immediate industry impact fell on Europe’s largest gas infrastructure operators, including Snam (Italy), Enagás (Spain), and GRTgaz (France), which collectively operate hundreds of thousands of kilometres of pipelines. A 2022 report by the Environmental Defense Fund (EDF) found that 80% of surveyed EU gas infrastructure had no systematic leak detection. Operators now face a scramble to deploy drones, fixed sensors, and aircraft-mounted lidar to find and fix leaks within deadlines that range from 5 days for large leaks to 30 days for small ones.

On coal, the regulation hurts the 40 or so active mines still operating in Poland, Czechia, and Bulgaria, which must install methane capture systems or face venting bans. For abandoned mines — there are thousands across the EU, particularly in Germany’s Ruhr region and Poland’s Silesia — the rules require measurement and remediation where technically feasible, a requirement that mining associations warned could cost hundreds of millions of euros without clear benefit.

Importers — companies like TotalEnergies, Shell, and Eni — face the most complex burden: they must prove by 2027 that their foreign sellers (from U.S. LNG producers to Algerian Sonatrach) meet equivalent monitoring rules. The Commission’s 2023 analysis acknowledged that total compliance costs could reach €1.2 billion annually for importers, but noted that market losses from discounted "methane-certified" gas could outweigh these costs for early movers.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU need a separate law for methane when it already fights CO₂?

Methane is far more powerful at trapping heat than CO₂ — roughly 80 times over 20 years — but it breaks down faster, so cutting leaks now can slow warming within a decade. The IEA found the energy sector could cut 75% of methane leaks at near-zero or negative cost because captured gas can be sold. Existing EU climate laws like the Emissions Trading System barely cover methane leaks, and voluntary pledges failed to deliver measured reductions.

### Will these rules affect natural gas imports from Russia or the US?

Yes, from 2027. Importers of gas into the EU must prove that the source country applies equivalent monitoring, reporting, and verification rules for methane leaks. By 2030, imports must meet a methane intensity performance standard. This directly impacts Russia’s Gazprom, which the European Commission cited in its 2023 impact assessment as "a major source of unmeasured methane emissions" from flaring and leaks in Siberia. For U.S. LNG, the rule creates pressure on U.S. producers to comply, though the U.S. EPA’s own methane rules (finalized 2023) are broadly aligned.

### Which parts of the energy industry are exempt from these rules, and why?

Agriculture — the largest EU methane source, mainly from livestock — is entirely exempt. The Commission argued in a 2021 statement that agricultural methane is mostly biological and harder to regulate through point-source leak detection, but green groups like the EEB criticized this as a concession to the powerful farming lobby under the Common Agricultural Policy. Small operators (producing under 500 tonnes of oil equivalent per year) also get simplified rules, and coal mines have until 2031 — a delay won by Poland and Czechia in Council negotiations.

### What fines or penalties do companies face if they ignore these rules?

The regulation does not set a fixed EU fine — each member state must establish its own penalty framework. However, the Commission warned in 2024 guidance that penalties must be "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive" — meaning fines at least high enough to offset compliance costs. For persistent offenders, the Commission can impose import restrictions on non-compliant foreign suppliers from 2030. The first public enforcement cases will likely emerge after 2025, as member states start publishing the transparency database of reported emissions.
