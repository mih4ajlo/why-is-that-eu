## What is it?

The Renewable Energy Directive of 2009 — commonly called RED I — was the first piece of EU law to set legally binding national targets for renewable energy. It committed the bloc as a whole to obtaining 20% of its final energy consumption from renewables by 2020, with each Member State assigned an individual target ranging from 10% for Malta to 49% for Sweden. It also imposed a uniform 10% target for renewable energy in transport on every country.

To get there, the directive required each Member State to submit a National Renewable Energy Action Plan setting out trajectories for electricity, heating and cooling, and transport. It introduced cooperation mechanisms — statistical transfers, joint projects, joint support schemes — so countries with surplus renewable production could help others meet their targets. And it laid down the EU's first sustainability criteria for biofuels, requiring that they deliver minimum greenhouse gas savings and not be produced from land with high biodiversity value or high carbon stock.

The directive sat alongside the Emissions Trading System revision and the Effort Sharing Decision as the third pillar of the 2009 Climate and Energy Package, often called the "20-20-20" package: 20% renewables, 20% emissions cuts, 20% energy efficiency improvement by 2020.

## Why was it introduced?

The political momentum came from a January 2007 gas crisis. Russia had cut supplies through Belarus, weeks after a similar dispute with Ukraine in January 2006 had left several Central European countries scrambling. Commission President José Manuel Barroso and Energy Commissioner Andris Piebalgs used the crisis to argue that energy security and climate policy were the same problem: every wind turbine and solar panel was a barrel of imported gas the EU did not have to buy from Gazprom.

That argument met an existing failure. The 2001 Renewables Directive (2001/77/EC) had set only indicative national targets for renewable electricity, and a 2003 biofuels directive had set indicative targets for transport. The Commission's own 2007 progress report acknowledged that most Member States were missing them. Without binding obligations, national governments treated the targets as aspirational. Germany, Denmark and Spain were racing ahead with feed-in tariffs; France, the UK and most of Central Europe were drifting.

The breakthrough came at the March 2007 European Council under the German presidency of Angela Merkel. Heads of state agreed to the 20% binding renewables target — a political commitment that Barroso's Commission then had to translate into a legislative proposal, tabled by Piebalgs in January 2008. The negotiation that followed was brutal. The 10% transport target became the most contested element: the biofuels industry, led by European producers of rapeseed biodiesel and ethanol, wanted a generous mandate; environmental NGOs and development organisations warned that crop-based biofuels would drive deforestation and food price spikes — concerns sharpened by the 2007–2008 global food price crisis. The compromise was the sustainability criteria written into Articles 17 to 19, but the underlying tension over "indirect land use change" would dog EU biofuel policy for the next decade.

The coalition that carried the directive through was held together by three things: German industrial confidence that it could lead the renewables market; Eastern European acceptance of differentiated national targets that reflected starting points and GDP per capita; and the political cover of the financial crisis, which had begun in autumn 2008 and made "green growth" a politically attractive story. The Parliament adopted the package in December 2008 with a large majority, and the Council formally adopted it in April 2009.

- **2001** — Directive 2001/77/EC sets indicative renewable electricity targets; most Member States fail to meet trajectory
- **January 2006** — First Russia–Ukraine gas dispute disrupts supplies to EU countries
- **January 2007** — Russia–Belarus dispute cuts oil and gas flows; Commission publishes "Energy Policy for Europe" communication
- **March 2007** — European Council endorses binding 20% renewables target under German presidency
- **January 2008** — Commission, under Commissioner Piebalgs, tables the climate and energy package
- **December 2008** — European Parliament approves the package in plenary
- **April 2009** — Council formally adopts Directive 2009/28/EC
- **June 2009** — Directive enters into force
- **December 2010** — Deadline for Member States to submit National Renewable Energy Action Plans
- **December 2010** — Transposition deadline
- **2020** — Target year; according to Eurostat, the EU collectively met the 20% headline target

## Impact and consequences

The directive triggered the largest renewable build-out in European history. Eurostat data show the share of renewables in EU final energy consumption rose from roughly 13% in 2009 to above 20% by 2020, with the bloc collectively meeting the headline goal. Sweden, Finland and Denmark overshot their national targets by wide margins; the Netherlands, France and Ireland missed theirs and had to purchase statistical transfers — the Netherlands buying renewable statistics from Denmark in 2020 was the most prominent example of the cooperation mechanisms being used in practice.

The financial impact was substantial but uneven. Generous feed-in tariffs in Spain, Germany, Italy and the Czech Republic produced solar booms followed by retroactive tariff cuts, leading to investor arbitration cases under the Energy Charter Treaty — Spain alone faced dozens of claims and lost a series of awards. Germany's Energiewende, accelerated by the directive's framework and Fukushima in 2011, drove down the cost of solar PV globally.

The transport target proved the most troubled element. Most Member States met the 10% figure largely through crop-based biofuels, and the Commission's own subsequent analyses concluded that indirect land use change emissions undermined much of the claimed climate benefit. This pushed the EU to amend the directive in 2015 (the ILUC Directive 2015/1513) capping crop-based biofuels at 7%, and ultimately to replace the entire framework with RED II in 2018, which raised the headline target to 32% by 2030 and refocused transport on advanced biofuels and renewable electricity.

## Key questions answered

### How did Europe end up with so much wind and solar power?

The 2009 directive forced it. Before then, EU renewable targets were voluntary and most countries ignored them. The 2007 Russia–Belarus gas crisis gave Commission President Barroso and Chancellor Merkel the political opening to make the targets binding, with each country assigned its own legally enforceable share. The result was roughly a doubling of the EU's renewable energy share between 2009 and 2020.

### Why did European biofuels become so controversial?

The 2009 directive's 10% transport renewables target was filled mainly by biodiesel from rapeseed, palm and soy, and by ethanol from grain. Studies commissioned by the EU itself found that clearing land to grow these crops — directly or indirectly, by displacing food production — released so much carbon that the climate benefit was small or negative. The Commission spent most of the 2010s walking back the policy, capping crop biofuels in 2015 and phasing out palm-oil-based biofuels in the 2018 update.

### Did all EU countries hit their 2020 renewable targets?

No. Eurostat data show that while the EU as a whole met the 20% headline goal, several Member States — including France, the Netherlands, Ireland, Belgium, Luxembourg and Slovenia — fell short of their individual national targets. The directive's cooperation mechanisms allowed laggards to buy "statistical transfers" of surplus renewables from over-performing countries; the Netherlands' purchase from Denmark in 2020 was the most visible example.

### Why did the EU push energy and climate policy together?

Because the 2006 and 2007 gas crises with Russia made Brussels see them as one problem. Every megawatt of wind or solar power was both a tonne of CO2 avoided and a cubic metre of imported gas not needed. This framing — energy security plus climate — held the political coalition together: Germany and Denmark wanted climate ambition, Poland and the Baltics wanted independence from Russian fossil fuels, and both got something from the same legislation.
