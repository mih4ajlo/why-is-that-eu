## What is it?

The Pay Transparency Directive obliges employers across the EU to lift the secrecy that has historically surrounded salaries. Job applicants must be told the pay range before the interview, and employers are forbidden from asking candidates about their salary history. Workers gain a right to request information on their individual pay level and on the average pay levels, broken down by sex, for categories of employees doing the same work or work of equal value.

Companies with at least 100 employees must publish gender pay gap figures at regular intervals — annually for the largest employers, every three years for those with 100–149 workers. Where reporting reveals a gender pay gap of more than 5% in any category of workers that cannot be justified by objective, gender-neutral criteria, the employer is required to carry out a joint pay assessment together with workers' representatives and remedy the gap.

The directive also reverses the burden of proof in pay discrimination cases — once a worker establishes a prima facie case, the employer must prove no discrimination occurred — and entitles victims to full compensation, including back pay and bonuses. Member States must transpose it by 7 June 2026.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was arithmetic that refused to move. After more than six decades of EU equal pay law — the principle has been in the treaties since the Treaty of Rome in 1957, and Directive 75/117/EEC codified it in 1975 — Eurostat data showed the gender pay gap in the EU still hovering around 13% in the late 2010s, with barely a percentage point of progress per decade. The 2006 recast Equal Treatment Directive (2006/54/EC) had given workers the right to sue, but litigation was rare: workers could not prove discrimination because they did not know what their colleagues earned, and asking was often forbidden by contract.

Ursula von der Leyen made pay transparency a signature pledge of her Commission's first 100 days when she took office in December 2019, framing it as part of a broader Gender Equality Strategy. Helena Dalli, the Commissioner for Equality, presented the proposal on 4 March 2021 — a year later than planned because COVID-19 had shifted Commission bandwidth. The Commission's 2020 evaluation of the existing equal pay framework found that the lack of pay transparency was the single biggest obstacle to enforcement, and that voluntary transparency measures adopted by some Member States — Germany's 2017 Pay Transparency Act, France's Index Egalité, Iceland's mandatory pay certification — produced uneven results.

The coalition that pushed it through was unusually broad. The European Parliament had been demanding binding measures since at least 2008 — its 2012 resolution called for legislative action and was largely ignored. By 2021 the political weather had shifted: the #MeToo movement, Iceland's high-profile equal pay certification scheme, and trade union pressure through the European Trade Union Confederation aligned with a Parliament where Socialists, Greens, Renew, and the EPP's women's network all backed action. BusinessEurope and employer federations pushed back hard on the administrative burden, particularly the joint pay assessment trigger, and lobbied successfully to raise the reporting threshold from 50 to 100 employees and to lengthen reporting cycles for smaller companies. Hungary and several Central European delegations resisted in Council but did not have a blocking minority.

The deal was struck in trilogue on 15 December 2022 under the Czech Council presidency. The Parliament adopted the text on 30 March 2023 with a comfortable majority — 427 in favour, 79 against, 76 abstentions — and the Council followed on 24 April 2023.

## Timeline

- **1957** — Treaty of Rome enshrines the principle of equal pay for equal work (Article 119, later 157 TFEU).
- **1975** — Directive 75/117/EEC codifies equal pay obligations but lacks enforcement teeth.
- **2006** — Recast Directive 2006/54/EC consolidates equal treatment law; pay gap still wide.
- **2014** — Commission Recommendation on pay transparency (non-binding); take-up by Member States is patchy.
- **2017** — Iceland becomes the first country in the world to require employers to prove equal pay through certification; Germany passes its Pay Transparency Act.
- **December 2019** — Ursula von der Leyen takes office pledging binding pay transparency measures in her first 100 days.
- **March 2020** — Commission publishes its Gender Equality Strategy 2020–2025.
- **4 March 2021** — Commissioner Helena Dalli presents the Pay Transparency proposal.
- **15 December 2022** — Trilogue agreement reached under the Czech presidency.
- **30 March 2023** — European Parliament adopts the text (427-79-76).
- **24 April 2023** — Council formally adopts the directive.
- **17 May 2023** — Directive published in the Official Journal; enters into force 6 June 2023.
- **7 June 2026** — Transposition deadline for Member States.
- **7 June 2027** — First gender pay gap reports due from employers with 250+ workers.

## Impact and consequences

The directive reaches roughly the entire EU formal workforce through employer obligations, with the heaviest reporting duties falling on companies above 250 employees, who must file annually. Mid-sized firms (150–249) report every three years, and smaller employers (100–149) also report every three years starting in 2031. Companies below 100 workers are exempt from mandatory reporting but still subject to the transparency rules on recruitment and worker information requests.

The recruitment provisions are already changing hiring practice in anticipation of transposition. Job platforms operating across Europe — LinkedIn, Indeed, StepStone — have begun rolling out pay-range fields, and large multinationals with workforces spread across several Member States are standardising salary band disclosure to avoid running parallel systems. Consultancies including Mercer, WTW, and Korn Ferry have built dedicated pay-equity audit practices around the joint pay assessment trigger.

Compliance costs are concentrated in the job evaluation work needed to define "work of equal value" within an organisation — a notoriously contested concept that has driven UK and Nordic equal pay litigation for decades. Employers' groups, including BusinessEurope, have warned the obligation will force costly job classification reviews; trade unions, including the ETUC, have argued the same exercise is precisely the point. Member States retain discretion on penalties but must ensure they are "effective, proportionate and dissuasive", and several — including Ireland and the Netherlands, which were already running pay gap reporting schemes — have signalled they will go beyond the minimum.

Enforcement will be tested first in countries with weak existing frameworks: Greece, Cyprus, and parts of Central and Eastern Europe never had any meaningful pay transparency regime, and their labour inspectorates will need to be resourced and trained before 2026.

## Key questions answered

### Can European employers still keep salaries secret?

No — not in the way they used to. Once the directive applies in mid-2026, workers across the EU will have the right to ask their employer about average pay levels for colleagues doing the same job or work of equal value, broken down by sex. Job applicants must be told the pay range before the interview, and contract clauses forbidding employees from discussing their wages are explicitly banned.

### Will I be able to see my colleagues' exact salaries?

Not their individual pay. The directive gives workers a right to information about average pay levels for categories of workers, broken down by sex — not a right to look up what the person at the next desk earns. Aggregated reporting is the mechanism: companies publish category-level gaps, and individuals can request the category-level data relevant to them.

### What happens if a company has a big gender pay gap?

If a gender pay gap above 5% appears in any category of workers and cannot be justified by objective, gender-neutral factors such as experience or qualifications, the employer must carry out a joint pay assessment with workers' representatives and take remedial action. Member States must also provide for "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" penalties, and workers who have suffered pay discrimination are entitled to full compensation, including back pay and bonuses.

### Why did it take the EU until 2023 to do this when equal pay has been law since 1957?

Equal pay as a principle was easy to agree on; equal pay as an enforceable individual right was not. For decades, workers could not prove they were being paid less than colleagues because pay information was confidential, and successive Commissions preferred non-binding recommendations — like the 2014 Recommendation on pay transparency — to avoid antagonising employers. The combination of stalled progress on the gap, the #MeToo political climate, von der Leyen's personal commitment, and a Parliament willing to push hard finally broke the deadlock in 2021–2023.
