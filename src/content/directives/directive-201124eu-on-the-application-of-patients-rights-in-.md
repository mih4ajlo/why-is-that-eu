## What is it?

Directive 2011/24/EU establishes the conditions under which a patient from one EU Member State can travel to another Member State to receive medical treatment and be reimbursed for the cost, either by their home health system or up to the amount their home system would have paid. It covers both hospital and non-hospital care, prescriptions, and telemedicine consultations, setting out rules on prior authorisation, information provision, and professional liability.

The directive requires each Member State to establish one or more National Contact Points that provide patients with accessible information on their rights, the procedures for reimbursement, and the quality and safety standards in other Member States. It also introduces the principle that patients can seek treatment abroad for non-hospital care without prior authorisation, while hospital care (or specialist care requiring expensive equipment) may require pre-approval from the home state.

Crucially, the directive does not harmonise healthcare systems: it creates a framework for cross-border reimbursement within the existing diversity of national schemes. It exists alongside Regulation 883/2004, which coordinates social security systems, but for planned care, the directive offers a more flexible, patient-driven route than the prior authorisation-heavy coordination regulation.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was the EU’s legislative answer to a decade of legal chaos triggered by patients taking their health systems to court. The story begins not in Brussels but in Luxembourg, at the European Court of Justice, with a series of rulings that blew open the principle that healthcare services were subject to the single market’s free movement rules.

The critical case was *Kohl v. Union des Caisses de Maladie* of 1998 (Case C-158/96). Raymond Kohl, a Luxembourg national, had sought orthodontic treatment for his daughter in Germany without prior authorisation from his Luxembourg sickness fund. The CJEU ruled that medical services were “services” under Article 60 of the Treaty, meaning patients could seek treatment in another Member State and demand reimbursement at the rate of their home system—even without prior authorisation—unless the treatment involved hospital care or major equipment, where objective justification could apply. This was followed by *Smits and Peerbooms* (2001), *Müller-Fauré and van Riet* (2003), and *Watts* (2006), each progressively chipping away at Member States’ ability to require prior authorisation.

By 2006, the legal landscape was fragmented: some Member States (notably the Netherlands and the UK) had adapted their national systems to comply with the case law; others (including Germany and France) had not, leading to unequal patient access and legal uncertainty for health insurers and providers. The Commission began studying the need for legislation under Commissioners Markos Kyprianou (Health, 2004-2008) and Charlie McCreevy (Internal Market, 2004-2010), recognising that the CJEU’s ad-hoc rulings created inconsistencies that only a directive could resolve.

The structural tension was clear: the internal market’s free movement of services logically applied to healthcare, but healthcare systems were organised at national level, funded by compulsory social insurance schemes that were inherently territorial. How could a patient in one Member State claim reimbursement for treatment in another Member State when their home system had never contracted with that foreign provider? The CJEU had answered “yes, they can,” but left the practical mechanisms—reimbursement ceilings, administrative procedures, quality assurance—to be filled by legislation.

Coalition dynamics were fractious. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur was Italian centre-right MEP Giovanni Berlinguer (Socialist Group), who pushed for strong patient rights. The Health Commissioner, Androulla Vassiliou (2008-2010), faced fierce opposition from several Member States: Austria, Germany, and the Netherlands feared a surge of patients draining budgets, while Finland and Sweden worried about two-tier access for wealthy patients who could afford to travel. The Council made 120 amendments to the Commission’s 2008 proposal during three years of negotiation.

The breakthrough came in 2009, when the Lisbon Treaty entered into force, granting the EU explicit competence in public health (Article 168 TFEU) but also reinforcing subsidiarity, meaning Member States retained primary responsibility for organising and delivering healthcare. This constitutional clarity unblocked the Council: Ministers could agree to a directive because it explicitly stated that “nothing in this Directive shall affect Member States’ laws on the use of health services, nor the organisation and delivery of healthcare” (Article 1(3)).

The final vote in the European Parliament on 19 January 2011 was 523 in favour, 75 against, 28 abstentions. The strongest opposition came from social health insurance states (Germany, Austria, the Netherlands), where sickness funds warned that the directive would destabilise their cost-containment mechanisms. The health insurance association GKV-Spitzenverband in Germany formally opposed the directive, arguing it undermined the principle of solidarity financing.

## Timeline

- **1998** — CJEU rules in *Kohl* that medical treatment is a service under Article 60 of the Treaty; prior authorisation for non-hospital care is not compatible with free movement.
- **2001** — CJEU *Smits and Peerbooms* ruling clarifies that hospital care may require prior authorisation if it affects the financial balance of the social security system.
- **2003** — CJEU *Müller-Fauré* ruling confirms that prior authorisation cannot be required for non-hospital care even if it circumvents waiting lists.
- **2006** — CJEU *Watts* ruling on UK NHS waiting lists; the court orders the UK to reimburse Mrs Watts for hip-replacement surgery in France, including travel costs.
- **2 July 2008** — European Commission (Vassiliou) publishes proposal for a directive on patients’ rights in cross-border healthcare (COM(2008) 414 final).
- **2009** — Council of the EU makes 120 amendments to the proposal; the Lisbon Treaty (Art. 168 TFEU) provides new legal clarity on EU health competence.
- **19 January 2011** — European Parliament adopts directive by 523 votes to 75.
- **9 March 2011** — Council formally adopts directive.
- **24 April 2011** — Directive enters into force (20 days after publication in OJ).
- **25 October 2013** — Member States must transpose the directive into national law (transposition deadline).
- **2014-2015** — European Court of Justice rules in several cases (e.g., *Commission v. Italy* C-596/14) against Member States that failed to transpose correctly.
- **2017** — EU Health Commissioner Vytenis Andriukaitis launches public consultation on the directive’s functioning; 67% of respondents reported that awareness of cross-border healthcare rights remained low.
- **2018** — European Commission Staff Working Document SWD(2018) 465 final reports that only 0.004% of total healthcare expenditure in the EU involves cross-border planned care, much lower than predicted by opponents.
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic temporarily suspends non-urgent cross-border healthcare; the Commission issues guidance on interpretation of the directive during health emergencies.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s impact has been significantly smaller than either its proponents hoped or its opponents feared. According to the Commission’s 2018 staff working document, only 0.004% of total public healthcare expenditure in the EU was spent on cross-border care under the directive—a negligible fraction of the €1.4 trillion EU healthcare market. The German health insurance association GKV expected 500,000 patients annually; the actual figure across all Member States was far lower.

The main impact has been administrative. Every Member State established a National Contact Point by 2014, though the Commission found in 2018 that nine Member States still had contact points with limited or no online information in English, limiting practical access for patients. The Netherlands and the UK saw the highest outward patient flows, primarily for dental treatment and elective orthopaedic procedures.

The directive also accelerated e-Health interoperability. Article 14 required the Commission to adopt measures enabling cross-border exchange of patient data; this led to the eHealth Digital Service Infrastructure (eHDSI), which by 2020 connected 22 Member States for electronic prescriptions and patient summaries, funded under the Connecting Europe Facility.

The most significant unintended consequence was litigation over reimbursement of cancer drugs. In 2015, the CJEU ruled in *Commission v. Hungary* (Case C-311/14) that Hungary’s requirement for prior authorisation for outpatient specialist consultations breached the directive. Hungary subsequently amended its legislation.

Patient organisations—notably the European Patients Forum and the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC)—have consistently criticised poor transposition. The European Ombudsman opened an inquiry in 2019 into delays in updating the EU’s cross-border healthcare portal. According to the Commission’s 2022 report on the directive, only 14 Member States had fully transposed the provisions on telemedicine by that year.

## Key questions answered

### Why didn’t EU rules on getting healthcare in another country cause the flood of medical tourism that many worried about?

Because the reimbursement mechanism—patients must pay upfront and are reimbursed only up to the cost their home system would have paid—removes the financial incentive. For patients from low-cost Member States, treatment in high-cost countries remains unaffordable; for patients from high-cost states, treatment abroad rarely saves money. The Commission’s 2018 evaluation found that the main barrier is not legal but practical: language, travel costs, and lack of awareness keep flows minimal.

### Why did it take three years (2008–2011) for EU countries to agree on rules for patients seeking treatment abroad?

Three factors. First, the Lisbon Treaty negotiations (2007-2009) created legal uncertainty about how a health directive would fit under the new Article 168 TFEU. Second, the financial crisis of 2008 made Member States defensive about national health budgets. Third, the German and Austrian governments, backed by social insurance funds, insisted on strong protections for national systems. The Council ultimately accepted the directive only after the Commission added provisions explicitly stating that Member States retain control over the organisation of their health systems.

### Why did European courts decide patients could get care in other EU countries before the EU made official rules about it?

Because healthcare was traditionally excluded from EU single-market rules under earlier treaties (e.g., the 1957 Rome Treaty contained no health article). When patients started challenging national barriers using free-movement provisions—originally designed for goods, services, and workers—they forced the CJEU to decide case by case whether healthcare fell within EU law. The Court created the legal framework; the directive then codified and standardised it. This is a rare example of patient litigation—not Commission ambition—driving the single market into a sensitive social policy area.
