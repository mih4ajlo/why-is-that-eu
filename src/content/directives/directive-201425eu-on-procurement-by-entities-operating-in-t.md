## What is it?

Directive 2014/25/EU (the "Utilities Directive") requires EU utilities operating in water, energy, transport, and postal services to subject large contracts for supplies, services, and works to competitive tendering overseen by national authorities and the European Commission. It covers companies like power grids, water suppliers, railway operators, and postal services — whether publicly or privately owned. The threshold for mandatory open tenders is €413,000 for supplies and services, and €5,186,000 for works (as of 2024).

The directive aims to prevent these utilities from favouring national suppliers or affiliates, ensuring cross-border competition. It replaces the 2004 Utilities Directive (2004/17/EC) and updates rules for electronic procurement, framework agreements, and the ability of member states to exempt certain sectors if they are demonstrably open to competition (e.g. via the "market-access" test).

## Why was it introduced?

The directive’s roots lie in a 1983 ruling by the European Court of Justice in *Commission v. Ireland* (Case 249/81), where the court found that Ireland’s state-owned electricity utility had illegally restricted tenders to Irish suppliers. The case, brought by the Commission under Commissioner Étienne Davignon, exposed a structural tension: while the EU had dismantled tariff barriers for goods, national utilities routinely excluded foreign bidders via technical specifications, short deadlines, and informal preferences.

The 1992 single market programme (under Commission President Jacques Delors) made utilities procurement a priority. A 1993 Commission report found that only 2% of utilities contracts were awarded cross-border, with 90% going to incumbent national suppliers. The first Utilities Directive (93/38/EEC) was adopted in 1993 over strong opposition from Germany and the UK, which argued that energy and water utilities should remain nationally regulated. The German utility association VDEW warned of “crippling bureaucracy” and forecast job losses — though no independent study confirmed this.

The 2014 revision was triggered by two events. First, the 2012 Commission evaluation (SWD(2012) 189 final) showed that cross-border procurement remained stalled at 3-5% of total utilities contracts, with 55% of contracts awarded without any competition. Second, the European Parliament’s IMCO Committee, chaired by MEP Malcolm Harbour, pushed for simplification after surveys found that 78% of utilities cited complex procedures as the biggest barrier to cross-border bidding.

Commissioner Michel Barnier (Internal Market and Services) launched the proposal in December 2011, aiming to reduce administrative burdens by 20-30% via e-procurement and to clarify when utilities could be exempted. The main opposition came from German and French railway operators (Deutsche Bahn, SNCF) who wanted to keep track-access contracts outside the rules. The final compromise, reached in June 2013, allowed member states to exempt entire sectors by demonstrating effective competition — a concession to Germany, which had threatened to block the directive in the Council.

## Timeline

- **1983** — European Court of Justice rules that Ireland’s state electricity utility illegally discriminated against foreign bidders (Case 249/81), establishing that utilities procurement falls under Treaty rules on free movement of goods.
- **1985** — Commission publishes White Paper on Completing the Internal Market, identifying utilities procurement as a priority area.
- **1990** — Commission proposes first Utilities Directive; opposed by Germany, UK, and France at various points.
- **1993** — Directive 93/38/EEC adopted, covering water, energy, transport, and telecommunications sectors.
- **2004** — Directive 2004/17/EC consolidates and simplifies rules; introduces competitive dialogue procedure.
- **2011** — Commissioner Barnier proposes revision as part of the Single Market Act; impact assessment estimates benefits of €6-27 billion over ten years if cross-border procurement rises.
- **2013** — European Parliament approves final text (467 votes in favour, 105 against); Council adopts on 21 March.
- **2014** — Directive 2014/25/EU published in Official Journal on 26 February; member states have until 18 April 2016 to transpose.
- **2016** — Transposition deadline; Commission opens infringement proceedings against 12 member states (including Germany and France) for late implementation.
- **2018** — Commission’s 2018 evaluation report (SWD(2018) 220 final) finds that e-procurement usage rose from 5% to 15% of utilities contracts, but cross-border award remains below 4%.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has had mixed results. The European Commission’s 2021 Single Market Scoreboard reported that cross-border procurement stood at 3.8% of total utilities contracts in 2020, barely changed from the 1990s. However, the number of bidders per contract rose from 3.2 in 2014 to 4.1 in 2020, according to the Commission’s TED (Tenders Electronic Daily) database. The largest beneficiaries appear to be large construction and engineering firms — such as Vinci, Bouygues, and Siemens — which can navigate the procedural complexity.

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) reported limited gains: a 2019 survey by the European Construction Industry Federation (FIEC) found that only 12% of SMEs bid on utilities contracts, citing high legal costs and short bid deadlines. The directive also created a “market-access” loophole: by 2022, 15 member states had exempted over 60% of their electricity markets from the directive, effectively handing control back to national incumbents such as EDF in France and Enel in Italy.

The most significant enforcement action came in 2017, when the Commission fined the Greek water utility EYDAP €5.1 million for awarding a €45 million contract without tender to a state-owned construction firm. A 2020 Court of Auditors report (Special Report No 18/2020) found that the Commission had not systematically checked compliance, with only 2% of utilities contracts subject to ex-ante review.

## Key questions answered

### Why does Directive 2014/25/EU set different thresholds for utilities vs. general public procurement?

Utilities were historically national monopolies with close ties to government, so the EU set higher thresholds (€413,000 vs. €215,000 for supplies) to avoid overwhelming them with small contracts while still opening large ones to competition. The Commission’s 2011 impact assessment argued that the higher threshold reflected utilities’ “special characteristics” — such as technical complexity and security-of-supply concerns — but critics, including the European Parliament’s IMCO Committee, said it let too many contracts escape scrutiny.

### Why is cross-border procurement still so low after 30 years under Directive 2014/25/EU?

The Commission’s 2018 evaluation identified three main barriers: first, 28% of utilities use confidentiality clauses to justify local-only bidding; second, technical specifications often implicitly favour national suppliers (e.g. requiring grid equipment built to national standards); third, legal remedies are weak — the 2017 Commission report on review procedures found that average court proceedings in member states take 18 months. A 2021 academic study by Bovis and Möslein concluded that “the directive has not fundamentally changed utilities’ purchasing behaviour.”

### Why does Directive 2014/25/EU exempt sectors on the basis of “direct exposure to competition”?

The 2014 revision introduced a “market-access” test allowing member states to exempt entire sectors if they can show that the EU’s 2009 electricity and gas directives have created effective competition. This was a direct concession to Germany and France, which argued that deregulated energy markets no longer needed procurement rules. The European Commission approved Germany’s exemption for all electricity transmission in 2016, after Deutsche Naturschutzring (a German consumer group) complained that this would reinforce the dominance of RWE and E.ON.
