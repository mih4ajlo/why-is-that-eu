## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2016/399 is the consolidated "Schengen Borders Code" — the single legal text that governs how people cross both the internal borders between Schengen states (where checks are normally abolished) and the external borders of the Schengen area (where systematic checks apply). It replaced the original 2006 Schengen Borders Code after a series of amendments left the rules scattered and politically contested.

The Code does three main jobs. First, it sets out who can cross the external border and under what conditions (valid travel document, justified purpose, sufficient means, not flagged in the Schengen Information System). Second, it guarantees the absence of checks at internal borders and prohibits "equivalent" controls disguised as something else. Third — and most politically charged — it lays down the narrow circumstances in which a member state may temporarily reintroduce internal border controls: a serious threat to public policy or internal security (Article 25), unforeseeable emergencies (Article 28), or persistent serious deficiencies at the external border (Article 29).

In practice, the 2016 codification kept the substance of the post-2013 reform package but tidied it into one regulation. Substantive tightening of the suspension rules — including a longer maximum duration of internal controls — came later, with Regulation (EU) 2024/1717 adopted in June 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

When Hungarian authorities began waving migrants north toward Austria in September 2015 and Germany then reinstated checks on its border with Austria, the legal scaffolding of Schengen suddenly mattered to everyone. Within weeks, Austria, Slovenia, Hungary, Sweden, Denmark, Norway and France had all reintroduced internal border controls under various articles of the existing Borders Code. Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker warned publicly that the loss of Schengen would mean the loss of the euro — framing the recodification as existential rather than technical.

The structural tension was that the original 2006 Schengen Borders Code had been drafted for an era of stable external borders and predictable internal mobility. Its suspension clauses assumed brief, localised emergencies (a football final, a G8 summit), not a sustained migratory movement of the scale the Commission and Frontex recorded in 2015, when the EU's external border agency reported more than 1.8 million irregular crossings — by far the highest figure since records began. Meanwhile the Court of Justice's case law (notably *Melki and Abdeli*, C-188/10) had made clear that police checks near internal borders could not become a substitute for abolished border controls, narrowing what governments could lawfully do without formally invoking Article 25.

The 2016 recodification itself was the legally simpler step: it merged the 2006 Code with successive amendments — including the 2013 "Schengen governance package" pushed through after France and Italy clashed over Tunisian migrants in 2011 — into a single readable instrument. The harder political fights came around it. The 2013 reform had already introduced Article 29, which allows the Council, on a Commission proposal, to recommend coordinated internal controls when external border management fails persistently. That mechanism was used for the first time in May 2016, when the Council authorised Austria, Germany, Denmark, Sweden and Norway to maintain controls in response to deficiencies the Commission had identified at the Greek external border.

The coalition behind recodification was therefore broad but uneasy: northern member states (Germany, Sweden, Denmark, Austria) wanted clearer legal cover for keeping controls in place; southern and eastern frontline states (Greece, Italy, Hungary) wanted external-border solidarity rather than internal punishment; and the Commission under Dimitris Avramopoulos, then Migration Commissioner, wanted to preserve the principle of free movement while conceding that the rules needed teeth. A 2017 Commission proposal to formally extend the maximum duration of internal controls stalled in the Council for years, blocked by Parliament's civil liberties committee and member states unwilling to legitimise what they saw as semi-permanent controls — France had by then continuously reintroduced internal checks since the November 2015 Paris attacks, citing terrorism rather than migration.

That deadlock only broke after the COVID-19 pandemic, when nearly every Schengen state unilaterally closed borders in March 2020, exposing the gap between the Code's text and member state behaviour. The Commission's December 2021 proposal — eventually adopted as Regulation 2024/1717 — added pandemic-style "large-scale public health emergencies" as a legitimate ground and lengthened maximum durations, while strengthening the procedural review by the Commission.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Schengen Agreement signed by five member states near the Luxembourg village of Schengen.
- **1995** — Internal border controls effectively abolished between the original Schengen states.
- **2006** — Original Schengen Borders Code adopted (Regulation 562/2006).
- **2011** — France reintroduces controls on the Italian border over Tunisian migrant arrivals, prompting calls to clarify suspension rules.
- **2013** — Schengen governance package amends the Code, introducing Article 29 (coordinated controls in response to external-border deficiencies).
- **2015** — Migration crisis: Germany, Austria, Sweden, Denmark, Hungary, Slovenia, Norway and France reintroduce internal controls under various articles.
- **March 2016** — Regulation (EU) 2016/399 adopted, recodifying the Schengen Borders Code into a single text.
- **April 2016** — Code enters into force.
- **May 2016** — Council activates Article 29 for the first time, on Commission recommendation, authorising five states to maintain controls.
- **2017** — Commission proposes lengthening maximum duration of internal controls; proposal stalls.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 pandemic triggers near-total reintroduction of internal controls across Schengen.
- **December 2021** — Commission tables fresh reform proposal in light of pandemic and continued French/German controls.
- **June 2024** — Regulation (EU) 2024/1717 adopted, amending the Code to extend maximum durations and add public health grounds.

## Impact and consequences

The most visible consequence is that "temporary" internal controls have become semi-permanent for a core group of states. According to Commission notifications published on the DG HOME website, France, Germany, Austria, Denmark, Sweden and Norway have notified successive periods of internal border controls almost continuously since 2015, with rotating legal bases (terrorism, secondary migratory movements, Russia's invasion of Ukraine). The Commission has repeatedly stated in its annual State of Schengen reports that some of these notifications stretch the Code's intent, but it has declined to open infringement proceedings.

The Court of Justice has tested the limits. In *Landespolizeidirektion Steiermark* (C-368/20 and C-369/20), decided in April 2022, the Court ruled that a member state cannot reintroduce or prolong internal border controls beyond the total six-month maximum then set in the Code on the basis of the same threat — a judgment that, on its face, called into question the legality of Austria's then-ongoing controls. Austria's government effectively continued the controls under reformulated justifications, prompting the Commission's reform proposal to acknowledge legal reality by lengthening the maximum.

The economic costs have been documented but disputed. The Commission's 2016 communication "Back to Schengen" estimated that the full reintroduction of internal border controls could cost the EU economy between €5 billion and €18 billion per year, with road haulage, cross-border workers, and tourism the hardest hit. Industry associations representing freight operators and cross-border commuters — particularly in the Greater Region around Luxembourg and along the German-Polish corridor — have repeatedly complained to the Commission about queues and lost time.

## Key questions answered

### Can EU countries just close their borders whenever they want?

No — but in practice the legal threshold has proved elastic. The Schengen Borders Code allows internal controls only in response to a "serious threat to public policy or internal security," for limited periods, after notifying the Commission. The Court of Justice ruled in 2022 that the same threat cannot justify endless renewals, yet France, Germany, Austria, Denmark and Sweden have maintained controls almost continuously for nearly a decade by reformulating the justification — terrorism, then migration, then Ukraine, then secondary movements.

### Why didn't Schengen survive the 2015 migration crisis intact?

It legally survived but politically frayed. When Greece's external border system buckled and over a million people moved north toward Germany and Sweden, transit states reimposed checks unilaterally. The Council then activated Article 29 of the Code in May 2016 — designed for exactly this scenario — to convert improvised national controls into coordinated, Brussels-sanctioned ones. The recodification preserved the principle of free movement on paper, but the era of unmonitored internal travel for the whole Schengen area ended in practice.

### What happens at the EU's external border under these rules?

Every person entering Schengen from outside must undergo a check: travel document, purpose of stay, sufficient resources, no alert in the Schengen Information System. EU citizens get a "minimum" check (identity verification); third-country nationals get a "thorough" check including stamping and database queries. Frontex (now the European Border and Coast Guard Agency under Regulation 2019/1896) supports member states but the legal responsibility for the border remains national — a tension the Code does not resolve.

### Did COVID-19 change the rules?

Yes — but only retroactively. In March 2020, member states closed borders without legal basis in the Code, which did not list pandemics as grounds for suspension. The Commission tolerated this and issued guidelines on "green lanes" for freight. The 2024 amendment (Regulation 2024/1717) formally added "large-scale public health emergencies" as a legitimate ground, codifying what governments had already done.
