## What is it?

The Product Liability Directive recast (2024/2853/EU) updates the EU's 40-year-old strict liability framework to cover software, AI systems, and digital services that modify products. For the first time, a "product" explicitly includes digital files, algorithms, and AI models — meaning a defective navigation app that causes a crash, or a flawed medical AI that misdiagnoses a patient, now falls under strict liability without requiring proof of negligence.

The directive abolishes the €500 threshold for damage claims, extends liability to cover data loss and psychological harm, and shifts the burden of proof for consumers when technical complexity makes it impossible to identify the defect. It also introduces a 25-year liability period for latent defects that take decades to manifest.

## Why was it introduced?

The single most dramatic trigger was the **Dieselgate scandal** of September 2015. When Volkswagen's defeat devices were exposed, EU regulators discovered that national product liability laws — largely based on the 1985 Directive — were powerless to hold software-makers responsible. German courts ruled the 1985 Directive covered only physical components, not the software that manipulated emissions tests. The scandal cost Volkswagen over €30 billion globally but left EU consumers with no recourse under product liability law for "cheating" software.

The structural tension was clear: the 1985 Directive was written for typewriters and toasters. By 2018, the Commission's own evaluation found that "smart products" — from smart thermostats to autonomous vehicles — could malfunction through software updates, AI training data flaws, or cybersecurity breaches, yet none of these were covered. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment estimated that **3.2 million EU citizens are injured annually by defective products**, with untold more from software-related harms in healthcare, transport, and industrial automation.

The coalition for reform was unusually broad. **BEUC (the European Consumer Organisation)** and consumer rights groups had pushed for digital product liability since 2016. The **European Parliament** was a key driver, with MEPs from the JURI Committee — particularly rapporteur **Pascal Arimont (EPP, Belgium)** — arguing that consumers needed protection equivalent to what they had for physical products. The **European Commission**, under President **Ursula von der Leyen** and Internal Market Commissioner **Thierry Breton**, made it a priority in the 2020 "New Consumer Agenda".

Opposition came from the **technology and software industry**, led by **DigitalEurope**, whose members included Google, Microsoft, Apple, and Siemens. They warned that strict liability would stifle AI innovation and hold developers liable for "unforeseeable" algorithmic outcomes. Germany's **Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie (BDI)** also pushed back, arguing that software should be treated as a service rather than a product. The debate was intense: the Commission's proposal was delayed from 2021 to September 2022 after three rounds of stakeholder consultations.

The critical breakthrough came from **two unexpected sources**. First, the **ECJ's 2019 "Wamo" ruling** (Case C-149/17) forced EU courts to consider whether digital content was a "product" under the original directive — leaving national courts divided. Second, the **COVID-19 pandemic** exposed massive software failures in contact-tracing apps, vaccine distribution algorithms, and remote medical diagnostics. The Commission cited "dozens of reported defects" from May 2020 to June 2021 in health-related software.

The final vote in the European Parliament was **637 in favour, 21 against, with 16 abstentions** — a near-consensus. The Council's Qualified Majority Vote was also overwhelming, with only a handful of member states (notably Germany) expressing last-minute reservations about the burden on SMEs.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Original Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC) adopted, covering only physical products
- **2015** — Volkswagen Dieselgate exposes gap: software cannot be held liable as a "product"
- **2018** — Commission publishes evaluation of 1985 Directive, identifies legal gaps for AI and software
- **2019** — ECJ's Wamo ruling (C-149/17) creates legal uncertainty over digital content as "product"
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic triggers software failures in health apps; Commission announces recast
- **September 2022** — Commission publishes proposal for recast directive
- **March 2023** — European Parliament adopts position with amendments strengthening consumer protections
- **June 2024** — Trilogue agreement reached; final legal text published in Official Journal
- **December 2024** — Directive enters into force (20 days after publication)
- **December 2028** — Member states must transpose into national law
- **December 2029** — Application date for the new rules

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate impact is on **AI system developers**. Any company placing an AI product on the EU market — whether a chatbot, autonomous vehicle algorithm, or medical diagnostic tool — is now strictly liable for defects that cause harm, even if the defect arises from the AI's "learning" behaviour after deployment. The Commission's impact assessment estimated that **4.5 million companies** in the EU that produce AI-enabled products would need to review their insurance, testing, and compliance procedures.

The **automotive industry** was hit hardest. German carmakers like Volkswagen and BMW — who had previously argued that software updates were free "improvements" with no liability — now face that every over-the-air update creates a new product for liability purposes. The directive explicitly states that software updates constitute "substantial modifications" triggering new liability.

The **open-source community** initially worried about liability for volunteer contributions, but the final text exempts open-source software developed and supplied "other than in the course of a commercial activity" — though companies using open-source AI components in commercial products remain fully liable.

Consumer groups celebrated the removal of the **€500 threshold** for property damage claims. The Commission estimated that the threshold previously prevented **millions of small claims** for damaged smartphones and laptops caused by defective chargers or batteries.

The directive also clarified **cybersecurity liability**: a product with "inadequate security features" that causes harm (e.g., a hacked smart home device leading to physical injury) now falls under strict liability. This was a direct answer to the 2020-2021 rise in ransomware attacks on medical devices, which affected over 560 hospitals in Germany alone according to the BSI.

## Key questions answered

### Can you sue a company for harm caused by an AI or software bug in the EU?

Yes, definitively. The recast Directive explicitly classifies software, AI models, and digital files as "products" under strict liability rules. A consumer who suffers harm from a defective navigation app, a flawed medical AI, or a smart home device hacked due to security flaws can now bring a claim without having to prove the manufacturer was negligent — only that the product was defective and caused the harm.

### Does this mean every AI chatbot or app developer needs product liability insurance?

Not every small app developer — but anyone placing an AI product on the EU market commercially almost certainly does. The directive exempts non-commercial open-source AI development, but once it's sold or used in a professional context, strict liability applies. The Commission's impact assessment noted that liability insurance premiums for AI products could increase 20-40%, and the directive specifically requires that claims be "viable" through sufficient insurance coverage for commercial operators.

### Why did EU law change its definition of "product" after 40 years?

Because the 1985 Directive was written for the physical world — typewriters, washing machines, and cars. The 2015 Volkswagen Dieselgate scandal was the moment EU regulators realised software that deliberately manipulated emissions tests was not legally a "product" that could be sued for. Then came the explosion of smart devices, AI diagnostics, and over-the-air software updates, each exposing the same gap. The ECJ's own rulings in 2019 showed courts were hopelessly divided on whether software counted. The pandemic, with its health-app failures, broke the political deadlock.

### Who opposed this change and why?

The software and tech industry, represented by **DigitalEurope** (Google, Apple, Microsoft, Siemens), fought it hard, warning that strict liability would make innovation "legally toxic" for small AI startups. German industry group **BDI** argued software was a service, not a product. The opposition was intense enough to delay the Commission's proposal for over a year, but the final vote in the European Parliament was 637-21, showing the political will was overwhelming. The industry's main concession was the exemption for non-commercial open-source development.
