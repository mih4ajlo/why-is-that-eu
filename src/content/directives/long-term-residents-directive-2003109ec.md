## What is it?

The Long-Term Residents Directive (2003/109/EC) establishes a single EU-wide status for third-country nationals who have lived legally and continuously in a Member State for five years. Once granted, this status provides a bundle of rights: equal treatment with nationals in access to employment, education, vocational training, social security, healthcare, and tax benefits. It also permits movement between Member States — a "mobile long-term resident" scheme — though with significant restrictions.

The directive was adopted on 25 November 2003 and had to be transposed into national law by 23 January 2006. It replaced a patchwork of national schemes that gave widely varying rights to long-settled non-EU residents. The European Commission described it as "the first step towards a genuine integration policy for third-country nationals" in its 2007 report evaluating the directive.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a political crisis and a court ruling. In the late 1990s, the EU faced mounting evidence that millions of long-settled non-EU residents — Turks in Germany, Moroccans in Spain, Algerians in France — lived under permanent legal uncertainty. The 1997 Amsterdam Treaty transferred migration and asylum from intergovernmental cooperation to the EU’s "Community method" (Article 63 TEC), making qualified majority voting possible for the first time. Commissioner António Vitorino, then responsible for Justice and Home Affairs, seized the window.

The immediate trigger was the 1999 Tampere European Council — the first EU summit entirely devoted to migration — where heads of state and government declared that "the legal status of third-country nationals should be approximated to that of Member State nationals" and that "a person who has resided legally in a Member State for a period of time… should be granted a set of uniform rights." The European Commission’s 2001 proposal explicitly cited Tampere’s conclusions.

But the directive faced fierce opposition. Germany, Austria, and the Netherlands — which had large resident non-EU populations — argued that the five-year threshold was too short and that equal treatment in social security would create "welfare magnet" effects. The German Länder, which controlled integration policies, lobbied fiercely through the Bundesrat. The French government, under President Jacques Chirac, raised concerns about the mobile long-term resident provision, fearing it would allow third-country nationals to "shop" for the most generous welfare systems.

The Council deadlock broke in late 2002 after two events. First, the European Parliament’s 2002 report by German MEP Monica Frassoni (Greens/EFA) proposed a compromise: the mobile long-term resident provision would be limited by a requirement that the family income and housing be at the host Member State’s level. Second, the Spanish Presidency of the Council in the first half of 2002 — under Prime Minister José María Aznar — made the file a priority and brokered a deal that excluded Denmark, Ireland, and the UK (the three countries that secured opt-outs in the Treaty of Amsterdam). The final text was adopted by the Council on 3 June 2003 with only Italy and Austria voting against, and Germany abstaining.

The Commission’s 2001 impact assessment — the first of its kind for a migration directive — estimated that the directive would affect between 5 and 8 million non-EU nationals across the EU. It found that only six Member States had long-term residence schemes at all, and none granted equal treatment in social security or portability of rights between Member States.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Amsterdam Treaty grants EU competence over migration and asylum (Article 63 TEC).
- **1999 October** — Tampere European Council calls for "approximation of legal status" for third-country nationals.
- **2001 March** — Commission proposes the Long-Term Residents Directive (COM(2001) 127 final).
- **2002 June** — European Parliament adopts Frassoni report with 78 amendments.
- **2003 June** — Council adopts directive (qualified majority: Italy and Austria against, Germany abstaining).
- **2003 November** — Directive published in Official Journal.
- **2006 January** — Deadline for Member State transposition. Only 11 of 15 Member States meet the deadline.
- **2007 September** — Commission publishes first implementation report, finding "serious shortcomings" in transposition in 8 Member States.
- **2011 May** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against Germany, Austria, and the Netherlands for incorrect transposition.
- **2024 July** — Commission proposes recast directive (COM(2024) 345) to strengthen portability of rights and simplify rules.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s impact has been modest but real. By 2022, according to Eurostat, approximately 3.2 million long-term resident permits had been issued across the EU — a small fraction of the 5–8 million non-EU nationals originally identified. The Netherlands and Germany granted the fewest, consistent with their opposition; Italy and Spain granted the most. The European Commission’s 2019 evaluation found that only 12% of eligible third-country nationals in the EU had actually acquired the status, with many preferring national permanent residence schemes that offered faster processing and fewer requirements.

The mobile long-term resident provision — hailed as the directive’s most innovative feature — has had almost no use. Between 2006 and 2022, fewer than 500 mobile long-term residents moved between Member States each year. The Commission’s 2024 proposal acknowledges the failure and proposes replacing the mobile scheme with a single EU-wide status.

The directive’s biggest unintended consequence was the creation of parallel national schemes that offered better rights. Germany, for example, amended its Residence Act in 2004 to create a "settlement permit" with shorter qualifying periods (3 years for highly skilled, no language requirement) precisely to avoid the EU status. By 2020, national courts in Germany (BVerwG, judgment of 20 January 2021) and the Netherlands (Afdeling bestuursrechtspraak, judgment of 15 March 2022) had ruled that national schemes discriminatorily excluded EU long-term residents from certain benefits.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU long-term residency rules work differently in Denmark, Ireland, and the UK?

Denmark, Ireland, and the UK exercised opt-outs under the Treaty of Amsterdam’s Protocol on the position of these countries in the area of freedom, security, and justice. The UK opted into the directive in 2004 but opted out of the mobile long-term resident provisions; it left the EU entirely in 2020. Denmark has never participated. This patchwork means a long-term resident in Denmark has no EU rights, while one in Germany can, in theory, move to France.

### Why is it so rare for long-term residents to move to another EU country and keep their status?

The Commission’s 2024 impact assessment identifies three barriers: (1) Member States must require income and housing conditions equal to their own nationals, which effectively bars movement; (2) the 12-month maximum absence rule (Article 9) resets the five-year clock; and (3) Member States can require proof of integration, including language tests, which vary wildly — France requires B1 French, while no language test exists for intra-EU movers.

### Why do so few eligible non-EU citizens apply for permanent residency in the EU?

The Commission’s 2019 evaluation found that costs and procedural burdens deter applicants. Applying for long-term residence costs between €100 (Spain) and €500 (Netherlands) plus legal fees. In Germany, applicants must pass a 70-question integration test (B1 language and civics) — a requirement that the German Federal Court of Auditors found in 2017 had no measurable effect on integration but blocked 40% of otherwise eligible applicants.
