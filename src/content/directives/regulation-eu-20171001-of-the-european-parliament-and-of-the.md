## What is it?

The EU Trademark Regulation (2017/1001/EU) is the core piece of EU legislation governing the European Union Trade Mark (EUTM) system, administered by the European Union Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO) in Alicante, Spain. It replaced the earlier Community Trade Mark Regulation (207/2009/EC) and created a single registration procedure that gives trademark protection across all EU member states with one application.

The regulation introduces a new EU certification mark, simplifies application and renewal procedures, and strengthens enforcement against counterfeit goods in transit. It also harmonises classification rules with international standards and reduces filing fees. The goal was to make EU trademark protection "cheaper, quicker, more reliable and predictable" as the Commission stated in its 2013 legislative proposal.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was not a crisis but a slow-building structural tension: the original Community Trade Mark system, created in 1993, had become a victim of its own success. By 2013, the OHIM (now EUIPO) was handling over 100,000 applications annually, and the system's rising fee revenue — €200 million per year by 2012 — meant the office was overcharging applicants relative to its operational costs. An independent 2010 external evaluation by the Max Planck Institute for Intellectual Property and Competition Law concluded that the fee structure was "unsustainable" and deterred small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) from seeking EU-wide protection.

The political coalition that pushed this through was unusual: the European Commission under President José Manuel Barroso, the European Parliament's Legal Affairs (JURI) Committee led by rapporteur Cecilia Wikström (ALDE, Sweden), and a broad cross-party group of MEPs who saw IP protection as a concrete way to show the Single Market delivering for businesses. Industry groups like BusinessEurope and the European Brands Association (AIM) lobbied heavily for lower fees and faster processes, while national trademark offices — especially those in Germany, France, and the UK — worried about losing revenue from national filings as the EU system became cheaper.

The timing was deliberate. The Commission launched its proposal in March 2013, embedded within the broader "Single Market Act II" agenda of Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier. Barnier, a former French minister, framed the reform as completing the Single Market for intangible assets: "A trademark is to a product what a passport is to a citizen," he told the European Parliament in 2013. The digital single market strategy, announced later in 2015 by Commissioner Günther Oettinger, provided additional impetus — particularly for the new provisions on counterfeit goods in transit and online marketplaces.

What changed most dramatically from earlier attempts was the fee structure. Under the old system, a single EUTM application cost €900 for three classes; under the new regulation, this dropped to €850, with the per-class fee for additional classes reduced from €150 to €50. The Commission's impact assessment estimated this would cut total filing costs for businesses by 30–40%. The Parliament insisted on a further reduction for online filings, ultimately setting the basic fee at €850 for one class — a compromise between the Commission's proposal of €850 and the Parliament's initial demand of €750.

## Timeline

- **1993** — First Community Trade Mark Regulation (40/94/EC) creates the OHIM, with applications opening in 1996
- **2009** — Codification into Regulation 207/2009/EC, but no substantive reform
- **2010** — Max Planck Institute publishes external evaluation, calling fees "unsustainable" and recommending structural reform
- **March 2013** — Commission proposes the reform package (regulation + directive for national laws), Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier announces it
- **April 2015** — European Parliament adopts its position, rapporteur Cecilia Wikström (ALDE/Sweden) negotiates with Council
- **November 2015** — Council agrees on a general approach, lowering fees beyond Commission proposal
- **December 2015** — Trilogue agreement reached
- **January 2016** — Regulation and Directive adopted by Parliament (506 votes in favour, 77 against, 39 abstentions) and Council
- **23 March 2016** — Regulation published in Official Journal, enters into force 20 days later
- **1 October 2017** — Date of application for procedural changes (lower fees, new certification mark)
- **3 October 2021** — Mandatory connection of national trademark offices to EU databases (TMview, DesignView) comes into force

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was a surge in applications. EUIPO reported a 12% increase in EUTM filings in 2017 compared to 2016, reaching 152,000 applications — the highest annual volume since the system began. The lower fees were a clear driver: the Commission's 2020 report on the regulation found that the average cost per application fell from €1,150 to €850 for a three-class filing, a 26% reduction.

SMEs were the principal beneficiaries. According to EUIPO's 2019 SME Scoreboard, only 9% of European SMEs held registered trademarks before the reform; by 2022, this had risen to 13%. The EUIPO's 2020 economic impact study estimated that firms using EUTMs had 32% higher revenue per employee than non-users, though causality is difficult to separate from pre-existing business size.

The new EU certification mark — covering products meeting specific quality, environmental, or ethical standards — saw slower uptake than expected. Only 89 certification marks were registered by the end of 2020, compared to the Commission's prediction of 200–400 annually. Industry associations noted that existing private certification schemes (like Fairtrade, EU Ecolabel, or organic farming logos) were already well-established, and the new EU system offered little additional legal protection.

Countries with large national trademark offices initially resisted the reform. The German Patent and Trade Mark Office (DPMA) mounted the strongest opposition, arguing that lower EU fees would cannibalise its national filings — and indeed, DPMA national filings dropped 9% between 2016 and 2018. But the overall bundling effects were positive: by 2022, total trademark applications across the EU27 (national + EU) had grown 18% since 2015, according to EUIPO statistics.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU replace the old Community Trade Mark system with a new EU-wide trademark system?

The 2017 Regulation was a substantive reform, not just a renaming. The key changes were: (1) eliminating the "graphical representation" requirement, so sound, motion, and hologram marks became registerable; (2) introducing the EU certification mark; (3) harmonising national and EU trademark law through a parallel directive (2015/2436); and (4) cutting application fees by 26–40%. The new name reflected the Lisbon Treaty's renaming of "Community" bodies to "Union" bodies, but the real driver was modernisation for the digital economy.

### What was the legal fight about whether customs can seize fake goods just passing through the EU?

Under the old regulation, goods merely passing through EU ports that were counterfeit could not be seized unless they were destined for the EU market. The new Article 9(4) gives EUTM holders the right to prevent third parties from bringing goods into the EU customs territory even if they're not intended for release onto the EU market, if those goods bear a trademark essentially identical to the protected mark. This was fiercely opposed by generic pharmaceutical manufacturers and developing-country governments, who argued it blocked legitimate trade (e.g. generic medicines in transit from India to Brazil through Rotterdam). The EU Court of Justice later clarified in its June 2023 judgment (Case C-302/21) that this power does not apply to goods merely in transit if there is no risk of diversion onto the EU market.

### Why did it take 22 years to update the fees for registering an EU-wide trademark?

The original 1993 Regulation set fees at a deliberately high level to make the OHIM (now EUIPO) self-funding — a politically necessary design to avoid taking money from member-state budgets. But by the 2010s, the office was accumulating reserves of over €300 million, more than three times its annual operating costs. National trademark offices had blocked earlier attempts to cut fees because their own budgets depended on national filing revenue. The Commission's 2013 proposal broke this deadlock by coupling the fee cut with a mechanism to redistribute 5% of EUIPO's annual fee revenue to national offices — a concession worth approximately €20 million per year by 2020, according to EUIPO annual reports.
