## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2020/1783 replaces a 2001 Council Regulation on cross-border evidence gathering in civil and commercial matters. Its central innovation is forcing national courts to accept evidence via video-conference when a party requests it, unless it is incompatible with the fundamental principles of that member state's law. It also mandates that evidence be transmitted through a secure, decentralised IT system — the e-CODEX system — rather than by post or fax.

The Regulation applies to all EU member states except Denmark, which opted out of the area of freedom, security and justice. It entered into force on 1 July 2022. Under the new rules, a court in one member state can request a court in another to take evidence directly (a "letter of request"), or a party can ask their own court to take evidence from a witness located abroad via video-link — a faster, cheaper alternative to flying witnesses across borders for small claims.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the COVID-19 pandemic. In March 2020, as courts across Europe shut down physical hearings, the European Commission's Justice Directorate (DG JUST) realised that the existing 2001 Regulation — written when video-conferencing was rare and email was still novel — did not explicitly permit remote testimony across borders. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment noted that only 8% of member states had implemented any form of video-link for cross-border evidence before the pandemic. This created an absurd situation: during lockdowns, a court in Italy could request a witness statement from a court in Germany, but the German court could only proceed if the witness physically appeared — which was impossible.

But the pandemic was the catalyst, not the cause. The deeper structural problem had been festering for two decades. The 2001 Regulation (EC) No 1206/2001 had been written when the internet was still dial-up. It allowed transmission "by any suitable means" — which in practice meant fax, post, or courier. By 2017, the European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee (JURI) found that the average time for a cross-border evidence request was 58 days, and 30% of requests were never fulfilled. Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) were disproportionately affected: a 2018 European Commission survey of law firms found that 45% of respondents said the cost of cross-border evidence gathering was a deterrent to pursuing legitimate claims abroad.

The coalition that pushed the revision was unusual. On one side, the European Commission's DG JUST under Commissioner Didier Reynders (2019-2024) was driven by the broader e-Justice strategy launched in 2008 but never fully implemented. On the other side, the European Parliament's JURI Committee rapporteur, Emil Radev (EPP, Bulgaria), had chaired hearings where judges complained about paper-heavy procedures. The European Law Institute issued a 2018 report explicitly calling for video-link as the default method. The strongest opposition came from France and Germany, whose judicial councils feared that remote testimony would undermine the principle of immediacy — the idea that a judge must directly observe a witness to assess credibility. The compromise was the "fundamental principles" escape clause, allowing member states to refuse video-link if it clashed with their legal traditions, but forcing them to explain why in writing.

Three earlier attempts to reform the regulation had stalled. A 2007 Commission report proposed amendments, but member states blocked them over cost concerns — they didn't want to pay for video-conference equipment. A 2014 European Parliament resolution called for reform, but the Juncker Commission (2014-2019) prioritised data protection and digital single market files. What finally broke the deadlock was the 2018 e-CODEX project going live — a decentralised IT system for judicial cooperation funded by the EU's Connecting Europe Facility. Once the technology existed, the argument that reform was premature collapsed.

The final text passed the European Parliament on 25 November 2020 with 639 votes in favour, 12 against, and 18 abstentions. The Council adopted it unanimously on 4 December 2020. The Regulation entered into force on 1 July 2022, with a further two-year transition period for member states to connect to e-CODEX.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Council Regulation (EC) No 1206/2001 enters into force, establishing rules for cross-border evidence gathering but relying on fax and post.
- **2008** — European Commission launches the e-Justice strategy, calling for digitalisation of judicial cooperation.
- **2014** — European Parliament resolution (2013/2104(INI)) calls for reform of the 2001 Regulation; no Commission proposal follows.
- **2018** — e-CODEX system becomes operational, funded under the Connecting Europe Facility. European Law Institute publishes report recommending video-link as default.
- **2019** — Commissioner Didier Reynders (Justice) takes office, making e-Justice a priority.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 lockdowns shut down physical court hearings across Europe; Commission accelerates work on revision.
- **25 November 2020** — European Parliament adopts Regulation 2020/1783 by 639 votes to 12, with 18 abstentions.
- **4 December 2020** — Council adopts Regulation unanimously.
- **1 July 2022** — Regulation enters into force and applies directly in all member states except Denmark.
- **1 July 2024** — Deadline for all member states to connect to the e-CODEX system for evidence transmission.

## Impact and consequences

The Regulation's most immediate impact was on the conduct of cross-border litigation. According to a 2023 European Commission report, the number of video-link requests under the new Regulation increased by 340% in the first year compared to the previous five-year average. However, the same report noted that only 12 member states had fully connected their courts to e-CODEX by the July 2024 deadline. The others, including France and Italy, were given extensions citing technical complexity.

For small businesses, the change was significant. A 2022 study by the European Small Business Alliance found that the cost of gathering evidence from another member state dropped by an estimated 40-60% where video-link was used, compared to sending a lawyer abroad to attend a hearing. The European Commission's 2023 impact evaluation estimated that the Regulation saved EU businesses approximately €120 million in legal costs in its first year of operation.

The video-link provisions also had unintended consequences. Several German courts initially refused to accept live video testimony from witnesses in other member states, arguing it violated the German principle of "Unmittelbarkeit" (immediacy). The Commission issued a reasoned opinion in 2023, and Germany amended its Zivilprozessordnung (civil procedure code) to explicitly permit video-link for cross-border evidence. Similar adjustments were made in Austria and Luxembourg.

## Key questions answered

### Can a witness in another EU country testify by video rather than flying to court?

Yes, under Regulation 2020/1783. A court in one EU member state must accept a request to take evidence via video-conference from a witness located in another member state, unless the receiving court can demonstrate that video-link is incompatible with its fundamental legal principles. The requesting court must cover the costs of the video equipment. This applies to civil and commercial matters only, not criminal cases.

### Why did Europe need a new law to allow video evidence after the pandemic?

The old 2001 Regulation did not explicitly forbid video-link, but it also did not require courts to accept it. In practice, most courts simply refused, citing the need for physical presence. The pandemic revealed this as unworkable. The 2020 Regulation made video-link the default option unless a member state formally objects, shifting the burden of proof from the party requesting video to the objecting court.

### Does every EU country have to use the same computer system for evidence?

Yes, but with a transition period. The Regulation mandates that all evidence requests be transmitted through the e-CODEX system — a decentralised IT platform connecting national judicial systems. By July 2024, all member states except Denmark were supposed to be connected. As of late 2024, several countries including France and Italy had only partially connected, so paper and email are still used in practice in some jurisdictions.

### Can a company refuse to send evidence to another EU country under this Regulation?

Not easily. The Regulation obliges courts to execute requests for evidence without delay — generally within 90 days. A company can only refuse if the requested evidence falls outside the scope of civil and commercial matters (e.g., criminal or tax disputes), or if the request would require the disclosure of privileged information. There is no general "commercial confidentiality" exception for ordinary business records.
