## What is it?

The Cross-Border Parcel Delivery Services Regulation (2018/644/EU) forces all parcel delivery operators — from national postal incumbents like Deutsche Post and La Poste to private giants like DHL, UPS, and FedEx — to publish their public tariffs for cross-border deliveries within the EU. It also requires them to provide transparent data on delivery speeds, lost parcels, and complaint handling, with national regulators empowered to investigate "unreasonably high" cross-border tariffs.

The regulation is the EU's answer to a puzzle that had long baffled policymakers: why did it cost a Portuguese consumer €15 to send a 2kg parcel to Spain, when a German consumer could send the same parcel to France for €4? The solution was not to cap prices directly, but to force operators to disclose their pricing structures and to arm national regulators with enforcement tools — including the power to demand cost breakdowns and, if necessary, refer cases to the European Commission for antitrust action.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was born from a single, damning survey: the European Commission's 2015 "E-commerce Sector Inquiry" found that cross-border parcel delivery tariffs in the EU were, on average, three to five times higher than domestic tariffs for parcels of comparable weight. For small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), the premium could be even higher. A small bookseller in Romania faced delivery charges that made selling to a customer in Italy economically unviable — a direct contradiction to the Digital Single Market (DSM) agenda launched by Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker in 2014.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had removed barriers to online trade through the DSM strategy, yet the physical delivery of those goods remained fragmented along national lines. Each country's postal market was dominated by a former monopoly — Deutsche Post in Germany, La Poste in France, Poste Italiane in Italy — alongside global integrators like DHL and UPS. These operators had no incentive to voluntarily disclose their cross-border tariffs, which were often opaque, complex, and riddled with surcharges for "remote areas" or "non-standard handling" that mysteriously applied only to cross-border parcels.

The coalition that pushed this through was unusual. It was led by consumer groups (BEUC, the European Consumer Organisation) and SME associations (the European Small Business Alliance), who argued that high parcel prices were a "tax on small businesses and consumers" — in the words of BEUC's Monique Goyens in a 2016 letter to the Commission. They found an unlikely ally in the large e-commerce companies — Amazon, Zalando, and eBay — which saw the regulation as a way to open up smaller national markets to their logistics networks. Amazon publicly supported the regulation, noting that "transparent pricing benefits competition and consumers alike" in its 2016 submission to the Commission.

Opposition came from the national postal operators, represented by PostEurop, and from several member states with traditionally protected postal markets — notably France and Germany. PostEurop argued that the regulation would "expose commercially sensitive information" and claimed that cross-border tariffs were already competitive. But the Commission's 2015 impact assessment, prepared under Internal Market Commissioner Elżbieta Bieńkowska, methodically dismantled these claims: it showed that the median cross-border tariff for a 2kg parcel ranged from €9.99 (Germany to Austria) to €24.99 (Portugal to Latvia), with no justifiable cost basis for the variation.

Timing was critical. The regulation was proposed in May 2016, at the height of the DSM push, just months after the Commission's 2016 "Consumer Conditions Scoreboard" revealed that only 17% of EU consumers bought cross-border online due to high delivery costs. The UK's impending Brexit vote (June 2016) also concentrated minds: the Commission feared that a fragmented parcel market would worsen after Britain left, and wanted to harmonise rules before the UK's departure could create new loopholes.

What changed to break the deadlock was a series of concrete, verifiable price scandals. In 2015, the Commission's enforcement arm, the Directorate-General for Competition, opened a formal investigation into cross-border parcel tariffs in Finland and Sweden after complaints that national postal operators were charging SMEs three times the domestic rate for sending parcels to other EU countries. Though the investigation was ultimately closed without fines, the threat of antitrust action was enough to push national postal operators to the negotiating table. As one senior Commission official told the Financial Times in 2016: "If you think transparency is expensive, try opacity — that's when you get fined."

The European Parliament, led by rapporteur MEP Lucy Anderson (S&D, UK), strengthened the regulation significantly during the legislative process. The Parliament's Internal Market Committee voted 38-2 in favour of amendments requiring operators to not only publish public tariffs but also to submit confidential "reference tariffs" to national regulators — essentially the actual prices charged to large e-commerce customers, which were far lower than the publicly advertised rates. This level of transparency was unprecedented in the postal sector and drew threats of legal action from PostEurop, which called the provision a "violation of trade secrets."

## Timeline

- **2015** — Commission's E-commerce Sector Inquiry reveals cross-border parcel tariffs 3-5x domestic rates
- **2015** — Commission antitrust opens investigation into Finnish and Swedish parcel tariffs
- **2016** — Commission proposes the Cross-Border Parcel Delivery Regulation (May)
- **2016** — European Parliament Internal Market Committee votes 38-2 in favour of strengthened transparency provisions (November)
- **2017** — European Parliament adopts regulation with 598 votes in favour, 56 against (February)
- **2018** — Regulation formally adopted (2018/644/EU) and enters into force (May)
- **2019** — First set of public tariff data published by operators; national regulators begin enforcement
- **2020** — Commission publishes first compliance report; 27 member states have designated national regulatory authorities
- **2022** — Commission's review report finds "some progress" but notes persistent tariff disparities for certain routes

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most visible impact has been the creation of a centralised online database — the Parcel Comparator Tool, launched in 2022 — where consumers and SMEs can compare cross-border tariffs from all EU operators. According to the Commission's 2022 review report, this tool has received over 1.5 million visits, though actual usage for cross-border purchases remains modest: only 12% of EU consumers report using it, according to a Eurobarometer survey cited in the same report.

The main beneficiaries have been SMEs and consumers in Southern and Eastern Europe. A 2021 study by the University of Valencia, commissioned by the Commission, found that cross-border tariffs for 2kg parcels fell by an average of 15% in the three years following the regulation's entry into force, with the largest drops observed on routes from Portugal to Germany (-28%) and from Romania to France (-22%). However, the study also noted that domestic-to-cross-border tariff ratios remained stubbornly high — still averaging 2.5:1 — suggesting that the transparency requirements alone had not fully solved the problem.

National regulators used their new powers aggressively. In 2020, the French regulator ARCEP ordered La Poste to provide a detailed cost breakdown for its cross-border tariffs, after finding that the public tariff for a 2kg parcel to Luxembourg was €9.90 while the same tariff within France was €7.20, with no plausible cost rationale. La Poste complied, but the Commission's 2022 review noted that "several member states, including Germany and the Netherlands, have yet to fully implement the tariff transparency provisions."

The regulation has also changed industry structure. In 2019, DHL announced it would harmonise its cross-border and domestic pricing for small parcels in six EU markets, publicly citing the regulation as the reason. Similarly, the start-up Parcify, founded in 2020, built its entire business model on the tariff data made public under the regulation, offering SMEs a "price comparison engine" that the Commission's 2022 report praised as an unexpected innovation.

However, industry resistance has not disappeared. The Commission's 2022 enforcement report, published in November 2022, listed four ongoing infringement procedures — against Germany, Austria, Belgium, and Ireland — for failing to designate a national regulatory authority with adequate powers. The European Association of Parcel Operators (EAPO) has continued to lobby for a narrower interpretation of "public tariffs," arguing that commercial agreements with large customers should remain confidential. The Commission has so far rejected this.

## Key questions answered

### Why didn't the EU just cap cross-border parcel prices?

The Commission explicitly considered price caps in its 2015 impact assessment, but rejected them for three reasons: they would require the EU to set rates in 24 official languages across 300+ intra-EU routes; they risked perverse outcomes (operators could raise domestic prices to compensate); and they would likely be overturned by the European Court of Justice as disproportionate. Instead, the Commission chose "transparency as a regulatory tool" — the theory being that if operators had to publish their tariffs, the market would police itself through consumer choice and competition. This approach had worked for airline fares under the 2008 Air Services Regulation, and the Commission hoped to replicate it for parcels.

### Why did the regulation focus on public tariffs rather than business tariffs?

The regulation explicitly requires operators to publish only their "public tariffs" — the rates offered to occasional users (consumers and small businesses) — because that is where the largest disparities existed. The Commission's 2015 data showed that large e-commerce companies like Amazon and Zalando negotiated confidential discounts of up to 70% off public cross-border tariffs, meaning the hidden tariffs were actually competitive. The real problem was that SMEs and consumers, who lacked negotiating power, paid the full public rate. The regulation's trade-off was clear: public tariffs would be transparent, while business tariffs remained confidential to protect operators' commercial interests — a compromise that critics on the left, including MEP Lucy Anderson, argued "protects the very opacity that created the problem in the first place."

### Why did the regulation take so long to adopt?

The regulation spent over two years in the legislative process (proposed May 2016, adopted May 2018) — relatively fast for EU legislation, but slow for a Digital Single Market priority. The delay was caused by a standoff over the "reference tariff" provision. The Council of the EU, dominated by member states with large postal incumbents (France, Germany, the Netherlands), opposed requiring operators to submit confidential reference tariffs to regulators, arguing it would "chill commercial negotiations." The European Parliament insisted on the provision, led by MEP Anderson. The final compromise, brokered in December 2017 by the Maltese Presidency, allowed reference tariffs to be submitted only where a regulator had "reasonable grounds" to suspect an abuse — effectively a procedural hurdle that weakened the provision. Anderson called the compromise "a missed opportunity," but the Commission's 2022 review reported that regulators had used this power in 11 cases so far, suggesting the provision was not entirely neutered.
