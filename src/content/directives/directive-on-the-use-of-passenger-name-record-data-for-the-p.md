## What is it?

The PNR Directive (2016/681/EU) requires airlines operating flights to or from the European Union to transfer Passenger Name Record data — the booking information passengers provide when reserving a ticket — to national authorities, known as Passenger Information Units (PIUs). This data includes names, travel dates, itineraries, seat numbers, baggage details, contact information, and payment methods. Airlines must hand over this data 24 to 48 hours before departure, and for a period of up to five years, national authorities can retain and analyse it, though after six months the data must be depersonalised by masking out passenger names. The directive applies only to extra-EU flights; a 2017 Commission proposal to extend it to intra-EU flights remains stalled.

The data is used for what the directive terms "risk assessment" — automated profiling of passengers against pre-defined indicators and law enforcement databases. Unlike the earlier 2004 API Directive (2004/82/EC), which only covered passport-controlled advance passenger information (name, nationality, document details), PNR data gives authorities a much richer picture: travel companions, booking patterns, and payment methods. The directive explicitly prohibits using PNR data for a person’s racial or ethnic origin, religion, or political opinions, and restricts use to terrorism and serious crime (defined as offences carrying a maximum sentence of at least three years imprisonment). The European Court of Justice upheld the directive in 2022 under strict conditions, but struck down blanket data retention periods in a 2024 judgment on a related Belgian case.

## Why was it introduced?

The PNR Directive was the culmination of a decade-long push by EU interior ministries for systematic access to airline booking data, repeatedly blocked by the European Parliament on privacy grounds. The decisive breakthrough came in 2015: two Paris and one Brussels terrorist attack within seven months turned privacy objections into political liabilities.

The story begins after 9/11. The US had mandated PNR collection from all international airlines in 2001 under the Aviation and Transportation Security Act, threatening fines of $6,000 per passenger and landing bans. EU carriers like Lufthansa, Air France, and KLM faced an impossible choice — violate EU data protection law or lose access to US airports. The European Commission negotiated an interim agreement in 2004, but the European Parliament took the case to the European Court of Justice, which annulled the agreement in 2006 on an inadequate legal basis (a first pillar transport issue rather than a third pillar police cooperation matter). A replacement US-EU agreement was struck in 2007, but the controversy had convinced the Commission’s then-Directorate General for Justice, Freedom and Security that the EU needed its own PNR system. In November 2007, the Commission — with Home Affairs Commissioner Franco Frattini leading — proposed an EU PNR directive. The Parliament, under the co-decision procedure, rejected it in 2008 on data protection grounds.

The deadlock held for seven years. A 2011 Commission proposal was again stalled in the Parliament’s Civil Liberties Committee (LIBE), where MEPs like Jan Philipp Albrecht (Greens/EFA, Germany) argued the system constituted mass surveillance without evidence of effectiveness. The UK Home Office, under successive ministers Theresa May and Amber Rudd, pushed hardest for the directive, arguing that the UK’s existing PNR system had been critical in operations — though a 2014 Home Office review found PNR was used in only 1% of counter-terrorism intelligence reports. The UK, along with France, Sweden, and Ireland, formed the core coalition for adoption. Germany, under Chancellor Angela Merkel, was internally divided — the Interior Ministry supported PNR while the Justice Ministry raised constitutional concerns. The Netherlands and Belgium expressed privacy reservations but supported the principle.

The blocking minority was led by the European Parliament’s Greens/EFA group and elements of the Socialists & Democrats, supported by digital rights organisations including European Digital Rights (EDRi) and the civil liberties campaign group Statewatch. Their argument was twofold: first, that mass retention of passenger data violated the proportionality principle of EU data protection law; second, that no rigorous study had demonstrated PNR’s effectiveness versus existing law enforcement tools like the Schengen Information System. The Commission itself acknowledged in a 2011 impact assessment that "the actual effectiveness of PNR data in combating terrorism has not been systematically evaluated".

What changed was the sequence of terrorist attacks in 2015-16. The Charlie Hebdo attack in Paris on 7 January 2015 killed 12 people. The coordinated Paris attacks on 13 November 2015 killed 130. The Brussels airport and metro bombings on 22 March 2016 killed 32. In each case, investigators found that the attackers had travelled by air between EU member states — the Paris attackers had transited through Greece, and the Brussels bombers had returned from Syria. The French government under President François Hollande declared a state of emergency and demanded the Parliament unblock the PNR file. Under the Dutch EU Presidency in early 2016, the Council pushed the issue to a vote. On 14 April 2016, the European Parliament adopted the directive by 461 votes to 179, with 9 abstentions. The Greens/EFA and much of the the Socialists & Democrats group voted against, but the centre-right European People’s Party (EPP), the centre-left Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe (ALDE), and the European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) provided the majority. The Belgian MEP Sophie in 't Veld (ALDE), who had campaigned against PNR for a decade, called the vote "a terrible mistake". The directive was formally adopted by the Council on 21 April 2016.

The timing was not accidental. The attacks created a window where interior ministers could frame the debate in binary terms: adopt PNR or accept responsibility for future attacks that might have been prevented. The Commission’s 2015 European Agenda on Security explicitly linked the directive to the Paris attacks, stating it would "provide a systematic, comprehensive and consistent approach to the use of PNR data". No rigorous cost-benefit analysis was publicly debated during the final push; the focus was on speed.

## Impact and consequences

The PNR Directive created a significant compliance burden for airlines. Every carrier flying to or from the EU must now have the technical infrastructure to transmit PNR data to each member state's Passenger Information Unit, often via the API/PNR messaging system called the Common PNR Interface (CPI). For low-cost carriers like Ryanair and EasyJet, which operate high-frequency point-to-point services, the per-flight data transmission requirement created substantial operational costs. Major legacy carriers like Lufthansa Group and Air France-KLM had already been transmitting data to the US for years but faced adapting systems for EU member states individually.

By 2021, the Commission reported that all 26 member states subject to the directive (Denmark, Ireland, and the UK had opt-outs; the UK later implemented its own PNR system post-Brexit) had established PIUs, though 18 member states faced infringement proceedings for missing the May 2018 transposition deadline. The European Parliament's LIBE committee oversight reports noted in 2020 that several member states' PIUs lacked adequate independent oversight mechanisms. Germany's transposition, for example, was delayed until June 2018 after the Bundestag passed the law just weeks before the deadline, following constitutional challenges that were rejected by the Federal Constitutional Court.

The directive's effectiveness remains disputed. Europol's 2021 report on PNR use stated that PNR data supported 1,500 investigations across the EU between 2018 and 2020, but did not specify how many resulted in convictions. The UK Home Office's 2019 evaluation — the only public member state assessment — found PNR contributed to 2% of its serious crime operations. Digital rights organisations like Privacy International and EDRi have consistently pointed to the 2022 ECJ judgment (Opinion 1/15) that the directive's five-year retention period "goes beyond what is strictly necessary" and the 2024 ECJ ruling in C-817/19 that struck down blanket retention entirely. European Data Protection Supervisor Wojciech Wiewiórowski has repeatedly called for the directive to be revised to align with the court's proportionality requirements.

The directive's most significant institutional impact has been the creation of permanent Passenger Information Units within member state police or border agencies, employing thousands of analysts dedicated to processing flight data. France's PNR unit, created in 2017, is staffed by approximately 300 officers from the interior ministry, customs, and gendarmerie, according to the French National Assembly's 2019 evaluation.

## Key questions answered

### Do EU authorities automatically screen all passengers on flights from outside Europe?

Yes, airlines must send Passenger Name Record data for every passenger on flights to or from the EU before departure. National Passenger Information Units run automated checks against law enforcement databases and behavioural "risk indicators" — such as booking a last-minute one-way ticket with cash payment and a prepaid phone. The directive prohibits profiling based on race, religion, or political opinion, but critics say the risk indicators can produce discriminatory results. Passengers are not informed if they are flagged; the data is analysed silently before the flight lands.

### Can law enforcement keep my flight booking data for five years?

Under the current directive, yes — but with critical restrictions that the European Court of Justice has tightened in 2022 and 2024. The data cannot be kept forever: after an initial six months, the data must be depersonalised by masking out passenger names, though the travel information itself can remain. The 2022 ECJ ruling said the overall five-year period was acceptable only for preventing serious threats, and the 2024 Belgian case (C-817/19) struck down blanket retention entirely, requiring that retention be targeted and justified. Several member states, including Belgium and Luxembourg, have begun revising their national laws to set shorter retention periods.

### Why did EU countries need new rules for sharing flight data instead of just using the passenger info they already collect before boarding?

Existing Advance Passenger Information (API) systems, covered by the 2004 API Directive, only collect passport details from a document check — name, nationality, date of birth, and document number — transmitted once the passenger has checked in. PNR data comes from the booking system and includes everything: it tells authorities if you booked a seat for someone else, if you paid in cash, if you bought the ticket three hours or three months before departure, and if you booked connecting flights through high-risk countries. Law enforcement agencies argued this richer data was essential for detecting unknown threats — a person not previously on any watchlist. The Commission's 2011 impact assessment cited the case of the 2006 transatlantic liquid bomb plot, where plotters' PNR data would have revealed suspicious booking patterns, though it acknowledged this was a retrospective analysis.
