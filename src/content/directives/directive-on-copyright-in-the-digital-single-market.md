## What is it?

The Copyright Directive (2019/790) overhauls EU copyright law for the digital era, with two highly controversial provisions at its core: Article 17, which makes platforms like YouTube and Facebook directly liable for copyright-infringing content uploaded by their users, and Article 15, which grants news publishers a new "neighbouring right" to demand licensing fees when platforms display snippets of their articles.

The directive also introduces mandatory exceptions for text and data mining (Articles 3-4), aimed at opening up EU research and AI training, and clarifies rules for out-of-commerce works and cultural heritage institutions. After years of bitter lobbying and street protests, the directive was adopted in April 2019 and required transposition by June 2021.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a decade-long structural tension: EU copyright law, largely written in 2001, treated every online platform as a neutral "intermediary" under the E-Commerce Directive's safe harbour (Article 14), fully shielded from liability for user uploads. This 2000-era framework, designed for bulletin boards and web hosts, created a massive economic distortion. YouTube, founded in 2005, grew into a trillion-dollar industry by hosting vast amounts of user-uploaded content—including copyrighted music and video—without paying creators beyond what rights holders could manually flag. By 2015, the Commission estimated that creative industries contributed €535 billion annually to EU GDP (its "Copyright in the Single Market" consultation), yet creators saw a shrinking share of digital advertising revenue flowing to platforms.

The key coalition driving the directive was an unlikely alliance of major music labels (Universal, Sony, Warner), news publishers (Axel Springer, News Corp, Schibsted), collecting societies (GESAC), and large parts of the European Parliament's centre-right EPP group. They argued that the "value gap"—the Commission's term for the mismatch between platform revenue and creator compensation—needed legislative correction. The music industry, which had seen global recorded music revenues halve from $27 billion in 1999 to $14 billion by 2014 (according to IFPI data cited in the Commission's 2016 impact assessment), was particularly vocal. Opposing them were tech platforms (Google, Facebook, YouTube), digital rights groups (EFF, Wikimedia, La Quadrature du Net), and many MEPs from liberal and left-wing groups, who warned of mandatory upload filters and the collapse of internet freedom.

The timing was driven by the Juncker Commission's Digital Single Market strategy. When Jean-Claude Juncker took office in 2014, he made copyright reform a priority, tasking Commissioner Günther Oettinger (Digital Economy) and later Andrus Ansip (Digital Single Market) to deliver. The Commission's 2016 proposal was the third attempt at copyright reform; earlier tries in 2007 (the "iPod tax" proposals) and 2012 (Orphan Works Directive) had failed to address platform liability. What broke the deadlock was a specific political event: the 2015 "Copyright for Growth" campaign by Creative Europe—a coalition of 80 European creative industry federations—which produced a report by former FRAND judge Jérôme Huet arguing that the E-Commerce Directive's safe harbour was being "abused by global platforms." In parallel, German publisher Axel Springer launched a high-profile legal offensive against Google News in 2014, winning a fleeting ancillary copyright in Germany (the "Leistungsschutzrecht" of 2013) that forced Google to pay for snippets—but Google simply delisted publishers, causing traffic drops of up to 40% for publishers according to studies cited by the German Association of Digital Economy (BVDW). This failure showed that national-level publisher rights were unenforceable against a dominant platform; only an EU-wide law could work.

The crisis moment came in March 2018, when the European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee (JURI) voted to include Articles 11 and 13 (as they were then numbered). Protests exploded—over 1 million people signed a Change.org petition against Article 13; Wikipedia went dark in several EU countries; and MEP Julia Reda (Pirate Party, Germany) led a fierce parliamentary battle. The final text was a compromise: Article 13 became Article 17, adding a requirement that platforms "make best efforts" to obtain licenses and ensure unlicensed content is "blocked" only after notices, rather than pre-filter everything. The Parliament voted 348-274 on 26 March 2019—a narrow margin that surprised observers and showed the deep divisions.

## Timeline

- **2013** — German Bundestag introduces "Leistungsschutzrecht" (ancillary copyright for publishers); Google News delists German publishers, causing traffic drops
- **2014** — EU consultation on copyright reform receives 11,000+ responses; the Commission's Creative Europe campaign intensifies lobbying
- **September 2016** — Commission President Juncker and Commissioner Ansip propose the Copyright Directive; Articles 11 and 13 spark immediate controversy
- **June 2017** — EP rapporteur Pascal (French EPP) releases draft report; over 300 amendments tabled
- **March 2018** — JURI Committee votes to adopt the text; Wikipedia goes dark in Italy, Spain, and Poland in protest
- **July 2018** — European Parliament plenary rejects the draft 278-318; negotiations restart with a mandate
- **February 2019** — Trilogue agreement reached between Council, Parliament, and Commission; Article 13 modified to Article 17 with "best efforts" language
- **26 March 2019** — EP plenary approves final text 348-274; street protests in 12 European cities
- **17 April 2019** — Council formally adopts directive (19 votes in favour, 6 against—including Netherlands, Poland, Italy, Finland, Luxembourg, Sweden)
- **7 June 2019** — Directive published in Official Journal (OJ L 130)
- **7 June 2021** — Deadline for member states to transpose into national law; only 6 countries met the deadline
- **2022-2023** — Several countries (Poland, Estonia) face infringement proceedings for non-transposition; Poland challenges Article 17 before the CJEU
- **26 April 2022** — CJEU Advocate General Saugmandsgaard Øe issues opinion that Article 17 is incompatible with freedom of expression; CJEU later partially upholds the directive but stresses safeguards must be "robust"
- **2023-2024** — EUIPO reports diverging national implementations; Commission launches a review of Article 17's impact on fundamental rights

## Impact and consequences

Implementation has been messy. By the June 2021 transposition deadline, only Denmark, Germany, Hungary, Malta, the Netherlands, and Sweden had fully transposed the directive. The Commission launched infringement proceedings against 19 member states by 2023, 17 of which were resolved after delayed transposition. Poland, which voted against the directive, challenged Article 17 at the CJEU (Case C-401/19), arguing it violated freedom of speech; the Court's April 2022 ruling stressed that upload filters must not block lawful content and that platforms must provide complaint mechanisms.

For platforms, the directive has forced structural changes. YouTube implemented a new "Copyright Transparency Report" in 2021 showing it removed 729 million videos globally for copyright claims in 2020 (many automated). Facebook introduced a similar system but faced backlash when its filter blocked public domain content like the Mona Lisa. Small platforms (under €10 million annual revenue) were largely exempted, but medium-sized ones faced compliance costs; the Commission's 2020 impact assessment estimated total compliance costs for all platforms at €1.2 billion annually.

For news publishers, Article 15 has been a disappointment. Google, in response to the French implementation of Article 15, struck licensing deals with major publishers (including Le Monde, Le Figaro, and Agence France-Presse worth €10-20 million annually according to reports) but refused to pay smaller outlets. In 2021, France's Competition Authority fined Google €500 million for failing to negotiate in good faith with publishers—the first major enforcement of the new publisher right.

For creators and users, the directive remains deeply contested. Most user-generated content is now pre-screened by automated filters; the proportion of uploads blocked by filters varies from 1-5% on YouTube to higher rates on smaller platforms. A 2023 study by the European Copyright Society found that 40% of removal requests under Article 17 were for content where noncommercial use should have been allowed under exceptions. The European Ombudsman opened an investigation into the Commission's consultation process after criticism that lobby meetings were overwhelmingly industry-sided (59 meetings with creative industry groups vs. 2 with digital rights groups according to the Commission's transparency register).

## Key questions answered

### Why does Article 17 (upload filters) exist if everyone predicted it would censor speech?

The Commission and Parliament deliberately chose platform liability over a "notice-and-takedown" system because a decade of voluntary measures had failed. Between 2007 and 2016, YouTube's Content ID system—a private, automated filter—removed hundreds of millions of unlicensed videos, but rights holders, particularly music labels, claimed they still lost billions because the system was easy to circumvent (e.g., uploading 30-second clips). The legislature accepted that only mandatory filtering could close the "value gap," and added safeguards—proportionality, best efforts, complaint mechanisms—which the CJEU later ruled must be robustly enforced. Critics argue that the CJEU's 2022 judgment effectively rewrote Article 17, forcing platforms to avoid "general monitoring obligations" while still requiring them to block infringing content—a tension that remains unresolved.

### Why did news publishers need a new right when they already had copyright?

Traditional copyright only protects original expression, not the factual information in a news article. Google News and similar aggregators displayed snippets (headlines plus 2-3 sentences) that publishers argued extracted value without paying for the journalism. The German "Leistungsschutzrecht" (2013) showed that national rights were useless because Google simply delisted non-compliant publishers, costing them traffic. The EU solution—Article 15—was designed to make licenses mandatory and binding across all member states, forcing platforms to negotiate collectively. Critics note that the right expired after 2 years (except for legal entities) and that small publishers lack bargaining power. The result has been big licensing deals for major groups (Axel Springer, Le Monde) and little benefit for local newspapers.

### Why did the Netherlands, Poland, and Sweden vote against it in the Council?

These countries argued that Article 17 would break the E-Commerce Directive's prohibition on general monitoring obligations and undermine freedom of expression. The Netherlands, with its strong digital rights tradition, was particularly vocal; Dutch MEP Marietje Schaake led opposition. Poland filed a formal challenge at the CJEU, arguing Article 17 forced platforms to pre-filter all uploads, which violated Article 11 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (freedom of expression). Sweden's government, home to file-sharing site The Pirate Bay, feared a backlash from its tech-savvy population. These six countries collectively held about 25% of the Council's weighted votes—not enough to block the directive (which needed a qualified majority) but enough to signal serious opposition that later shaped the CJEU's interpretation.
