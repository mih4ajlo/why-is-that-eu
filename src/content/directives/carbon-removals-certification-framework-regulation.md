## What is it?

The Carbon Removals Certification Framework Regulation (2024/3012/EU) creates Europe's first unified certification system for activities that remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. It covers three categories: permanent carbon storage (like direct air capture with geological storage), carbon farming (soil carbon sequestration on agricultural land), and carbon storage in long-lasting products (like timber in construction). The regulation sets quality criteria—quantification, additionality, long-term storage, and sustainability—but does not create a trading market or mandate use of the certified credits.

The framework is voluntary: operators can apply for certification under national schemes that follow EU rules, and the certified units can then be used in voluntary carbon markets or potentially incorporated into future EU climate policy. The European Commission will develop tailored certification methodologies for specific activities by 2028, with a review clause that could make certification mandatory or link it to the EU's 2040 climate target framework.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a scandal that exposed the scandal-ridden global voluntary carbon market. In January 2023, an investigation by *The Guardian*, *Die Zeit*, and *SourceMaterial* found that over 90% of rainforest carbon credits issued by Verra, the world's largest certifier, were "phantom credits" that did not represent real emission reductions. The European Commission's own Joint Research Centre had warned in 2022 that many soil carbon offset projects in agriculture showed "no detectable climate benefit." This credibility crisis threatened to sink the entire concept of carbon removals before it could be scaled up.

The political push came from the Farm to Fork Strategy, published in May 2020, which first proposed a "carbon farming initiative" as part of the European Green Deal. Agriculture Commissioner Janusz Wojciechowski championed the idea as a way to give farmers a new income stream from climate action, while Climate Commissioner Frans Timmermans saw it as essential for reaching net-zero emissions by 2050. The two commissioners formed an unlikely alliance: agriculture interests wanted new revenue sources, climate hawks wanted to count emissions reductions from land use.

The timing was driven by the EU's 2023 climate ambition upgrade. Under the European Climate Law (2021/1119/EU), the EU committed to net-zero emissions by 2050, and the Commission's 2023 impact assessment for the 2040 target showed that the EU would need to remove 300-500 million tonnes of CO2 annually by 2050—roughly 10% of current emissions. Without a certification system, it would be impossible to verify whether these removals actually occurred.

The proposal faced heavy lobbying. The European Carbon Farming Association, backed by major agri-business groups like COPA-COGECA and fertiliser giant Yara International, pushed for broad inclusion of agricultural practices. Environmental NGOs including ClientEarth and the European Environmental Bureau warned that weak soil carbon standards would allow greenwashing. The Parliament's Environment Committee, led by rapporteur Martin Hojsík (Renew Europe, Slovakia), fought for strict additionality rules and exclusion of continued forest management from certification.

The final text represents a compromise. The Parliament wanted to ban certification of forest management that would have happened anyway; the Council, led by France and Poland with large forestry sectors, insisted on keeping it. The regulation settled on a "no net harm" principle for biodiversity and added a requirement for the Commission to develop separate methodologies for carbon removal and carbon storage in products—a win for the wood construction industry, which had lobbied through CEI-Bois and the European Organisation for Technical Approvals.

For opponents, the risk was that weak certification would become another "greenwashing machine," in the words of Green MEP Bas Eickhout. The alternative considered by the Commission was a regulatory approach that would have banned uncertified carbon credits from being used in corporate sustainability claims, but this was deemed too heavy-handed and would have disrupted existing voluntary markets.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's immediate effect is on the voluntary carbon market. Verified carbon units certified under EU rules will carry a premium over uncertified credits, potentially reshaping the $2 billion global market. The Commission estimated in its 2022 impact assessment that the framework could create a market for 200 million tonnes of carbon removals annually by 2040, worth €5-10 billion.

Member states with large agricultural sectors face the biggest implementation challenge. France, with 27 million hectares of farmland, began pilot carbon farming schemes in 2023 under its Label Bas-Carbone framework; Germany and Poland have similar national programmes that will need to align with EU rules by 2028. The regulation's requirement for permanence monitoring over decades creates administrative burdens: soil carbon can be lost within years if farming practices change, while geological storage requires centuries of liability.

Industry response has been mixed. The European CO2 Storage Alliance—representing companies like Equinor, TotalEnergies, and Wintershall Dea—welcomed the certification for permanent storage as a way to monetise their CCS infrastructure. The cement and steel sectors, through CEMBUREAU and Eurofer, advocated for inclusion of carbon storage in building materials to count credits. Agricultural groups were disappointed that soil carbon credits were capped at a "buffer" of 20% of total removals, limiting their revenue potential.

Environmental groups' concerns remain unresolved. ClientEarth has warned that the regulation does not prevent double-counting of carbon credits used in both national greenhouse gas inventories and voluntary markets—a problem that could allow companies to claim reductions that countries already count toward their Paris Agreement targets. The European Environmental Bureau criticized the "weak sustainability safeguards" in the final text.

## Key questions answered

### Are companies allowed to claim carbon neutrality using certified carbon removal credits?

Yes, but the regulation does not automatically validate such claims. Certification only verifies that the removal activity meets EU quality standards—it does not create a legal basis for "net-zero" or "carbon neutral" product claims. Those are governed by separate EU law: the Green Claims Directive (proposed in 2023) and the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC). A company using a certified carbon credit to offset its emissions must still comply with rules against misleading advertising. Early legal analysis suggests that claiming "carbon neutrality" based solely on certified removals may still be legally risky without full lifecycle emissions accounting.

### Does this set a price for removing carbon from the air?

No. The regulation is purely a certification framework—it does not set a price, create a trading system, or mandate use. The certified units can be bought and sold on voluntary markets, but their price will be determined by supply and demand. The Commission's 2026 review may consider linking certified removals to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which would create a direct price signal. The current ETS does not allow offsets from removals, but Member States including Denmark and Spain have pushed for inclusion as emissions-reduction targets become harder to meet.

### What happens if carbon that was stored underground or in forests leaks back out?

The regulation requires monitoring and liability provisions. For permanent storage (geological), liability typically lies with the storage operator under existing CCS directives. For carbon farming and product storage, the operator must demonstrate that removals last for at least 35 years (for carbon farming) or as long as the product's lifetime (for products). If carbon is released—e.g., a farmer tills a field that had sequestered soil carbon—the certification is revoked. The operator may be required to compensate by purchasing credits elsewhere, but the regulation leaves liability details to national certification schemes. The Commission is required to develop harmonised liability rules by 2028.
