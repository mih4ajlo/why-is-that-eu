## What is it?

The Battery Regulation 2023 (Regulation (EU) 2023/1542) is the EU's first cradle-to-grave law governing batteries. It replaces the 2006 Batteries Directive (2006/66/EC), which had become dramatically outdated as the world shifted from disposable AA batteries to a battery-powered economy of electric vehicles, e-bikes, smartphones, and grid storage.

The regulation covers every battery placed on the EU market in five categories: portable batteries, starter (SLI) batteries for cars, light means of transport (LMT) batteries for e-bikes and scooters, electric vehicle (EV) batteries, and industrial batteries. Each category faces its own rules on labelling, performance, durability, carbon footprint, recycled content, collection, and recycling efficiency.

Crucially, it is a *regulation* rather than a *directive*, meaning it applies directly in all member states without national transposition. It also introduces new instruments such as the **digital battery passport** (mandatory from 2027 for EV and large industrial batteries), supply-chain due diligence obligations, and design rules requiring portable batteries in consumer products to be removable and replaceable by end-users.

## Why was it introduced?

Three forces converged to make the old 2006 directive obsolete: the EU Green Deal, the EV revolution, and geopolitics around critical raw materials.

**The Green Deal and climate targets.** When Ursula von der Leyen announced the European Green Deal in December 2019, batteries were identified as a strategic technology. Reaching the 2050 climate-neutrality target requires electrifying transport — and that requires an unprecedented scale-up of battery production. The Commission projected that battery demand in Europe would grow 14-fold by 2030. Without rules on sustainability, this scale-up could simply offshore Europe's emissions and create a new mountain of toxic waste.

**The dependency problem.** Europe was waking up to the fact that China dominated nearly every step of the battery value chain — from refining lithium, cobalt, and graphite to producing cathodes and cells. The 2017 European Battery Alliance, championed by Commission VP Maroš Šefčovič, aimed to build a European battery industry. But European producers warned they could not compete on cost with Chinese giants like CATL and BYD. Their solution: compete on *sustainability*. If the EU set strict carbon footprint and recycled content rules, European producers (running on greener grids and tighter standards) would have a structural advantage. This is regulatory protectionism dressed in environmental clothing — and it was openly acknowledged.

**The right-to-repair backlash.** For over a decade, consumer groups, repair advocates (notably iFixit and the Right to Repair Europe coalition), and environmental NGOs had complained that smartphone makers — particularly Apple, Samsung, and Huawei — had glued batteries into devices, shortening usable life and creating mountains of e-waste. Surveys showed battery degradation was the top reason consumers replaced phones. The Commission's own studies estimated that extending smartphone life by just one year would save 2.1 million tonnes of CO₂ annually. The new regulation enshrines user-replaceability as a legal right.

**Cobalt and child labour.** Reporting from the late 2010s — particularly Amnesty International's investigations into cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo — created political pressure for mandatory due diligence. Voluntary industry schemes were widely seen as insufficient. The EU borrowed the framework from its 2017 Conflict Minerals Regulation and OECD due diligence guidance.

The Commission tabled its proposal in December 2020. Negotiations took two and a half years, with intense lobbying from automakers (worried about EV battery rules), electronics makers (fighting the replaceability rule), and recyclers (pushing for higher recovery targets). The final text was adopted in July 2023.

## Timeline

- **2006** — Original Batteries Directive (2006/66/EC) adopted, focused mostly on heavy metals (mercury, cadmium) and basic collection targets.
- **2017** — European Battery Alliance launched to build EU battery industry; Amnesty publishes major report on cobalt and child labour.
- **2019** — European Green Deal announced; batteries identified as strategic.
- **December 2020** — Commission proposes the new Battery Regulation.
- **March 2022** — Council reaches general approach.
- **December 2022** — Provisional political agreement between Parliament and Council.
- **July 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/1542 formally adopted and published.
- **18 February 2024** — Regulation enters into application; old directive repealed.
- **August 2024** — Due diligence rules begin to apply.
- **2025** — First carbon footprint declarations required for EV batteries.
- **2027** — Battery passport mandatory for EV and large industrial batteries; portable batteries in consumer products must be user-replaceable.
- **2031** — Mandatory recycled content thresholds begin (e.g., 16% cobalt, 6% lithium, 6% nickel).

## Impact and consequences

**Smartphones and consumer electronics.** Apple, Samsung, and other manufacturers must redesign devices so that average end-users (not just specialists) can replace batteries with commonly available tools. This affects waterproofing, adhesives, and casing design. Apple has been gradually shifting toward easier repairs, partly in anticipation.

**Automakers.** EV manufacturers face new obligations on carbon footprint disclosure, recycled content, and the battery passport — a digital identity tracking each EV battery's chemistry, origin, state of health, and repair history. This is expected to enable a second-life market for EV batteries (e.g., for grid storage) and far higher recycling rates.

**Recyclers.** Higher collection and recovery targets (e.g., 73% portable battery collection by 2030; 95% cobalt and 80% lithium recovery by 2031) create a guaranteed market for European recyclers like Northvolt's Revolt, Umicore, and Veolia.

**Chinese imports.** The carbon footprint thresholds and due diligence obligations are widely understood to disadvantage batteries produced on coal-heavy grids — a thinly veiled targeting of Chinese cells. Several Chinese producers (CATL, Envision AESC, BYD) have responded by building factories inside the EU.

**Mining and trading firms** dealing in cobalt, lithium, natural graphite, and nickel must conduct OECD-aligned supply chain due diligence, comparable to the obligations under the Conflict Minerals Regulation but broader in scope.

## Key questions answered

### Why replace the 2006 directive instead of amending it?

The old directive was designed for a world of AA batteries and laptop packs. It said nothing about EVs, grid storage, or supply chains. A patchwork of amendments would have been unworkable, and a regulation (directly applicable) was preferred over a directive to avoid 27 different national interpretations of complex technical rules.

### Why force user-replaceable batteries when waterproof glued designs are popular?

Because the Commission concluded that planned obsolescence via battery degradation was the single largest driver of premature smartphone replacement. The environmental cost outweighed the design preferences. Manufacturers can still meet IP-rated waterproofing — they just need to engineer it differently.

### Why include carbon footprint rules in a battery law?

Because batteries are extremely energy-intensive to produce, and Europe wanted to avoid a situation where its EV transition was powered by coal-fired Chinese gigafactories. By making carbon footprint a market-access criterion, the EU rewards cleaner production — which happens to favour European and Korean producers running on cleaner grids.

### Why a battery passport?

To enable a circular economy. Without standardised data on what's actually in a battery, recyclers cannot efficiently process it, second-life applications are hard to develop, and consumers cannot make informed choices. The passport also makes due diligence enforceable: regulators can trace a battery back to its mineral sources.
