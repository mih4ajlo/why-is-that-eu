## What is it?

The General Product Safety Regulation (GPSR) is the EU's horizontal safety net for consumer products — anything sold to or used by consumers that isn't already covered by sector-specific rules (toys, cosmetics, machinery, medical devices). If a kettle, a baby monitor, or a phone charger doesn't fall under a vertical directive, the GPSR governs it.

It does three new things compared to the 2001 Directive it replaces. First, it imposes obligations on online marketplaces — Amazon, eBay, Temu, Shein, AliExpress — which must register with the Safety Gate portal, design their interfaces so sellers can display safety information, and remove dangerous listings within two working days of a national authority's order. Second, it requires that every product sold in the EU has an "economic operator" physically established in the Union who is legally responsible for its compliance. Third, it harmonises recall procedures: standardised recall notices, mandatory remedies (repair, replacement, or refund), and direct consumer notification.

The Regulation entered into force on 12 June 2023 and applies from 13 December 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a flood of cheap, dangerous goods entering EU homes through non-EU online platforms — and a 2001 Directive that pre-dated Amazon Marketplace, never mind Wish or Temu. A 2020 joint investigation by BEUC and five European consumer groups bought 250 products from AliExpress, Amazon, eBay and Wish; two-thirds failed EU safety tests. Children's costumes burst into flames, USB chargers electrocuted, smoke alarms didn't sound. The 2001 Directive imposed no obligations whatsoever on the marketplaces hosting these listings.

The Rapex/Safety Gate alert system was buckling under the volume. In 2021 alone, EU authorities issued 2,142 alerts on dangerous non-food products, with toys (27%) and motor vehicles (16%) topping the list — and "China" listed as country of origin in roughly half of cases. National regulators complained they had no leverage: the seller was a shell entity in Shenzhen, and Amazon or Wish would simply relist the same product under a new SKU after takedown.

Commissioner Didier Reynders (Justice and Consumers) tabled the proposal on 30 June 2021, framing it explicitly as a response to e-commerce. Reynders cited the 2020 Court of Justice ruling in *Coty v Amazon* (C-567/18), which had highlighted how marketplaces could escape liability under existing rules. The Toy Industries of Europe association and ANEC (the European consumer voice in standardisation) had lobbied since 2017 for marketplace obligations, arguing legitimate EU manufacturers were being undercut by non-compliant imports. The Council and Parliament negotiated a compromise text in November 2022; Parliament adopted it on 30 March 2023 by 569 votes to 13, with 11 abstentions — an unusually broad majority that reflected cross-party alarm at platform-driven imports.

The choice of a Regulation rather than a Directive was deliberate. The 2001 Directive had been transposed unevenly — Germany's BAuA enforced robustly, while smaller member states had minimal market surveillance — creating loopholes that Chinese sellers exploited by routing shipments through the weakest link. A directly applicable Regulation eliminates that variation.

## Timeline

- **2001** — General Product Safety Directive 2001/95/EC adopted, before mass-market e-commerce.
- **2018** — Commission's "Product Safety Pledge" signed by Amazon, eBay, AliExpress and Rakuten — voluntary, widely judged ineffective.
- **2020** — BEUC investigation finds two-thirds of online-marketplace products fail EU safety tests; CJEU rules in *Coty v Amazon*.
- **30 June 2021** — Commissioner Reynders tables GPSR proposal.
- **November 2022** — Trilogue agreement reached.
- **30 March 2023** — European Parliament adopts text, 569–13.
- **25 April 2023** — Council formally adopts.
- **12 June 2023** — Regulation enters into force.
- **13 December 2024** — Date of application; Directive 2001/95/EC repealed.

## Impact and consequences

The most visible effect has been on small UK and US sellers using Amazon, Etsy and eBay to ship into the EU. Without an EU-based "responsible economic operator," listings cannot legally appear. Etsy emailed hundreds of thousands of sellers in late 2024 warning that products without a named EU representative would be delisted; many small British craft sellers exited the EU market entirely, citing the cost of hiring a compliance representative (typically €500–€2,000 per year per product line). The British Chambers of Commerce flagged GPSR as the single biggest post-Brexit trade friction for small exporters in early 2025.

For Temu and Shein, the Regulation arrived alongside Digital Services Act enforcement. The Commission designated both as Very Large Online Platforms in 2024; under GPSR, they must now process Safety Gate takedown orders within two working days and trace sellers. France's DGCCRF launched coordinated sweeps in early 2025, ordering removal of thousands of listings for non-compliant cosmetics and toys.

Consumer remedies have also tightened. Recall notices must now offer a genuine choice between repair, replacement and refund — ending the industry practice of offering only a discount voucher. The Stiftung Warentest welcomed the change after years of complaints that German consumers received €5 vouchers for recalled €80 appliances.

## Key questions answered

### Why does every product need an EU-based "responsible person"?

Because national market surveillance authorities had no one to fine, sue or summon when a dangerous product came from a Chinese drop-shipper. Under Article 4 of Regulation 2019/1020 (extended in scope by GPSR), there must be a natural or legal person in the EU who can be served legal notice, hold technical documentation, and cooperate with authorities. This effectively forces non-EU sellers to either set up an EU subsidiary or hire an authorised representative.

### Why was the 2018 voluntary Product Safety Pledge insufficient?

The Pledge was signed by only four marketplaces and contained no enforcement mechanism. BEUC's 2020 testing showed signatories continued to host dangerous products, and Wish — the worst offender in many tests — never signed. Reynders explicitly cited the Pledge's failure when proposing binding rules.

### Why a Regulation instead of updating the 2001 Directive?

Uneven transposition had created a regulatory race to the bottom: importers routed goods through whichever member state had the laxest enforcement. A Regulation applies identically and directly in all 27 states, eliminating forum-shopping. It also allowed faster alignment with the parallel Market Surveillance Regulation (2019/1020) and Digital Services Act (2022/2065).

### Why are small Etsy and eBay sellers complaining loudest?

Large brands already had EU subsidiaries; small UK, US and Australian sellers did not. The cost of an authorised representative is fixed per product family, making it disproportionate for low-volume artisan sellers. The Commission has acknowledged the issue but argued that consumer safety cannot depend on seller size.
