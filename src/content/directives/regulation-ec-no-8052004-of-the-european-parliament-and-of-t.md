## What is it?

Before this Regulation, if a French seller had an unpaid invoice from a German buyer and obtained a French court judgment, they could not enforce it in Germany without first obtaining German court approval — the exequatur procedure — which took months, cost thousands of euros, and required local lawyers. The European Enforcement Order (EEO) Regulation eliminated that step for uncontested claims — debts owed where the debtor never disputed the sum. If the original judgment was issued under minimum procedural safeguards (proper service, clear notice of default), it could circulate freely across borders as a "European Enforcement Order", enforceable in any member state as if a local judgment.

The Regulation applied only to uncontested claims where the debtor had explicitly agreed to the debt by contract or admission, or had failed to object within a deadline. It covered civil and commercial matters but excluded family law, bankruptcy, and social security. Creditors could request the EEO certification from the court that issued the original judgment. Once certified, the judgment bypassed any intermediate recognition procedure in the enforcement state.

## Why was it introduced?

The problem was not abstract — it was costing small and medium enterprises (SMEs) across Europe millions of euros in unrecovered debts and legal fees. In 2000, the Commission estimated that cross-border enforcement of uncontested claims took between six months and two years longer than domestic enforcement, and cost 25–45% of the claim value in legal fees for exequatur. A Spanish plumber could not afford to sue a Portuguese company for €5,000 if enforcement required another Portuguese lawyer and six months of court proceedings.

The political driver was the Tampere European Council of October 1999, where EU leaders declared that "judgments should be recognised and enforced throughout the Union without intermediate proceedings." This was part of the broader creation of an "area of freedom, security and justice" after the Amsterdam Treaty transferred judicial cooperation in civil matters from intergovernmental to Community competence.

The specific trigger was a Commission green paper in 2002, followed by a rapid legislative proposal under the Italian Commissioner for Justice and Home Affairs, Antonio Vitorino. The European Parliament strongly supported abolition, with MEPs arguing that exequatur was "a relic of mutual suspicion" between member states. The main opposition came from legal professions relying on exequatur fees — notably German notaries and some national judges' associations — but their influence was weakened by evidence that the vast majority of cross-border claims never reached court in the first place because enforcement costs outweighed claim values.

The Regulation passed on 21 April 2004, just before the EU's eastern enlargement on 1 May 2004, when ten new member states joined with widely varying judicial systems. The timing was deliberate: the Commission wanted to establish a principle of mutual recognition in civil matters before the new members had to integrate into the existing judicial cooperation framework.

## Timeline

- **1997** — The Amsterdam Treaty transfers civil judicial cooperation from intergovernmental to Community method, enabling EU-wide legislation.
- **October 1999** — Tampere European Council calls for abolition of intermediate recognition procedures for small claims.
- **2002** — Commission publishes green paper on a European Enforcement Order, seeking stakeholder input.
- **18 December 2002** — Commission tables formal proposal for the Regulation (COM(2002) 746 final).
- **2 April 2003** — European Economic and Social Committee delivers favourable opinion.
- **21 April 2004** — Regulation (EC) No 805/2004 adopted by European Parliament and Council.
- **21 January 2005** — Regulation enters into force.
- **21 October 2005** — Regulation applies in all member states except Denmark, which opted out.
- **2008** — First Commission report on application finds limited uptake, with most creditors still using traditional enforcement routes due to lack of awareness.

## Impact and consequences

The Regulation's practical impact was initially modest. According to the Commission's 2010 report on its application, only about 1,500 EEO certifications were issued annually across the entire EU in the first three years, compared to hundreds of thousands of domestic enforcement orders. The main reason was lack of awareness among SMEs and lawyers.

However, the symbolic impact was profound. The Regulation established the principle that a judgment from any EU member state could circulate without intermediate recognition — a principle later extended to other civil matters in the Brussels I recast (Regulation 1215/2012/EU) and to small claims (Regulation 861/2007/EU). It effectively killed the exequatur procedure for uncontested claims, saving creditors an estimated 20–40% of enforcement costs according to a 2005 European Business Law Review study.

The Regulation also forced national courts to harmonise minimum procedural standards for serving documents and notifying debtors, particularly the requirement that debtors must be informed in writing of the amount claimed, the deadline to object, and the consequences of failing to do so. Countries where local practice had tolerated sloppy service — notably Greece and Italy — had to tighten procedures to qualify for EEO certification.

Denmark, exercising its opt-out from justice and home affairs measures, never adopted the Regulation, creating a gap where judgments against Danish debtors still required exequatur. This was partially addressed by a 2006 agreement between the EU and Denmark extending the Regulation's effect.

## Key questions answered

### Can a French company enforce an unpaid invoice from a German customer without a German court?

Yes, if the French company first obtains a French court judgment for the unpaid debt and that judgment is certified as a European Enforcement Order. The judgment is then enforceable in Germany as if it were a German judgment, without any additional approval from a German court. The key requirement is that the debt was "uncontested" — meaning the German customer never disputed the invoice in writing or failed to respond to a payment demand with a warning of legal action.

### Why did it take so long for EU countries to trust each other's court judgments?

The obstacle was mutual suspicion about procedural fairness. German courts did not trust that a Spanish court had properly notified a German debtor of the proceedings; French courts doubted whether Italian procedures met minimum standards. The Regulation solved this by requiring the original court to certify that it had followed EU-wide minimum procedural safeguards — proper service, clear notice of default, a deadline to object — before granting the EEO status. Once those safeguards were verified, mutual trust replaced suspicion.

### Does this Regulation still apply after the Brussels I Recast?

Yes, but only for uncontested claims. The Brussels I Recast (Regulation 1215/2012/EU) abolished exequatur for all civil and commercial judgments from 10 January 2015, making the EEO Regulation technically redundant for most cases. However, the EEO Regulation remains relevant for judgments issued before that date, and for Denmark (which adopted the Brussels I Recast under a separate agreement but with some differences). The EEO still offers a simpler procedural path for uncontested claims in practice.
