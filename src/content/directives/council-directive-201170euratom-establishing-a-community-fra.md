## What is it?

The Nuclear Waste Directive is the first binding EU law that compels every member state to have a formal, Commission‑scrutinised plan for managing all radioactive waste and spent fuel — from medical isotopes to high‑level waste from power reactors. It does not order countries to build deep geological repositories, but it does force them to justify any indefinite surface storage and to report progress every three years.

The directive creates a cascade: each national programme must be based on a national policy, both of which are notified to the European Commission. Member states must demonstrate that they have a technically and financially viable path from waste generation to final disposal. A 2019 implementation report found that all EU countries except Austria, Italy, and Lithuania had by then submitted programmes, though the quality varied widely.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was not a single accident but a decades‑long deadlock: by 2010, the EU had accumulated roughly 1.6 million cubic metres of radioactive waste, according to the Commission’s impact assessment, yet half of member states had no disposal facility, and several had not even decided on a long‑term strategy. The only EU‑wide provisions were non‑binding recommendations from 2004 that had produced little progress.

The political crisis that broke the inertia was the 2009 European Council, which, after years of fruitless discussion, formally asked the Commission to propose binding legislation. Energy Commissioner Günther Oettinger took up the task, arguing that public trust in nuclear energy required a “credible end‑of‑pipe solution.” The 2009 Nuclear Safety Directive (2009/71/Euratom) had already set binding safety standards for reactors; the waste directive was the logical next step to close the lifecycle gap.

Opposition came mainly from France and the United Kingdom, which argued that national sovereignty over energy policy should preclude EU interference in waste‑management choices. They were backed by industry associations such as FORATOM, which warned that binding deadlines for disposal could force premature and costly decisions. In the Council, unanimity was required under the Euratom Treaty, so the final text allowed member states to choose their own technical solutions (e.g., geological disposal versus long‑term interim storage) as long as they could justify them. The European Parliament, consulted but not co‑deciding, had pushed for a explicit target date for geological disposal; the Council removed it.

The final compromise — adopted in July 2011 — required national programmes to be submitted by August 2015, with a review every six years. The Fukushima disaster in March 2011, which unfolded during the final negotiations, gave extra political momentum by reinforcing the message that “safety culture” must cover the entire fuel chain.

## Timeline

- **2004** — EU adopts non‑binding “Community Plan for the Safe Management of Radioactive Waste and Spent Fuel”; few member states implement it.
- **2009** — Nuclear Safety Directive (2009/71/Euratom) enters into force; Council invites the Commission to propose a waste directive.
- **October 2010** — Commission publishes proposal (COM(2010) 618 final), citing 1.6 million m³ of waste and 7 000 m³ generated annually.
- **July 2011** — Council adopts Directive 2011/70/Euratom unanimously.
- **23 August 2013** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **August 2015** — Deadline for member states to notify their national programmes to the Commission.
- **2017** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against Italy, Lithuania, and Austria for failing to notify programmes on time.
- **2019** — Commission publishes first implementation report, noting that while all programmes had been submitted, several lacked detailed timelines and cost estimates.

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced national governments that had long deferred disposal decisions to create concrete timelines. Finland, Sweden, and France already had advanced plans for deep geological repositories; they used the directive to formalise them. Other countries — such as Poland, the Czech Republic, and Romania — started from scratch, setting up dedicated waste‑management agencies by 2015.

However, implementation has been uneven. By 2019, the Commission had opened infringement cases against three member states for missing the programme deadline. The most striking failure is Italy, which had no national programme approved until 2021, despite receiving a reasoned opinion from the Commission. Even countries that submitted programmes often did not include credible financial provisions, as the Commission’s 2019 report highlighted.

The directive also strengthened the role of the European Atomic Energy Community (Euratom) in supervising waste flows: cross‑border shipments of waste require prior consent, and the directive closes a loophole by requiring that any exported waste be sent to a recipient country with an “equivalent” management framework.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU require countries to bury their nuclear waste underground?

No — the directive does not mandate a specific disposal method. It requires each country to adopt a national policy and a programme that “demonstrates that the planned final solution is technically feasible and achievable within a reasonable period.” That can be deep geological disposal, but also long‑term storage in monitored surface facilities, as long as the country explains why that is preferable and how it will eventually move to disposal.

### Why did it take more than 50 years after the Euratom Treaty for binding waste rules?

The Euratom Treaty (1957) gave the Community competence over health and safety, but member states fiercely guarded their sovereignty over nuclear energy. Non‑binding recommendations in 2004 failed to produce results, and only after the 2009 Council request — combined with growing waste volumes and the 2009 nuclear safety directive creating a precedent — did member states accept legal obligations. The unanimity requirement in the Council meant that France and the UK could veto any text that imposed specific deadlines or technologies.

### What happens if a country has no disposal facility and no credible plan?

The Commission can open infringement proceedings and ultimately refer the case to the Court of Justice. The directive requires each national programme to include a clear schedule for the construction of disposal facilities. If a country cannot meet its own schedule, it must revise the programme and justify the delay. So far, the Commission has used the infringement procedure as a compliance tool: Italy only finalised its programme after the Commission sent a reasoned opinion.
