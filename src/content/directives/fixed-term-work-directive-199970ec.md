## What is it?

The Fixed-Term Work Directive (1999/70/EC) establishes a binding EU framework to ensure fixed-term workers are not treated less favourably than comparable permanent employees, unless objective justification exists. It requires Member States to take concrete measures—through legislation, collective agreements, or national practice—to prevent the abusive use of successive fixed-term contracts.

The directive applies to all fixed-term employment contracts, including those in the public sector, and covers both full-time and part-time fixed-term workers. It does not set a maximum total duration for fixed-term contracts, leaving specific implementation—such as maximum total duration or number of renewals—to Member States. Alongside the Part-Time Work Directive (97/81/EC) and the Temporary Agency Work Directive (2008/104/EC), it forms the EU's core legal framework for non-standard employment.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's roots lie in a structural tension that became acute in the 1990s: EU labour law had long been built around the standard permanent, full-time employment model, but by the mid-1990s, both employers and governments were increasingly turning to fixed-term contracts to bypass employment protections and reduce costs. This created a two-tier labour market, with permanent workers enjoying full rights while fixed-term workers—often younger, female, or migrant workers—were left legally disadvantaged.

The trigger was a political crisis in the European social model. In 1997, the newly elected social democratic governments in France, Germany, and the UK (Tony Blair's Labour government had taken power in May 1997) pushed for a stronger EU social agenda to counter the deregulatory trends of the 1980s and early 1990s. The 1997 Amsterdam Treaty, signed in October 1997, gave the EU new powers to legislate on employment and social policy, and the new "employment chapter" allowed the Council to adopt directives on equal treatment in employment.

The coalition that drove the directive was unusual: trade unions (the European Trade Union Confederation, ETUC) and employer organisations (BusinessEurope, then UNICE) had, under the Maastricht Social Protocol, negotiated a framework agreement on fixed-term work themselves. The ETUC pushed hard for strong anti-abuse rules, while UNICE opposed any fixed maximum duration for contracts, arguing labour market flexibility was essential for competitiveness. The compromise was reached in March 1999: the framework agreement would set principles but leave Member States discretion on implementation, including whether to adopt a maximum total duration, a maximum number of renewals, or an objective justification requirement.

The European Commission, under Social Affairs Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (appointed in September 1999), then proposed the directive to implement the agreement. The European Parliament, where the Party of European Socialists held the largest group, gave strong support. The Council of Ministers adopted the directive on 28 June 1999, with the UK and Denmark voting against in the Council—the UK's Conservative government under John Major had already been on its way out, but Blair's Labour government still opposed the directive, arguing it could reduce employment flexibility. The final vote in the Council was 11 in favour, 2 against, with 2 abstentions (Austria and Portugal).

What broke the previous deadlock was timing: the Amsterdam Treaty's new social policy provisions, combined with the political momentum from the 1997 Luxembourg Jobs Summit (which had launched the European Employment Strategy), created a window for social partner agreements to be turned into binding EU law. Earlier attempts in the 1980s and early 1990s had stalled on the question of whether fixed-term contracts could be limited by duration—employers had successfully argued that any EU cap would be an unjustified constraint on labour markets. The 1999 compromise allowed Member States to choose their own anti-abuse mechanism, breaking the deadlock.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Amsterdam Treaty signed, extending EU competence on employment and social policy
- **March 1999** — Social partners (ETUC, UNICE, CEEP) reach a framework agreement on fixed-term work, negotiated under the Maastricht Social Protocol
- **28 June 1999** — Council adopts Directive 1999/70/EC implementing the framework agreement; UK and Denmark vote against
- **10 July 1999** — Directive published in the Official Journal
- **10 July 2001** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law (with possibility of up to one additional year for complex cases)
- **2002-2006** — Commission opens infringement proceedings against several Member States for late or incorrect transposition, including Italy, Spain, Greece, and Luxembourg
- **2002** — Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU) delivers first ruling on the directive in *Case C-212/99*, confirming its direct effect in certain circumstances
- **2004** — CJEU ruling in *Case C-307/01* (the "Marrosu" case) establishes that the directive applies to public sector fixed-term contracts, rejecting Italy's exclusion of the public sector
- **2006** — CJEU ruling in *Case C-144/04* (the "Mangold" case) becomes landmark, confirming the principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age as a general principle of EU law, using the fixed-term directive framework
- **2014** — CJEU ruling in *Case C-116/13* (the "Sibilio" case) establishes that national courts must disapply contrary national legislation if the directive's anti-abuse provisions are not properly implemented
- **2021** — European Commission publishes a report on the implementation of the directive, finding persistent problems with abuse of successive contracts in several Member States

## Impact and consequences

The directive fundamentally changed the legal landscape for temporary employment across the EU. By 2003, all 15 Member States had transposed the directive into national law, though with significant variation: Germany set a maximum total duration of two years (with up to three renewals), France set 18 months for most contracts, while the UK adopted only the "objective justification" test without any fixed maximum.

The impact was uneven. Countries with strong collective bargaining traditions, such as Sweden and Denmark, relied primarily on collective agreements to implement the directive. Southern European countries—Spain, Italy, Greece, and Portugal—faced persistent enforcement problems. Spain, in particular, saw its fixed-term contract rate surge past 30% in the early 2000s, leading to a 2006 reform that attempted to reduce temporary employment by expanding permanent contracts and raising dismissal costs for temporary workers.

The CJEU became a central actor. Between 2002 and 2020, the Court issued over 40 preliminary rulings on the directive, often pushing Member States toward stricter enforcement. The 2006 "Mangold" ruling was particularly consequential, establishing the principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age as a general principle of EU law, with repercussions far beyond fixed-term work.

The directive's main achievement was to establish equal treatment for fixed-term workers as a binding legal principle across the EU. Its main limitation was that it did not reduce the overall use of fixed-term contracts, which by 2020 accounted for 14% of all EU employment (Eurostat Labour Force Survey data). Critics argue that the directive, by legitimising fixed-term work while failing to effectively prevent abuse, may have contributed to the normalisation of precarious employment—a criticism reflected in the 2021 Commission implementation report, which noted that several Member States still had inadequate or poorly enforced anti-abuse measures.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the directive not set a maximum total duration for fixed-term contracts?

The absence of a fixed EU-wide maximum was the central political compromise. BusinessEurope (then UNICE) successfully argued during the 1997-1999 social partner negotiations that labour markets needed flexibility, and that any EU-imposed cap would be a "one-size-fits-all" constraint. The compromise allowed each Member State to choose its own anti-abuse mechanism—either a maximum total duration, a maximum number of renewals, or an objective justification requirement. This left implementation to national parliaments and social partners, ensuring the directive could be adapted to different national labour market traditions.

### Does the directive apply to fixed-term contracts in the public sector?

Yes. The Court of Justice established this definitively in the 2004 *Marrosu* ruling (Case C-307/01), after Italy had tried to exclude the public sector from transposition. The Court ruled that the directive's scope "covers all workers, including those in the public sector", rejecting Italy's argument that constitutional traditions justified different treatment. This ruling forced several Member States with large public sectors—including Italy, Greece, and France—to bring their public sector fixed-term contract rules into compliance.

### Why did the UK and Denmark vote against the directive in the Council?

The UK's Conservative government under John Major had been defeated in May 1997, but the incoming Labour government under Tony Blair still opposed the directive. Blair's position was that any EU regulation of temporary employment would reduce labour market flexibility and increase unemployment, reflecting the UK's preference for deregulated labour markets with minimal statutory protections. Denmark voted against on similar grounds, preferring its flexicurity model based on collective agreements rather than binding EU law. However, both countries were bound once the directive was adopted by qualified majority voting under the Amsterdam Treaty's social provisions.

### Why did the CJEU's "Mangold" ruling become so controversial?

The 2006 *Mangold* ruling (Case C-144/04) extended the directive's principle of non-discrimination to cover age discrimination, even though the directive itself only prohibits less favourable treatment for fixed-term workers compared to permanent workers. The Court ruled that non-discrimination on grounds of age is a "general principle of EU law" that must be upheld even in cases where the national transposition deadline had not yet passed. This was highly controversial because it effectively gave the directive a broader scope than its text, and because it allowed individuals to invoke the directive against national law even before the transposition period expired. The ruling was criticised by several Member States and legal scholars as an overreach of judicial power, but it has since been reinforced by subsequent CJEU cases.
