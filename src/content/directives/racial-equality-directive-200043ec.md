## What is it?

The Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) outlaws discrimination based on race or ethnic origin across all EU member states. It covers not just employment, but also social protection (including social security and healthcare), education, housing, and access to goods and services — a much broader scope than earlier EU anti-discrimination law. The directive defines direct and indirect discrimination, harassment, and victimisation, and requires member states to establish bodies for the promotion of equal treatment.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct political response to the electoral breakthrough of Jörg Haider's far-right Freedom Party in Austria in February 2000, which shocked EU capitals. Haider's party won 27% of the vote and entered a coalition government, triggering the EU's first-ever sanctions against a member state — the other 14 EU governments froze bilateral diplomatic contacts with Vienna. The Austrian crisis created an urgent political imperative for the EU to demonstrate its commitment to combating racism and xenophobia.

The legislative groundwork had been laid years earlier. In 1997, the Treaty of Amsterdam added a new Article 13 (now Article 19 TFEU), giving the EU explicit competence to adopt anti-discrimination legislation — something the original treaties had not provided. But the political will to use this power was limited until the Haider crisis. Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou, the Greek socialist who held the Employment and Social Affairs portfolio in the Prodi Commission, seized the moment. She proposed the directive in November 1999, before the Austrian elections, but the vote in Austria accelerated its passage dramatically.

The directive was adopted on 29 June 2000 — just five months after the Austrian coalition was formed and seven months after the Commission's original proposal. This was extraordinarily fast by EU standards. The Council of the European Union adopted it unanimously, with no member state voting against. The European Parliament's rapporteur was German Socialist MEP Joke Swiebel, who pushed for the broadest possible scope.

A coalition of civil society organisations — including the European Network Against Racism (ENAR), the Starting Line Group, and the European Women's Lobby — had been campaigning for such a directive since the mid-1990s. Business opposition was muted, partly because the directive's employment provisions were seen as inevitable after the Amsterdam Treaty, and partly because the expanded scope (housing, education, goods and services) was novel but enforcement mechanisms were weak.

The directive also reflected a structural tension: EU law prohibited discrimination on grounds of nationality in the single market (Article 12 TEC), but had no parallel ban on racial or ethnic discrimination. This created a legal absurdity where an EU citizen from one member state working in another had stronger protection from nationality-based discrimination than a racial minority citizen of the same country.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam introduces Article 13, giving EU competence to combat discrimination on grounds of race, ethnic origin, religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation.
- **November 1999** — The Prodi Commission proposes the Racial Equality Directive.
- **February 2000** — Jörg Haider's Freedom Party enters Austrian government; EU-14 impose diplomatic sanctions on Austria.
- **29 June 2000** — Council of the European Union adopts Directive 2000/43/EC unanimously.
- **19 July 2000** — Directive published in the Official Journal.
- **19 July 2003** — Implementation deadline for member states.
- **2004** — European Commission launches first infringement proceedings against member states that failed to transpose the directive on time.
- **2007** — The European Court of Justice delivers its first judgment interpreting the directive (Case C-54/07, *Feryn*), establishing that public statements by an employer that they will not hire immigrants constitute direct discrimination.
- **2014** — Commission report on the application of the directive finds uneven implementation across member states, with significant gaps in housing and education.
- **2020** — European Commission publishes a "Union of Equality" strategy, identifying the directive as a cornerstone but calling for stronger enforcement.

## Impact and consequences

The directive transformed the legal landscape of anti-discrimination in Europe. By 2005, all member states had transposed it into national law, though the quality varied significantly. The Commission's 2014 report found that 20 member states had established equality bodies as required, but many lacked the independence and resources to be effective.

The directive has been cited in hundreds of ECJ cases and has influenced national court decisions across the EU. The most significant early case was *Feryn* (2007), where the Court ruled that a Belgian company's public statement that it "would not employ Moroccans" constituted discrimination even without an identifiable victim — a landmark shift that made it easier to challenge racist hiring practices.

Critics argue the directive has had limited practical impact on inequality. The EU Fundamental Rights Agency's surveys consistently show that people of African descent, Roma, and other ethnic minorities face widespread discrimination in housing, employment, and policing. The 2019 FRA survey "Being Black in the EU" found that 45% of respondents of African descent had experienced racial discrimination in the previous five years — roughly the same proportion as when similar surveys began in 2008.

The directive's enforcement gap has been most acute in housing and education, where discrimination is harder to prove and equality bodies have fewer powers. The Commission's 2021-2025 "Anti-racism action plan" acknowledged that the directive's potential remained under-realised and promised stronger monitoring, including country-specific recommendations.

## Key questions answered

### Can a landlord in the EU refuse to rent an apartment to someone because of their race or ethnicity?

No. The Racial Equality Directive explicitly prohibits discrimination based on race or ethnic origin in access to housing, including rental properties. EU member states must ensure that landlords, real estate agents, and housing authorities cannot refuse to rent, impose worse conditions, or evict someone because of their ethnic background. However, enforcement varies — housing discrimination can be difficult to prove, and in some countries, equality bodies lack the resources to investigate complaints effectively.

### Does EU law protect against racial discrimination beyond the workplace?

Yes, and this is what makes the 2000 directive groundbreaking. Unlike earlier EU anti-discrimination laws that only covered employment, the Racial Equality Directive covers social protection (including healthcare and social security), education, and access to goods and services (including housing, banking, and public transport). This broader scope was a deliberate choice by the European Commission and the member states after the Amsterdam Treaty, and it remains wider than comparable protections for other grounds (religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation) which are still limited to employment under the parallel Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC).

### Why did the EU need its own law on racial discrimination when member states already had national laws?

Before 2000, EU law contained no explicit prohibition of racial discrimination — only a ban on nationality-based discrimination, which primarily protected EU citizens moving between member states. This meant that a Romanian citizen working in Germany was protected from being treated worse than a German citizen, but a German citizen of Turkish origin had no EU-level protection against racial discrimination by their employer. The Racial Equality Directive filled this gap and also set a common minimum standard across the EU, preventing a race to the bottom where countries with weaker protections could attract employers.

### Does the directive require member states to set up an official equality body?

Yes. Article 13 of the directive obliges all member states to designate one or more bodies with a mandate to promote equal treatment, analyse discrimination, conduct independent surveys, and publish reports. These equality bodies — such as the UK's Equality and Human Rights Commission, France's Défenseur des Droits, or Germany's Antidiskriminierungsstelle des Bundes — must be independent of government influence and have the power to assist victims of discrimination in pursuing complaints. The Commission's 2014 report found that while all member states had established such bodies, their independence, resources, and enforcement powers varied considerably.
