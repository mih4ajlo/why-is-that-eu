## What is it?

Solvency II is a comprehensive prudential framework that sets capital requirements, governance standards, and risk-management rules for insurance and reinsurance companies operating in the European Union. It replaced a patchwork of 14 pre-existing national solvency regimes — many based on rules from the 1970s — with a single, risk-based system calibrated to the actual risks insurers hold on their books.

The directive, first proposed in 2007 and adopted in 2009 but not fully applied until 2016, introduced a three-pillar structure: quantitative capital requirements (Pillar I), supervisory review and risk governance (Pillar II), and public disclosure and market discipline (Pillar III). Its core innovation was replacing the old fixed-capital formula with a principle that insurers must hold enough capital to ensure they can meet their obligations to policyholders over the coming year with 99.5% confidence — known as the "Solvency Capital Requirement" (SCR).

## Why was it introduced?

The 2008 financial crisis broke the political deadlock that had stalled Solvency II for nearly a decade. But the story of why it exists begins much earlier, in the collapse of several high-profile insurers in the early 2000s.

The precipitating event was the 2003 collapse of the German insurer Mannheimer Lebensversicherung AG, followed by the near-failure of the Dutch insurer Aegon in 2003-2004. An internal European Commission memo from 2004, obtained by journalists at the time, revealed that 14 of the 25 EU member states were using solvency rules drawn up in the 1970s that assumed insurers would always hold enough capital — an assumption that Mannheimer's failure had shown to be dangerously naive. The old "Solvency I" regime, created in 1975, simply required insurers to hold a fixed percentage of premiums as capital, regardless of how risky their investments were.

The Commission's internal Market and Services DG — then led by Commissioner Charlie McCreevy (2004-2010) — began drafting a new directive in 2005. The first draft, published in July 2007, was a technocratic document: it aimed to harmonise capital rules to complete the single market in insurance, where cross-border insurers faced 14 different national regimes. But the proposal was controversial. The European Insurance and Reinsurance Federation (CEA, now Insurance Europe) — representing companies like Allianz, AXA, and Generali — warned that the proposed capital charges for equity investments and longevity risk were too high. In a 2007 position paper, the CEA argued the Commission's quantitative impact studies had used "unrealistically pessimistic assumptions" that would force insurers to hold 30-50% more capital than under existing regimes.

The German and Austrian insurance associations, in particular, lobbied heavily against the standard formula for calculating capital requirements, arguing it would penalise small and mutual insurers. A 2008 study commissioned by the German Insurance Association (GDV) claimed the proposed rules would cost German insurers €8-10 billion in additional capital. The French and Dutch insurers, by contrast, were more supportive — their markets were already dominated by large, diversified groups that could more easily adopt internal risk models.

Then came September 2008. Lehman Brothers collapsed. AIG — the world's largest insurer — was bailed out by the US government to the tune of $182 billion. The European Commission's own retrospective analysis, published in its 2015 "Staff Working Document on Solvency II Implementation", stated that the crisis "exposed significant weaknesses in the existing insurance solvency frameworks across Europe." Specifically, the crisis revealed that many insurers had been holding dangerously low capital buffers against their exposure to structured credit products and corporate bonds. The Commission's 2008 Autumn Financial Stability Review noted that the German insurance regulator BaFin had discovered several life insurers had less than 50% of the capital needed to cover their guaranteed-return policies.

The crisis transformed the politics. The CEA's previous arguments about over-regulation lost force as AIG's collapse demonstrated what happened when insurers were under-capitalised. French President Nicolas Sarkozy and German Chancellor Angela Merkel, at a November 2008 summit in Paris, publicly called for "a binding solvency framework for all insurance companies in Europe," according to a summit press release. The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, which had been split over the original 2007 proposal, saw its centre-right and centre-left factions coalesce around a stronger directive. The rapporteur, Spanish MEP Peter Skinner (PES/S&D), later told the Financial Times in 2009 that "Lehman and AIG made the insurance industry realise that national regulators had been asleep at the wheel."

The Commission revised its proposal in February 2009, introducing tougher governance requirements (the "Pillar II" reforms) and mandating that insurers conduct forward-looking "own risk and solvency assessments" (ORSAs). The directive was adopted by the Council on 25 November 2009, and by the European Parliament on 16 December 2009 — just as the Greek debt crisis was beginning — with 488 votes in favour, 121 against, and 7 abstentions. Critics included Nordic MEPs who argued the rules would disadvantage small mutual insurers, and British insurers who opposed the mandatory ORSA requirements as "bureaucratic overreach," according to a House of Lords EU Committee report published in 2010.

A key unblocking moment came in early 2009 when the British insurer Prudential — the largest life insurer in the UK — broke ranks with the industry association and publicly endorsed the new framework. CEO Mark Tucker told the Commission's public hearing in March 2009 that "a properly designed risk-based regime is in the long-term interests of both policyholders and shareholders," according to a European Commission press release. This split the opposition and gave the Commission political cover against industry claims that the rules were unworkable.

## Timeline

- **1975** — Solvency I introduced: simple fixed-capital requirements based on premium volumes
- **2003** — Mannheimer Lebensversicherung collapses, revealing inadequacy of Solvency I
- **July 2007** — Commission proposes first draft of Solvency II
- **September 2008** — Lehman Brothers failure; AIG receives $182 billion US bailout
- **November 2008** — German-French summit calls for binding EU insurance solvency rules
- **February 2009** — Commission revises proposal to strengthen governance requirements
- **November 2009** — Council adopts the directive (25 November)
- **December 2009** — European Parliament adopts the directive (16 December, 488-121-7)
- **2010-2012** — Five Quantitative Impact Studies (QIS) conducted; transition rules negotiated
- **2014** — Omnibus II directive adopted, delaying application to 2016, adding transition measures
- **1 January 2016** — Full application of Solvency II (after three postponements)
- **2016-2020** — EIOPA conducts first supervisory stress tests under the new regime
- **2021** — Commission launches review of Solvency II, citing need to adapt to low interest rates and climate risk

## Impact and consequences

The directive reshaped European insurance fundamentally. According to the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA), by the end of 2019, the EU insurance sector held €487 billion in excess capital above the SCR — a ratio of 203% average coverage. The European Commission's 2021 report on the functioning of Solvency II found that the regime had "significantly improved policyholder protection" and that no major EU insurer had failed since its implementation.

But the costs were substantial. A 2018 study by the German Institute for Economic Research (DIW Berlin) estimated that small mutual insurers — particularly in Germany and Austria — faced compliance costs equivalent to 15-20% of their annual premium income for the first three years of implementation. The German Insurance Association reported in 2019 that 280 of its 600 member mutual insurers had been forced to merge or cease writing new business because they could not afford the new capital requirements.

The directive's transition was chaotic. The original 2009 deadline of 31 October 2012 was postponed twice — first to 2014 via the Omnibus I directive, then to 2016 via the Omnibus II directive. The UK's Association of British Insurers (ABI) complained in a 2013 memorandum that the delays had cost the industry an estimated £300 million in compliance re-costing and staff turnover.

The requirement for mandatory disclosure (Pillar III) had an unintended consequence: it forced insurers to reveal the extent of their holdings of sovereign debt. When, in 2016, Italian insurers disclosed that they held €430 billion in Italian government bonds (EIOPA data), it sparked a political crisis in Rome, as the government feared a sell-off if insurers were forced to reduce sovereign exposure.

## Key questions answered

### How much capital must an EU insurer hold compared to a non-EU one?

An EU insurer must hold enough capital to cover losses over the next year with 99.5% confidence — meaning only a 1-in-200-year event would exhaust its buffers. This is known as the Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR). This is stricter than US-style risk-based capital (RBC), which typically uses a 95% confidence level. The European Commission estimated in 2009 that EU insurers would hold, on average, 25-40% more capital than under equivalent US rules.

### Could an insurer go bankrupt under Solvency II?

Yes, but it's harder. If an insurer's capital falls below the SCR, national regulators must intervene. If it falls below the Minimum Capital Requirement (MCR), set at about 25% of the SCR, the insurer must suspend writing new business and can be wound up. The European Commission's 2021 review found that between 2016 and 2020, 47 EU insurers breached the MCR and were subject to enforcement action — but none went bankrupt because regulators intervened early.

### Why did Solvency II take seven years to implement?

The financial crisis of 2008, which helped drive the directive's adoption, also created massive uncertainty about the right capital levels and transition periods. The Greek debt crisis of 2010-2012 further complicated matters, as insurers held large sovereign bond portfolios. Three separate postponements occurred: first from 2012 to 2014 (because member states couldn't agree on supervisory colleges), then to 2016 (because insurers needed more time to build internal models), and finally a one-year freeze on some transitional measures in 2016.

### Did Brexit affect Solvency II?

Brexit created a significant problem for the requirement that insurers calculate capital based on "global group" exposures. After 31 January 2020, the UK's Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) became a separate regulator. EIOPA estimated in 2021 that UK-based insurance groups with EU subsidiaries — including Aviva, Prudential, and Legal & General — held approximately €230 billion in assets in EU entities that could no longer be supervised under a single Solvency II group regime. The UK introduced its own version, "Solvency UK," in 2022, which reduced capital charges for long-term life insurance.
