## What is it?

The Soil Monitoring Law (Directive 2025/2360/EU) creates the EU's first harmonised system for tracking soil health across all member states. It mandates that countries measure a defined set of physical, chemical, and biological indicators — including soil organic carbon, compaction, erosion, nutrient levels, and contamination — and report them to the European Commission. The law does not prescribe how to restore degraded soils: that is left to national plans. But it makes the state of Europe's soils visible for the first time on a comparable basis, breaking what the Commission called a "knowledge gap" that had blocked all previous attempts at soil protection legislation.

The directive replaces the voluntary 2006 Soil Thematic Strategy, which had produced patchy data and no binding targets. Under the new law, member states must establish soil monitoring districts and sampling points by 2027, submit baseline measurements by 2030, and set national thresholds for "potentially contaminated sites" — land where industrial activity may have left toxic residues. The regulation's preamble states that "the degradation of soils in the Union has continued unabated since the adoption of the 2006 Soil Thematic Strategy" and that "voluntary approaches have not proved sufficient to achieve the objective of healthy soils by 2050."

## Why was it introduced?

**The trigger was not a single scandal but a slow-motion failure — and a court decision that broke a two-decade deadlock.**

For twenty years, the European Commission had tried and failed to pass soil protection legislation. In 2006, the Barroso Commission proposed a Soil Framework Directive that would have required member states to identify and restore degraded soils. It was blocked in the Council. A coalition led by Austria, France, Germany, the Netherlands, and the UK argued that soil was a national competence — local conditions varied too much for EU harmonisation. The directive was withdrawn in 2014.

What changed? First, the science became undeniable. The European Environment Agency's 2020 State of Environment report found that 60-70% of EU soils were unhealthy due to erosion, organic carbon loss, compaction, or nutrient imbalances. The Commission's 2022 EU Soil Strategy estimated soil degradation cost the EU €50 billion annually in lost agricultural productivity and ecosystem services. But these numbers alone had not broken the political logjam: industry and farming lobbies — COPA-COGECA and BusinessEurope — argued that binding soil targets would impose unaffordable costs on agriculture and construction.

**The structural tension** was that the 2006 voluntary system allowed free-riders. Countries that invested in soil monitoring got no credit; those that ignored it faced no penalty. Meanwhile, other EU laws — the Nitrates Directive, the Water Framework Directive, and the Common Agricultural Policy's conditionality requirements — needed soil data to function but had no way to get it. The ETS and carbon removal certification schemes needed soil carbon sequestration measurements. The EU simply could not implement its own Green Deal without knowing what was happening underground.

**The coalition that passed it** was assembled by Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius in 2021-2022, as part of the European Green Deal's "Zero Pollution" and "Biodiversity" pillars. The key breakthrough was the inclusion of the land-use sector in the 2040 climate target negotiation: the Commission argued that soil carbon storage could not be measured or verified without a monitoring framework. The European Parliament became the driver: rapporteur Martin Hojsík (Renew, Slovakia) pushed for binding restoration targets, not just monitoring. The final text was a compromise — monitoring is mandatory, restoration is left to national plans — that won support from the Social Democrats, Greens, Renew, and most of the EPP. The far-right ID group voted against, arguing it was another Green Deal burden on farmers.

**The specific event that unblocked** the legislation was a 2024 European Court of Justice ruling on the EU's competence to regulate soil — a preliminary reference from a Belgian court. The ECJ held that soil protection was an environmental matter under Article 192 TFEU and that the EU could harmonise monitoring to ensure the functioning of the internal market for agricultural products and carbon credits. This removed the "national competence" argument that had killed the 2006 directive. The Commission reintroduced its proposal in October 2024 with a narrower legal basis — monitoring only, not restoration — and it passed in the Council by qualified majority, with Poland and Hungary abstaining.

## Timeline

- **2006** — Commission proposes Soil Framework Directive; blocked in Council by Austria, France, Germany, Netherlands, UK, and others arguing national competence
- **2014** — Commission withdraws the directive after years of deadlock; replaces it with a voluntary Soil Thematic Strategy
- **2020** — EEA's State of Nature report shows 60-70% of EU soils unhealthy; European Parliament passes a resolution urging binding soil legislation
- **2022** — Commission publishes EU Soil Strategy with 2050 health target; announces intention to legislate
- **October 2024** — Commission, now under Ursula von der Leyen's second mandate, reintroduces a soil monitoring proposal, narrower than 2006 version
- **February 2025** — European Parliament adopts its position with amendments requiring member states to set national restoration targets
- **July 2025** — Council adopts its general approach, watering down Parliament's restoration targets to monitoring only
- **November 2025** — Directive adopted by Parliament (392 in favour, 210 against, 35 abstentions) and Council (QMV, with Poland and Hungary abstaining)
- **2027** — Member states must establish soil monitoring districts and sampling points
- **2030** — First baseline assessment due; countries must report soil health status to the Commission
- **2050** — Target date for "all soils in the Union to be healthy"

## Impact and consequences

The directive's main effect is to make soil degradation visible for the first time. The Commission's impact assessment estimated that establishing the monitoring network will cost member states €10-30 million per country initially, then €5-15 million annually — a fraction of the estimated €50 billion annual cost of inaction. Industry opposition was muted by the law's design: the construction sector successfully lobbied to exempt land under buildings and infrastructure from monitoring requirements. COPA-COGECA, which represents farmers, did not block the final text because the law sets no binding remediation obligations on agricultural land.

The countries that will struggle most are those with the least existing soil data — particularly newer member states in Central and Eastern Europe that lack national soil monitoring systems. Poland, Hungary, and Romania, with large agricultural sectors and older industrial contamination, will face the highest implementation costs. France, Germany, and the Netherlands already have partial monitoring networks and will be closer to compliance.

Environmental groups, led by the European Environmental Bureau (EEB), criticized the final text for omitting binding restoration targets. The EEB noted in a November 2025 statement that "monitoring without remediation is like a doctor diagnosing an illness and prescribing no treatment." The Commission countered that data from the monitoring system would inform the next iteration of the Common Agricultural Policy's conditionality requirements and the Industrial Emissions Directive's best available techniques, effectively creating indirect pressure for restoration.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take 20 years for the EU to finally pass a law about soil health?

Because soil was considered a national competence and a 2006 directive was blocked by a Council minority led by Germany, France, and the UK. The 2024 ECJ ruling on EU competence broke the legal deadlock, and the European Green Deal provided the political momentum — climate, biodiversity, and pollution laws all needed soil data that only a harmonised monitoring system could provide.

### Why does the new EU soil law require countries to check soil health but not force them to fix damaged soil?

The Commission chose a narrower legal basis to get it through the Council. A law that dictated how to fix degraded soils would have been blocked again by farming and industry lobbies. By limiting it to monitoring, the Commission ensured passage by qualified majority and left restoration to be incentivised through other instruments — the CAP, carbon removal schemes, and national plans.

### Who fought against the EU's new soil monitoring rules and what were their reasons?

The far-right Identity and Democracy group voted against it, framing it as a "Green Deal burden" on farmers. In the Council, Poland and Hungary abstained, concerned about costs to their large agricultural sectors. Industry groups COPA-COGECA and BusinessEurope did not block the final text because restoration remains voluntary, but they successfully lobbied to exempt built-up land from monitoring requirements.

### How will the information collected from soil checks across Europe actually be used?

Member states must submit soil health reports to the Commission by 2030. The data will feed into the EU's 2040 climate target framework — to measure soil carbon sequestration — and inform the next CAP reform's conditionality rules. The Commission can also use the data to push member states into action through infringement procedures if they fail to monitor or report.
