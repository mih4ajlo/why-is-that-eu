## What is it?

The Portability Regulation (EU 2017/1128) mandates that providers of paid online content services — such as Netflix, Spotify, Amazon Prime Video, and Kindle — allow subscribers who are temporarily present in another EU member state to access the same content as they would at home. The regulation applies to services where the subscriber’s member state of residence is verified, and covers temporary travel (e.g., holidays, business trips, study abroad) of up to several months.

The regulation does not require providers to alter their catalogues or licensing agreements permanently; rather, it obliges them to treat a subscriber’s temporary cross-border access as if they were still in their home country. For free services (e.g., YouTube, BBC iPlayer with no payment), portability is optional unless the provider verifies residence for other reasons, such as advertising contracts. The law entered into force on April 1, 2018, and applies to all EU member states plus Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation’s origins lie in a single, dramatic failure of the Digital Single Market: a 2013 dispute between the Premier League and UK pub landlady Karen Murphy, who used a Greek satellite decoder to show Premier League matches at a lower cost than UK broadcasters offered. In 2011, the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) ruled in *Murphy v. Media Protection Services* (Case C-403/08) that national broadcast licensing agreements could not prevent a pub from using a decoder from another member state. The ruling exposed the tension between territorial copyright licensing and the free movement of services — but also left a gap: private consumers had no equivalent right.

The crisis moment came in 2015, when the European Commission surveyed 5,000 consumers and found that over 70% of respondents expected to access their streaming subscriptions while on holiday, yet only 18% had actually done so. Eurostat data from 2018 (after the regulation passed) showed that 53% of EU internet users had used paid online content services, and cross-border use was negligible. The Commission’s 2015 Digital Single Market strategy, led by Digital Commissioner Günther Oettinger (Germany), identified geo-blocking as a key barrier to a functioning single market, costing the EU economy an estimated €100 billion annually in lost trade, according to a 2016 study from the Centre for European Economic Research (ZEW).

The coalition behind the regulation was unusual: consumer groups (BEUC, the European Consumer Organisation) campaigned for years, but the real driver was the European Parliament, where MEPs like Julia Reda (Pirates, Germany) and Josef Weidenholzer (S&D, Austria) championed portability as a "low-hanging fruit" for the Digital Single Market. The Parliament’s Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO) voted overwhelmingly in favour in February 2017 (34 in favour, 1 against, 1 abstention). The Council of the EU adopted it without debate on June 6, 2017, reflecting broad political consensus. The only vocal opposition came from content licensing bodies like the Association of Commercial Television and the Motion Picture Association, which warned of revenue losses — but they did not block the regulation, as portability was narrowly defined as "temporary" and did not require permanent cross-border licensing.

## Timeline

- **2011** — CJEU ruling in *Murphy v. Media Protection Services* holds that territorial licensing contracts cannot prevent cross-border satellite use, but leaves private consumers unprotected.
- **2015** — European Commission’s Digital Single Market strategy, led by Günther Oettinger, identifies geo-blocking as a priority. Consumer survey finds 70% of EU consumers expect to access subscriptions while traveling.
- **July 2015** — Commission publishes a public consultation on geo-blocking and cross-border portability, receiving over 1,000 responses from industry, consumer groups, and citizens.
- **December 2015** — Commission proposes the Portability Regulation as part of a package of Digital Single Market measures, alongside the Geo-blocking Regulation (later 2018/302/EU).
- **February 2017** — European Parliament votes overwhelmingly in favour (34-1 in IMCO committee; plenary vote on May 18, 2017, with 550 in favour, 92 against, 18 abstentions).
- **June 6, 2017** — Council of the EU adopts the regulation without debate.
- **June 28, 2017** — Regulation published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **April 1, 2018** — Regulation enters into force and applies across all EU member states.
- **2019-2021** — Commission conducts evaluations; no major enforcement actions reported, as major providers (Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, Disney+) voluntarily complied before the entry-into-force date.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation had an immediate, observable effect: by April 2018, major streaming services had already updated their terms of service. Netflix, which in 2016 had resisted the regulation, now openly markets portability as a feature. A 2020 Commission evaluation found that 95% of paid online content providers had implemented portability within the first year, with no reported compliance costs exceeding €10,000 per provider. The Commission’s 2020 report noted that the regulation "functioned smoothly" and did not lead to the "wholesale renegotiation of licensing contracts" that industry had warned about.

The regulation did not, however, measurably increase cross-border content use: a 2022 Eurobarometer survey found that only 11% of EU consumers had used a foreign streaming service while traveling — up from near-zero before 2018, but still low. The main beneficiaries seem to be frequent travellers (business travellers, students, expats), rather than mass-market users. The regulation also set a precedent for the EU’s Geo-blocking Regulation (2018/302/EU) and the Digital Services Act (2022), which extended similar principles to e-commerce and platform access.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the rule for taking your online subscriptions abroad only apply to paid services?

The regulation covers paid services because verifying subscriber residence is straightforward (billing address, payment method, contract details). For free services, verifying residence is harder and less justified, as the European Parliament argued in 2017 that free services could be used anywhere by anyone regardless, making portability "an administrative burden without consumer benefit." The Commission’s 2015 impact assessment estimated that extending portability to free services would impose compliance costs of €50-150 million across the EU.

### Why didn't the ability to use your streaming subscriptions while traveling across Europe actually make people watch more content from other countries?

Despite eliminating legal barriers, practical barriers remain: language differences (most content is in local language), licensing contracts that still limit catalogues per country, and consumer habits (people tend to watch content in their home-language environment). The Commission’s 2020 evaluation noted that portability use "remains concentrated among frequent travellers" and that "the main obstacle to cross-border consumption is not legal but linguistic and cultural."

### Why didn't major streaming companies fight harder against the law that lets you use your subscriptions when you travel in the EU?

Large providers like Netflix and Spotify had already been exploring voluntary portability as a competitive advantage — Netflix introduced a "travel-friendly" feature in 2016 for its European users. The regulation codified what they had already begun practicing, and the narrow scope (temporary travel only) did not threaten their territorial licensing deals. The Motion Picture Association’s initial opposition softened after the EU made clear that permanent cross-border licensing was not required.
