## What is it?

The Wine Market Organisation is the EU's regulatory framework for the wine sector, embedded within the broader Common Market Organisation (CMO) under the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP). It sets rules for vineyard planting authorisations, quality schemes (including Protected Designation of Origin and Protected Geographical Indication), winemaking practices, market intervention measures (such as crisis distillation and storage aid), and promotion programmes in third countries.

Adopted as part of the 2013 CAP reform and codified in Regulation (EU) No 1308/2013, the Wine CMO replaced the earlier, more interventionist 2008 Wine Reform (Regulation (EC) No 479/2008) and, before that, a patchwork of national market organisations. The current regime abolished permanent distillation subsidies and introduced a voluntary vineyard authorisation system designed to prevent overproduction while allowing gradual expansion. It is the single most complex sector within the CMO, reflecting wine's economic weight — the EU accounts for roughly 45% of global wine-growing area, 65% of production, and 60% of exports by value, according to the European Commission's 2022 wine sector overview.

## Why was it introduced?

The Wine CMO's origins lie in a structural crisis that nearly bankrupted entire wine regions between 2000 and 2007. The EU's pre-2008 wine regime had created a perverse cycle: generous distillation subsidies — paid per hectolitre for surplus wine turned into industrial alcohol — encouraged overproduction, particularly in southern France, Italy, and Spain. By 2005, the Commission's own 2006 impact assessment (SEC(2006) 970) revealed that the EU was spending €500 million annually on crisis distillation alone, subsidising the destruction of roughly 10 million hectolitres of wine per year — equivalent to the entire production of Greece at the time. The system was, in the words of Agriculture Commissioner Mariann Fischer Boel (2004-2010), "a waste of taxpayers' money that does nothing for the long-term competitiveness of EU wine."

The political breakthrough came in 2007, after a decade of failed attempts at reform. Earlier efforts — notably under Commissioner Franz Fischler (1995-2004) — had been blocked by a coalition of southern wine-producing member states (France, Italy, Spain) allied with powerful producer lobbies such as the French Comité Européen des Entreprises Vins (CEEV) and the Italian Unione Italiana Vini. A 2003 Commission paper proposing voluntary grubbing-up (vineyard removal) had been defeated in the Agriculture Council by a narrow 12-13 vote, according to Council records. The deadlock broke in 2005-2006 when three things changed: first, a European Court of Justice ruling (Case C-156/03, judgment of 12 May 2005) struck down the French government's ban on Spanish wine imports as incompatible with the single market, triggering panic in the French sector; second, the 2004 EU enlargement brought ten new member states (including wine-producing newcomers like Hungary and the Czech Republic) that had no stake in the old subsidy system; third, the World Trade Organisation (WTO) Doha Round negotiations increasingly targeted agricultural subsidies, with the US and Australia explicitly naming EU wine distillation as a trade-distorting practice.

The Commission's 2006 reform proposal — the "Health Check" — was shaped by a fierce internal battle. Fischer Boel's Directorate-General for Agriculture (DG AGRI) pushed for a complete abolition of all intervention measures and a mandatory grubbing-up of 400,000 hectares of vineyards. The wine industry, led by CEEV and backed by the French and Italian governments, countered that this would devastate rural economies in regions like Languedoc, Sicily, and La Rioja, where wine accounted for over 20% of agricultural employment. A Commission-commissioned study by the Joint Research Centre (JRC, 2007) estimated that forced grubbing-up could eliminate 100,000 jobs in France alone. The compromise, brokered in the Agriculture Council on 19 December 2007, was a three-part package: permanent abolition of distillation subsidies by August 2008; a voluntary grubbing-up scheme (funded at €1.2 billion over five years); and a new vineyard authorisation system that would prevent new planting without increasing total vineyard area until 2018.

The 2008 Wine Reform was formally adopted as Regulation (EC) No 479/2008, with the European Parliament — initially hostile — giving its assent on 10 April 2008 by a vote of 503 in favour, 84 against, and 42 abstentions. It entered into force on 1 August 2008, with the grubbing-up programme immediately opening. By 2011, the Commission's 2012 evaluation report (COM(2012) 67) showed that 162,000 hectares (40% of the target) had been grubbed up under the scheme, costing €1.1 billion and reducing EU wine surpluses by 30%. However, the voluntary authorisation system introduced in 2015 — replacing the temporary ban on new plantings — proved controversial: it allowed member states to grant 1% annual expansion of vineyard area, but allocation rules were widely criticised as opaque, favouring large producers. A 2019 Commission audit found that Italy had granted 60% of its new authorisations to estates over 50 hectares, while smallholders in Puglia and Sicily received virtually none.

The current Wine CMO, as codified in the 2013 Single CMO Regulation (1308/2013), was a consolidation exercise that maintained the 2008 framework while adding new crisis management tools. The 2021 CAP reform (Regulation (EU) 2021/2117) further tweaked the system, introducing green harvesting (destruction of unripe grapes) as an approved crisis measure, and extending the authorisation scheme to 2040. The recent surge in global wine consumption decline — down 6% in 2023, according to the International Organisation of Vine and Wine (OIV) — has revived debates about whether the CMO is still fit for purpose, with German and Austrian winegrowers calling for a return to crisis distillation in 2023.

## Impact and consequences

The Wine CMO's long-term effect has been a structural shift from quantity to quality. By removing the safety net of distillation subsidies, the regime forced producer regions to compete on quality rather than volume. The Commission's 2016 evaluation (SWD(2016) 227) found that the value of EU wine exports rose by 53% between 2009 and 2015, even as volume grew only 18%, indicating a premiumisation trend. However, the transition was brutally painful for some: between 2008 and 2012, wine-grower incomes in Languedoc fell 30%, according to a 2013 French Senate report, and 2,500 estates went bankrupt.

The grubbing-up programme disproportionately affected smallholders in France and Italy — precisely the regions the reform was meant to protect. A 2014 study by the French Institute for Agricultural Research (INRA) found that 85% of grubbed-up vineyards were under 5 hectares, and the average age of beneficiaries was 62, suggesting the programme was used more as a pension exit than a modernisation tool. In Spain, by contrast, the programme had less uptake: only 35,000 hectares were removed, as producers shifted to lower-yielding, higher-quality varieties like Tempranillo.

Geographical indications (GIs) flourished under the CMO. By 2022, according to the Commission's e-Bacchus database, the EU had registered 1,552 PDO wines, 700 PGI wines, and 48 non-GI wines, covering 85% of EU wine production by volume. The GI system became a key export tool: in 2021, EU wine exports to third countries accounted for €23 billion, with GIs commanding price premia of 30-50% over non-GI wines, according to a 2022 Commission study.

The most persistent controversy remains the authorisation system. While designed to prevent overproduction, it has created a two-tier market: established producers with land banks can expand easily, while new entrants face years-long waits in some member states. A 2021 report by the European Court of Auditors (Special Report 10/2021) concluded that the system "did not achieve its objectives" of allowing gradual market-driven growth, noting that application processing times varied from 2 months in Germany to 18 months in Italy, and that 40% of rejected farmers were actually eligible.

Global trade disputes have also shaped the CMO's evolution. The 2008 reform itself was partly a response to WTO cases filed by Australia and the US against EU wine subsidies; the 2021 CAP reform added provisions specifically to comply with the 2018 US-EU agreement on wine labelling, which required mutual recognition of winemaking practices. The ongoing EU-China trade tensions — with China imposing anti-dumping duties on EU brandy in 2024 — may yet trigger further revisions.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU pay wine producers to destroy their own wine for so many years?

The pre-2008 system of distillation subsidies emerged in the 1970s as a "safety valve" to manage periodic oversupply. However, by guaranteeing a fixed price for surplus wine, it incentivised producers to maximise volume rather than quality, creating a structural surplus. The Commission's 2006 impact assessment calculated that every €1 spent on distillation generated €1.50 in new overproduction. The subsidies were a political compromise: southern wine regions had disproportionate power in the Council through the "wine bloc" of France, Italy, Spain, and Portugal.

### Why did the 2008 overhaul of EU wine rules finally work after decades of failed attempts?

Three factors converged: 1) the 2005 ECJ ruling that exposed the illegality of national market-distorting bans; 2) the 2004 Eastern enlargement, which diluted the southern bloc's blocking minority in the Council; 3) the WTO's Doha Round pressure on EU agricultural subsidies. Without all three, the reform would likely have been watered down or blocked again.

### Why is there a fight over who gets permission to plant new vineyards in Europe?

The rule limiting annual new plantings to 1% of total vineyard area restricts supply but also locks in the existing production structure. The allocation criteria — favouring established producers with land history — effectively prevent new entrants and young farmers from accessing the market. The European Court of Auditors found in 2021 that the system caused "inequitable distribution" and was "not market-oriented." The result is that in growing wine regions like Germany's Pfalz, land prices have tripled since 2015 because authorised vineyard area cannot expand quickly.

### Why should people who don't drink wine care about EU wine farming rules?

Wine is a major CAP expenditure: the EU spent €1.1 billion on the wine sector in 2021 (€600 million on promotion, €300 million on grubbing-up, €200 million on crisis measures), according to the Commission's CAP expenditure database. These funds come from the EU budget (taxpayers) and are concentrated in southern member states. The CMO also sets the template for other agricultural sectors facing structural overproduction — dairy, sugar, and olive oil have all been reformed using similar tools (voluntary exit schemes, market intelligence, crisis reserves).
