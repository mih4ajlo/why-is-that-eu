## What is it?

The Europol Regulation (EU) 2016/794 is the legal framework for the European Union Agency for Law Enforcement Cooperation, commonly known as Europol. It governs how Europol collects, processes, and shares personal data, especially with law enforcement authorities of non‑EU countries and international organisations. The regulation turned Europol from a supporting centre into a proactive operational hub, able to join Joint Investigation Teams and launch alerts in the Schengen Information System.

Before 2016, Europol operated under a 2009 Council Decision that had been drafted under the EU’s old “third pillar” of intergovernmental justice and home affairs. The Lisbon Treaty moved police cooperation into the ordinary legislative procedure, making the European Parliament a co‑legislator. The new regulation therefore reflects the post‑Lisbon balance between operational efficiency and fundamental rights.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was born from two shocks: the 2013 Snowden revelations and the 2015 Paris terrorist attacks. In June 2013, Edward Snowden’s leaks showed that the US National Security Agency had accessed data held by EU‑based companies and reportedly monitored Europol’s own systems. That same year, the Court of Justice of the EU struck down the Data Retention Directive (2006/24/EC) in the *Digital Rights Ireland* case, ruling that sweeping data retention violated privacy rights. EU institutions faced a twin challenge: police cooperation needed more data, but that data required solid legal safeguards.

The European Commission, under First Vice‑President Frans Timmermans and Home Affairs Commissioner Dimitris Avramopoulos, proposed the new regulation in March 2013. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, Spanish MEP Agustín Díaz de Mera (EPP), led a committee that insisted on strict data‑protection clauses, including a ban on bulk data transfers to third countries and the creation of a Joint Parliamentary Scrutiny Group (JPSG) to oversee Europol’s activities. Civil‑liberty organisations such as EDRi and Statewatch opposed any extension of data‑sharing without independent judicial oversight, while national police authorities, backed by the Council, wanted fewer restrictions on urgent operational exchanges.

The deadlock broke after the November 2015 Paris attacks, when it emerged that several of the attackers had been on national watch lists but that information had not been shared in real time through Europol. A Council report in early 2016 pointed out that under the old legal framework, national liaison bureaus, not Europol analysts, controlled the flow of intelligence. The regulation accelerated the shift to a single Europol platform. In April 2016 the Parliament and Council reached a compromise: data transfers to third countries would require a Commission adequacy decision or an international agreement approved by the Council and Parliament, but Europol could share data in “exceptional” urgent cases without prior consent.

The regulation was adopted on 11 May 2016 and entered into force twenty days later, with application from 1 May 2017.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Europol Decision (2009/371/JHA) adopted under the third pillar, giving Europol limited operational powers.
- **2013** — Snowden leaks reveal NSA access to EU data; Court of Justice strikes down Data Retention Directive (joined cases C‑293/12 and C‑594/12).
- **March 2013** — European Commission publishes proposal for a new Europol regulation (COM(2013) 173).
- **2015** — November Paris attacks expose information‑sharing gaps; EU leaders call for a more operational Europol.
- **2016** — Parliament and Council reach political agreement on the regulation.
- **11 May 2016** — Official adoption of Regulation (EU) 2016/794.
- **1 May 2017** — Regulation applies, replacing the 2009 Decision.
- **2019** — First evaluation report by the Commission (SWD(2019) 345).
- **2020** — Europol’s JPSG holds its first formal meeting to scrutinise the agency’s activities.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation immediately increased Europol’s capacity to process large datasets. According to the Commission’s 2019 evaluation report, the number of operational messages exchanged through the Secure Information Exchange Network Application (SIENA) rose by over 60 % in the first two years of the regulation. However, the strict third‑country data‑transfer rules created friction. After the UK left the EU, Europol could only maintain data flows with the UK under an adequacy decision (adopted in 2021) and a separate law enforcement agreement. The European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) complained in 2020 that Europol had not yet fully implemented the “data minimisation” principles required by the regulation for its large analytical datasets (such as the Europol Standard and Terrorist Finance Tracking Programme). In 2022, the EDPS ordered Europol to delete data on individuals with no clear link to a crime, forcing the agency to redesign its data‑storage procedures.

Member states also struggled to adapt their national liaison structures. Several countries, including Belgium and Germany, reformed their domestic police‑data laws to allow direct access to Europol’s system, something the new regulation encouraged. The JPSG, composed of MEPs and national parliamentarians, began reviewing Europol’s annual reports and can invite the Executive Director to hearings, but it lacks binding powers to block operations.

## Key questions answered

### Can EU police agencies share personal data with the United States?

Yes, but only under strict conditions. The regulation requires a Commission adequacy decision (finding that the third country ensures an equivalent level of data protection) or a binding international agreement approved by the Council and Parliament. For the US, Europol relies on the EU‑US Umbrella Agreement (2016) and, for specific cases, the EU‑US data protection adequacy decisions for commercial data. In urgent threats, Europol can transfer data without prior approval but must notify national authorities and the EDPS.

### Why did the 2015 Paris attacks change how Europol works?

Investigators discovered that several attackers were already flagged in national police databases, but those alerts had not been shared automatically with Europol’s counter‑terrorism unit. The old system relied on liaison officers who manually forwarded information; the new regulation made Europol a central node that can access and cross‑reference data from all member states in real time. It also gave Europol the right to request information from national authorities when an investigation has a cross‑border dimension.

### How does Europol protect personal data from misuse?

The regulation created two oversight layers: the European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) monitors compliance with EU data‑protection rules, and a Joint Parliamentary Scrutiny Group (JPSG) of MEPs and national MPs reviews Europol’s strategic activities. Europol must delete personal data after specified retention periods (generally three years for operational data) unless an investigation justifies longer storage. Transfers to third countries are also subject to an adequacy mechanism. In 2022, the EDPS fined Europol for non‑compliance with retention rules and ordered the deletion of millions of records.

### What happened to the old Europol framework?

The 2009 Council Decision was repealed when the 2016 regulation took effect. Under the old system, Europol was an intergovernmental body with little democratic oversight; the European Parliament had only a consultative role. The Lisbon Treaty moved police cooperation into the EU’s ordinary legislative procedure, making the Parliament a co‑legislator. The new regulation therefore gave MEPs and national parliaments more scrutiny powers, including the right to approve the agency’s budget and appoint its Executive Director.
