## What is it?

Directive 2013/34/EU is the EU's master rulebook for how limited-liability companies prepare their annual accounts and consolidated accounts. It merged and replaced the Fourth Directive (78/660/EEC) on annual accounts and the Seventh Directive (83/349/EEC) on consolidated accounts — both products of the 1970s harmonisation push — into a single, modernised text. It sets out balance-sheet and profit-and-loss layouts, recognition and measurement principles, audit obligations, and rules on the management report.

A core innovation was the "think small first" approach: the directive categorises companies as micro, small, medium-sized and large by balance-sheet total, turnover and employee headcount, and prevents Member States from piling extra disclosures onto small firms beyond a maximum harmonised list. Micro-entities can publish drastically abbreviated accounts.

Its most politically charged feature sits in Chapter 10: a country-by-country reporting (CBCR) regime requiring large undertakings and public-interest entities active in extractive industries (oil, gas, minerals) and primary logging of forests to disclose, for each country and each project, the payments they make to governments — taxes, royalties, licence fees, production entitlements and bonuses above €100,000.

## Why was it introduced?

The push for project-level transparency in extractive industries came not from Brussels but from Washington. Section 1504 of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act forced US-listed resource extraction firms to disclose payments to foreign governments, and NGOs led by the Publish What You Pay coalition — including Global Witness, ONE and Oxfam — immediately demanded the EU match it. Without an EU equivalent, European-listed majors like Shell, BP, Total, ENI and Glencore would face lighter scrutiny than US peers, and Member States risked being undercut as transparency standards. Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier made matching Dodd-Frank an explicit policy goal when he tabled the proposal in October 2011.

The political context was the post-2008 backlash against tax avoidance and the resource curse. Investigative work on opaque payments in Angola, the DRC and Equatorial Guinea, plus the LuxLeaks-era atmosphere building through 2012-13, made it politically impossible for centre-right MEPs to defend secrecy for oil majors. The European Parliament, with rapporteur Klaus-Heiner Lehne (EPP) on the main file and Arlene McCarthy (S&D) leading on transparency, pushed Barnier's proposal further than the Commission originally wanted: project-level disclosure with no materiality exemption for politically sensitive countries — the so-called "tyrants' veto" that industry had lobbied hard to insert. Shell and Total argued publicly through their associations that host-country confidentiality clauses would force them to choose between EU law and contract law in producer states; Parliament rejected the carve-out.

The second driver was simply that the Fourth and Seventh Directives had aged badly. Drafted before IFRS existed, before the single market was completed, and before SMEs were a regulatory priority, they had been amended piecemeal more than a dozen times. Commissioner Barnier's team argued in the 2011 impact assessment that recasting the two texts into one would deliver meaningful annual savings for Europe's small companies, the bulk of EU enterprises. The "Small Business Act" agenda under Commission President Barroso provided the political wrapper: cutting paperwork for SMEs sold the package to centre-right governments wary of new corporate obligations, while CBCR sold it to the left and the Greens. That coalition — burden reduction for the right, transparency for the left — got the directive through the Council and Parliament in June 2013 with comfortable majorities.

## Timeline

- **1978** — Fourth Company Law Directive on annual accounts adopted.
- **1983** — Seventh Company Law Directive on consolidated accounts adopted.
- **2010** — US Dodd-Frank Act §1504 requires resource-extraction issuers to disclose payments to governments.
- **October 2011** — Commissioner Michel Barnier proposes the recast accounting directive, including CBCR for extractives and loggers.
- **April 2013** — Council and Parliament reach political agreement after trilogues.
- **26 June 2013** — Directive 2013/34/EU adopted.
- **20 July 2013** — Entry into force.
- **20 July 2015** — Deadline for Member State transposition.
- **2016** — First payments-to-governments reports published by EU-listed extractive firms covering 2015 financial year.
- **2014** — Directive 2014/95/EU amends 2013/34/EU to add non-financial (sustainability) reporting for large public-interest entities.
- **2021** — Directive 2021/2101/EU extends public CBCR to large multinationals across all sectors (income tax disclosures).
- **2022** — Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (2022/2464/EU) further amends 2013/34/EU, replacing the NFRD regime.

## Impact and consequences

The directive standardised the SME definition across the EU and, according to the Commission's own impact assessment, was expected to deliver substantial recurring savings for micro and small companies through reduced disclosure obligations. Several Member States — Germany and France in particular — used the new micro-entity option aggressively, allowing the smallest firms to file barely more than an abbreviated balance sheet.

In extractives, the CBCR regime produced the first systematic public dataset of project-level payments by European majors. Shell, BP, Total, ENI, Statoil (now Equinor), BHP, Rio Tinto and Glencore began publishing annual payments-to-governments reports listing royalties and taxes by country and by project. NGOs including Publish What You Pay and Resources for Development Consulting have used the data to flag discrepancies between corporate disclosures and government revenue figures, particularly in Nigeria, Angola and Iraq. The UK, even after Brexit, retained an equivalent regime, and Canada adopted a comparable law (ESTMA), creating a transatlantic transparency standard.

The directive's biggest indirect impact was opening the door for broader tax transparency. Once project-level CBCR was established as workable for extractives, the political argument that confidentiality protected legitimate business interests weakened. By 2016 the Commission had proposed public CBCR for all large multinationals on income tax — eventually adopted as Directive 2021/2101 after five years of Council deadlock broken by the Portuguese presidency. The 2013 directive remains the legal backbone onto which the EU's sustainability reporting (CSRD) and tax transparency rules have been bolted.

## Key questions answered

### Why do European oil companies publish what they pay to foreign governments?

Since financial year 2015, large EU-based or EU-listed extractive and logging companies have had to publish every payment above €100,000 made to any government, broken down by country and by project. The rule was deliberately modelled on the US Dodd-Frank Act and pushed by anti-corruption NGOs arguing that secrecy around oil and mining deals fuels the "resource curse" in producer countries. Industry lobbied for an exemption when host countries forbade disclosure, but the European Parliament refused.

### Why did EU accounting rules need to be rewritten in 2013?

The original directives dated from 1978 and 1983, before IFRS, the euro, and the single market as we know it existed. They had been amended more than a dozen times and were widely considered out of step with both modern accounting practice and SME realities. The recast bundled simplification for small firms with new transparency rules for multinationals — a left-right compromise that gave both sides a win.

### Do small businesses in Europe have to file the same accounts as large companies?

No. Since 2013 the EU has had four harmonised size categories — micro, small, medium and large — defined by balance sheet, turnover and headcount. Micro-entities can file drastically abbreviated accounts, and Member States are barred from requiring small companies to disclose more than a capped list of items. The aim was to cut paperwork costs for the millions of small firms that dominate the EU economy.

### How is this connected to the EU's later tax transparency push?

The 2013 directive established the legal and political precedent for forcing multinationals to publish data on a country-by-country basis. Once that principle was accepted for extractives, the Commission used the same legal instrument as the vehicle for further reforms: non-financial reporting in 2014, public tax CBCR for all large multinationals in 2021, and the full Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive in 2022. Each was technically an amendment to Directive 2013/34/EU.
