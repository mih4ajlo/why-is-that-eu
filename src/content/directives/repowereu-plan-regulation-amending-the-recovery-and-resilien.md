## What is it?

The Regulation amending the Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) — formally 2023/435/EU — transformed the EU's pandemic recovery fund into a vehicle for ending dependence on Russian fossil fuels. Adopted in February 2023, it required all 27 Member States to add a "REPowerEU chapter" to their national recovery plans, dedicating billions to energy efficiency, renewable energy, grid modernisation, and domestic supply diversification.

In practice, this meant that countries seeking access to the remaining €225 billion in RRF loans and grants had to commit to specific energy reforms — from permitting reform for renewables to methane emission reductions. The regulation also created a new €20 billion grant facility financed by the auctioning of Emissions Trading System allowances, bypassing the usual national contributions stalemate. It was the EU's most direct fiscal response to the energy crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine.

## Why was it introduced?

Russia's invasion of Ukraine on 24 February 2022 shattered the EU's long-standing assumption that energy could be decoupled from geopolitics. Within weeks, the European Commission — led by President Ursula von der Leyen and Energy Commissioner Kadri Simson — realised that the €400 billion the EU paid Russia for gas in 2021 was directly financing the war. On 8 March 2022, the Commission presented the REPowerEU communication, sketching a plan to cut Russian gas imports by two-thirds within a year. The problem was money.

The existing Recovery and Resilience Facility, created in February 2021 under the NextGenerationEU package, was already distributing €723.8 billion in grants and loans for post-pandemic recovery. But the original RFF Regulation (2021/241/EU) had no energy independence provisions — its "green transition" pillar was broad and voluntary. Commissioner for Economy Paolo Gentiloni realised that adding a new funding vehicle would take years and require new national contributions, something Germany and the 'Frugal Four' (Austria, Denmark, Netherlands, Sweden) would block. The structural tension was clear: the EU had a funding pipeline but the wrong rules, and a crisis that demanded immediate action.

The solution emerged from a coalition of the European Parliament's environmental committees and the Commission's energy and climate directorates. Lead MEPs like Siegfried Mureșan (EPP, Romania) and Eider Gardiazabal (S&D, Spain) pushed to fund REPowerEU through accelerated ETS allowance auctions — monetising carbon permits, which the Frugal states could not veto because it didn't require new national budget commitments. The compromise needed to hold: the ETS auction revenues would generate €20 billion for grants, distributed via a formula favouring coal-dependent Eastern Member States (Poland got €4.5 billion, Czechia €1.5 billion).

Timing was everything. The regulation was proposed in May 2022, negotiated through the summer, and adopted on 18 February 2023 — barely 12 months after the invasion. This speed was unprecedented for EU fiscal legislation. What broke the earlier deadlock over EU energy financing (which had stalled for years over sovereignty concerns) was the existential threat of a Russian gas cutoff. By summer 2022, Gazprom had already reduced flows via Nord Stream 1 to 20% capacity, and several Member States faced winter supply emergencies.

The regulation required Member States to request up to 7.7% of their RRF allocation for REPowerEU chapters, but with a critical rule: investments had to be additional to existing national climate spending, and at least 37% of each REPowerEU chapter had to fund cross-border projects and infrastructure — a provision pushed by Eastern Baltics who feared they'd otherwise get no interconnectors.

## Timeline

- **2021, February** — Recovery and Resilience Facility Regulation (2021/241/EU) adopted, creating €723.8 billion fund for post-pandemic recovery
- **2022, 24 February** — Russia invades Ukraine; gas prices surge to €335/MWh by March
- **2022, 8 March** — Commission presents REPowerEU communication, proposing to cut Russian gas imports by two-thirds within a year
- **2022, 18 May** — Commission formally proposes amending the RRF Regulation to add REPowerEU chapters
- **2022, 14 July** — European Parliament adopts its negotiating position (535 votes in favour, 92 against)
- **2022, 14 December** — Council and Parliament reach provisional agreement on the regulation
- **2023, 18 February** — Regulation 2023/435/EU published in Official Journal, enters into force
- **2023, 30 April** — Deadline for Member States to submit REPowerEU chapters to the Commission
- **2023–2024** — All 27 Member States submit chapters; first disbursements begin late 2023

## Impact and consequences

The regulation forced a dramatic reorientation of national spending plans. By January 2024, Member States had dedicated €275 billion to REPowerEU objectives — exceeding the original target. Germany committed €52 billion to hydrogen infrastructure and industrial decarbonisation; Poland redirected €24 billion from its original plan to coal mine closures and renewable zones; Italy allocated €18 billion to grid digitalisation and biomethane.

The most controversial consequence was permitting reform. The regulation required Member States to implement "fast-track" procedures for renewable projects — a provision that provoked legal clashes in France and Germany where local anti-wind groups challenged expedited approvals. The Commission's 2024 implementation report found that 14 Member States had missed their April 2023 submission deadline — but none lost funding because the regulation allowed for phased approvals.

Industry response was divided. SolarPower Europe and WindEurope strongly supported the new funding, while the European chemical industry lobby Cefic warned that accelerated permitting could weaken environmental impact assessments. Russia's state-owned Gazprom saw its European pipeline gas exports fall from 155 billion cubic metres in 2021 to 43 bcm in 2023 — a direct consequence of the investments enabled by this regulation.

The regulation also created a major administrative burden: Member States had to renegotiate their existing RRF agreements, a process that overwhelmed small national administrations like Malta's and Cyprus's. The European Court of Auditors reported in 2024 that the simultaneous operation of REPowerEU chapters and the original RFF created "confusing overlaps" in reporting requirements.

## Key questions answered

### How is the EU paying to end its reliance on Russian gas?

The regulation created a €20 billion grant fund financed by selling carbon allowances from the EU Emissions Trading System — essentially polluters paid for energy independence. This bypassed the usual need for national contributions, which the Frugal Four had previously blocked. Member States could also borrow from the remaining €225 billion in RRF loans.

### Did every EU country have to add new spending on green energy?

Yes. The regulation required all 27 Member States to submit a REPowerEU chapter to their existing recovery plan, and at least 37% of each chapter had to fund cross-border energy projects. Countries that failed to submit risked losing their allocated RRF funds. The Baltic states used this to secure funding for the synchronisation of their electricity grids with continental Europe, a project that previously had no dedicated EU budget line.

### Why did the permitting rules change for solar and wind?

The regulation included a provision requiring Member States to designate "renewables acceleration areas" with shortened approval timelines — down from years to months. This was a direct response to the Commission's finding that permitting bottlenecks were the single biggest barrier to scaling renewables, with the average European wind farm taking 5–8 years to get approval. The provision forced Germany, France, and Spain to rewrite planning laws.

### What happened to countries that couldn't spend the money fast enough?

The regulation required that RRF funds be committed by 2026 and spent by 2028 — a tight timeline that created absorption problems. Poland and Hungary, both slow to draw down original RRF funds due to rule-of-law disputes, faced the added complexity of renegotiating their plans with the Commission. The regulation allowed for grace periods but warned that unspent funds would be redistributed to faster-spending Member States — a threat that spurred Spain and Portugal to accelerate their renewable auctions.
