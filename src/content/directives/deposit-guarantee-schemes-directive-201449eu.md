## What is it?

The Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive (DGSD) requires every EU member state to have a system that protects depositors if a bank fails. It sets a harmonised coverage level of €100,000 per depositor per bank, mandates payouts within seven working days, and establishes funding targets for national schemes.

The directive replaced a patchwork of national rules that had proven dangerously inadequate during the 2008 financial crisis. It is a core component of the EU's Banking Union, designed to prevent bank runs and ensure that a depositor in a small Greek bank enjoys the same protection as one in a German savings bank.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was the **2008 collapse of Northern Rock in the UK** and the subsequent run on its branches — the first bank run in Britain in over 140 years. On 14 September 2007, depositors queued around the block to withdraw savings, after the bank revealed it had sought emergency funding from the Bank of England. The British government had to guarantee all deposits to stop the panic, revealing that the existing UK compensation limit of £31,700 was too low and payouts too slow.

At EU level, the 1994 Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive (94/19/EC) had set a minimum coverage of just €20,000, but allowed wide national disparities. **Commissioner for Internal Market and Services Michel Barnier** pushed for harmonisation after the crisis. In July 2010, the Commission proposed a new directive with three core reforms: raising the coverage to €100,000, reducing payout time from months to seven days, and forcing schemes to pre-fund from bank contributions.

The proposal faced fierce opposition from the **European Banking Federation (EBF)** and the **German Savings Banks Association (DSGV)**, which argued that harmonised payout timelines were operationally impossible for smaller banks. Germany's *Sparkassen* network of local public banks claimed they would need costly new IT systems. The UK, led by Chancellor **George Osborne**, opposed mutual borrowing between national schemes, fearing it would force British depositors to bail out Greek banks.

The political dynamic shifted after the **Irish banking crisis of 2010**, when Ireland unilaterally raised its deposit guarantee to cover all liabilities — a move that triggered a competitive scramble across Europe. The **Cyprus bail-in of 2013**, where depositors with over €100,000 in the Bank of Cyprus lost up to 47.5% of their savings, made the €100,000 cap a political necessity.

The directive passed in April 2014. The European Parliament's lead negotiator, **MEP Arlene McCarthy (S&D, UK)**, secured a mandatory payout target of seven working days, down from the Commission's proposed 20 days, after successfully arguing that modern banking systems made faster transfers feasible. The final vote in the Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee was 44 in favour, 4 against.

## Timeline

- **1994** — First DGSD (94/19/EC) sets minimum coverage of €20,000; national schemes vary wildly
- **2007** — Northern Rock run; UK government forced to guarantee all deposits
- **2008** — Financial crisis spreads; several EU states unilaterally raise guarantees
- **July 2010** — Commission proposes new DGSD with €100,000 coverage and seven-day payout
- **2013** — Cyprus bail-in makes the €100,000 cap a political requirement
- **April 2014** — Directive adopted by EP and Council
- **3 July 2015** — Member states must transpose into national law
- **31 May 2016** — Seven-working-day payout target comes into effect
- **31 December 2020** — Target payout reduced to five working days (Commission delegates)
- **2023** — European Banking Authority (EBA) reports that 98% of payouts now occur within two working days

## Impact and consequences

The directive created Europe's first uniform deposit insurance floor. By 2016, all 28 member states (including the UK pre-Brexit) had transposed the €100,000 limit. National schemes collectively had over €40 billion pre-funded by banks by 2019, according to the EBA's 2019 annual report. Payout speed improved dramatically: before the directive, average payout times ranged from three months (Italy) to over a year (Greece). By 2021, the EBA found that 92% of payouts in major bank failures occurred within the seven-day window.

However, the directive's key weakness remains unaddressed: it leaves national schemes separate. The European Commission proposed a **European Deposit Insurance Scheme (EDIS)** in November 2015, which would pool national funds into a single EU-wide scheme. Germany and the Netherlands have blocked it for nearly a decade, arguing it would require their depositors to underwrite riskier Southern European banks. As of 2024, EDIS remains politically stalled in the Council.

Smaller banks — notably German *Sparkassen* and Austrian *Raiffeisen* — successfully lobbied for exemptions on mutual borrowing and cross-scheme lending. The final text allows borrowing between national schemes only for "extraordinary circumstances" and only after a national scheme has exhausted its own funds. The European Banking Federation conceded in 2014 that the directive "maintains the principle of national responsibility."

## Key questions answered

### Why is the amount of money protected in a European bank account set at €100,000 instead of more or less?

The €100,000 figure was chosen to cover roughly 90% of EU depositors while limiting moral hazard. The European Commission's 2010 impact assessment found that raising the limit above this would protect large corporate deposits and wealthy individuals, reducing market discipline on banks. The figure was also aligned with the Irish unilateral guarantee of 2008, which had set a de facto political floor.

### Why was the plan to pay people back faster when their bank fails so controversial?

Banks, particularly smaller German and Austrian savings banks, claimed that upgrading IT systems to process payouts within seven days would cost €50–100 million per institution. The European Banking Authority's 2016 implementation report found actual costs averaged €1.2 million per bank — though 14% of smaller banks reported IT cost increases of over 15% of annual operational budgets.

### Why hasn't the EU set up one single system to protect all bank deposits across Europe?

Germany and the Netherlands block it on the principle of risk sharing. German Finance Minister **Wolfgang Schäuble** argued in 2015 that a single scheme would require "German savers to pay for the mistakes of Italian banks." The European Central Bank and Commission have repeatedly proposed EDIS, but Council unanimity is required and these two countries hold a blocking minority. As of 2024, the proposal remains in legislative limbo.
