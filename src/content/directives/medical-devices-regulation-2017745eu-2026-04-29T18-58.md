## What is it?

The Medical Devices Regulation (MDR), fully applied from May 2021, replaced two earlier directives (the Medical Devices Directive 93/42/EEC and the Active Implantable Medical Devices Directive 90/385/EEC) with a significantly stricter framework for placing medical devices on the EU market. It tightens clinical evidence requirements, strengthens the role of notified bodies (the private organisations that certify devices), and introduces a new Unique Device Identification (UDI) system for traceability.

The MDR also creates a central database (EUDAMED) for device registration, incident reporting, and market surveillance, and imposes new obligations on importers and distributors. It reclassifies many devices — for example, most software used in healthcare now requires notified body involvement where it previously did not. The regulation targets high-risk devices, including implants and class III devices, with the most stringent scrutiny.

## Why was it introduced?

The MDR is a direct consequence of the Poly Implant Prothèse (PIP) breast implant scandal, the single most damaging medical device safety crisis in modern European history. Between 2001 and 2010, French company PIP manufactured around 400,000 breast implants using industrial-grade silicone — not the medical-grade silicone they were certified for — to cut costs. The implants had an abnormally high rupture rate (the French drug safety agency ANSM later estimated 35-40% of devices had ruptured within 8-10 years, according to a 2012 EU Commission assessment). PIP’s CEO, Jean-Claude Mas, was sentenced to four years in prison in 2013 for aggravated fraud.

The shocking detail: PIP’s implants had been certified as safe under the existing Medical Devices Directive. The certification was performed by TÜV Rheinland, a German notified body that audited PIP’s quality management system but tested neither the raw materials nor the final product. TÜV Rheinland’s audits consisted largely of reviewing documents in PIP’s offices; they never independently verified the silicone used. When French authorities raided PIP in 2010, they found five different grades of silicone in storage, only one of which was medical-grade.

The scandal exposed four structural failures that made new legislation inevitable:

1. **Notified body conflicts of interest.** Notified bodies were paid by the manufacturers they certified, creating a commercial incentive to approve devices. The Commission’s 2012 impact assessment found that notified bodies rarely refused certification — refusal rates for some bodies were below 1%.

2. **No centralised oversight.** Device incidents were reported to national authorities, but there was no EU-wide database or coordination mechanism. A dangerous device could be withdrawn in one country while remaining on the market in others. The Commission later admitted that member states had no systematic way to share vigilance data.

3. **Weak clinical evidence requirements.** The 1993 directive allowed manufacturers to demonstrate “equivalence” to existing devices rather than conducting new clinical investigations. This created a chain of devices claiming equivalence back to devices approved decades earlier with minimal clinical data. The 2012 Commission report on the directive’s implementation found that 80% of high-risk devices reached the market without any clinical investigation data specific to that device.

4. **No traceability for patients.** When PIP’s fraud was discovered, hospitals and clinics across Europe had no way to identify which patients had received the faulty implants. Patients were left to self-report to national hotlines.

The political coalition for reform was unusually broad. The European Parliament’s Environment and Public Health Committee (ENVI), led by rapporteur Dagmar Roth-Behrendt (S&D, Germany), pushed for the strongest possible measures — including a pre-market authorisation system for high-risk devices, similar to the US FDA’s process. The Parliament voted in favour of pre-market authorisation in its first reading in 2013.

The Council, backed by the medical device industry (led by MedTech Europe, the industry association, and major companies including Johnson & Johnson, Siemens Healthineers, and Medtronic), fiercely opposed this. Industry warned the reform would “destroy Europe’s medtech ecosystem,” arguing it would raise development costs by 30-40% and delay patient access to innovations. The German government, home to the largest medtech sector in Europe (€34 billion in revenue in 2018, according to the German medical technology association BVMed), was the most vocal opponent of pre-market authorisation.

The final compromise — reached in trilogue negotiations in 2016 — replaced pre-market authorisation with stricter notified body oversight, including unannounced factory inspections and mandatory scrutiny of high-risk devices by a new expert panel. The five-year legislative process was one of the most intensely lobbied in EU health law, with MedTech Europe recording over 1,000 meetings with MEPs and Commission officials between 2012 and 2016.

The timing was driven by the 2014-2019 Commission under Jean-Claude Juncker, which made patient safety a headline priority. Health Commissioner Vytenis Andriukaitis (2014-2019) personally championed the regulation, using his visits to national capitals to pressure reluctant member states. A pivotal moment came in 2015 when the EMA found that metal-on-metal hip implants — another device approved under the old directives — had failure rates of up to 15% within five years, adding further momentum to the reform.

## Timeline

- **2010** — French authorities raid PIP’s factory; CEO Jean-Claude Mas arrested
- **2011** — EU Commission asks the Scientific Committee on Emerging and Newly Identified Health Risks (SCENIHR) for an urgent assessment; the committee recommends stricter pre-market scrutiny
- **2012** — Commission publishes the impact assessment and legislative proposal; TÜV Rheinland faces multiple lawsuits from PIP patients
- **2013** — European Parliament adopts first reading with pre-market authorisation; Jean-Claude Mas sentenced to 4 years in prison
- **2014** — Council adopts its position, rejecting pre-market authorisation; trilogue negotiations begin
- **2016** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council; the text is published in the Official Journal
- **2017** — Regulation 2017/745/EU enters into force on 25 May, with a transition period
- **2021** — Date of application (originally May 2020, delayed by one year due to the COVID-19 pandemic)
- **2024** — EU Commission notes that many smaller manufacturers have struggled with MDR compliance; some device types face market shortages

## Impact and consequences

The MDR’s most immediate consequence was a sharp reduction in notified body capacity. As of 2025, only 38 notified bodies have been designated under the MDR, down from around 80 under the old directives. The remaining bodies have stricter accreditation requirements, including mandatory unannounced audits and the hiring of clinical specialists. This has created bottlenecks: the Commission’s 2024 progress report estimated that average certification times for class III devices increased from 12-18 months under the old directive to 24-36 months under the MDR.

Industry consolidation followed. Small and medium-sized medical device manufacturers — which represent 95% of the sector’s 27,000 companies in Europe, according to MedTech Europe — have been hit hardest. Some smaller firms have withdrawn products from the EU market rather than bear the compliance cost. The Commission’s 2023 survey of manufacturers found that 30% of SME device makers reported they had discontinued at least one product line due to MDR costs, with average compliance costs per device rising from €50,000-100,000 under the old directive to €200,000-400,000 under the MDR.

Patient groups, including the European Patients’ Forum and BEUC (the European Consumer Organisation), have generally welcomed the regulation but expressed concern about device shortages. In 2023 and 2024, several member states (including France, Germany, and the Netherlands) reported shortages of specific medical devices — particularly paediatric products and orphan devices — where manufacturer consolidation left only one or two suppliers.

The MDR also had a significant impact outside Europe. Non-EU manufacturers account for approximately 40% of medical devices on the EU market, according to MedTech Europe data. US companies, which dominate the global medical device market, have had to adapt their quality systems to meet EU requirements, and some have chosen to launch new devices in the US before the EU to avoid MDR delays.

## Key questions answered

### Why were breast implants with industrial silicone allowed on the EU market for nearly a decade?

Under the old Medical Devices Directive (93/42/EEC), notified bodies — private companies hired and paid by device manufacturers — were responsible for certification. They audited quality management systems and documents, but tested neither raw materials nor final products. PIP’s notified body, TÜV Rheinland, conducted all its audits at PIP’s offices and never independently verified the silicone used. No EU system existed to cross-check or challenge such certifications until the MDR introduced mandatory unannounced factory inspections and material testing for high-risk devices.

### Does the MDR make it harder for new medical devices to reach patients?

Yes — by design. The MDR requires clinical investigations for high-risk devices rather than allowing “equivalence” claims to earlier devices. Certification times have roughly doubled, from 12-18 months to 24-36 months, and compliance costs have risen significantly. The European Commission has acknowledged that some device types — especially for rare diseases — are now less commercially viable. However, the MDR also includes a “clinical evaluation consultation procedure” for the highest-risk devices, which forces manufacturers to produce robust clinical data before market entry.

### Can patients with faulty medical devices now be traced?

The MDR introduces a Unique Device Identification (UDI) system that requires each device — and each implantable device implant — to carry a unique identifier. By 2027, all implantable devices must be identifiable through EUDAMED, the centralised database, allowing authorities to trace which patients received which specific device. This directly addresses the PIP scandal, where thousands of patients could not be identified because no traceability system existed.

### Are notified bodies still paid by the manufacturers they certify?

Notified bodies still receive fees from manufacturers, but the MDR imposes strict conflicts of interest rules. Notified bodies must now have separate audit teams, conduct unannounced factory inspections at least once every 12 months, and cannot have any financial or organisational link to the manufacturers they certify. The European Commission can also suspend or withdraw a notified body’s designation if it fails to meet requirements.
