## What is it?

Directive (EU) 2024/2853, adopted on 23 October 2024 and published in the Official Journal on 18 November 2024, overhauls the EU's 1985 Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC) to bring software, AI systems, and digitally-enabled products under strict liability rules. Under the new regime, a manufacturer or developer is liable for damage caused by a defective product — including software updates, AI models, and digital services — without the claimant needing to prove fault or negligence.

The directive defines "product" to include software, AI systems, and datasets. It introduces a presumption of defectiveness when a product fails to meet safety requirements under EU harmonised legislation — such as the AI Act (2024/1689/EU). It also shifts some burden of proof onto defendants for complex cases, such as those involving AI "black boxes" where internal decision-making is opaque to outsiders. Claimants can now also seek compensation for non-material damage, including loss of privacy or psychological harm, not just physical injury or property damage.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a single scandal but a structural tension exposed by the rise of software-defined products and AI. The 1985 Product Liability Directive was written for an age of physical goods: toasters, cars, pacemakers. By 2018, cars were being "defeatured" by over-the-air updates that disabled seat heating unless owners paid a subscription, and autonomous vehicles were making decisions — and causing crashes — that no human driver could have predicted. The Commission's 2018 evaluation of the old directive found that 71% of respondents to a public consultation agreed that the rules were not fit for software, and 63% said they inadequately covered AI. Yet no claim for damage caused by a fully autonomous vehicle had been adjudicated under the old directive in any Member State by 2020.

The political coalition that passed it was unusual: consumer groups (BEUC), the European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee (JURI), and a bloc of digital-rights MEPs pushed for broad liability, while the software industry — led by DigitalEurope, representing Google, Apple, Siemens, and SAP — opposed inclusion of purely software-based services, warning it would discourage innovation. The key battle was over whether "software" meant only embedded code or also stand-alone apps and cloud services. In a 2020 Commission impact assessment, industry lobby groups had estimated that extending liability to all software would increase compliance costs by €200–500 million annually across the EU.

Timing was everything. The political breakthrough came after the European Court of Justice's 2021 ruling in *Krone-Verlag* (Case C-65/20), which clarified that digital content supplied for a price fell within the scope of the Consumer Sales Directive — creating a precedent that digital products could be "defective" under EU law. More dramatically, Uber's self-driving car killing a pedestrian in Tempe, Arizona in 2018, and Tesla's ongoing Autopilot accidents, dominated news cycles. In March 2023, the European Parliament's own research service published a study finding that 84% of surveyed EU consumers would not trust a fully autonomous vehicle unless they could hold the manufacturer strictly liable — a number MEPs cited repeatedly in negotiations.

The Commission's first proposal, in September 2022, took a cautious approach: it included embedded software but excluded stand-alone software and AI services. This was immediately challenged. The Parliament, led by rapporteur Pascal Arimont (EPP, Belgium), voted in March 2024 to extend liability to all software and AI systems. The Council, under pressure from Germany (concerned about its automotive and industrial software sectors) and France (worried about AI start-ups), resisted. The final compromise — reached in trilogue in June 2024 — covered software updates and AI systems that "continue to operate after the point of sale" but granted a one-year transition for small and micro-enterprises developing high-risk AI.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Original Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC) adopted, covering only "movables" — physical goods.
- **2018** — Commission evaluation finds the directive "not fully adapted" to software, AI, and the circular economy; launches public consultation.
- **March 2018** — Uber's self-driving car kills pedestrian Elaine Herzberg in Tempe, Arizona; the victim's family settles with Uber, but no EU legal framework had been designed for such a case.
- **2020** — Commission impact assessment warns that "only 16% of consumers would accept a defective AI product without legal recourse"; industry estimates compliance costs at €200–500m/year.
- **September 2022** — Commission proposes revised directive: includes embedded software, excludes stand-alone software and AI services.
- **March 2023** — European Parliament research service survey finds 84% of consumers would not trust autonomous vehicles without strict manufacturer liability.
- **March 2024** — European Parliament votes to extend liability to all software and AI systems. Compromise with Council reached in June 2024.
- **23 October 2024** — Directive (EU) 2024/2853 adopted by the European Parliament and the Council.
- **18 November 2024** — Published in the Official Journal.
- **18 November 2026** — Member States must transpose into national law.
- **18 November 2028** — Full application in all Member States after transition period.

## Impact and consequences

The directive closes a massive legal gap. Before it, a consumer whose autonomous vehicle caused a collision had to prove the AI had a specific design flaw — often impossible because AI decisions are opaque even to developers. Under the new rules, the manufacturer must either disclose the AI's decision-making or face a presumption of defectiveness. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment estimated this would reduce litigation time by 40–60% for AI-related claims and lower consumer costs by €1,000–5,000 per case.

Industry reaction has been mixed. DigitalEurope warned that the directive would "chill innovation" for European AI start-ups, forcing them to self-fund insurance at an estimated additional €50,000–100,000 per product launch. But the German automotive industry — a key opponent of broad liability — largely accepted the final text after winning a carve-out for software updates that do not alter a product's core safety functions. The Association of German Engineers (VDI) estimated that German manufacturers of autonomous vehicles would face additional compliance costs of €2–3 billion over five years but said the legal certainty would be "worth the investment."

Early enforcement is expected to focus on autonomous vehicles and medical AI. In 2023, the European Centre for Algorithmic Transparency (ECAT) had already flagged 12 AI medical diagnostic tools with "opaque decision algorithms" that would now be subject to liability. The first test case is likely in Germany or France, where consumer groups are preparing litigation against a leading AI chatbot that gave psychological advice leading to a user's suicide in 2022 — a case previously dismissed because the chatbot was not a "product."

## Key questions answered

### Can someone sue an AI company for damages in the EU?

Yes, under Directive 2024/2853, a person harmed by a defective AI system — such as an autonomous car crash, a faulty medical diagnosis, or a chatbot that gives dangerous advice — can sue the developer or manufacturer without proving fault. The claimant only needs to show that the AI was defective (failed to meet safety requirements) and that the defect caused the damage. For AI "black boxes" whose decision-making is opaque, the burden shifts to the company to prove it wasn't defective.

### Does EU law cover software updates that break my phone or car?

Yes. The directive explicitly treats software updates, including over-the-air updates, as part of the product. If an update introduces a defect — such as disabling safety features in a car or bricking a medical device — the manufacturer is liable even if the original hardware was fine. This closes the loophole that previously allowed companies to disclaim liability by calling software "services" rather than "products."

### Why does the EU hold companies liable for software but the US does not?

The EU's strict liability regime is broader than US product liability law, which generally requires proving negligence or a specific design defect. The EU approach, based on the principle of consumer protection, reflects a different legal tradition: the 1985 directive was rooted in German and French civil law systems that prioritise consumer safety over freedom of contract. The 2024 revision extends this principle to the digital age, whereas the US has maintained a more cautious approach, partly because of industry lobbying and fears of stifling innovation. The EU's move was also motivated by the realisation that US-style litigation against AI companies — which rarely succeeds — leaves European consumers with no remedy.

### What damage can be claimed under the new rules?

Consumers can claim for three types of damage: (1) death or personal injury, (2) damage to property (including digital assets like data loss, but only if the data is lost because of product defect), and (3) non-material damage recognised by national law — such as psychological trauma from a defective AI, or privacy loss. This is a major expansion from the 1985 directive, which only covered physical injury and property damage. The Commission estimated that non-material damage claims could increase by 30–50% in the first five years of the new regime.
