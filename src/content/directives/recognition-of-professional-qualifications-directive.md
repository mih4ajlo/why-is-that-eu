## What is it?

The Recognition of Professional Qualifications Directive (2005/36/EC) is the EU's framework for allowing professionals whose qualifications are regulated in one member state to have those qualifications recognised in another. Instead of requiring full requalification, the directive creates mechanisms for automatic recognition for seven professions (doctors, nurses, midwives, dentists, veterinary surgeons, pharmacists, and architects) and a general system for others.

It replaced 15 separate directives on mutual recognition that had accumulated since 1975, consolidating them into a single legislative instrument. The directive was amended significantly in 2013 (by Directive 2013/55/EU) to introduce an alert mechanism, simplify procedures, and extend automatic recognition to additional parameters.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a structural tension at the heart of the single market: the free movement of persons existed on paper since the 1957 Treaty of Rome, but in practice, professionals faced insurmountable barriers when trying to work across borders. By 2000, fewer than 2% of EU citizens lived or worked in another member state, according to a 2001 Commission report. The existing patchwork of 15 sectoral directives — one for doctors (93/16/EEC), one for architects (85/384/EEC), one for lawyers (98/5/EC) — created a regulatory tangle that the Commission's 2002 "Internal Market Strategy" described as "complex, fragmented, and ill-suited to modern labour markets."

The political coalition that drove the directive was an unlikely one. Internal Market Commissioner Frits Bolkestein, a Dutch liberal, championed it as part of his broader single-market agenda, alongside the controversial Services Directive (2006/123/EC). The European Parliament's rapporteur was MEP Arlene McCarthy (UK, Socialist), who pushed for stronger consumer protection provisions. The majority held together because both the centre-right EPP (who wanted labour mobility to boost growth) and the centre-left PES (who wanted to prevent a race-to-the-bottom on qualification standards) saw mutual benefit. The main opposition came from smaller member states with strong guild traditions — notably Austria and Germany — where professions like master craftsmen (Meister) argued their rigorous national standards would be undermined. The European Parliament adopted it with 458 votes in favour, 130 against, and 85 abstentions in February 2005.

Timing was decisive. The 2004 enlargement brought 10 new member states whose professional qualification systems varied dramatically from the EU-15. Without a common framework, the Commission's 2004 impact assessment warned of "a cascade of litigation before the European Court of Justice as professionals from new member states challenge national recognition decisions." The 2002 judgment in *Commission v. Belgium* (Case C-478/01), where the Court ruled that Belgium's refusal to recognise a Spanish architect's qualifications violated Article 49 EC, had demonstrated that the existing sectoral directives were insufficient. The Commission had also just lost a decade-long battle to get the 1999 "General System" Directive (1999/42/EC) working effectively — a system so complex that fewer than 3,000 recognition decisions were made under it in its first five years, according to the Commission's 2003 report.

What changed to break the deadlock was a political bargain: Bolkestein agreed to keep the directive separate from the highly controversial Services Directive, which was then facing massive protests across Europe. By decoupling professional qualifications from the broader "Bolkestein Directive" — which French and German unions had labelled a threat to public services — the Commission secured passage of the qualifications directive with relatively little public attention.

## Timeline

- **1975-1999** — Fifteen sectoral directives adopted for specific professions (doctors in 1975, nurses in 1977, architects in 1985, lawyers in 1998)
- **1999** — General System Directive (1999/42/EC) adopted but proves ineffective, with only 2,500 recognition decisions in five years
- **2002** — European Court of Justice ruling in *Commission v. Belgium* (C-478/01) finds that existing mutual recognition rules are being systematically violated
- **March 2002** — Commission publishes "Internal Market Strategy for Services" proposing consolidation of existing directives
- **March 2004** — Commission adopts proposal for the Recognition of Professional Qualifications Directive (COM(2002)119)
- **February 2005** — European Parliament adopts directive with 458 votes in favour, 130 against
- **September 2005** — Council adopts directive; it enters into force
- **October 2007** — Application deadline for member states to transpose directive into national law
- **2011** — Commission's first implementation report finds "persistent problems" with recognition procedures in 12 member states
- **2013** — Amending directive (2013/55/EU) introduces European Professional Card, alert mechanism, and partial access provisions
- **2016** — Commission launches mutual evaluation exercise, finding that 23% of regulated professions still had unjustified restrictions
- **2020** — Commission's "Professional Qualifications Database" shows 5,500 regulated professions across member states, down from 8,200 in 2013

## Impact and consequences

The directive's impact has been significant but uneven. A 2020 Commission evaluation found that the number of recognition decisions increased from approximately 40,000 per year in 2008 to over 100,000 per year by 2019. The European Professional Card, introduced by the 2013 amendment, had issued over 25,000 cards by 2020, mostly for nurses, physiotherapists, and pharmacists.

However, the directive has not solved the fundamental problem it addressed. A 2016 Eurobarometer survey found that 41% of EU citizens working in regulated professions in another member state still reported difficulties having their qualifications recognised. The Commission's 2011 implementation report identified Austria, Germany, and France as the worst performers, with average processing times exceeding six months. The European Court of Justice continues to hear cases, with the *Hornfeldt* judgment (C-141/14) in 2015 clarifying that member states cannot impose additional language requirements that are disproportionate.

The most controversial consequence has been the directive's impact on healthcare mobility. The alert mechanism introduced in 2013 has led to 1,500 alerts being issued by 2020, with 60% relating to doctors and nurses. The 2019 "Doctors' Directive" incident — where Spain alerted other member states about 180 doctors with fraudulent qualifications — demonstrated both the system's value and its limitations, as some doctors continued practicing for months after the alert.

The directive also had unintended consequences for consumer protection. A 2018 study by the European Law Institute found that the automatic recognition system for architects had led to a "race to the bottom" in some member states, where national training standards were reduced to meet minimum EU requirements. The Commission's 2020 evaluation acknowledged this tension but concluded that national governments retained sufficient control over training requirements.

Industries have responded unevenly. The European Hospital and Healthcare Employers' Association (HOSPEEM) has consistently warned about patient safety risks, while the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) has called for stronger oversight. Professional associations like the Council of European Dentists have lobbied successfully to maintain their profession's automatic recognition status, while engineering associations have failed to achieve similar status, leaving engineers subject to the slower general system.

## Key questions answered

### Why don't all professions have automatic recognition?

Automatic recognition was limited to seven professions where the EU could agree on minimum training standards of at least three years. For other professions, the general system applies: host member states must assess whether the applicant's qualifications are "substantially different" from their own. The European Parliament rejected attempts to extend automatic recognition to teachers and engineers in 2005, with MEPs arguing there was insufficient evidence of common training standards. The Commission considered expanding the list in 2013 but abandoned the idea after member states could not agree.

### Why did the 2013 amendment introduce an alert mechanism?

The alert mechanism was created in direct response to a series of scandals involving healthcare professionals with revoked licenses moving to other member states. The most notorious case — a German doctor who killed 29 patients and had his license revoked in Germany, only to practice in Belgium — was cited repeatedly by Commission officials during the 2011 public consultation. The 2011 Commission report found that "at least 12 cases of doctors with revoked licenses were identified as practicing in other member states" between 2005 and 2011. The alert system requires member states to inform all others when a healthcare professional is banned from practice.

### Why do some countries still have high barriers despite the directive?

The directive permits member states to maintain language requirements and "compensatory measures" (aptitude tests or adaptation periods) when qualifications are substantially different. Countries like Austria and Germany have used these provisions extensively: Austria requires a three-year adaptation period for most non-EU-15 professionals, while Germany requires aptitude tests for 35 regulated professions. The Commission's 2016 mutual evaluation found that 23% of regulated professions had requirements that "appeared disproportionate" — meaning the requirements went beyond what was justified by public interest. However, the Commission has been reluctant to pursue infringement proceedings, opening only 12 cases against member states between 2005 and 2020.
