## What is it?

The Nature Restoration Regulation (EU 2024/1991) is a legally binding framework that compels all 27 EU member states to implement restoration measures on at least 20% of their degraded terrestrial and marine areas by 2030. By 2050, the regulation mandates full recovery of all ecosystems in need of restoration. It is the first comprehensive EU law to set enforceable targets for ecosystem restoration, not just protection of existing habitats.

The regulation covers a broad range of ecosystems: wetlands, grasslands, forests, rivers, lakes, coastal habitats, and urban green spaces. Specific targets include reversing pollinator decline by 2030, restoring drained peatlands (at least 30% rewetted by 2030 and 50% by 2050), planting at least 3 billion additional trees by 2030, and removing barriers from at least 25,000 km of rivers to restore free-flowing water. Member states must submit national restoration plans by September 2026 detailing how they will meet these targets.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation arose from a stark contradiction: the EU had spent decades protecting biodiversity through the Birds Directive (2009/147/EC) and Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC), yet the European Environment Agency’s 2020 *State of Nature in the EU* report showed that only 15% of protected habitats and 27% of protected species were in good condition. This revealed that protection alone was insufficient—active restoration was needed.

The trigger was political, not scientific. In May 2020, the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen published the **EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030**, a flagship of the European Green Deal, which committed to legally binding restoration targets. But the strategy drew immediate opposition. The European People’s Party (EPP)—the largest group in the European Parliament—blocked the proposal in June 2023, claiming it would harm food production and burden farmers. Farmers’ associations, led by **Copa-Cogeca**, warned of "significant economic costs" and job losses in rural areas. The EPP’s lead negotiator, **Christophe Hansen** (Luxembourg), argued the targets were "unrealistic and unaffordable" without clear cost-benefit analysis.

The coalition that eventually passed it was fragile. The **Socialists & Democrats (S&D)**, **Renew Europe**, **The Greens/EFA**, and **The Left** formed a majority in the Parliament, helped by defections from some EPP MEPs. In the Council, a blocking minority led by **Finland, Italy, the Netherlands, and Poland** collapsed after Germany—driven by the **Green Party-led Environment Ministry under Steffi Lemke**—switched from abstention to support in November 2023, citing urgency of climate adaptation. The Council adopted its position on June 17, 2024, with 21 member states voting in favor, 4 against (Finland, Italy, Hungary, Sweden), and 2 abstentions (Austria, Bulgaria).

The final version included major concessions: a "brake" clause allowing member states to suspend restoration targets if agricultural production is threatened, and a delay in the entry into force for specific peatland targets until 2026. The Commission’s impact assessment (2022) estimated the regulation would cost €8 billion annually in implementation but generate €1.5–3 trillion in ecosystem service benefits over 30 years—a ratio that critics called "fictional" but which the Parliament's own research service defended.

What changed the deadlock was the **2023 summer of extreme weather**—record heatwaves in southern Europe, wildfires in Greece and Portugal, and catastrophic floods in Slovenia and Germany—which swung public opinion. A Eurobarometer survey in September 2023 found 79% of EU citizens supported legally binding restoration targets. The European Commission President von der Leyen, facing a re-election bid to lead the next Commission, made the law a personal priority in her 2024 State of the European Union speech, framing restoration as "not luxury but survival."

## Impact and consequences

The regulation faces severe implementation challenges. **Finland** and **Sweden**, which voted against it, have warned they may challenge the law at the European Court of Justice, arguing that restoration on their peatland-rich terrains would conflict with renewable energy expansion (bioenergy crops). **Italy’s** national restoration plan, leaked in draft form, proposes meeting targets almost entirely through existing protected areas—a method the Commission has said would violate the law’s requirement to expand restoration beyond current Natura 2000 sites.

Industry response has been mixed. **Forestry associations** across Central Europe, particularly **Austria’s Landwirtschaftskammer**, have lobbied national governments to request the "brake" clause immediately after the 2026 deadline. **Fertilizer producers** (e.g., **Yara International**, **BASF**) have warned that peatland rewetting will reduce agricultural land and increase fertilizer demand elsewhere. Conversely, **construction companies** like **Saint-Gobain** and **Heidelberg Materials** have welcomed urban greening targets as a new market for green infrastructure.

The expected enforcement mechanism is significant: the Commission can impose infraction fines (€1,000–7,000 per hectare per year of non-restored area, calibrated to member state GDP). The first test will be **Poland**, which has the highest share of degraded ecosystems in the EU according to the EEA’s 2022 reporting, and which has already missed earlier environmental targets under the Water Framework Directive.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU now require countries to actively restore nature, not just protect it?

Yes. The regulation shifts EU biodiversity policy from passive protection (prohibiting destruction of existing habitats) to active restoration (requiring member states to bring degraded areas back to healthy condition). This is a fundamental departure from the Birds and Habitats Directives, which only required member states to prevent further damage. Under the new law, a country cannot simply maintain the status quo—it must improve the health of its ecosystems measurably.

### Can a country stop restoration if it threatens food production?

The regulation includes a "brake" clause (Article 11) that allows a member state to suspend specific restoration targets—particularly agricultural ecosystem targets like rewetting drained peatlands—if the Commission declares that implementation would reduce agricultural land availability by more than 5% at the national level. However, this brake is temporary and must be accompanied by an alternative plan. Environmental NGOs like **WWF Europe** have warned the brake could become a permanent loophole; agricultural associations have demanded its activation immediately after the 2026 national plans are submitted.

### How does the EU enforce nature restoration across 27 countries with very different ecologies?

The regulation sets EU-wide targets but allows each member state to design its own restoration plan, tailored to its specific ecosystems (e.g., Finland focuses on boreal forests and peatlands; Greece on Mediterranean shrublands and coastal wetlands). The Commission reviews plans for "sufficiency" and can request revisions. If a member state fails to achieve its targets, the Commission can launch infringement proceedings at the European Court of Justice, with potential fines. The key challenge—already visible in the first year—is that legal targets for 2030 are still years away, making detection of non-compliance difficult until 2029–2030.

### Why did farmers and their political allies oppose this law so fiercely?

Agricultural associations, led by **Copa-Cogeca** and **Farm Europe**, argued that restoration—particularly rewetting drained peatlands for agriculture—would reduce the land area available for food production by up to 10% in some member states (e.g., the Netherlands, Ireland). They claimed this would increase food imports and harm EU food sovereignty. The **European People's Party** made this the core of their opposition in the Parliament, voting unanimously to reject the bill in June 2023. However, the compromise included the agricultural brake and a delay in peatland targets, which failed to fully satisfy farm groups but won enough support from member states like Germany and Spain where farmers were already receiving subsidies for "greening" measures under the Common Agricultural Policy.
