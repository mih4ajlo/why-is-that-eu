## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2022/612, adopted on 6 April 2022, is the fourth major piece of EU legislation on mobile roaming since 2007. It permanently prohibits retail roaming surcharges inside the European Economic Area (EEA) – meaning EU citizens can use their mobile phone data, calls, and texts abroad without paying anything extra beyond their domestic plan. In short, it makes “Roam Like at Home” (RLAH) a permanent right rather than a temporary measure set to expire.

The regulation also caps the wholesale prices that domestic operators pay to foreign networks when their customers roam. These caps – €2 per GB of data in 2022, gradually falling to €1 per GB by 2027 – are designed to prevent the permanent RLAH rule from bankrupting smaller operators or encouraging abuse. To protect against permanent loss of revenue, the regulation includes a “fair use policy” (restricting unlimited, indefinite roaming) and a “sustainability mechanism” that allows operators to seek temporary permission to reintroduce surcharges if they can prove their costs exceed their roaming revenue by a damaging margin.

## Why was it introduced?

The road to permanent RLAH began with a simple outrage: despite the EU having a legal commitment to a single market for telecoms, roaming prices in the early 2000s were extortionate. A British tourist calling home from Spain might pay €1.50 per minute; downloading 1 MB of data cost around €5. In 2005, the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) documented that roaming charges were up to six times higher than the cost of termination on the home network. Pressure mounted as mobile phone adoption soared and cross-border travel increased after EU enlargement.

Commissioner Neelie Kroes, who took charge of the Digital Agenda portfolio in 2010, made ending roaming surcharges a personal mission. In a 2011 speech to the Mobile World Congress in Barcelona, she lambasted operators for failing to lower prices voluntarily, warning that regulation was coming. The Commission’s 2011 impact assessment found that wholesale roaming rates had fallen by only about 20% between 2007 and 2010, far slower than domestic prices. Kroes proposed a cap on retail roaming in 2011, but the telecom industry – led by the European Telecommunications Network Operators’ Association (ETNO), whose members included Deutsche Telekom, Orange, Telefónica, and Vodafone – lobbied fiercely. They argued that roaming accounted for a tiny share of revenue and that regulation would distort investment. However, in a rare display of consumer-friendly unity, the European Parliament strongly backed Kroes, pushing for an end to all roaming surcharges by 2015.

The first concrete breakthrough came under the “Connected Continent” package proposed by Kroes’ successor, Günther Oettinger, in 2013. The package stalled over net neutrality provisions, but a deal on roaming was struck in 2015 between the Commission, Parliament, and Council. Under the 2015 regulation (2015/2120), retail surcharges were abolished starting 15 June 2017, with a temporary “Roam Like at Home” period initially set to expire in June 2022. The deal included the fair use policy and a sustainability clause, inserted at the insistence of Greece, which feared an influx of tourists would overwhelm its networks.

The timing of the 2022 recast was driven by the looming expiry. The Commission, now under Ursula von der Leyen and Commissioner for Internal Market Thierry Breton, wanted to make RLAH permanent to avoid a cliff edge. Telecom operators, still unhappy, argued that the 2017 wholesale caps had allowed certain operators – especially smaller “mobile virtual network operators” (MVNOs) from Eastern Europe – to profit from arbitrage: they would sell cheap domestic plans and allow unlimited roaming, undercutting incumbents. ETNO warned of “unsustainable cost burdens” and demanded higher wholesale caps. The Parliament, led by rapporteur Angelika Niebler (EPP, Germany), pushed for lower wholesale caps to protect consumers. The final compromise, adopted in April 2022, cut wholesale data caps significantly – from €6 per GB in 2015 to €2 per GB in 2022 – and retained the sustainability mechanism. The vote in Parliament was 581 in favour, 2 against, with 5 abstentions, reflecting an overwhelming consensus that roaming surcharges were a relic of a fragmented market.

## Timeline

- **2007** – First Roaming Regulation (717/2007) introduces the “Eurotariff”, capping retail voice and SMS charges.
- **2009** – Second regulation sharpens caps and extends them to data.
- **2011** – Commissioner Neelie Kroes threatens to ban roaming surcharges entirely; industry resists.
- **2013** – “Connected Continent” legislative proposal includes roaming abolition, but stalls over net neutrality.
- **2015** – Regulation (2015/2120) sets a hard deadline of 15 June 2017 for removing retail surcharges, with temporary RLAH and a sustainability clause.
- **June 2017** – RLAH comes into effect; consumers stop paying roaming fees across the EEA.
- **2020** – Commission begins review of the 2015 regulation; conducts a public consultation (over 20,000 responses, mostly from consumers demanding permanence).
- **Early 2022** – Commission proposes recast; Parliament and Council agree.
- **6 April 2022** – Regulation (2022/612) adopted.
- **1 July 2022** – Regulation applies; RLAH becomes permanent.

## Impact and consequences

The impact has been dramatic. According to the Commission’s 2022 evaluation report, data roaming usage exploded: in the first year after the 2017 RLAH launch, data roaming consumption increased tenfold, while average retail data prices fell by over 90% from 2015 levels. The 2022 regulation locked in those gains. Large operators like Vodafone and Orange initially reported revenue losses of 2–3%, but those were largely offset by increased domestic usage and higher data consumption overall. Smaller operators, particularly in low-cost markets (e.g., Baltic states, Poland), faced the greatest strain from inbound roaming by tourists and from “permanent roamers” (people who use a foreign SIM primarily). The fair use policy helped: operators can restrict roaming if a subscriber spends more than 4 consecutive months abroad or uses more than double their domestic volume.

The sustainability mechanism has been invoked rarely. In 2018, several German operators applied but were rejected by the national regulator. In 2021, a Romanian operator (Digi) was allowed to reintroduce a €2 surcharge after demonstrating that its wholesale costs exceeded roaming revenue by 15% – but the COVID-19 travel collapse made this temporary. The 2022 regulation tightens the sustainability threshold, requiring a cost-revenue gap of at least 3% over a rolling year.

Industry response has been mixed. ETNO continues to argue that wholesale caps are set too low, claiming they distort investment in 5G infrastructure. Consumer groups like BEUC have praised the regulation but warn that the fair use policy can be opaque and that some operators still apply de facto surcharges to certain plans (e.g., very cheap data-only SIMs). The Commission has launched infringement proceedings against several member states for not enforcing the rules properly, including against Luxembourg in 2022 for failing to ensure Danske Bank employees could roam at domestic rates while working across borders.

## Key questions answered

### Can I still be charged extra for using my phone abroad in the EU?

No, as long as you are within the EU/EEA. Regulation (EU) 2022/612 prohibits any retail surcharge for roaming on a regular mobile plan. This means your domestic minute, text, and data allowances apply exactly as at home. However, there is a “fair use policy”: if you roam for long periods (typically over 4 months) or consume far more than your domestic allowance, your operator may charge a small usage fee (currently capped at €2 per GB of data, falling to €1 by 2027).

### What happens if I travel outside the EU? Are those countries covered?

Outside the EU/EEA, EU roaming rules do not apply, and roaming charges can still be high. However, some non-EU countries (e.g., Switzerland, Andorra, San Marino) have bilateral agreements that effectively extend RLAH. In other countries, your domestic operator sets its own rates. The regulation does encourage operators to offer affordable “roaming pass” options for non-EU travel, but they are not required to.

### Why do some cheap mobile plans not include free roaming?

The regulation applies to all “public mobile communications networks”, but operators can still design their domestic plans in a way that minimises roaming risk. For very cheap, data-only SIMs (e.g., €5/month for 1 GB), the fair use policy may be applied from day one, effectively excluding them from free roaming. The Commission’s 2022 impact assessment noted that about 5% of plans on the market do not include free roaming due to such design. If an operator uses a “la carte” pricing (e.g., pay-as-you-go without a fixed allowance), they must still offer a base roaming allowance equal to the domestic value of the same amount of money.

### Can my operator ever bring back roaming charges?

Yes, but only under strict conditions. The “sustainability mechanism” allows a national regulator to approve temporary roaming surcharges if an operator can prove that its wholesale costs for providing roaming exceed its roaming revenue by a significant margin (at least 3% of total roaming revenue). Only one operator (Digi in Romania) has successfully used this mechanism, and only for a short period during the pandemic. The 2022 regulation made the threshold higher and requires annual reporting. In practice, given the political backlash and regulatory burden, reintroducing surcharges is extremely difficult and unlikely.
