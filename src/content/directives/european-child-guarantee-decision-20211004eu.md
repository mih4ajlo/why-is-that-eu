## What is it?

The European Child Guarantee is a Council Recommendation adopted in June 2021 that asks EU member states to guarantee effective access to six essential services for children at risk of poverty or social exclusion: early childhood education and care, education and school-based activities, at least one healthy meal per school day, healthcare, adequate housing, and healthy nutrition. It is not a binding regulation but a political commitment backed by coordination, reporting, and EU funding obligations.

Each member state must nominate a national coordinator, submit a national action plan, and report every two years on progress. The Guarantee explicitly targets vulnerable groups including Roma children, children with disabilities, migrant and refugee children, and children in alternative care institutions. The Commission monitors implementation through the European Semester and the Social Scoreboard.

## Why was it introduced?

The European Child Guarantee was a direct response to a political and statistical emergency. In 2019, according to Eurostat’s EU-SILC survey, 22.2% of children in the EU — roughly 18 million — were at risk of poverty or social exclusion, a rate significantly higher than the adult population. The situation was most acute in Romania (41.4%), Bulgaria (36.2%), Spain (33.5%), and Greece (33.4%). These figures had barely budged since the 2008 financial crisis, despite strong economic recovery in many countries.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had repeatedly declared social rights ambitions in treaties and proclamations — the 2017 European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 11 specifically on child poverty), the 2020 Porto Social Summit commitments — but national policies remained fragmented, underfunded, and in some cases regressive. Meanwhile, austerity-driven cuts to family benefits, school meals, and housing support after 2008 had hit children hardest, with long-term scarring effects on health, education, and future earnings documented by UNICEF and the OECD.

The coalition that drove this forward was unusual by EU social policy standards. The European Parliament had been pushing for a binding directive since at least 2015, led by MEPs from the Socialists & Democrats and the Greens/EFA groups, notably Portuguese MEP Sandra Pereira and Belgian MEP Maria Arena. The Parliament’s 2019 resolution called for a "Child Guarantee with binding targets and adequate funding." However, the Council — controlled by centre-right and liberal governments — resisted binding legislation, arguing child poverty fell under national competence.

What broke the deadlock was a combination of timing and crisis. The COVID-19 pandemic, which began in early 2020, massively worsened child poverty across the EU. School closures deprived 18 million children of school meals, and lockdowns exposed housing overcrowding, digital exclusion, and mental health deterioration. The Commission’s 2021 impact assessment estimated that an additional 2 million children had fallen into poverty risk during 2020 alone. The EU could no longer credibly claim to care about social cohesion without acting.

Commissioner for Jobs and Social Rights Nicolas Schmit (Luxembourg, Socialists & Democrats) — appointed in 2019 as part of Ursula von der Leyen’s Commission — made the Child Guarantee a flagship of the European Pillar of Social Rights Action Plan, published in March 2021. Schmit’s background as a former Social Affairs Minister who had negotiated the 2016 EU Youth Guarantee gave him credibility with both the Parliament and the large coalition of civil society organisations — Eurochild, Save the Children, Caritas, the European Anti-Poverty Network — that had campaigned for years.

European industry groups, notably BusinessEurope, raised concerns about administrative burden and cost, warning in a 2021 position paper that "binding targets on national budgets would constrain fiscal flexibility." The Council’s legal service also flagged that a directive could infringe subsidiarity principles. The final compromise was a Council Recommendation — legally non-binding — but backed by a dedicated €9 billion allocation from the European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) for the 2021-2027 period, with 5% earmarked specifically for the Child Guarantee. This funding mechanism did not require new budget from member states, which made it politically palatable.

## Timeline

- **2008-2013** — Eurostat records persistently high child poverty rates; the financial crisis and subsequent austerity deepen inequality
- **2015** — The European Parliament passes a resolution calling for a "Child Guarantee" modelled on the 2013 Youth Guarantee
- **2017** — The European Pillar of Social Rights is proclaimed at the Gothenburg Summit, including Principle 11 on child poverty
- **2019** — The Parliament's EP electoral resolution again demands binding legislation; von der Leyen Commission pledges social policy action
- **March 2021** — The European Pillar of Social Rights Action Plan includes the Child Guarantee as a headline commitment
- **June 2021** — The Council adopts the Child Guarantee as a Recommendation; member states have 6 months to nominate national coordinators
- **2022** — National action plans start being submitted; first monitoring reports begin; ESF+ programming for 2021-2027 includes earmarked allocations
- **2023** — The Commission publishes the first biennial monitoring report noting uneven progress

## Impact and consequences

Implementation has been slow and uneven. By 2023, the Commission’s first monitoring report found that only 12 of 27 member states had fully operational national action plans. Countries with pre-existing comprehensive anti-poverty strategies — Finland, Sweden, Denmark — integrated the Guarantee quickly. Others, particularly in Southern and Eastern Europe, struggled to align existing fragmented services. Romania, which had the highest child poverty rate in 2019, submitted a plan that civil society groups criticised as lacking measurable targets.

The most concrete impact has been through ESF+ funding. A 2023 European Court of Auditors report noted that while the €9 billion allocation was significant, only €1.4 billion had been disbursed by mid-2023 due to slow absorption capacity in poorer regions. The Commission has since launched a technical support facility to help member states design and implement interventions, particularly for school meals and municipal housing.

The Guarantee has also triggered a wave of litigation. In March 2023, the European Network of Ombudspersons for Children reported a 40% increase in complaints from families about school meal exclusion and housing discrimination since 2021. The Bulgarian Supreme Administrative Court cited the Guarantee in July 2023 when it ordered municipalities to provide free school lunches to all children in poverty — a ruling that affected roughly 250,000 children.

## Key questions answered

### What rights does the EU guarantee for children living in poverty?

The European Child Guarantee asks EU countries to ensure children at risk of poverty can actually access six services: early childhood education, school-based activities, one healthy meal per school day, healthcare, adequate housing, and healthy nutrition. It does not create new individual rights but sets minimum standards and requires countries to report on progress.

### Can a family in the EU take their government to court for not providing free healthcare, education, or housing to children?

Only indirectly. The Guarantee is a Council Recommendation, not a directive — it is legally non-binding. However, courts in some countries have used it to interpret existing national law. In Bulgaria, the Supreme Administrative Court cited it as a basis to order free school meals. A family cannot sue at EU level, but national courts may reference it.

### Which EU countries were against guaranteeing basic services to poor children, and why?

Several fiscally conservative governments — notably Austria, Denmark, and the Netherlands — expressed concern about binding EU interference in national social policy. BusinessEurope opposed binding targets and funding obligations. The compromise to keep it as a Recommendation with voluntary participation and linked to ESF+ funding that was already budgeted made it possible for all member states to agree.

### How is the EU funding this program to help children in need?

Through the European Social Fund Plus (ESF+), the EU's main social policy fund. For 2021-2027, €88 billion is allocated to ESF+ overall, with at least 5% of total ESF+ resources in each member state — roughly €9 billion across the EU — dedicated to the Child Guarantee. This amounts to roughly €2.6 per child at risk of poverty per day, according to the Commission's 2021 impact assessment.
