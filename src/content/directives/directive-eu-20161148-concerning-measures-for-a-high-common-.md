## What is it?

The NIS Directive established the EU's first coordinated cybersecurity framework. It required each Member State to adopt a national cybersecurity strategy, designate a national competent authority and a single point of contact, and set up a Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT). It also created the CSIRT Network and the Cooperation Group to facilitate cross-border coordination.

The Directive imposed two main obligations on "operators of essential services" (OES) in sectors like energy, transport, banking, health, water, and digital infrastructure, and on "digital service providers" (DSPs) like cloud computing platforms, online marketplaces, and search engines: take appropriate technical and organisational measures to manage cybersecurity risks, and report significant incidents to national authorities. OES were subject to ex-ante identification by Member States, while DSPs were subject to lighter ex-post supervision.

## Why was it introduced?

**The NIS Directive was a direct response to the 2013 cyberattack on the Belgian telecommunications company Belgacom that compromised EU Commission computers, and the 2015 hack of the German Bundestag that stole 16 gigabytes of data from Chancellor Merkel’s office.**

These attacks exposed a structural tension: Europe’s critical infrastructure — power grids, banks, hospitals — was increasingly digitised and interconnected, but each Member State set its own cybersecurity standards. A 2014 European Commission communication noted that only three Member States had adopted national cybersecurity strategies. The result was that a weak link — say, a Romanian energy company or a Greek hospital — could be a backdoor into a European-wide network.

Coalition dynamics were unusually fractured. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, Andreas Schwab (EPP, Germany), pushed for a binding, sector-specific regime with mandatory reporting thresholds. The Council of the EU, led by Germany and the UK, resisted this, arguing for flexibility and light-touch oversight to avoid burdening businesses. The UK, under Prime Minister David Cameron, was particularly vocal about not imposing prescriptive EU rules on its financial sector. In contrast, the Dutch delegation, backed by Commissioner Günther Oettinger (Digital Economy and Society, 2014–2016) and Commissioner Julian King (Security Union, 2016–2019), argued that voluntary cooperation had failed and that a minimum legal baseline was necessary.

The timing was decisive. On 5 May 2016, just three weeks before the Directive’s final political deal, the European Parliament voted on the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), creating a political tailwind. The GDPR’s steep fines (up to 4% of global turnover) set a precedent that made the NIS Directive’s weaker enforcement — enforcement was left almost entirely to Member States — seem politically acceptable to industry. The official legislative process: the Commission proposed the Directive on 7 February 2013, the Parliament adopted its position on 13 March 2014, and the Council agreed a general approach on 18 December 2015. Trilogue negotiations resolved the most contentious issue — how strictly to regulate digital service providers — by creating a two-tier system: OES under strict ex-ante supervision, DSPs under lighter ex-post supervision. The Directive entered into force on 8 August 2016, with Member States required to transpose it into national law by 9 May 2018.

## Timeline

- **2013** — Belgacom hack and Bundestag breach. Commission proposes NIS Directive on 7 February 2013.
- **2014** — European Parliament adopts position on 13 March 2014, pushing for broader scope.
- **2015** — Council agrees general approach on 18 December 2015, watering down enforcement powers.
- **2016** — Final political deal reached in May. Directive enters into force on 8 August.
- **2018** — All Member States miss the 9 May transposition deadline. The Commission opens infringement proceedings against 19 countries including Germany, France, and Spain.
- **2019** — First biennial implementation report highlights major gaps: reporting standards differ wildly, only 15 Member States have identified key OES.
- **2020** — The Commission proposes NIS 2, citing 2019 report findings and the SolarWinds (December 2020) hack.
- **2022** — NIS 2 Directive (2022/2555/EU) adopted, replacing NIS 1.

## Impact and consequences

**The Directive’s impact was underwhelming in practice.** The European Commission’s 2019 implementation report found that only 15 of 27 Member States had submitted lists of identified OES — meaning the core enforcement mechanism, ex-ante identification, barely functioned. The UK, the largest single market, identified 238 OES in energy and transport by 2020; Germany identified 145; France identified just 87. By contrast, small states like Estonia (population 1.3 million) identified 267.

Industry response was defensive. The European Banking Authority (EBA) warned in its 2018 assessment that many financial institutions simply copied their existing Basel III or internal audit frameworks as NIS compliance, making the regime a paperwork exercise. A 2020 EU Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) survey found that only 34% of OES had implemented incident response plans aligned with the Directive.

**No fines were ever publicly imposed under the NIS Directive.** This is because the Directive left enforcement to national authorities, and none issued sanctions that became public. A Commission official described the regime to *Politico* in 2020 as "a paper tiger" that "proved voluntary reporting is still voluntary."

The main consequence was political: the NIS Directive’s weaknesses directly led to the NIS 2 Directive (2022/2555/EU). NIS 2 expanded the scope to 15 sectors, introduced mandatory incident reporting within 24 hours, and imposed real enforcement teeth — administrative fines up to €10 million or 2% of global turnover.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe’s first cybersecurity law take three years to pass?

Because national governments fiercely resisted giving the EU oversight of their critical infrastructure, especially in defence and financial sectors. The UK, Germany, and France each wanted to preserve their own cybersecurity agencies’ primacy. The three-year deadlock was only broken when the GDPR’s passage created a precedent for tough EU digital regulation, and when a series of high-profile attacks (Belgacom 2013, Bundestag 2015, Ukrainian power grid 2015) made inaction politically untenable.

### Does every EU country have to follow the same cybersecurity rules?

Under the NIS Directive, no — and that was the problem. Member States had wide discretion to define "essential services," set their own identification thresholds, and choose their own enforcement approach. This created a patchwork: an energy company in Germany faced stricter oversight than one in Romania, even though both fed the same European grid. The NIS 2 Directive (2022/2555) fixed this by harmonising sector lists, incident reporting thresholds, and fine levels across all Member States.

### What happens if a company does not report a cyberattack?

Under the NIS Directive, very little. The Directive required operators to report incidents that "significantly" disrupted services, but each Member State defined "significant" differently. No public fines were ever issued. The system was widely criticised as toothless. The NIS 2 Directive changed this drastically, making failure to report a punishable offence with fines up to €10 million or 2% of global turnover, and requiring initial reports within 24 hours.
