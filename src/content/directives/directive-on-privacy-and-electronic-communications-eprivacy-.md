## What is it?

The ePrivacy Directive (Directive 2002/58/EC), often called the "Cookie Law," is the EU's central piece of legislation governing privacy in the realm of electronic communications. It complements the broader data protection framework (originally Directive 95/46/EC, now the GDPR) by setting specific rules for telecoms operators, internet service providers, and any service that uses electronic communications networks.

The directive covers a wide range of issues: the confidentiality of communications, the use of cookies and similar tracking technologies, unsolicited marketing (spam emails, automated calls), the handling of traffic and location data by telecoms, and the security of networks. Its most visible legacy for ordinary internet users is the cookie consent banner that appears on virtually every European website.

The directive was substantially amended in 2009 by Directive 2009/136/EC (the "Citizens' Rights Directive"), which introduced the explicit opt-in consent requirement for cookies. A proposed ePrivacy Regulation, intended to replace the directive and align it with the GDPR, has been stalled in EU negotiations since 2017.

## Why was it introduced?

By the late 1990s, the EU had a comprehensive Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC) that set general rules for processing personal data, plus an earlier sector-specific directive (97/66/EC) covering telecoms. But the digital landscape was changing rapidly. The dot-com era brought mass internet adoption, mobile phones became ubiquitous, and new techniques for tracking users — cookies, web beacons, location-based services — were emerging faster than the law could keep up.

Several pressures converged. First, telecoms liberalisation across Europe meant private companies, not state monopolies, were now handling sensitive communications data, raising new accountability questions. Second, spam was exploding — by the early 2000s, unsolicited commercial email was overwhelming inboxes and costing businesses billions, prompting demands for harmonised rules. Third, member states were taking divergent approaches: some required opt-in for marketing, others opt-out, creating a fragmented single market. Fourth, civil liberties groups and the European Parliament were increasingly worried about the surveillance potential of new technologies, particularly mobile location tracking.

The 2002 directive was part of a broader telecoms regulatory package that overhauled EU electronic communications rules. It introduced opt-in for unsolicited commercial email (the "soft opt-in" exception for existing customers was a compromise with industry), rules on retaining traffic data, and — controversially — an initial framework for cookies that required only that users be "informed" and given the chance to opt out.

The 2009 amendment was driven by mounting evidence that the opt-out cookie regime was failing. Behavioural advertising had matured into a multi-billion-euro industry led by Google (after its 2007 DoubleClick acquisition), Facebook, and ad networks that tracked users invisibly across the web. Data protection authorities, particularly in Germany, France, and the UK, argued that informed consent — not buried opt-outs — was needed. The shift to opt-in consent for cookies in Article 5(3) was the result, despite intense lobbying from advertisers and publishers who warned (correctly, as it turned out) of "consent fatigue."

## Timeline

- **1995** — Data Protection Directive 95/46/EC adopted, establishing the EU's general data protection framework.
- **1997** — Directive 97/66/EC adopted, the first telecoms-specific privacy directive (the predecessor to ePrivacy).
- **2000** — European Commission begins consultation on a new electronic communications regulatory package.
- **July 2002** — ePrivacy Directive 2002/58/EC adopted as part of the new telecoms package.
- **October 2003** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2006** — Data Retention Directive (2006/24/EC) extends retention obligations on telecoms (later struck down by the CJEU in 2014).
- **November 2009** — Directive 2009/136/EC amends ePrivacy, introducing explicit opt-in consent for cookies.
- **May 2011** — Deadline for transposing the cookie consent rules; cookie banners begin appearing across EU websites.
- **January 2017** — European Commission publishes proposal for an ePrivacy Regulation to replace the directive.
- **May 2018** — GDPR enters into force; ePrivacy Regulation negotiations stall as member states disagree on scope.
- **2019–2024** — Multiple Council compromise texts on the ePrivacy Regulation fail to achieve agreement; directive remains in force.

## Impact and consequences

The ePrivacy Directive's most visible impact is the cookie banner — now an inescapable feature of European web browsing. Critics argue it has produced "consent theatre" rather than meaningful privacy protection, with users mechanically clicking "accept all" to dismiss intrusive pop-ups. Defenders point out that the underlying problem is not the law but the ad-tech industry's resistance to genuinely privacy-friendly design.

For the advertising industry, the directive (especially after 2009) forced a fundamental rethink. Behavioural advertising businesses had to build consent management platforms (CMPs), and major regulators have since fined companies — including Google, Amazon, and Meta — for cookie consent violations under the combined ePrivacy/GDPR framework. France's CNIL has been particularly aggressive, issuing record fines in 2020 and 2021.

For telecoms, the directive established baseline confidentiality rules that constrain how operators can monetise traffic and location data — a continuing point of tension as telcos watch internet platforms profit from data the telcos themselves are largely forbidden to use.

The proposed ePrivacy Regulation, which would harmonise rules across the EU as directly applicable law and extend coverage to "over-the-top" services like WhatsApp and Skype, has been blocked since 2017 by disagreements over encryption, child safety scanning, and the scope of cookie rules. The aging 2002 directive thus continues to govern a digital ecosystem it was never designed for.

## Key questions answered

### Why does every European website have a cookie banner under the ePrivacy Directive?

Article 5(3) of the directive, as amended in 2009, requires prior informed consent before storing or accessing information on a user's device — including cookies — unless strictly necessary to provide a service the user requested. Banners are the industry's chosen (if widely criticised) implementation.

### Why is there a separate ePrivacy law alongside the GDPR?

The GDPR is general data protection law; ePrivacy is sector-specific (lex specialis) covering electronic communications. ePrivacy protects communications confidentiality and device integrity even when no personal data is involved (e.g. cookies on shared computers), and applies to legal persons, not just individuals.

### Why hasn't the ePrivacy Regulation replaced the ePrivacy Directive yet?

Negotiations have been gridlocked since 2017 over deep disagreements: tech companies and publishers want lighter cookie rules, civil society wants stronger protections, law enforcement wants exceptions for child sexual abuse material scanning, and member states are split on encryption. No qualified majority has emerged.

### Why does the ePrivacy Directive cover spam separately from cookies?

Both reflect the same underlying principle — that users should control intrusions into their personal digital space — but spam (Article 13) and cookies (Article 5(3)) address different mechanisms. The 2002 opt-in rule for spam was one of the first such regimes globally and influenced laws like the US CAN-SPAM Act.
