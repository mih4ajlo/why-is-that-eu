## What is it?

The Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR), formally adopted in 2025, is the EU’s overhaul of the 1994 Packaging Directive (94/62/EC). Unlike the directive, the PPWR is a regulation — directly binding across all member states without national transposition — to eliminate fragmentation in the single market for packaging. It sets mandatory rules requiring all packaging placed on the EU market to be recyclable by 2030, with specific recycled content targets for plastic packaging (e.g., 50% recycled content in contact-sensitive plastic packaging by 2040) and waste reduction targets of 10% per capita by 2035 compared to 2018 levels. The regulation also bans certain single-use packaging formats, including disposable plastic packaging for fruit and vegetables, mini-shampoo bottles in hotels, and single-use portions of sauces, creams, and sugar in the hospitality sector.

## Why was it introduced?

The PPWR was driven by a structural failure of the 1994 Packaging Directive, which left packaging waste generation rising faster than GDP growth. According to Eurostat, packaging waste in the EU grew by 22% between 2010 and 2021, reaching a record 84 million tonnes in 2021, while recycling rates stagnated and varied wildly between member states — from below 40% in Malta to over 80% in Belgium. The directive’s framework relied on national implementation, creating a patchwork of rules that the Commission’s 2022 impact assessment found imposed €2-4 billion annually in compliance costs for cross-border businesses.

The political breakthrough came from a specific coalition dynamic. In November 2022, the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen (with Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius as lead) proposed the PPWR. The proposal was pushed by a strong coalition of Nordic and Benelux countries (Denmark, Sweden, the Netherlands, Belgium, and Luxembourg) joined by environmental groups like the European Environmental Bureau (EEB) and the Ellen MacArthur Foundation, who had campaigned for binding recycled content targets since 2018. Industry opposition was fierce: Europen (the packaging trade association) and FoodDrinkEurope argued that mandatory recycled content would disrupt supply chains and raise food safety risks. The plastics industry, including PlasticsEurope and companies like Amcor and DS Smith, warned the Commission’s 2020 impact assessment that targets could increase packaging costs by 30%.

The timing was crucial: the 2019 European Green Deal created political momentum, and the Covid-19 pandemic had exposed the fragility of recycling supply chains as global plastic waste trade collapsed. A key unblocking event was the European Parliament’s Environment Committee vote in October 2023, which adopted amendments including the ban on single-use hotel amenities and portion packs — a move that surprised many observers because it went further than the Commission’s proposal. The full Parliament backed this in November 2023, and after trilogue negotiations, the Council adopted the final text in December 2024.

A dramatic element in the debate was the March 2023 leaked internal Commission documents, obtained by investigative journalists, showing that a 2022 industry-commissioned study had underestimated the economic viability of recycling targets — a scandal that weakened industry claims.

## Timeline

- **1994** — Packaging Directive (94/62/EC) adopted, setting initial recovery and recycling targets
- **2018** — EU Circular Economy Package revises targets, but packaging waste continues growing
- **2022** — Eurostat reports record 84 million tonnes of packaging waste generated in 2021
- **30 November 2022** — European Commission proposes Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation under von der Leyen Commission
- **October 2023** — European Parliament Environment Committee votes for stricter single-use bans
- **22 November 2023** — European Parliament adopts its position (426 votes in favour, 125 against, 74 abstentions)
- **December 2024** — Council formally adopts the final text after trilogue negotiations
- **11 February 2025** — Regulation published in the Official Journal
- **2030** — Full recyclability requirements enter into force; plastic packaging recycled content targets begin (30% for contact-sensitive, 10% for other)
- **2035** — 10% per capita waste reduction target compared to 2018

## Impact and consequences

The PPWR directly affects every company placing packaged goods on the EU market — estimated at over 4 million businesses by the Commission’s 2022 impact assessment. The food and beverage industry, particularly dairy producers using plastic cups and yogurt pots, faced the biggest compliance challenge: the ban on single-use portion packs forced companies like Danone and Nestlé to redesign packaging lines. The hotel sector, led by Accor and Marriott, warned of significant job losses from the mini-shampoo bottle ban, but the EEB’s analysis showed that the ban affected only 0.03% of total plastic packaging waste.

Enforcement falls on national authorities, with penalties including fines of up to 4% of annual turnover. The first major enforcement case is expected in 2028, when the Commission will scrutinise member states’ implementation plans. The regulation also introduces a harmonised labelling system for packaging recyclability, requiring companies to invest in new packaging artwork — the Commission estimated compliance costs at €1.5-2.5 billion industry-wide.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU move from a directive to a regulation for the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation?

The 1994 directive allowed member states significant leeway, creating 27 different national systems that made cross-border trade costly. A 2021 study by the European Court of Auditors found that national transposition led to €1.9 billion in additional administrative costs annually for businesses operating in multiple member states. The regulation’s direct applicability removes these barriers and ensures uniform rules across the single market.

### Why are single-use packaging forms like mini-shampoo bottles banned under the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation?

The ban targets packaging formats that are disproportionately wasteful relative to their utility. According to the Commission’s 2022 impact assessment, mini-shampoo bottles account for only 0.5% of total hotel packaging but generate 10 times the waste per use of larger formats. The EEB argued that such bans also serve to shift consumer behaviour by eliminating the convenience of single-use packaging in hospitality.

### How will businesses comply with recycled content targets under the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation?

The regulation allows companies to meet recycled content targets through a system of compliance credits traded among industry players, similar to the approach in the Single-Use Plastics Directive (2019/904). The Commission’s 2023 implementing acts specify that recycled content must come from post-consumer waste, not production scrap, and that chemical recycling counts only if it meets life-cycle analysis standards set by the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA).
