## What is it?

The EU Conflict Minerals Regulation (2017/821) imposes binding supply chain due diligence obligations on EU importers of tin, tungsten, tantalum, and gold (collectively known as 3TG) — whether in their ore, concentrate, or metal form. It covers raw materials originating from conflict-affected and high-risk areas (CAHRAs), defined broadly as regions with widespread armed conflict, weak governance, or systematic human rights violations.

Rather than banning imports, the regulation requires importers to adopt a five-step due diligence framework based on the OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas. This includes establishing a company management system, identifying and assessing risks in the supply chain, designing and implementing a response strategy, commissioning independent third-party audits, and publicly reporting on due diligence practices.

The regulation entered into force in June 2017 but its obligations only applied from 1 January 2021, giving industry a three-and-a-half-year transition period. It is a mandatory scheme for EU importers of the raw materials, while downstream companies that use processed metals are only encouraged — not required — to participate, through a voluntary "chain of custody" certification system managed by the Commission.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the violent legacy of mineral-funded conflict in the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC). A 2009 UN Group of Experts report documented how armed groups, including the Democratic Forces for the Liberation of Rwanda (FDLR) and the Congolese army, were earning hundreds of millions of dollars annually from the illegal extraction and trade of cassiterite (tin ore), wolframite (tungsten), coltan (tantalum), and gold — often through supply chains that reached legitimate smelters and electronics manufacturers worldwide.

The United States acted first. In July 2010, the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act included Section 1502, which required US-listed companies to disclose whether their products contained conflict minerals from the DRC or adjoining countries. This created a de facto supply chain screening obligation for global companies listed in the US, while their European competitors faced no equivalent requirement. European industry quickly warned of a competitive disadvantage: EU-based smelters and electronics makers risked being excluded from the highest-ethics consumer segments while also facing potential reputational damage.

Trade Commissioner Cecilia Malmström tabled the Commission’s proposal in March 2014. Initially, the Commission envisaged a partly voluntary system — mandatory only for importers of raw ores, voluntary for downstream users of processed metals. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, Romanian MEP Iuliu Winkler (EPP), led a push for a far stronger instrument. Parliamentary committees supported extending mandatory due diligence to all companies in the chain, including smelters and refiners, and on 16 March 2016 the Parliament voted 546 to 23 in favour of a mandatory, all-chain approach. The Council of the EU, however, resisted on grounds of administrative burden and competitiveness, and the final text — adopted in May 2017 after six months of inter-institutional negotiations — represented a compromise: mandatory for raw-material importers but voluntary for downstream processors.

The coalition that secured passage was unusual: progressive MEPs such as Judith Sargentini (Greens) joined forces with conservative trade sceptics who wanted to align EU rules with US standards, while industry associations were split. Eurometaux, representing metals producers, and DigitalEurope, representing tech companies, warned of compliance costs that could reach €1.5 million per company according to the Commission’s 2014 impact assessment. On the other side, the Fair Trade Advocacy Office, Global Witness, and Amnesty International campaigned heavily for mandatory rules, arguing that voluntary schemes had failed to stop conflict financing.

A crucial political shift came in 2011 when the OECD formally adopted its Due Diligence Guidance, providing a ready-made international framework the EU could reference. The 2014-2019 Juncker Commission made "trade as a force for good" a headline objective, and the adoption of the regulation aligned with that narrative. Moreover, the Lisbon Treaty gave the EU exclusive competence over trade policy, removing the risk of divergent national laws if the EU did not act.

## Timeline

- **2009** — UN Group of Experts reports detail systematic mineral-funded conflict in the DRC.
- **July 2010** — US Dodd-Frank Act, Section 1502, requires conflict minerals disclosure for US-listed companies.
- **2011** — OECD adopts the Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas, immediately referenced in EU policy discussions.
- **March 2014** — European Commission proposes a regulation on conflict minerals, with mandatory due diligence only for raw-material importers.
- **May 2015** — The European Parliament adopts its first-reading position, calling for mandatory due diligence for all companies in the supply chain.
- **November 2016** — Trilogue negotiations conclude with a compromise: mandatory for raw-material importers, voluntary for downstream users.
- **17 May 2017** — Regulation (EU) 2017/821 is adopted by the European Parliament and Council.
- **9 June 2017** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 January 2021** — Obligations apply for EU importers of raw ores and concentrates; voluntary scheme for downstream companies opens.
- **2021–2023** — The Commission publishes annual compliance reports; no enforcement actions have been made public, but member state authorities are tasked with conducting post-import checks.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation primarily affects the roughly 600–1,000 EU importers of 3TG raw materials, mainly located in Belgium, Germany, the Netherlands, and the UK (pre-Brexit). Many of these are small trading companies, not large smelters. The Commission’s 2020 evaluation noted that conversion costs were "moderate" for most — between €5,000 and €60,000 per company — but that certain importers struggled with identifying their smelter of origin and distinguishing between conflict-affected and low-risk sources.

Non-EU countries have introduced comparable legislation: China and Switzerland have enacted their own due diligence rules, partly to align with OECD standards and partly to keep access to the EU market. Domestic industries in the DRC also faced adjustments: the Congolese government banned artisanal mining of cobalt and coltan in some areas, and the number of formally certified mines increased from near zero in 2013 to several dozen by 2021, according to the OECD.

One unexpected consequence was that some smelters and refiners chose to discontinue sourcing from the Great Lakes region altogether, a phenomenon known as "de-risking" or "de facto embargo". The 2021 Commission report acknowledged this risk and called for better support of responsible mining initiatives. By 2023, most major conflict mineral supply chain initiatives — such as the Responsible Minerals Initiative (RMI) and the London Bullion Market Association’s Responsible Gold Programme — had aligned their audit protocols with the regulation’s requirements.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU require all companies to trace where their minerals come from?

No — the obligation only applies to EU importers of tin, tungsten, tantalum, and gold in their raw forms (ores, concentrates, and metals). Downstream companies that manufacture electronic components, jewellery, or batteries are not legally required to carry out due diligence, though they are encouraged to join the voluntary EU chain-of-custody scheme. Many choose to do so anyway because their customers — automakers, electronics giants — increasingly demand certified conflict-free supply chains.

### How is this different from the US conflict minerals rule?

The original US rule (Dodd-Frank Section 1502) covers only the Democratic Republic of the Congo and its nine neighbouring countries, while the EU regulation applies globally to all conflict-affected and high-risk areas. The US rule requires a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission; the EU regulation requires a due diligence report submitted to national authorities. The US standard has been subject to legal challenges and a 2017 guidance memo reducing its scope; the EU rule has remained stable.

### What happens if a company fails to comply?

EU member states must designate national competent authorities to conduct checks. Penalties vary by country but must be “effective, proportionate and dissuasive”. The regulation does not set a common fine level. In practice, the Commission’s 2021 report noted that enforcement capacity was still weak: several member states had not yet appointed an authority or performed inspections. The first known administrative penalty was imposed in 2023 in Belgium where a small importer failed to produce any due diligence document.

### Do small businesses have to follow the same rules?

Yes, but only if they import the raw materials above a very low threshold. There is no exemption for micro-importers — the regulation applies to any company placing 3TG on the EU market, regardless of size. However, the Commission issued practical guidance in 2020 that recommends simplified risk assessment templates for small importers. The OECD also provides a free, step-by-step due diligence toolkit.
