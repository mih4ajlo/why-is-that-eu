## What is it?

The Medical Devices Regulation (MDR) is a comprehensive EU regulation that governs the design, clinical evaluation, manufacturing, and post-market surveillance of medical devices placed on the European market. It replaced two earlier directives — the Medical Devices Directive (MDD, 93/42/EEC) and the Active Implantable Medical Devices Directive (AIMDD, 90/385/EEC) — which had been in place since the 1990s.

The MDR introduces significantly stricter requirements for clinical evidence, device traceability (via a Unique Device Identification system), and the designation of notified bodies — the private organisations that assess whether devices meet safety standards. It applies to a broadened scope of products, including those without a medical purpose, such as coloured contact lenses and cosmetic implants. Full application began on 26 May 2021, with a phased implementation timeline extending for certain legacy devices until 2028.

## Why was it introduced?

The MDR's roots lie in two catastrophic medical device failures that destroyed patient trust and exposed a regulatory system built on self-certification and weak oversight.

The decisive scandal was the Poly Implant Prothèse (PIP) breast implant fraud. Between 2001 and 2010, French company PIP used industrial-grade silicone — not medical-grade — in its implants, leading to abnormally high rupture rates (the French National Agency for Medicines and Health Products Safety (ANSM) found a rupture rate of 5.7% in 2012, compared to less than 1% for competitors). The fraud was uncovered in 2010, affecting an estimated 300,000 women in 65 countries. The scandal was not just about a bad actor — it revealed systemic failures: the notified body TÜV Rheinland, which certified PIP products, had conducted only a single unannounced inspection in 2009, and the company had stockpiled compliant silicone to deceive auditors. TÜV Rheinland was later ordered by French courts to pay substantial damages.

The second driver was the metal-on-metal hip implant disaster, particularly the DePuy ASR hip system. By 2010, Johnson & Johnson subsidiary DePuy had recalled its ASR hip system after data showed that 40% of patients required revision surgery within five years, according to a 2012 study in The Lancet. The system had been certified under the MDD without any large-scale clinical trial — a 2003 study involved only 95 patients. British orthopaedic surgeons, led by Professor David Murray of the Nuffield Orthopaedic Centre, documented the failures in the UK's National Joint Registry, which the MHRA was slow to act upon.

These events triggered a political crisis. In 2012, the European Commission launched a public consultation, with then-Health Commissioner John Dalli (later replaced by Tonio Borg) acknowledging in a 2011 speech that the PIP case had "shaken confidence in our system." The European Parliament's ENVI Committee, led by MEPs like Glenis Willmott (S&D, UK) and Dagmar Roth-Behrendt (S&D, Germany), pushed for fundamental reform. The Council of the EU, under pressure from member states like France and Germany, supported a move from directives (which required national transposition and allowed loopholes) to a directly binding regulation.

The Commission's 2012 impact assessment estimated that the MDD system had allowed "significant variability" in notified body assessments, with some bodies certifying devices within days. A 2013 study by the Commission's Joint Research Centre found that the number of notified bodies had dropped from 57 in 2007 to 49 in 2012, but oversight remained fragmented. The Parliament's final vote on 5 April 2017 was 508 in favour, 33 against, with 103 abstentions — a strong cross-party consensus driven by patient safety groups, insurers, and a few large medtech companies who saw harmonisation as a competitive advantage.

Key opponents included a coalition of smaller medical device manufacturers, particularly in Germany and Italy, who warned through bodies like the German Medical Technology Association (BVMed) that the MDR would cost them 50-70% more in compliance costs and could lead to a 25% reduction in notified body capacity (a prediction that later proved accurate). The MedTech Europe industry association argued in 2014 that the MDR could push 5,000-7,000 device types off the market.

- **2012** — Commission consultation on medical devices regulation; Health Commissioner John Dalli resigns amid unrelated tobacco lobbying scandal; replaced by Tonio Borg

- **2012** — DePuy ASR hip system recall in the UK after National Joint Registry data shows 40% failure rate at five years

- **2013** — UK Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) publishes review of DePuy ASR failures
- **2013-2016** — Trilogue negotiations between Commission, Parliament, and Council; key battle is over scope of clinical evidence requirements and notified body oversight
- **5 April 2017** — European Parliament adopts MDR (vote: 508-33-103); Council approves on 7 May 2017

- **25 May 2017** — Date of entry into force; date of application set as 26 May 2021
- **2020** — COVD-19 pandemic delays implementation; Commission issues postponement proposals
- **2021** — MedTech Europe reports that only 12 of 57 notified bodies have applied for designation under MDR; fewer than 20% of previously certified devices have transitioned to MDR certification
- **26 May 2021** — MDR becomes applicable for new devices; transition period for legacy devices extended several times due to capacity crisis
- **2022** — Commission reports that only 22 notified bodies have been designated under MDR, leading to serious shortages of certified devices
- **2023** — Parliament reforms MDR through a targeted amendment to extend transition deadlines for legacy devices until 2027-2028
- **2024** — Commission report notes that MDR has led to a 10% reduction in available device types, with particular shortages in paediatric devices and orphan devices

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence of the MDR was a severe bottleneck in notified body capacity. By 2022, only 22 notified bodies had been designated under the MDR — down from over 50 under the MDD — meaning that many manufacturers faced delays of 12-18 months for fresh certifications. This led to a widely reported "device desert" for smaller products like stents, catheters, and diagnostic equipment used in paediatrics.

The Commission's 2023 impact assessment projected that around 8,000-10,000 device types would be withdrawn from the EU market by 2027, with an estimated net economic cost to the industry of €4-8 billion. The German Medical Technology Association reported in 2023 that 50% of its members had reduced their product range in the EU due to MDR costs, shifting some production to the US or Asia.

Patient safety improved, but at a cost. The number of device-related serious incident reports to the European Database on Medical Devices (EUDAMED) rose by 30% in 2022 compared to pre-MDR levels, according to the Commission's 2023 report, but this was partly due to better reporting rather than more incidents. The number of unannounced inspections of notified bodies increased significantly — a key demand from Parliament.

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) were disproportionately affected. A 2021 study by the European Commission estimated that SMEs faced compliance costs of €300,000 to €1.5 million per device — 5-10 times higher than under the MDD — leading to industry consolidation. Larger players like Siemens Healthineers, Philips, and Medtronic invested heavily in MDR compliance; Siemens reported spending €800 million on regulatory upgrades between 2018 and 2023.

## Key questions answered

### Why was the MDR a regulation rather than a directive?

A regulation is directly binding in all member states without national transposition, preventing the fragmented implementation that plagued the MDD. The MDD had been interpreted differently across countries — for example, the UK's MHRA permitted less rigorous clinical evidence than Germany's BfArM. The PIP scandal showed that a directive allowed notified bodies to operate under inconsistent national rules, with TÜV Rheinland's lax inspections in Germany going unpunished by regulators in France, where the devices were sold.

### Why did the MDR cause a shortage of medical devices?

The MDR demanded far more clinical evidence — for many devices, full clinical trials where the MDD had allowed literature reviews or small studies. Notified bodies, the private organisations that certify devices, were also required to meet stricter standards (more expert staff, unannounced inspections, liability insurance). Many existing notified bodies simply applied for designation under the MDR and failed to qualify; the Commission's 2022 report showed only 22 had been designated by 2024. This created a capacity crisis where manufacturers waited 12-18 months for certifications, forcing them to withdraw products that could not afford the wait or the cost.

### Why did the PIP scandal not lead to reform of the Medical Devices Regulation (MDR) sooner?

The PIP scandal broke in 2010, but the legislative process took seven years because of intense industry lobbying and technical complexity. The European Parliament wanted a "strict liability" regime for notified bodies, while the Council (member states) and the Commission initially favoured a lighter touch. The metal-on-metal hip scandal of 2012-2013, which killed the "self-certification" model for hip implants, provided the political momentum to overcome industry objections. A key turning point was the 2013 loss-of-innocence report by the UK's Standard Procedure Committee, which found that DePuy's hip system had been certified with "inadequate clinical evidence" under the MDD.

### How does the MDR differ from the US FDA's approach?

The FDA requires pre-market approval for high-risk devices (Class III) with independent agency review, while the MDR relies on notified bodies — private, for-profit organisations — designated by national governments. The FDA also mandates device tracking and recalls more aggressively. However, the MDR now requires significantly more clinical evidence for all devices than the MDD did, bringing it closer to the FDA's threshold. A key difference: the MDR applies to a broader range of "non-medical" devices like coloured contact lenses and liposuction cannulae, which are not regulated as medical devices in the US.
