## What is it?

Council Regulation (EC) No 834/2007 establishes the legal basis for organic farming in the European Union. It defines the principles, objectives, and production rules for organic agriculture and sets out a mandatory labelling system for organic products. The regulation replaced a patchwork of earlier national and EU-level rules, creating a single, coherent framework that applies across all member states.

The regulation covers all stages of production, processing, distribution, and import of organic products, including crops, livestock, aquaculture, and processed foods. It prohibits the use of synthetic pesticides, chemical fertilisers, genetically modified organisms (GMOs), and growth hormones in livestock. It mandates strict animal welfare standards and requires farmers to use natural methods like crop rotation and biological pest control.

Products meeting these standards can carry the EU organic logo (the green leaf with stars) and the term “organic” in any EU language. The regulation also sets out control systems, with national authorities and accredited private inspection bodies ensuring compliance. Amendments in 2018 (Regulation 2018/848) updated the framework, with full application from 2022.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a fragmented and confusing legal landscape that had left consumers uncertain and organic farmers at a competitive disadvantage. Before 2007, organic farming rules were scattered across several directives — most notably 2092/91/EEC — which had been patched together over a decade and a half. These earlier rules governed only unprocessed crops, leaving processed foods, livestock, and aquaculture in a regulatory grey zone where member states applied wildly different standards.

The structural tension was clear: the European Commission’s 2004 impact assessment found that the existing framework had created “legal uncertainty and unequal conditions of competition” for producers. A French organic dairy farmer, for instance, followed production rules that could differ significantly from those in Germany or Italy, yet both could label their milk as “organic.” The Commission’s own 2005 evaluation noted that this inconsistency was undermining consumer trust — the central asset of the organic sector.

The political coalition that drove Regulation 834/2007 forward was an unusual alliance of environmentally-conscious member states — led by Denmark, Austria, and Germany — and the burgeoning organic industry itself. The organic farming lobby group IFOAM EU (now part of IFOAM Organics Europe) pushed aggressively for harmonisation, arguing that the lack of EU-wide rules was stalling market growth. The European Parliament’s Agriculture Committee, chaired at the time by MEP Neil Parish of the UK, served as a key platform for these arguments, with hearings in 2005 and 2006 spotlighting anecdotal evidence of fraudulent labelling and consumer deception.

Opposition came primarily from conventional farming associations, particularly COPA-COGECA, the main umbrella organisation for European farmers. They warned that stricter organic rules would create a two-tier system, disadvantaging small farmers who could not afford the compliance costs. The European Crop Protection Association (now CropLife Europe) also lobbied against the ban on synthetic pesticides, arguing it would reduce yields and increase food prices. The final vote in the Council — 25 member states in favour, with only Malta abstaining after concerns about implementation costs — showed the overwhelming political momentum for reform.

Timing was everything. Regulation 834/2007 was drafted under the Commission of José Manuel Barroso, with Mariann Fischer Boel as Commissioner for Agriculture and Rural Development. Fischer Boel, a Danish liberal, was a vocal advocate for the organic sector. Earlier attempts to revise the organic rules had stalled in the 1990s due to industry resistance and the complexity of aligning different national schemes. But by the mid-2000s, three factors had shifted decisively. First, the 2003 report from the European Court of Auditors had been scathing: ‘Special Report No 5/2003’ found that “the control systems for organic products are not harmonised,” with one member state reporting no inspections at all in some years. The ECA concluded that the EU’s organic label was “not trustworthy.” Second, consumer demand for organic food was surging — Eurostat data shows the organic market in the EU grew by roughly 10% annually between 2002 and 2006. Third, the 2004 enlargement to ten new member states, many with weak agricultural enforcement, made the existing patchwork unworkable.

What changed specifically was a 2005 scandal in Germany, where an investigation by the consumer protection agency of North Rhine-Westphalia uncovered wine and honey sold as “organic” that contained residues of synthetic pesticides. The case was widely reported by Der Spiegel and other outlets. The German farmers’ association, the Deutscher Bauernverband, had initially been sceptical of stricter rules but after the scandal, its president Gerd Sonnleitner publicly shifted position, stating the sector needed “clear, enforceable rules to protect the integrity of the organic brand.”

The Commission’s original proposal, published in December 2005, had faced criticism from the European Parliament for being too weak on GMO contamination thresholds and for allowing too many national derogations. A December 2006 vote in Parliament approved the regulation with 482 votes in favour, 114 against, and 32 abstentions — a comfortable majority that reflected cross-party consensus after amendments strengthening animal welfare rules and adding a five-year review clause.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Council Regulation 2092/91/EEC establishes the first EU-wide rules for organic crop production and labelling.
- **2001** — The European Court of Auditors begins work on an audit of organic control systems; its 2003 report becomes damning.
- **2003** — EC Audit Special Report No 5/2003 exposes major gaps in organic enforcement; Commission begins internal discussions on reform.
- **2005** — The German organic wine scandal breaks; Commission publishes its proposal for a new regulation on December 21.
- **2006** — European Parliament votes on amendments; strong support for stricter GMO rules and animal welfare provisions.
- **2007** — Council formally adopts Regulation 834/2007 on June 28; enters into force on July 20.
- **2009** — Regulation 889/2008 implementing rules (detailed production methods and control procedures) applies from January 1.
- **2012** — Commission conducts its first review; finds market growth continues but enforcement still varies by member state.
- **2018** — Regulation 2018/848 adopted, replacing 834/2007 from January 1, 2022, with updated rules on imports, group certification, and fraud prevention.
- **2022** — New regulation becomes fully applicable; organic area in the EU reaches 16.9 million hectares, per Eurostat.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed the organic sector in Europe. By 2020, the EU’s organic market had grown to over €45 billion in retail sales, according to Eurostat and the Commission’s 2021 report on organic farming. The area of certified organic agricultural land in the EU more than tripled from 6.5 million hectares in 2007 to 16.9 million hectares by 2022, representing roughly 10% of total EU farmland.

However, enforcement remained uneven for years. A 2015 audit by the European Commission found that in Italy and Spain, some inspection bodies had not conducted physical checks on farms for over three years. The European Court of Justice ruling in Case C-652/17 (Nexus Ltd) in 2019 dealt with fraudulent organic labelling from third countries, and the Commission responded by tightening import rules in the 2018 revision.

Industry response was mixed. Large retailers like Carrefour and Tesco adopted the EU organic logo as a value proposition, while small farmers complained about the bureaucratic burden. The European Commission’s own 2013 evaluation of the regulation noted that “many micro-enterprises found the compliance costs disproportionate” — a finding that led to inclusion of group certification provisions in the 2018 update.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU regulate organic farming so comprehensively in Council Regulation (EC) No 834/2007?

The core reason was trust. The 2003 European Court of Auditors report showed that national control systems were failing, with consumers at risk of buying mislabelled products. Without harmonised EU rules, the organic label — which commands a price premium — became vulnerable to free-riding and fraud. Regulation 834/2007 was designed to make the label credible across all 27 member states.

### Why did Council Regulation (EC) No 834/2007 prohibit GMOs so strictly?

The prohibition reflected both the organic sector’s founding philosophy and intense consumer pressure. In the 1990s, the emergence of genetically modified crops raised alarm among organic farmers, who feared contamination of their fields through cross-pollination. The European Parliament during the 2004-2009 term, led by the Greens, successfully pushed for a zero-tolerance approach on GMO presence in organic products, despite opposition from the biotechnology industry represented by EuropaBio. The final text prohibits all GMO use, with a limit of 0.9% for accidental presence — the same threshold used for mandatory labelling of GM content in conventional food.

### Why did it take until 2007 for Council Regulation (EC) No 834/2007 to pass, given earlier organic rules existed?

Earlier attempts to update Regulation 2092/91/EEC had failed because the conventional farming lobby effectively blocked reform in the 1990s, arguing that the market was small enough to manage nationally. The shift came when the organic sector grew sufficiently large — with retail sales doubling between 2000 and 2005 — that the cost of fragmentation became unbearable. The German wine scandal of 2005 was the political lightning rod, proving that even wealthy member states could not enforce their own rules effectively.

### Does Council Regulation (EC) No 834/2007 apply to imported organic products?

Yes, but with significant complexity. Under Regulation 834/2007, imports could enter the EU organic market through two pathways: equivalence (where the exporting country’s rules were judged equivalent, not identical) or by direct compliance with EU rules. This created tensions with trading partners like the US, where the National Organic Program allowed different practices. The 2018 revision (Regulation 2018/848) moved toward a stricter ‘compliance’ model, requiring imported products to be produced under EU-level rules by 2026, a change that sparked protests from developing country exporters.
