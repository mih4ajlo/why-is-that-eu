## What is it?

The In Vitro Diagnostic Regulation (IVDR) is an EU regulation that sets new requirements for the design, clinical evidence, and market surveillance of in vitro diagnostic medical devices — defined as tests performed on human samples like blood, urine, or tissue outside the body. These include HIV tests, pregnancy tests, hepatitis screening, and cancer biomarker tests. The IVDR replaces the 1998 In Vitro Diagnostic Directive (98/79/EC) that had been criticised as too weak.

Under the IVDR, devices are classified into four risk categories (Class A, B, C, D), with higher-risk tests — such as those used for blood transfusion compatibility or detecting infectious diseases like HIV — requiring review by an independent, designated Notified Body before they can receive CE marking and be sold in the EU. The regulation also mandates a unique device identifier (UDI) system for traceability, a centralised EU database (EUDAMED) for device registration, and stricter clinical performance studies for high-risk products. The regulation entered into force on May 26, 2017, but had a staggered transitional period that ended in full application from May 26, 2022 for most devices.

## Why was it introduced?

The IVDR's origins lie not in a single scandal but in a cascade of failures that began with the PIP breast implant affair in 2010. While breast implants are medical devices covered by the Medical Devices Directive (93/42/EEC), the scandal — in which French company Poly Implant Prothèse used industrial-grade silicone instead of medical-grade — exposed systemic weaknesses in the EU's device oversight system. The European Parliament launched an inquiry, and its 2013 report, led by MEP Dagmar Roth-Behrendt, found that Notified Bodies (private companies that certify devices) were underfunded, understaffed, and had financial conflicts of interest with manufacturers. The report recommended a complete overhaul.

In 2011, the Commission, under Health Commissioner John Dalli, began drafting revisions. After Dalli resigned in a tobacco lobbying scandal, his successor Tonio Borg took over. But the real political acceleration came from the Mediator (benfluorex) diabetes drug scandal in France and the continued fallout from PIP. The Commission's 2012 impact assessment found that the existing directive had no requirement for clinical performance data for most IVDs, and that only 8% of IVDs required Notified Body scrutiny — the rest were self-certified by manufacturers. According to the Commission's 2016 explanatory memorandum, under the old system "about 80% of IVDs were placed on the market without any prior assessment of their safety or performance by an independent body."

The specific trigger for the IVDR, rather than just patchwork amendments, was the recognition that diagnostic tests were rapidly proliferating in complexity. In 2014, a study by the Joint Research Centre found that of 150 genetic tests on the market after 1998, 30% had no published clinical validity data. The Commission, now under President Jean-Claude Juncker and Health Commissioner Vytenis Andriukaitis, proposed the IVDR alongside the Medical Devices Regulation (MDR) in September 2012. The Parliament, led by rapporteur Glenis Willmott of the Socialists & Democrats, pushed for stricter rules, including a ban on Notified Bodies owned by consultants or industry.

Coalition dynamics were stark: the medical device industry, represented by MedTech Europe, warned that the new rules would cost manufacturers up to €300,000 per device and delay patient access. They lobbied for longer transition periods. The European Society of Human Genetics warned that the regulation could stifle rare-disease diagnostics. The strongest opposition came from Germany and the Netherlands, which feared the centralisation of oversight and higher costs for their small- and medium-sized diagnostic companies. The Council voted 26 in favour, 2 against (Germany, Netherlands), with 1 abstention (Luxembourg) in 2016. The Parliament adopted it with 577 votes in favour, 87 against, and 17 abstentions.

What broke the deadlock was the 2015 publication of the "Rücker Report" by the German government, which showed that German Notified Bodies had approved 40% of medical devices without any clinical data. Combined with the 2014 outbreak of Ebola in West Africa, which exposed the EU's reliance on non-EU diagnostic tests, the political momentum became unstoppable. The regulation was published in the Official Journal on May 5, 2017, setting a five-year transition period.

## Timeline

- **1998** — In Vitro Diagnostic Directive (98/79/EC) comes into force, establishing a light-touch regulatory system
- **2010** — PIP breast implant scandal breaks, exposing Notified Body failures
- **2012** — European Parliament inquiry (Roth-Behrendt report) published; Commission proposes both MDR and IVDR in September
- **2013** — Mediator drug scandal shocks France; industry warns of cost impacts
- **2014** — JRC study finds 30% of genetic tests lack clinical validity data; Ebola outbreak tests EU diagnostic capacity
- **2015** — German Rücker Report shows 40% of devices approved without clinical data
- **2016** — Council adopts IVDR (26-2); Parliament adopts overwhelmingly
- **2017** — IVDR published in Official Journal on 5 May; enters into force on 26 May
- **2022** — Full application date for most devices (26 May); Notified Body capacity crisis emerges
- **2023** — Commission proposes amendments to extend transition periods (adopted in December 2022)
- **2024** — Extended transitional periods in effect; EUDAMED still not fully operational

## Impact and consequences

The IVDR has had a profound and disruptive effect on the European diagnostic market. The most immediate and stark impact has been the collapse in Notified Body capacity. According to the Commission's own 2023 progress report, before the IVDR, 22 Notified Bodies were designated under the old IVD Directive. As of early 2024, only 8 Notified Bodies had been designated under the IVDR, with just 3 capable of handling the highest-risk Class D devices. This bottleneck has caused massive delays in certification.

Industry has been hit hard. MedTech Europe, the trade association, reported in 2022 that more than 80% of its member companies with IVD portfolios had at least one device facing certification delays. Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which make up 80% of the IVD sector (according to the Commission's 2021 SME panel survey), have been disproportionately affected. Some estimates from the sector suggest several hundred lower-risk devices, such as pregnancy and fertility tests, have been withdrawn from the EU market because manufacturers found it uneconomical to recertify under the stricter rules.

The regulation has also created a geographic divide. Countries with strong existing Notified Body infrastructure, like Germany (with TÜV SÜD and TÜV Rheinland) and the Netherlands (with DEKRA), have adapted better. Southern and Eastern European countries — such as Italy, Spain, and Poland — have struggled to retain diagnostic test availability. In 2023, the Polish Ministry of Health reported that several routine blood-typing tests for rare blood groups had become unavailable due to certification issues.

The biggest unintended consequence has been a crisis in the availability of "orphan diagnostics" — tests for rare diseases or small populations. The European Society of Human Genetics (ESHG) warned in 2020 that the regulation could make it "commercially unviable" to develop tests for conditions affecting fewer than 50,000 patients. Several European reference laboratories for rare diseases have reported having to use non-CE marked tests under national derogation procedures, effectively undermining the regulation's stated goal of harmonised safety.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the IVDR succeed where earlier attempts to regulate medical devices failed?

Earlier attempts in the 2000s to strengthen the IVD Directive were blocked by a coalition of industry (arguing for free movement and low costs) and national regulators (who wanted to maintain their role as certifiers). The PIP breast implant scandal in 2010-2011 broke this deadlock by moving the political debate from abstract market concerns to concrete patient harm. The 2013 Roth-Behrendt report in the European Parliament provided a credible, documented account of regulatory failures, creating a "now rather than later" dynamic. The JRC study showing 30% of genetic tests lacked clinical validity data added a specific, evidence-based argument that the system was not fit for 21st-century diagnostics.

### Why did German and Dutch opposition fail to block the regulation?

Germany and the Netherlands opposed because they had large numbers of SME diagnostic companies (over 400 in Germany alone, according to 2016 industry data) and two of the busiest Notified Bodies. They argued that centralised EU oversight would increase costs and reduce flexibility. However, they were outnumbered by the 26 other Member States that favoured reform, driven by patient safety concerns. The qualified majority voting system in the Council (55% of states representing 65% of population meant Germany could be outvoted despite its population share. The German Health Ministry's own 2015 Rücker Report, which showed weak Notified Body performance, undercut the credibility of Germany's anti-regulation stance.

### Why did the IVDR cause a Notified Body shortage?

The existing Notified Bodies under the old directive were mostly for-profit private companies that certified 92% of IVDs without any pre-market review. Under the IVDR, Notified Bodies must be re-designated by national competent authorities against much stricter criteria, including needing specialised clinical staff (e.g., pathologists, geneticists) for each device category. Many Notified Bodies — such as the British Standards Institution (BSI) in the UK — announced they would not seek designation because the cost and complexity of meeting the new requirements was too high relative to the market size. According to the Commission's 2023 report, the average Notified Body designation process took 26 months, far longer than anticipated, leaving only 8 bodies ready by the 2022 deadline. The resulting backlog meant some high-risk IVDs waited 18 months for certification.

### Why did the EU extend the transition period in 2022?

By late 2021, it was clear the Notified Body bottleneck would prevent many existing devices from being certified by the May 2022 deadline. MedTech Europe and national health ministries (especially from Poland, Italy, and Spain) warned of diagnostic test shortages. The Commission, under Health Commissioner Stella Kyriakides, proposed an amendment extending the transition period to May 2027 for lower-risk devices and May 2028 for medium-risk ones. The Parliament and Council adopted it in December 2022. The extension was controversial: patient safety groups argued it undermined the regulation's core aim of improving safety, while industry said it was necessary to avoid a public health crisis.
