## What is it?

The Payment Services Regulation (PSR) is a proposed EU regulation put forward by the European Commission in June 2023. Unlike the existing Payment Services Directive 2 (PSD2), which each member state transposed differently, a regulation applies directly and uniformly in all 27 countries. The PSR is paired with a new Payment Services Directive 3 (PSD3) that handles licensing and supervisory elements that still require national legislation.

The PSR represents the EU’s “open banking 2.0” effort. It tightens rules on payment initiation services and account information services — the two pillars of open banking introduced by PSD2 — and mandates stronger consumer protections against authorised push payment (APP) fraud. It also extends the scope to cover certain crypto‑asset transfers and requires banks to provide dedicated application programming interfaces (APIs) that meet common technical standards, rather than the fragmented implementations that emerged under PSD2.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was the rapid escalation of APP fraud, where criminals trick consumers into authorising payments to accounts they control. In the UK, APP fraud losses hit £583 million in 2021, according to UK Finance. The European Banking Authority (EBA) warned in its 2022 Opinion on fraud that APP fraud was the fastest‑growing type of payment fraud in the EU, yet PSD2 did not require banks to reimburse victims in cross‑border cases. A high‑profile incident in the Netherlands in 2022 — where a single fraud ring siphoned over €10 million from small businesses using fake invoices — pushed the issue onto the political agenda.

The technical implementation of PSD2 had also become a source of tension. Under PSD2, banks were required to open their customer data to third‑party providers (TPPs) via APIs, but many banks deliberately designed difficult‑to‑use interfaces or redirected TPPs to screen‑scraping, which posed security risks. The European Commission’s 2020 review of PSD2 found that “insufficient technical standardisation” had led to “inconsistent application” across member states, with some regulators enforcing the rules vigorously and others not at all. TPPs like Klarna and Revolut lobbied the Commission directly — Klarna’s CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski told *Politico* in 2022 that the lack of a common API standard was “killing open banking innovation at the point where it should be taking off.”

The political moment was also ripe. The Von der Leyen Commission’s digital finance strategy, published in September 2020, explicitly called for “open finance” to go beyond payments and into mortgages, savings, and insurance. That strategy was backed by a coalition of consumer groups (like BEUC) and fintech associations (like the European Fintech Association), who argued that PSD2 had delivered only half the promised benefits. Traditional banks, through the European Banking Federation (EBF), opposed the extension of liability rules for APP fraud, warning of higher compliance costs — but the series of high‑profile fraud cases and a 2022 European Parliament own‑initiative report on open banking (which passed 458 to 89) built enough political pressure for the Commission to move.

The one event that broke the deadlock on liability was the European Court of Justice’s March 2022 ruling in *Case C‑191/20* (the “Sofa Textile” case), which held that a payer’s mere negligence does not automatically exclude them from reimbursement under PSD2. The ruling created legal uncertainty for banks, making uniform EU rules more attractive to both industry and regulators.

## Timeline

- **2015** — PSD2 adopted; contained open banking provisions but left API standards to industry.
- **2018** — PSD2 transposition deadline; many member states delayed.
- **2020** — Commission publishes review of PSD2, flags fragmentation and fraud as key problems.
- **2020** — Digital finance strategy published; Commission announces intention to revise the payment services framework.
- **2022** — ECJ ruling on payer negligence (*Case C‑191/20*) undermines banks’ ability to deny reimbursement; EBA issues opinion on APP fraud.
- **2023** — Commission publishes the “retail payments package” on 28 June, including the PSR proposal, PSD3 proposal, and a regulation on open finance.
- **2024** — European Parliament and Council begin negotiations (trilogues) expected to conclude in 2025.

## Impact and consequences

If adopted, the PSR will have the most direct impact on three groups: banks, third‑party providers, and consumers.

For banks, the PSR removes the ability to refuse TPPs access with custom APIs that deviate from a standardised specification. The Commission’s 2023 impact assessment estimated that API harmonisation would cut developer costs by 30–40% across the industry. Banks are also required to refund consumers for APP fraud up to — initially — 50% of the loss, rising to 100% after a transition period. The European Banking Federation estimated that total fraud‑related costs could rise by €1.5 billion per year across the EU, though the Commission argued this is less than the current cost of non‑reimbursement disputes.

For TPPs and fintechs, the regulation provides a single rulebook for open banking across 27 countries, eliminating the need to deal with 27 different national competent authorities for cross‑border services. The Commission cited a 2022 study by Oliver Wyman that found open banking had the potential to unlock €15 billion in annual economic value by 2030 if harmonised.

Consumers gain a stronger right to reimbursement for fraud and clearer terms for payment initiation. The PSR also extends strong customer authentication (SCA) exceptions to small payments (under €50) for contactless transactions, a change welcomed by retailers but criticised by consumer groups for potentially increasing low‑value fraud.

The main implementation challenge is timeline. Member states that have failed to effectively enforce PSD2 (notably Bulgaria, Romania, and Poland, according to the EBA’s 2022 compliance reports) will need to adapt to the regulation’s direct effect. The Commission has proposed a phased application: 18 months after entry into force for most provisions, and 36 months for the new APP fraud liability rules.

## Key questions answered

### What does the EU's new open banking law mean for everyday payment users?

The new rules — once adopted — will mean that consumers who fall victim to authorised push payment fraud (where they are tricked into approving a payment) will be entitled to a refund from their bank, shared equally between the sending and receiving bank. Currently, many banks in the EU refuse reimbursement if the consumer was “grossly negligent”. The PSR also makes it easier to use payment initiation services (like checking your account balance via a budgeting app), because all banks must provide a standardised technical interface — no more “screen scraping” that stops working when a bank changes its website.

### Why don't all European banks already give third‑party apps the same access?

Under PSD2, banks were required to open their data, but the law let them design their own technical interfaces. Many banks created deliberately slow or hard‑to‑use APIs (application programming interfaces) to discourage fintechs from connecting. The PSR replaces this with a single, mandatory technical standard that all banks must follow, enforced by direct regulation from the European Banking Authority (EBA) rather than left to national supervisors.

### Will the EU's new payments regulation cover cryptocurrency payments?

Yes, partially. The PSR extends the definition of “payment services” to cover transfers of crypto‑assets as defined under the Markets in Crypto‑Assets Regulation (MiCA), but only when the crypto‑asset is used as a means of payment (not as an investment). Stablecoin transfers will also fall under the PSR’s fraud reimbursement rules, which traditional crypto platforms currently do not offer. This extension responds to the European Central Bank’s 2022 warning that unregulated crypto‑asset payments could bypass existing consumer protections and increase fraud risks.
