## What is it?

The European Green Bond Regulation, adopted in November 2023, establishes a voluntary standard for bonds to carry the "European Green Bond" (EuGB) label. The label is only available for bonds whose proceeds are fully allocated to economic activities that meet the EU Taxonomy’s stringent environmental criteria — meaning they contribute substantially to one of six environmental objectives, do significant harm to others, and comply with minimum social safeguards. Issuers must publish detailed pre-issuance fact sheets and post-allocation reports, all verified by an external reviewer registered with the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA).

The regulation does not ban other types of green bonds. Bonds labelled "green" under existing market standards (like the Climate Bonds Initiative or voluntary frameworks) can still be sold in the EU. But as of 2024, only a bond carrying the EuGB label can claim to be fully Taxonomy-aligned in its name. This creates a two-tier market: a regulated, gold-standard label backed by EU law, sitting alongside less regulated "green" self-labelling.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation arrived because of a fundamental structural tension: the explosive growth of the green bond market had no single, legally enforceable definition of what "green" meant. By 2021, the global green bond market had surpassed $500 billion in annual issuance, according to the Climate Bonds Initiative. Yet a 2022 study by the European Commission's Joint Research Centre found that up to 50% of bonds marketed as "green" contained activities that were potentially non-green — a figure the Commission described as "a significant risk to investor trust." The problem was not individual bad actors but the lack of a common rulebook: without a mandatory standard, bonds funding everything from solar farms to natural gas pipeline retrofits could all call themselves "green."

The immediate trigger was the 2023 controversy over the EU Taxonomy's inclusion of nuclear energy. In February 2023, the European Commission presented a complementary delegated act labelling certain nuclear and gas activities as "transitional" or "enabling" under the Taxonomy. This sparked a dramatic political battle. In the European Parliament, a group of MEPs led by Greens/EFA rapporteur Bas Eickhout (Netherlands) tried to reject the act entirely. The vote on June 14, 2022, was exceptionally close: 278 voted to reject the act, 328 opposed, and 33 abstained — falling 51 votes short of the 329 needed to block it. The gas and nuclear inclusion was also the main reason the market's largest potentially Taxonomy-aligned issuer — Germany's KfW, which had issued over €80 billion in green bonds — publicly refused to label its bonds under EU rules until the Taxonomy clarified what qualified.

The coalition that got the Green Bond Regulation passed was unusually broad — centrist and centre-right MEPs from EPP, Renew Europe, and S&D backed the core idea of a voluntary label with strict Taxonomy alignment — but the nuclear debate nearly fractured it. Industry groups split: the Association for Financial Markets in Europe (AFME) broadly supported the label as market-building, while the German banking association BdB feared it would create a two-speed market that penalised German banks already using voluntary standards. The key compromise, brokered by rapporteur Eickhout and shadow rapporteurs from EPP and Renew, was that the label remained voluntary, and that bonds financing nuclear or gas activities could still be sold as "green" — but only without the EuGB label.

Timing also mattered. The regulation was proposed in July 2021, just as the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen was launching the "NextGenerationEU" recovery bond programme — the EU's own debut as a green bond issuer. In October 2021, the Commission issued its first green bond, raising €12 billion, followed by a second of €12 billion in February 2022. If the EU itself was going to issue green bonds at scale (it planned to issue up to €250 billion in green bonds by 2026), it needed a credible standard to enforce. The Commission could not credibly enforce a green label on others if its own bonds lacked a rigorous framework.

What changed from earlier failed attempts? Previous efforts to create an EU-wide green bond standard, dating back to 2018 Technical Expert Group reports, stalled because the market — led by the International Capital Market Association (ICMA)'s Green Bond Principles — resisted binding regulation. Three events broke the logjam: first, the Taxonomy itself became law in July 2020, creating the underlying "green" definition the bond label could reference. Second, the 2021 EU green bond issuance turned the Commission from a regulator into a market participant — it now had skin in the game. Third, the 2022-2023 energy crisis and gas/nuclear debate showed that without a label, the market would fragment into competing, unverifiable claims, exactly the scenario the Capital Markets Union programme — a flagship Juncker and von der Leyen priority — was designed to prevent.

## Timeline

- **2018** — The European Commission's Technical Expert Group (TEG) on Sustainable Finance publishes preliminary work on an EU green bond standard, based on existing market practices.
- **July 2020** — EU Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852/EU) enters into force, providing the first comprehensive classification system for environmentally sustainable economic activities in EU law.
- **July 2021** — Commission proposes the European Green Bond Regulation, under the leadership of Commissioner Mairead McGuinness (Financial Services).
- **October 2021** — The Commission issues its first NextGenerationEU green bond for €12 billion, becoming a major issuer itself.
- **February 2022** — Commission issues second NextGenerationEU green bond of €12 billion.
- **June 2022** — European Parliament votes narrowly (328-278) not to reject the Taxonomy delegated act including nuclear and gas, clearing the way for final negotiations on the bond label.
- **October 2023** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement in trilogue negotiations.
- **November 30, 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/2631 is adopted and enters into force.
- **December 21, 2024** — The regulation becomes fully applicable, allowing issuers to apply for the EuGB label.
- **June 2025** (projected) — First EuGB-labelled bonds expected to be issued, as external reviewers register with ESMA under the new regime.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's primary impact is on the market structure of green bonds in the EU, rather than a direct regulatory burden. By creating a voluntary label, it leaves the vast majority of green bond issuers — the roughly 85% of EU green bonds that are not fully Taxonomy-aligned, according to the Commission's 2023 impact assessment — unaffected unless they choose to comply. The immediate effect was signalling: within weeks of the regulation's adoption, major sovereign issuers like France (a regular green bond issuer since 2017) and Germany's KfW began publicly assessing whether to adopt the label. KfW, which had issued €4.5 billion in green bonds in 2023 alone, announced in February 2024 that it would consider applying for the EuGB label for a portion of its issuance, conditional on clear external verification procedures.

The main industry response was not resistance but bifurcation. ICMA, the leading market standard-setter, adopted updated Green Bond Principles in June 2024 explicitly noting the EuGB label as "a regime complementing market standards," rather than competing with them. Meanwhile, the Climate Bonds Initiative warned that the voluntary nature could create consumer confusion — investors might assume "green" in a bond name is always the EuGB standard when it is not. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) echoed this concern in a 2023 position paper, calling for mandatory adoption of the label for all public-facing green bonds within five years.

The most significant enforcement issue to date has been verification. The regulation requires external reviewers to be registered with ESMA and subject to its oversight. As of mid-2024, ESMA had begun a three-phase consultation process to define the technical standards for reviewers, with the first deadline for reviewer registration in December 2024. Approximately 20 external reviewers — including Sustainalytics, ISS ESG, and DNV — submitted expressions of interest in the first quarter of 2024. The Commission's own evaluation projected that full implementation would cost the market an additional €15-25 million annually in verification fees, which it deemed "proportional" given a market size of roughly €100 billion annually.

The financial sector has been most affected, particularly asset managers and pension funds that had already issued "Article 8" or "Article 9" funds under the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR). For these funds, the EuGB label offers a clear path to verify their green bond holdings to their own SFDR disclosures — a significant compliance simplification.

## Key questions answered

### Can a company sell a bond as "green" in the EU without it being a European Green Bond?

Yes. The regulation is entirely voluntary. Any issuer can still sell a bond and label it "green" using existing market standards (like ICMA's Green Bond Principles) or self-defined criteria, as long as it does not use the specific European Green Bond (EuGB) label. Only bonds that carry the EuGB label must meet full EU Taxonomy alignment. This means an investor can still buy a "green" bond that funds gas or nuclear projects, but that bond cannot be called a European Green Bond.

### Does the EU Green Bond label allow bonds that fund nuclear or gas power?

A European Green Bond cannot fund new nuclear or gas power plants, because those activities do not meet the Taxonomy's "do no significant harm" criteria for the label. However, an ordinary "green" bond sold to investors in the EU can still fund nuclear or gas under the Taxonomy's complementary delegated act — it just cannot use the EuGB label. This was the core political compromise that allowed the regulation to pass in late 2023, as it avoided a direct ban on nuclear and gas financing while creating a pure label that excludes them.

### Will existing green bonds need to be re-issued to become European Green Bonds?

No. The regulation applies only to bonds issued on or after the application date of December 21, 2024. A bond issued before that date and marketed as "green" will not automatically become a EuGB. If an issuer wants to label an existing bond as a European Green Bond, it would need to cancel the old bond, issue a new one with the required Taxonomy-aligned prospectus and external verification, and meet all post-issuance reporting obligations — a costly process few issuers are expected to undertake. The label is only meaningful for new issuance.

### Why did European green bonds need a separate standard at all?

Before 2024, the green bond market was a patchwork of voluntary standards, each with different criteria. A bond sold as "green" under ICMA's principles could fund activities that a bond under the Climate Bonds Standard would prohibit, making it impossible for investors to compare offerings reliably. The lack of a single enforceable definition created a risk of widespread greenwashing, which undermined investor confidence precisely when the EU needed massive private capital to finance its €1 trillion European Green Deal. The EuGB label provides a single, auditable, legally enforceable benchmark against which all other "green" claims can be measured — even if the label itself is voluntary.
