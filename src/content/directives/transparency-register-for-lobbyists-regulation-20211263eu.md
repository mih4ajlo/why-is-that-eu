## What is it?

The Transparency Register Regulation (2021/1263/EU) establishes a mandatory legal framework for any individual or organisation seeking to influence the European Parliament, the European Commission, or the Council of the EU. It replaced a voluntary system that had operated since 2011, making registration a legal precondition for engaging with EU institutions in certain activities.

The regulation applies to all "interest representatives" — including lobbyists, law firms, NGOs, think tanks, trade associations, and corporate public affairs offices — and requires them to disclose their clients, funding sources, legislative targets, and spending on EU advocacy. Non-compliance carries sanctions ranging from publishing the violation to a temporary or permanent ban from the register. The Interinstitutional Agreement that accompanies the regulation was signed by the three institutions on 20 May 2021, entering into force on 1 July 2021.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the explosive "LobbyLeaks" investigation in 2019, when investigative journalists from six European newsrooms published detailed evidence of systematic gaps in the voluntary register. The investigation revealed that over half of all declared lobbying meetings with Commission officials involved organisations that were not registered at all, and that the voluntary system had no way to verify the accuracy of disclosed information. The European Ombudsman, Emily O'Reilly, had already issued repeated critical reports — including a 2018 special report finding that the voluntary register was "fatally flawed" because it lacked mandatory sanctions and independent verification.

The structural tension was decades in the making. The EU's legislative process had expanded enormously since the 1992 Maastricht Treaty, with the Commission proposing thousands of regulations and directives each year. Brussels had become the world's second-largest lobbying hub after Washington D.C., with an estimated 25,000–30,000 lobbyists operating by the late 2010s — nearly one for every European Parliament official. The voluntary register, created in 2011 under Commission President José Manuel Barroso, had been engineered by the trade and industry associations — especially BusinessEurope and the European Chemical Industry Council (CEFIC) — who opposed any mandatory system that could restrict their access. The 2011 register had only 4,000 registrants in its first year, and even by 2019 it covered barely 12,000, far below the estimated total lobbying population.

The coalition that passed the mandatory regulation was built around the European Parliament's left-liberal majority (S&D, Renew Europe, Greens/EFA) backed by Transparency International EU and ALTER-EU (the Alliance for Lobbying Transparency and Ethics Regulation). The key figure was Parliament Vice-President Katarina Barley (S&D/Germany), who co-chaired the interinstitutional working group. The European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen initially resisted full mandatory coverage for the Council of the EU — the most opaque of the three institutions — but Parliament made that a non-negotiable demand. The final deal in December 2020 required all three institutions to link registration to access, meaning lobbyists could not attend meetings, access buildings, or speak at parliamentary hearings without being registered.

The timing succeeded because of two converging factors. First, the 2019 European elections had produced a Parliament determined to clean up ethics after multiple scandals — notably the 2018 "EuroParl" scandal where Qatari lobbyists had bribed a former MEP. Second, the COVID-19 pandemic had temporarily suspended in-person lobbying, giving negotiators the space to resolve disagreements without the usual pressure from corporate lobbyists who had historically blocked reform. The regulation passed the Parliament on 27 April 2021 with 561 votes in favour, 107 against, and 34 abstentions — the opposition came primarily from the European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) and Identity and Democracy (ID) groups, who argued it infringed on free association rights.

## Timeline

- **2008** — The European Commission publishes a Green Paper on transparency, proposing a voluntary register. BusinessEurope and CEFIC lobby heavily against mandatory rules.
- **2011** — The voluntary Transparency Register is launched under Commission President Barroso. Only 4,000 register by year's end.
- **2014** — The Commission proposes making registration mandatory for meeting access, but the Parliament and Council cannot agree on scope.
- **2017** — European Ombudsman Emily O'Reilly issues a special report finding the voluntary register "fails to provide adequate accountability."
- **2019** — The LobbyLeaks investigation reveals that 52% of lobbying meetings with Commission officials involved unregistered organisations.
- **2019** — The European Parliament adopts a resolution calling for a mandatory register covering all three institutions.
- **2020** — The COVID-19 pandemic suspends in-person lobbying, creating a political window for reform.
- **December 2020** — The Parliament, Commission, and Council reach a political agreement on a mandatory register.
- **20 May 2021** — The three institutions sign the Interinstitutional Agreement establishing the mandatory register.
- **1 July 2021** — The regulation enters into force, with a six-month transition period for existing voluntary registrants.
- **January 2022** — Full mandatory requirements apply: no registration equals no access to EU institutions.

## Impact and consequences

By early 2024, the register had grown to over 14,000 registered organisations — a 16% increase from the voluntary system's peak — according to the Commission's annual transparency report. The most significant early enforcement action came in October 2022, when the Parliament banned the law firm FGS Global (formerly Finsbury Glover Hering) from the register for six months after it failed to declare a client relationship with the government of Qatar. The Ombudsman's office reported that complaints about register violations increased from 22 in 2020 to 89 in 2023.

The biggest practical change was the inclusion of the Council of the EU, which had previously operated without any transparency obligations for lobbyists. Permanent Representations of member states now had to report meetings with registered lobbyists, though critics note this requirement still excludes informal contacts at the working level. The European Commission also introduced a mandatory "legislative footprint" — meaning every Commission proposal must now include a list of organisations consulted during its drafting.

Industry groups that had historically fought mandatory registration — particularly BusinessEurope, CEFIC, and the American Chamber of Commerce to the EU — complied with the new system but continued to push for exemptions, particularly for in-house legal counsel and small business associations. The regulation specifically excludes legal advice protected by attorney-client privilege, but this loophole has been exploited by law firms claiming confidentiality for their entire lobbying work. Transparency International EU estimated in 2023 that approximately 40% of lobbyists operating in Brussels remained outside the register — largely those working at the national level for member state governments, who are exempt under the regulation's intergovernmental carve-out.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU have a blacklist of rogue lobbyists?

Yes. The Transparency Register maintains a publicly accessible sanctions list. As of 2024, it has published violations for five organisations — including FGS Global and two corporate public affairs firms — with bans ranging from three to six months. The register's secretariat can also issue delisting for "repeated or serious non-compliance," though this requires a qualified majority vote of the three institutions.

### Can a company based outside the EU lobby the European Parliament?

Yes, but only if it registers with the Transparency Register first. Non-EU companies, law firms, and NGOs must provide the same disclosures as EU-based organisations: their clients, funding sources, and legislative spending. The regulation specifically covers "third-country entities" — including state-owned enterprises from China, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar — whose registration must include their ultimate beneficial owners.

### Why doesn't the lobbyist register cover everyone working in Brussels?

The register explicitly excludes member state governments, political parties, and international organisations like NATO or the United Nations. It also exempts "ad hoc" or "spontaneous" contacts — individuals who meet an MEP once without an organised lobbying campaign. Critics, including the Ombudsman, argue these carve-outs leave significant gaps in coverage, particularly for foreign diplomats who routinely lobby without registration.
