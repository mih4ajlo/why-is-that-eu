## What is it?

The Anti-Coercion Instrument (ACI) is an EU regulation that empowers the European Commission to respond to "economic coercion" by non-EU countries — defined as a third‑country measure that interferes in the legitimate sovereign choices of the EU or a member state by applying pressure on trade, investment, or other economic activities. The ACI creates a multi‑step escalation process: from consultation and compensation demands, to countermeasures such as tariffs, quotas, restrictions on services, intellectual property limitations, and suspension of preferential market access.

The instrument is designed to be reactive and proportionate. Before imposing countermeasures, the Commission must notify the offending country and seek a negotiated solution. If no resolution is reached within a set period, the EU can apply measures calibrated to the level of coercion. The regulation also allows for temporary measures in exceptional circumstances. Any countermeasure must comply with the EU's obligations under the World Trade Organization (WTO), unless the Commission determines that invoking the "security exception" is justified.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was China’s trade blockade of Lithuania in 2021. In July 2021, Lithuania opened a de facto embassy in Taiwan — a "Representative Office" — in defiance of Beijing’s warnings. China retaliated in December 2021 by blocking Lithuanian goods from entering Chinese customs, halting customs clearance for products containing Lithuanian components, and pressuring other EU companies to cut ties with Lithuanian suppliers. Vilnius reported that exports to China, which had been worth roughly €300 million annually, effectively ceased overnight.

Lithuania’s foreign minister and Prime Minister called for EU solidarity, arguing that China was punishing a member state for exercising its sovereignty — and that the EU’s existing trade defense instruments were not designed for such geopolitical use of trade leverage. Within weeks, the European Commission, led by Executive Vice‑President Valdis Dombrovskis (trade commissioner), began drafting a legislative response. Dombrovskis stated publicly that the EU "cannot be economically coerced" and needed a “new tool to ensure that the EU and its member states can act quickly and decisively when subjected to coercion."

The structural tension was clear: the EU had no dedicated legal framework for countering economic coercion that fell short of formal sanctions. WTO dispute settlement was too slow (years) and its remedies too limited to stop short‑term coercive pressure. The 2021 incident broke a long‑standing deadlock within the EU. Earlier attempts to create an anti‑coercion instrument had stalled over fears that it would be seen as protectionist or trigger retaliatory trade wars. But the Lithuania case created a coalition of member states — led by the Baltic states, Poland, and backed by the Commission — that argued inaction would undermine the EU’s geopolitical credibility. Larger member states like Germany, whose companies had been caught in the crossfire (Volkswagen’s Lithuanian‑sourced components were delayed at Chinese ports), also supported the instrument, though they urged caution to avoid escalation.

The Parliament, led by the International Trade Committee rapporteur Bernd Lange (S&D, Germany), pushed for a broad scope and rapid‑response mechanisms. The Council reached a general approach in November 2022, with a qualified majority — only Hungary and some smaller states expressed reservations, fearing damage to bilateral relations with China. The final text was adopted in May 2023 and entered into force in December 2023.

What changed? The Lithuania incident turned economic coercion from a theoretical risk into a concrete, disruptive reality. The EU determined that without a credible deterrent, China – or other powers – could repeat the tactic against any member state that deviated from Beijing’s political preferences. The ACI was designed to credibly threaten escalation costs that would outweigh the benefits of coercive pressure.

## Timeline

- **July 2021** — Lithuania opens a representative office in Taiwan, drawing Chinese warnings.
- **December 2021** — China blocks Lithuanian goods at customs; EU companies report supply chains being disrupted due to Lithuanian content.
- **December 8, 2021** — European Commission proposes the Anti-Coercion Instrument as part of a broader trade policy review.
- **June 2022** — European Parliament adopts its position, strengthening the instrument’s scope to include intellectual property and investor protections.
- **November 2022** — EU Council agrees on a general approach with a qualified majority.
- **April 2023** — Trilogue negotiations between Commission, Council, and Parliament conclude.
- **May 10, 2023** — European Parliament votes in favor of the final text (578 in favor, 22 against, 51 abstentions).
- **November 22, 2023** — Regulation 2023/2675 adopted by the Council.
- **December 23, 2023** — Regulation enters into force.
- **December 28, 2023** — Regulation becomes fully applicable.

## Impact and consequences

As of early 2024, the EU has not formally invoked the Anti-Coercion Instrument against any country. The Lithuanian dispute was partially de‑escalated before the ACI came into force: China quietly resumed some trade with Lithuania in 2023, though mutual tensions remain high. The primary impact has been deterrent. The Commission has signaled that the ACI creates a legal baseline that it can invoke within weeks if a similar situation arises.

Business groups, particularly German and Dutch exporters with Chinese supply chains, have called for restraint. The European Association of Automotive Suppliers (CLEPA) warned that retaliatory tariffs could harm EU companies and escalate into a broader trade war. However, the instrument’s design — requiring a finding of coercion and an attempt at consultation — gives EU industry a measure of predictability.

The ACI has also influenced EU trade negotiations: the Commission has used it as a bargaining chip in discussions with China over market access and with the United States over the Inflation Reduction Act. It has been described by some trade analysts as a "nuclear option" that strengthens Europe’s hand in economic diplomacy.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU block Chinese imports if China blocks goods from a single EU country?

Yes — the Anti-Coercion Instrument allows the EU to impose countermeasures that target the coercing country broadly, including tariffs on its exports, suspension of market access for services, and restrictions on intellectual property licensing. The countermeasures are not limited to the sector affected by the coercion; they can be applied across the whole economy to inflict proportionate economic pressure.

### Does this instrument violate WTO rules?

The EU argues it does not. The ACI is designed to act as a "suspension of concessions" under the WTO’s security exception (Article XXI of GATT) when invoked in response to geopolitical coercion. The Commission’s legal service maintains that the instrument is consistent with WTO law because it is a reactive measure to an emergency in international relations. However, if the EU ever uses the ACI against a WTO member, that member could challenge it, creating a contentious legal dispute.

### Why was this law created specifically after the Lithuania incident?

Before 2021, the EU had no dedicated tool to counter economic coercion that was not covered by sanctions regimes (which require a foreign policy decision). The Lithuania case was the first time a large trading partner — China — used targeted trade restrictions to punish a member state for a democratic foreign policy choice. The Commission and most member states concluded that the EU’s unity and credibility required a structural response, not one-off diplomatic interventions. The ACI fills that gap by providing a legally defined, predictable process.

### Could the EU use the ACI against the United States?

Theoretically, yes — the regulation does not name any specific country. In practice, it is unlikely because the EU and the US are close allies and have other dispute‑resolution mechanisms. The Commission has stated that the ACI is intended for cases of "undue pressure" on the EU’s sovereignty, not for normal trade disputes. The US measures under the Inflation Reduction Act, for example, have been addressed through the WTO and bilateral talks, not the ACI.
