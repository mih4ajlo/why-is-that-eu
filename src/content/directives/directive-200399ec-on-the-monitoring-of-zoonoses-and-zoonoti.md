## What is it?

The Zoonoses Directive (2003/99/EC) requires EU member states to set up systematic surveillance of zoonotic agents—bacteria, viruses, parasites that pass from animals to humans—in food animals, food products, and animal feed. It obliges national authorities to collect and report data on outbreaks, and gives the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) a coordinating role to produce annual EU summary reports.

The Salmonella Control Regulation (2160/2003/EC) goes further: it sets legally binding national reduction targets for salmonella in breeding flocks of chickens, laying hens, broilers, turkeys, and in slaughter pigs. Achieving the targets requires testing, culling infected flocks, and hygiene measures. If a member state fails to meet its target, the European Commission can suspend trade of the affected animals or products. The regulation also requires national control programmes to be approved by the Commission.

Together, they turned a patchwork of voluntary reporting into a harmonised, enforceable public health surveillance system.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a series of food safety scandals that shattered public confidence in the EU’s food chain. The 1990s BSE crisis in cattle had already exposed catastrophic failures in monitoring and communication. Then, in 1999, the Belgian dioxin crisis—where contaminated feed caused PCB contamination in poultry, eggs, and pork—forced the resignation of two Belgian health ministers and led to a temporary collapse in poultry sales across Europe. That same year, salmonella outbreaks were at a peak: according to the European Commission’s 2000 White Paper on Food Safety, salmonella alone caused more than 100,000 reported human cases annually in the EU (the true number was likely far higher). The UK’s 1998–99 salmonella outbreak linked to eggs and chicken had become a political embarrassment.

The structural tension was clear: zoonoses were the most frequent cause of foodborne disease, yet national surveillance was voluntary, inconsistent, and often non-existent. Denmark and Sweden had already introduced aggressive salmonella control programmes in the 1990s and halved human cases, while other member states (notably southern countries like Italy and Spain) had far lower detection rates—not because they had less disease, but because they tested less. The existing directive (92/117/EEC) only covered salmonella in breeding poultry and had no targets for other flocks or species.

The coalition that pushed for stronger rules was led by Health and Consumer Protection Commissioner David Byrne, an Irish lawyer appointed in 1999. Byrne, supported by the European Parliament’s environment committee (then chaired by Caroline Jackson, UK Conservative), argued that the creation of EFSA in 2002 gave the EU a scientific body capable of setting evidence-based targets. The 2000 White Paper on Food Safety, which Byrne championed, explicitly called for “a comprehensive EU-wide monitoring of zoonoses” and “targets for the reduction of salmonella in certain animal populations.” The northern member states (Denmark, Sweden, the Netherlands, Finland) formed the driving force, backed by the consumer lobby group BEUC, which had been demanding mandatory labelling of eggs and poultry from high-risk sources.

Opposition came from southern member states, especially Spain and Italy, which feared the cost of mass testing and culling on their poultry industries—sectors with many small, family-run farms. The European livestock federations (COPA-COGECA) warned that mandatory targets would drive up prices and undermine competitiveness against non-EU imports. In the Council of Ministers, a qualified majority was only secured after the Commission agreed to a staggered timeline: targets for breeding hens in 2004, laying hens in 2005, broilers in 2007, turkeys and pigs in later phases. The Parliament, in its first reading, wanted stricter timelines and had also pushed for mandatory reporting of all foodborne outbreaks to a central database, a provision that survived.

The timing was critical: the BSE crisis had broken the taboo of EU intervention in national food safety. The 2002 General Food Law (Regulation 178/2002) had already created the legal foundation for traceability and risk analysis. The Zoonoses Directive and Salmonella Regulation were the next steps—giving EFSA the data it needed to produce the EU’s first harmonised zoonoses report in 2005, and giving the Commission the legal cudgel to force member states to act. Without these laws, the EU would have continued to count deaths without a systematic way to prevent them.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Belgian dioxin crisis; European Parliament launches inquiry into BSE and food safety.
- **12 January 2000** — European Commission publishes White Paper on Food Safety, calling for mandatory zoonoses monitoring and reduction targets.
- **2002** — Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 (General Food Law) establishes EFSA and the rapid alert system (RASFF).
- **5 November 2002** — Commission proposals for Directive 2003/99/EC and Regulation 2160/2003/EC are tabled.
- **13 May 2003** — European Parliament approves the proposals in second reading (after conciliation on timelines).
- **16 June 2003** — Council of Ministers formally adopts both acts.
- **1 July 2003** — Directive 2003/99/EC enters into force (transposition deadline 12 months later).
- **1 January 2004** — Regulation 2160/2003/EC begins to apply; first reduction targets for breeding flocks of chickens set.
- **2004–2006** — Phased implementation for laying hens, broilers, turkeys.
- **2007** — First full EFSA-ECDC annual zoonoses report based on harmonised data shows human salmonellosis down 35% from 2004 baseline.
- **2011** — Regulation (EU) No 1086/2011 sets salmonella targets for slaughter pigs (enters force 2012).
- **2018** — Commission report to Parliament concludes human salmonella cases have fallen by 56% compared to 2004–2006 baseline; targets now extended to Campylobacter.

## Impact and consequences

The laws transformed how the EU manages foodborne disease. By 2010, human salmonellosis cases in the EU had fallen by over 50% from the early 2000s, according to EFSA’s annual reports. The reduction was most dramatic in poultry meat: in 2004, 25% of broiler flocks tested positive for salmonella; by 2014, that figure had dropped to 2%, meeting the target set in the regulation. The Swedish model of on-farm control, once considered an expensive luxury, became the EU standard.

Industry faced significant costs. Poultry producers in southern Europe, many of which had not previously tested for salmonella, had to invest in hygiene barriers, feed treatment (e.g. organic acids), and, if an outbreak occurred, compulsory slaughter of the entire flock. The Commission estimated the total cost of the programme at around €300 million per year across the EU, but calculated health savings of €720 million annually from reduced medical bills and lost work days (2003 impact assessment). Smaller farms struggled most; in Spain and Italy, hundreds of small-layer farms closed rather than comply, accelerating a trend toward consolidation that the industry has blamed on the regulation.

Member states that dragged their heels faced enforcement. In 2006, Greece, Spain, and France were warned by the Commission for failing to submit timely control plans; Spain’s plan was ultimately rejected in 2007 and had to be resubmitted. Trade suspensions were rare but real: in 2008, the Commission banned intra-EU trade of live turkeys from Poland after it failed to meet the 2007 target for breeding flocks for two consecutive years. The Polish poultry industry quickly lobbied the government to intensify controls, and the ban was lifted the following year.

The laws also created a new monitoring architecture. EFSA’s annual zoonoses report now covers more than 20 diseases, including Campylobacter, Listeria, and hepatitis E. The regulation’s success led to the EU’s 2011 “Farm to Fork” strategy, which extended reduction targets to Campylobacter, the now most common foodborne infection.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU monitor diseases that jump from animals to humans?

EU countries must test animals at key points—farms, slaughterhouses, and feed mills—and report all positive salmonella results to EFSA. The agency then publishes an annual report that tracks human cases, outbreaks, and where the infection came from. This system, built by the Zoonoses Directive, makes it possible to spot trends and target controls. Before 2003, many countries only looked for outbreaks after people got sick.

### What are the salmonella reduction targets for poultry in the EU?

The regulation sets a maximum percentage of salmonella-positive flocks for each type of poultry. For example, by 2007, no more than 1% of breeding chicken flocks could test positive for five target strains. For laying hens, the target was 2%. National authorities must achieve these targets within a deadline, or the Commission can restrict the sale of eggs or meat from that member state. The targets are periodically tightened.

### Why did the EU need a separate law just for salmonella?

Salmonella was, and remains, the second most common cause of foodborne outbreaks in the EU (after Campylobacter). Before 2003, each country set its own rules: Denmark had near‑zero tolerance, while others barely tested. This gave salmonella‑producing countries a competitive advantage, because they could sell cheaper meat abroad. A single EU law levelled the playing field while cutting human illness by over half within a decade, as EFSA’s reports show.

### Has the EU’s salmonella control programme also reduced other foodborne infections?

Yes—the same surveillance systems and hygiene measures have helped drive down infections from Campylobacter, Listeria, and Yersinia. The regulation’s success inspired a sister law in 2011 that set targets for reducing Campylobacter in broiler chickens. The “Farm to Fork” strategy adopted in 2020 now uses the same logic to tackle antimicrobial resistance and whole‑chain surveillance.
