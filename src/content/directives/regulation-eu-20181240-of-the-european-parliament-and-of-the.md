## What is it?

The European Travel Information and Authorisation System (ETIAS) is an automated IT system that requires nationals from countries that currently do not need a visa to enter the Schengen Area to obtain a pre-travel authorisation before departure. It is modelled on similar systems already in use by the United States (ESTA), Canada (eTA), and Australia (ETA). The system is not a visa — it is a lighter, faster, online-only procedure that checks applicants against EU security, migration, and health databases.

Applicants complete an online form (estimated time: 10 minutes) providing biographic and passport data, plus answers to background questions about criminal records, past travel to conflict zones, and stays in specific countries. The application fee is set at €7 for travellers aged 18–70, with minors and those over 70 exempt. Most applications are processed automatically within minutes; if a flag is raised, manual processing by an ETIAS Central Unit (within Frontex) or a national ETIAS unit can take up to 96 hours. Once granted, the authorisation is valid for three years or until the passport expires, whichever comes first, and permits multiple entries for short stays (up to 90 days in any 180-day period).

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for ETIAS was the 2015 migration and security crisis. Between 2014 and 2016, over 2.5 million irregular border crossings were detected at EU external borders, according to Frontex data. The November 2015 Paris attacks, in which several of the attackers were EU nationals returning from the Middle East but travelled freely within Schengen, exposed a fundamental structural tension: the Schengen Area had abolished internal border checks but lacked a systematic, automated mechanism to screen visa-exempt travellers entering its external borders.

Before ETIAS, travellers from visa-exempt countries (like the United States, Canada, Australia, Japan, and Brazil) could simply board a plane or a ferry — their only check was a passport stamp upon arrival if border guards chose to query them. The EU had no way of knowing who was coming until they arrived. The Schengen Information System (SIS II) and the Visa Information System (VIS) covered only visa-required travellers, leaving a blind spot for over 1.4 billion people from visa-exempt countries who could enter the Schengen Area each year, according to a 2016 European Commission impact assessment.

The political coalition that pushed ETIAS through was unusual. It brought together security hawks in the European Parliament (notably from Manfred Weber's EPP group) who wanted to close what they called the "visa-waiver loophole" after the Paris and Brussels attacks, and border control advocates in the European Council led by Germany and France. The European Commission, under Jean-Claude Juncker, made ETIAS a centrepiece of its April 2016 "Smart Borders" package. Juncker himself stated on 6 April 2016: "We need to know who is crossing our borders. We cannot have a situation where we allow people to travel without any checks."

Opposition came primarily from civil liberties groups (EDRi, Statewatch) and privacy-focused MEPs like Jan Philipp Albrecht (Greens/EFA, Germany), who argued that pre-screening all visa-exempt travellers was disproportionate and would create a massive database of personal data on millions of innocent people. The European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS), Giovanni Buttarelli, warned in his 2016 opinion that the system risked "function creep" — mission expansion beyond its stated purpose. The travel industry also raised concerns: airlines and ferry operators warned they would be forced to deny boarding to passengers without valid ETIAS authorisations, potentially cutting into ticket sales for unplanned "last-minute" trips.

The timing was decisive. The Paris attacks (November 2015), followed by the Brussels bombings (March 2016), Berlin truck attack (December 2016), Manchester Arena bombing (May 2017), and Barcelona attacks (August 2017) created a security window where opposition from civil liberties groups was politically unsustainable. The Commission's proposal was tabled in April 2016; the European Parliament and Council reached political agreement in April 2018, with the regulation adopted in September 2018. Key negotiators included the then-Maltese EU Presidency (which prioritised border security), and the draft was shepherded by Liberal MEPs (ALDE) who saw it as a pragmatic middle ground between full visa requirements and no checks at all.

What changed that made ETIAS possible? The failure of earlier, more ambitious border screening proposals (like the EU Entry/Exit System, which had been stalled since 2008 over privacy concerns) meant the Commission needed to design a lighter system. ETIAS uses existing EU databases (SIS II, VIS, Europol data, Interpol's Stolen Travel Document database) rather than creating a new surveillance infrastructure. This compromise — no new data collection, just automated cross-checking — was crucial to winning over enough MEPs and member states.

## Impact and consequences

ETIAS affects approximately 1.4 billion potential travellers from over 60 visa-exempt countries — including the United States, Canada, the UK (post-Brexit), Australia, Japan, South Korea, New Zealand, Brazil, Argentina, and Israel. Its primary impact is procedural: travellers must now plan ahead and pay a small fee before their trip, adding friction to what was previously ticket-and-go travel.

For the industry, airlines and ferry operators bear the enforcement burden. They are required to verify that passengers hold a valid ETIAS authorisation before issuing boarding passes. This is similar to existing visa-check obligations under the 2004 Carrier Sanctions Directive (2004/82/EC), but applies to a much larger population. IATA (the International Air Transport Association) warned in 2018 that the new checks could cause delays at check-in and disrupt last-minute business travel.

Financially, the €7 fee (free for over-70s and under-18s) is designed to make ETIAS self-funding. The Commission's 2016 impact assessment estimated total development costs at €189 million for the central system, with annual operational costs of around €60 million. Fee revenue is expected to cover these costs; surpluses will fund the wider eu-LISA border management IT portfolio.

Countries most affected are those with high travel volumes to the Schengen Area: the United States (approximately 14 million Schengen visits per year pre-pandemic, according to Eurostat), the UK (around 10 million), and Canada (around 4 million). The UK's post-Brexit status as a visa-exempt third country means all British passport holders will require ETIAS authorisation for short stays in the EU.

Rights groups continue to criticise the system. In 2020, the EDPS issued a follow-up opinion noting that while ETIAS was less intrusive than some earlier proposals, the centralisation of data on all travellers still posed risks. The system has not yet gone live (as of early 2025, it remains delayed), so its practical impact on travel and its data retention policies have not been tested in operation.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU need ETIAS when it already has the Schengen Information System (SIS II)?

SIS II only contains data on people who have been flagged for specific reasons (e.g. wanted criminals, missing persons, refused entry). It does not screen the 1.4 billion visa-exempt travellers who have never been flagged — meaning a person with no criminal record could travel to the Schengen Area without any pre-check. ETIAS fills this gap by automatically querying SIS II, Interpol databases, and other systems for every single traveller before they arrive, not just those flagged in the past.

### Why is ETIAS not a visa? What is the difference between ETIAS and a visa?

A visa requires a formal application, often with an in-person interview at an embassy, submission of supporting documents (bank statements, letters of invitation), and a face-to-face decision. Processing can take weeks. ETIAS is a purely online, automated process that submits a traveller's data against existing security databases. There is no interview, no physical sticker in the passport, and the decision is usually instant. The key distinction is that ETIAS authorisation does not guarantee entry — it is a pre-screening that allows the traveller to board transport. Final entry decisions still rest with border guards at the point of arrival.

### Why did the UK not have ETIAS before Brexit?

The UK was an EU member state until 31 January 2020 and a full participant in the Schengen Area's border rules. Since ETIAS applies only to non-EU nationals entering the Schengen Area, British citizens travelling to other EU countries before Brexit were EU citizens and exempt. After Brexit, British passport holders became third-country nationals — and, because they remain visa-exempt for short stays, they must now obtain ETIAS authorisation to travel to the Schengen Area.
