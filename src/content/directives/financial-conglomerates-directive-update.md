## What is it?

The 2011 update to the Financial Conglomerates Directive (FICOD, originally Directive 2002/87/EC) tightened supervision of financial groups that operate in multiple sectors — most commonly a combination of banking, insurance, and securities activities. Before this update, these "mixed groups" could escape full consolidated oversight because sector-specific regulators focused only on the bank or the insurer within the group, without seeing the whole picture.

The directive required member states to identify all financial conglomerates within their jurisdiction, impose supplementary supervision at the group level, and ensure that cross-sector risks — such as double-counting of capital or contagion from one entity to another — were properly addressed. It also harmonised the definition of a "financial conglomerate" to prevent groups from falling between regulatory cracks.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2007-2008 financial crisis is the most specific and dramatic trigger. In October 2008, the near-collapse of the Belgian-Dutch bancassurer Fortis — a textbook financial conglomerate with both banking and insurance operations — sent shockwaves through European capitals. Fortis had amassed €870 billion in assets by 2007, according to a 2009 analysis by the Dutch central bank (De Nederlandsche Bank), but its dual structure meant banking supervisors and insurance supervisors each saw only part of the picture. When its acquisition of ABN AMRO's commercial banking arm in October 2007 stretched its capital too thin, no single regulator had the authority to demand group-level capital adequacy.

The European Commission's January 2009 communication "Driving European Recovery" explicitly linked the crisis to regulatory gaps for cross-sector groups. Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy, who had resisted calls for stronger conglomerates rules earlier in his mandate, was replaced by Michel Barnier in February 2010 — a shift to a commissioner far more willing to intervene in financial regulation. Barnier made the FICOD revision a priority, issuing a formal proposal on 16 November 2010.

The European Parliament's Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs (ECON) led the legislative work, with German MEP Burkhard Balz as rapporteur. Balz's report, adopted on 5 July 2011, called for even stricter capital requirements on conglomerates than the Commission had proposed, including a ban on "double gearing" — the practice where the same capital was counted as a buffer for both the bank and the insurer in the same group.

Industry opposition was significant. The European Banking Federation (EBF) and the International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS) warned that the updated rules would impose disproportionate compliance costs on smaller groups. According to the Commission's own impact assessment (SEC(2010) 1351), the estimated one-off compliance cost for a medium-sized conglomerate was between €100,000 and €500,000 — but industry bodies argued this underestimated the ongoing operational burden. The insurance sector in particular, represented by Insurance Europe, lobbied to exempt "industrial-insurance" conglomerates (non-financial parent companies with insurance subsidiaries) from supplementary supervision.

The final vote in the European Parliament on 13 September 2011 saw 540 votes in favour, 91 against, and 17 abstentions — a comfortable majority driven by a broad centre-right to centre-left coalition. The main opposition came from a small bloc of MEPs allied with the insurance industry, arguing the rules would harm competitiveness against US and Swiss groups.

## Timeline

- **2002** — Original Financial Conglomerates Directive (2002/87/EC) adopted, establishing supplementary supervision for cross-sector groups
- **October 2007** — Fortis strikes deal to acquire ABN AMRO commercial banking for €24 billion, overextending its capital base
- **September-October 2008** — Fortis partially nationalised by Belgium, Netherlands, and Luxembourg after a liquidity crisis; share price collapses 80%
- **January 2009** — Commission's "Driving European Recovery" communication identifies weak conglomerates oversight as a regulatory gap
- **February 2010** — Michel Barnier becomes Internal Market Commissioner, prioritises FICOD revision
- **16 November 2010** — Commission proposes Directive 2011/89/EU amending FICOD
- **5 July 2011** — European Parliament's ECON committee adopts report with amendments
- **13 September 2011** — European Parliament votes 540-91-17 in favour
- **16 November 2011** — Council of the EU formally adopts the directive
- **8 December 2011** — Directive published in Official Journal of the EU
- **10 June 2013** — Deadline for member states to transpose into national law
- **2016** — European Banking Authority (EBA) and European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) issue joint guidelines on conglomerates supervision under the new rules

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced member states to identify every financial conglomerate operating within their jurisdiction. By 2014, according to a Commission report on compliance, 14 member states had formally identified one or more financial conglomerates — Germany reported the most, with 24 groups, followed by France (18) and the UK (15). Six member states reported zero conglomerates, raising concerns that some groups may have escaped identification.

Industry adapted by restructuring cross-sector holdings. Deutsche Bank, which had maintained a separate insurance arm (Deutsche Postbank) under the same holding company, simplified its structure in 2013-2014 partly in response to supplementary supervision requirements. ING Group — a Dutch bancassurer — had already split its banking and insurance operations in 2009 under a Commission state aid ruling, but the FICOD update ensured that the post-split entities were still subject to group-level oversight.

The directive's impact was later absorbed into the broader framework of the Capital Requirements Directive IV (2013/36/EU) and Solvency II (2009/138/EC), which introduced more granular cross-sector rules. The European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs) — created in 2011 — took over oversight of conglomerates supervision, and by 2020 the Joint Committee of the ESAs had produced binding technical standards on capital adequacy for cross-sector groups.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU rules on large financial groups need updating after 2008?

The 2008 crisis exposed that regulators were each looking at only part of a financial group — banking supervisors watched the bank, insurance supervisors watched the insurer — but nobody watched the whole group. When Fortis, which combined both activities under one parent company, ran into trouble in 2008, no single regulator had the power to demand capital at the group level. The same capital was sometimes counted twice, once for the bank and once for the insurer.

### Does this directive set specific capital requirements for cross-sector financial groups?

No — it requires member states to identify these groups and impose supplementary supervision, but the actual capital levels are set in sector-specific rules like the Capital Requirements Directive (for banks) and Solvency II (for insurers). The directive ensures regulators talk to each other and that risks from one part of the group aren't hidden from the other. The European Banking Authority and the European Insurance Authority later issued joint guidelines to harmonise how capital is calculated across sectors.

### Which companies were most affected by these rules?

Large bancassurance groups — firms that own both a bank and an insurance company — were the primary targets. ING Group (Netherlands), Fortis/BNP Paribas Fortis (Belgium), Allianz (Germany, with its banking arm), and Crédit Agricole (France, with its insurance arm) all had to adjust their internal capital modelling and reporting. The directive also caught non-European groups operating in the EU, such as Swiss Re and Zurich Insurance, where their operations crossed sector boundaries.

### Were there any enforcement actions or penalties under this directive?

The directive itself was supervisory framework — it didn't carry direct penalties. However, the European Banking Authority (EBA) conducted a peer review in 2018 that found several countries were still failing to identify all conglomerates. Specifically, the EBA's October 2018 report showed that Ireland, Luxembourg, and Malta had identified zero conglomerates despite having significant cross-sector financial activity, raising questions about enforcement. The Commission opened infringement procedures against three member states for late transposition in 2014.
