## What is it?

The Net-Zero Industry Act (NZIA), formally Regulation (EU) 2024/1735, is the EU’s flagship industrial policy to boost domestic production of clean energy technologies. It sets a binding target: EU manufacturing capacity for strategically important net-zero technologies must meet at least 40% of the bloc’s annual deployment needs by 2030. The Act identifies eight "Strategic Net-Zero Technologies"—including solar photovoltaic panels, wind turbines, batteries, heat pumps, electrolysers, carbon capture and storage, and grid technologies—and accelerates permitting, public procurement, and access to financing for projects that fall into this category.

The NZIA also establishes a new governance structure: "Net-Zero Strategic Projects" (NZSPs), which receive priority status, shorter permitting timelines (12 months for manufacturing, 6 months for storage), and easier access to state aid and EU funds. It mandates a maximum auction ceiling for renewable energy auctions, requiring that no more than 65% of auctioned capacity can originate from a single country of supply by 2027, a measure aimed at reducing Chinese dominance in solar and batteries. Member states are also required to create "one-stop shops" for permitting and to conduct national assessments of their supply chain vulnerabilities.

## Why was it introduced?

The NZIA is a direct response to a triple shock: the EU’s near-total dependency on Chinese clean-tech supply chains, the US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of August 2022, and the energy crisis triggered by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. By late 2022, the EU was importing over 95% of its solar wafers and cells from China, according to the European Commission’s 2023 Critical Raw Materials report. The European Court of Auditors’ 2023 special report on EU solar energy warned that the bloc had "no domestic manufacturing base" for key components, leaving it vulnerable to supply disruptions.

The immediate political trigger was the IRA’s $369 billion in clean-tech subsidies, which prompted warnings from European industry leaders. In November 2022, the CEO of Northvolt, Peter Carlsson, publicly warned that the IRA threatened to "suck investment out of Europe." The French and German governments—led by Economy Ministers Bruno Le Maire and Robert Habeck, respectively—pressed for a European response. In December 2022, a joint letter from 14 EU industry associations (including SolarPower Europe and WindEurope) called for a "European Sovereignty Fund" and manufacturing targets.

Commission President Ursula von der Leyen announced the NZIA in her 2023 State of the Union address (September 13, 2023), framing it as part of the "Green Deal Industrial Plan." The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, MEP Christian Ehler (EPP, Germany), pushed for the 40% target, overriding initial Commission proposals for a non-binding benchmark. The Council’s position, coordinated under the Swedish presidency, introduced the supply diversification measure for renewable auctions.

Opposition came from two directions: free-trade advocates (led by the liberal Renew Europe group and the Dutch government) who warned of protectionism and WTO challenges, and environmental groups (notably Transport & Environment and ClientEarth) who argued the Act should include binding recycling targets and explicit bans on burning biomass for hydrogen. The final text dropped the biomass ban and watered down recycling requirements to a "voluntary recommendation."

The NZIA passed the European Parliament on April 22, 2024, by 498 votes to 73 (with 100 abstentions), entering into force on June 29, 2024. A prior attempt at similar legislation—the 2016 "Strategic Energy Technologies Plan"—had failed to secure binding targets and languished as a coordinating framework.

## Impact and consequences

The NZIA’s most immediate impact has been to redirect state aid approvals. Between June 2024 and February 2025, the Commission approved at least seven state aid schemes citing the NZIA as justification, totaling over €9 billion for battery, solar, and hydrogen projects (Commission State Aid Register, 2025). Member states with large industrial bases—Germany, France, Spain, Italy—submitted the most NZSP applications, with 120 proposals for NZSP designation submitted by January 2025 (Commission NZIA Dashboard, 2025).

Industry response has been mixed. Solar manufacturers—particularly European firms like Meyer Burger and Norwegian Crystals—welcomed the auction ceiling, which they argued would break China’s stranglehold. Meyer Burger’s CEO Gunter Erfurt publicly stated in October 2024 that the measure was "the single most important signal for European solar manufacturing." Chinese solar makers, notably Longi Green Energy and JA Solar, warned of potential WTO violations, but the Commission’s legal analysis argued the measure was a "prudential supply security" measure permissible under Article XX of GATT.

Wind turbine manufacturers, led by Vestas (Denmark) and Siemens Gamesa (Spain), pushed for streamlined permitting but expressed frustration that the NZIA did not include a mandatory "buy European" clause for wind components. The European Wind Energy Association (WindEurope) noted in a 2024 report that only 23% of new wind turbine nacelles installed in the EU in 2023 were manufactured in the EU, down from 45% in 2019.

Countries struggling with implementation include Poland and Hungary, which opposed the auction supply ceilings, arguing they would raise electricity costs. Poland’s Deputy Climate Minister Jacek Ozdoba stated in March 2024 that the measure "penalises newer member states that need cheaper Chinese modules to meet renewable targets."

The first major enforcement test concerns Member States’ failure to set up "one-stop shops" by the December 2024 deadline: the Commission has sent letters of formal notice to 11 Member States as of March 2025, including Romania, Bulgaria, and Slovakia.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU want to produce 40% of its own clean-tech equipment instead of a different target like 30% or 50%?

The 40% target was a compromise between the European Parliament (which initially pushed for 60%) and the Commission’s original non-binding benchmark. The Parliament’s industry committee commissioned a 2023 study from the Joint Research Centre showing that 40% of EU deployment demand equated to roughly €150 billion in annual manufacturing investment—enough to be "game-changing" but not so aggressive as to require a full "buy European" mandate that would violate WTO rules. The Council pushed back against a higher target, with Germany arguing that 60% would force member states to accept higher costs for domestic equipment.

### How do EU rules for boosting clean-tech manufacturing connect with rules for securing supplies of rare minerals and metals?

The NZIA and the Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA, 2024/1252/EU) are complementary halves of the EU’s clean-tech sovereignty push. The CRMA sets targets for domestic extraction and processing of rare earths, lithium, and other materials (10% of annual EU consumption extracted domestically by 2030; 40% processed). The NZIA covers the manufacturing stage. Together, they create a "from mine to factory" industrial policy. However, the NZIA does not require that NZSP manufacturers use CRMA-compliant inputs—a deliberate gap, as the Commission acknowledged in a 2024 internal memo that "binding vertical integration would exceed WTO constraints."

### Why didn't the EU's clean-tech manufacturing plan include a carbon border tax on imported solar panels, batteries, and other green equipment?

CBAM (Regulation 2023/956/EU) already covers aluminium, steel, and electricity—key inputs for clean-tech manufacturing—but not completed solar panels or wind turbines. A majority of Member States, led by Denmark and Ireland, supported extending CBAM to clean-tech products, arguing that Chinese-manufactured solar panels have embedded carbon emissions roughly double the EU average (based on JRC 2023 lifecycle analysis). However, the Commission’s Legal Service warned that applying CBAM to manufactured goods would require complex new methodologies for embedded carbon tracing, risking WTO challenges from China. The proposal was dropped in the December 2023 trilogue, with the promise of a review by 2027.
