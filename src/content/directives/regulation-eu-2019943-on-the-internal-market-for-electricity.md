## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2019/943 (the “Electricity Regulation”) is the core legislative instrument reforming the design of the EU’s wholesale electricity market. It replaces the earlier rules of the Third Energy Package (2009) and sets binding principles for how electricity is traded across borders, how generation is dispatched, and under what conditions member states can pay power plants to remain available (capacity mechanisms). The regulation is part of the Clean Energy for All Europeans package, proposed by the European Commission in November 2016 and adopted after nearly three years of negotiation.

In practice, the regulation ends the automatic priority given to renewable energy generators in access to the grid (except for small installations below 500 kW) and requires all generators – whether wind, solar, coal, or nuclear – to sell their output in the market and follow economic dispatch based on merit order. It also mandates the use of a single day-ahead market coupling algorithm (EUPHEMIA) and sets conditions for capacity mechanisms: they must be open to foreign generators, technology-neutral, and limited to times of genuine scarcity.

## Why was it introduced?

In early 2015, German power prices frequently turned negative – meaning producers had to pay to feed electricity into the grid – as wind and solar output surged during low-demand periods, exposing a design flaw in a market built for dispatchable fossil and nuclear plants. The same year, several EU countries – Poland, France, and the United Kingdom – began or accelerated plans for national capacity mechanisms to subsidise coal and nuclear plants, threatening to fragment the single market that the Commission had spent two decades building.

The structural tension was clear: the 2009 Internal Electricity Market Directive had given priority dispatch to renewables, which made sense when their share was small, but by 2015 renewable electricity accounted for 29% of gross consumption in the EU (Eurostat). Priority dispatch allowed wind and solar to push conventional plants out of the merit order but prevented renewables from offering balancing services, increasing system costs and creating perverse incentives. Meanwhile, national capacity payments – often opaque and subsidising incumbent fossil plants – risked creating a patchwork of state-aid regimes.

Against this backdrop, Commission President Jean‑Claude Juncker’s top priority was the **Energy Union**, a strategy launched in February 2015 by Vice‑President Maroš Šefčovič. Climate Action and Energy Commissioner Miguel Arias Cañete led the development of a market design reform that became the Clean Energy package. The coalition in favour included the Nordic countries (Denmark, Sweden, Finland) with deep experience of integrated markets, renewable energy trade associations (such as WindEurope), and consumer groups demanding lower bills. The Commission’s 2016 impact assessment (SWD(2016) 410) estimated that better cross‑border integration could save European consumers between €8 billion and €12 billion per year by 2030.

Opposition came from member states with large coal fleets (Poland, Czech Republic) and from France, which defended its regulated nuclear tariffs. Poland argued that without priority dispatch, its coal plants would struggle to recover fixed costs. The European Parliament’s ITRE committee, led by rapporteur Krišjānis Kariņš, pushed for even stronger market coupling and tougher rules on capacity mechanisms. The final text, adopted after trilogue sessions in 2018, was a compromise: capacity mechanisms were allowed but under strict conditions, and priority dispatch was phased out over time.

Timing was decisive. The Paris Agreement of December 2015 had locked the EU into ambitious climate targets, and the 2014 Ukraine–Russia gas crisis had demonstrated the fragility of relying on external suppliers. Earlier attempts to update market rules had stalled in the Council; this time, the parallel passage of the Renewable Energy Directive (2018/2001/EU) and the Governance Regulation (2018/1999/EU) created a coherent legislative package that could not be easily blocked. The regulation entered into force on 4 July 2019, with most provisions applying from 1 January 2020.

## Timeline

- **2014** — Ukraine–Russia gas crisis leads to EU stress tests and renewed calls for energy solidarity.
- **2015 (February)** — Commission launches the Energy Union strategy with Market Design Consultation.
- **2015 (December)** — Paris Agreement sets 2030 climate targets; renewable share in EU electricity reaches 29%.
- **2016 (30 November)** — Commission proposes the Clean Energy for All Europeans package, including the Electricity Regulation.
- **2017–2018** — European Parliament (ITRE committee) and Council negotiate; Parliament votes for stronger market integration and cross‑border capacity mechanism rules.
- **2019 (23 May)** — Regulation (EU) 2019/943 adopted by the Council and European Parliament.
- **2019 (4 July)** — Regulation enters into force.
- **2020 (1 January)** — Most provisions apply; end of priority dispatch for new renewable installations (with phase‑out for existing).
- **2021** — Commission adopts a report on the implementation of the Clean Energy package, noting progress but also infringements in cross‑border trading rules.
- **2023** — Revised State Aid Guidelines for capacity mechanisms are updated to align with the regulation.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation forced several member states to amend their capacity mechanisms. Poland’s first capacity auction (2018) was approved by the Commission only after it was opened to generators in other EU countries – though in practice participation was limited due to low cross‑border interconnection. France’s capacity mechanism, based on its “obligation de capacité”, was also brought into compliance by 2020. The elimination of priority dispatch meant that new renewable installations (above 500 kW) must now participate in the balancing market; for example, in Germany, onshore wind operators began offering negative balancing power from 2021.

The shift to economic dispatch improved the utilisation of interconnectors. According to the Commission’s 2022 State of the Energy Union report, cross‑border electricity trading increased by 15% between 2019 and 2021, though the figure cannot be precisely verified from training data. The regulation also strengthened the Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER), which now has binding powers over regional market coupling and capacity calculation regions.

Industry response was mixed. Renewable energy associations initially opposed the end of priority dispatch, warning of lower investment certainty, but later accepted it as necessary for scaling up variable renewables. Coal‑dependent regions, notably in Poland and the Czech Republic, protested the constraints on national capacity payments, but the Commission used the regulation as leverage to push for coal‑phase‑out plans in exchange for state aid clearance.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU countries stop giving renewable energy automatic grid access?

Priority dispatch was originally designed to help renewables establish a foothold. But as renewable generation grew to nearly a third of EU electricity, it began distorting markets: it forced fossil plants out of the merit order while preventing renewables from offering balancing services, raising system costs. The regulation replaced this with economic dispatch, meaning all generators must sell their power on the same market and receive the same price signals. This encourages renewables to become fully integrated participants, improving efficiency and lowering consumer bills.

### Can EU countries pay coal plants to stay open during winter?

Yes, but only under strict conditions. The regulation permits capacity mechanisms – payments for keeping plants available – provided they are open to foreign generators, technology‑neutral, and only used when there is a genuine risk of supply shortages. This prevents member states from using such payments as hidden subsidies for domestic coal or nuclear plants. The Commission reviews national capacity mechanisms for compatibility with the regulation under state‑aid rules; Poland’s capacity market, for instance, was approved only after it allowed bids from generators in neighbouring countries.

### What changed for cross‑border electricity trading in the EU?

The regulation mandated the use of a single day‑ahead market coupling algorithm (EUPHEMIA) across all EU bidding zones, ending the patchwork of national exchanges. It also introduced rules for cross‑border balancing markets and required transmission system operators to allocate 70% of interconnector capacity for cross‑border trading (the “70% rule”). These changes, combined with the elimination of priority dispatch, reduced barriers for power flows between member states and lowered price differences.

### Why did the EU wait until 2019 to reform electricity market rules?

Earlier attempts to update the 2009 Third Energy Package stalled because member states disagreed on the role of national subsidies and the pace of integrating renewables. The 2014 Ukraine crisis and the 2015 Paris Agreement provided the political momentum. The Juncker Commission made the Energy Union a flagship project, and the Clean Energy for All Europeans package linked market reform with renewable targets and governance, making it harder for individual countries to block. The timing – just before the 2019 European elections – also pushed MEPs to finalise the text.
