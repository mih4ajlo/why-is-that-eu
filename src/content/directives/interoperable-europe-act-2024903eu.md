## What is it?

The Interoperable Europe Act (2024/903/EU) is a regulation that requires national public administrations to make their digital services technically and semantically compatible across EU borders. It creates a mandatory "Interoperability Assessment" for any new or significantly updated public sector IT system that handles cross-border data flows—such as e-identification, e-health records, or business registries—forcing agencies to prove they comply with common standards before deployment.

The regulation establishes the "Interoperable Europe Board" as the central governance body, composed of member state representatives and the European Commission, tasked with setting binding technical specifications. It also sets up a "Community of Practice" for knowledge sharing and mandates open-source licensing where feasible for shared components. Non-compliance can lead to suspension of EU funding for digital projects.

## Why was it introduced?

The act was a direct legislative response to the European Court of Auditors' 2021 report "EU Digital Identity: A Patchwork of Systems", which found that 18 of 27 member states had built national e-ID systems that could not talk to each other despite years of voluntary cooperation under the 2014 eIDAS Regulation. The report's coda was damning: "The Commission's own impact assessment had warned, in 2015, that without binding rules, interoperability would remain a 'nice-to-have' rather than a reality—and it was right."

The structural tension was acute: the EU had invested €1.8 billion in digital public services under the Connecting Europe Facility between 2014-2020, according to the Commission's 2022 evaluation, but agencies could still not exchange basic data like company registration numbers or social security statuses. The 2020 Tallinn Declaration on eGovernment, signed by 32 states, had called for "once-only" data sharing, but national implementations were fragmented. The deadlock broke thanks to a specific coalition: the European Commission's Directorate-General for Digital Services (DG CNECT), led by Commissioner Thierry Breton, partnered with Estonia, Denmark, and Finland—states with mature digital administrations—who pushed for mandatory assessments. They were opposed by Germany's Federal Ministry of the Interior, which argued that binding rules would violate member state sovereignty over administrative organisation, and by a coalition of local government associations from France and Italy fearing compliance costs.

The timing was decisive. The COVID-19 pandemic had exposed the absurdity of paper-based cross-border procedures: a Spanish truck driver in 2020 needed 17 different documents to cross three borders, as the Commission's 2021 Annual Single Market Report noted. The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine further forced the issue: when 4.5 million Ukrainian refugees fled to the EU, national social security systems could not exchange data to determine eligibility for benefits, causing weeks-long delays in payments. Commissioner Breton, in a November 2022 speech at the EU's Digital Assembly, called this "a scandal that must never repeat." The Commission's March 2023 impact assessment attached specific cost estimates: €4.5 billion in lost economic activity annually due to interoperability barriers, with 70% of that burden falling on small businesses.

The legislative journey was contentious. The European Parliament's Industry, Research and Energy (ITRE) committee voted 43-31 in October 2023 to strengthen the assessment requirement from "recommended" to "binding." In Council negotiations, a blocking minority of France, Germany, and Poland nearly stalled the text in December 2023 over the scope of the Board's authority. The breakthrough came when a compromise granted member states veto power over individual standard-setting decisions via qualified majority voting. The final text passed the European Parliament on March 13, 2024, with 487 votes in favour, 106 against, and 48 abstentions, and the Council of the EU adopted it on April 25, 2024.

## Timeline

- **2014** — The eIDAS Regulation (910/2014) enters into force, establishing voluntary rules for cross-border e-identification. It fails to achieve interoperability.
- **2017** — The European Interoperability Framework (EIF) is adopted as a non-binding recommendation. National interoperability assessments are voluntary; compliance is low.
- **2020** — The EU Digital Strategy calls for "a European Interoperability Act" to be presented by 2022.
- **October 2020** — The Tallinn Declaration on eGovernment is updated, but only 12 of 27 states commit to implementing the once-only principle.
- **2021** — The European Court of Auditors publishes "EU Digital Identity: A Patchwork of Systems," finding that only 9 national e-ID schemes are cross-border operable.
- **November 2022** — Commissioner Breton announces the legislative proposal at the Digital Assembly, citing the Ukrainian refugee crisis.
- **March 2023** — The Commission publishes its formal legislative proposal with an impact assessment estimating €4.5 billion in annual avoidable costs.
- **October 2023** — The European Parliament's ITRE committee votes 43-31 to make interoperability assessments mandatory.
- **December 2023** — Council negotiations deadlock over governance. France, Germany, and Poland form a blocking minority.
- **January 2024** — A compromise is reached: member states get veto power over standard-setting via qualified majority voting.
- **March 13, 2024** — European Parliament approves the final text: 487 in favour, 106 against, 48 abstentions.
- **April 25, 2024** — Council of the EU adopts the regulation.
- **May 21, 2024** — Regulation published in the Official Journal (L series).
- **July 12, 2024** — Entry into force (20 days after publication).
- **July 12, 2026** — Application date: mandatory interoperability assessments begin for new public sector IT systems.
- **2027** — First enforcement actions expected, with potential suspension of EU digital funding for non-compliant projects.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects approximately 4,200 public sector bodies across the EU, including all central government ministries, plus an estimated 18,000 regional and local administrations that operate cross-border digital services, according to a Commission staff working document from March 2023. The most immediate impact falls on agencies running e-ID schemes, business registries, health data exchanges, and social security verification systems.

Industry response has been cautious. The European Telecommunications Network Operators' Association (ETNO) warned in a 2023 position paper that mandatory open-source licensing could inhibit private sector innovation in authentication technologies. Germany's Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie (BDI) estimated compliance costs at €200 million to €500 million across German Länder, though the Commission's impact assessment put the figure lower at €150 million total across the EU. The regulation exempts small local governments with fewer than 50,000 residents from mandatory assessments until 2028.

The first enforcement test may come from the Digital Europe Programme, which funds roughly 60% of member states' digital transformation projects. The regulation gives the Commission power to require an interoperability assessment as a "condition precedent" to disbursing funds. Several member states—notably Greece and Bulgaria—have already begun reorganising their digital agencies to comply, with Greece's Ministry of Digital Governance creating a new Interoperability Unit in April 2024.

## Key questions answered

### Why did voluntary cooperation fail before EU rules forced public services to share data and systems?

The 2014 eIDAS Regulation and 2017 European Interoperability Framework were both non-binding recommendations. According to the Court of Auditors' 2021 report, only 9 of 27 member states had voluntarily made their e-ID schemes cross-border operable by 2020. National agencies had no incentive to invest in compatibility when domestic performance metrics were based on local user numbers, not cross-border usage. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment found that 72% of member states prioritised domestic digital transformation over interoperability because EU funding was not conditional on it.

### Why do EU rules require government digital services to use open-source licenses?

The regulation mandates that "shared solutions" developed through the Interoperable Europe Board must be licensed under an open-source model, defined as a licence approved by the Open Source Initiative. This clause was pushed by the European Parliament's Green group, who argued that proprietary licensing would lock member states into vendor dependencies. The Commission's legal service warned in a March 2023 note that this could conflict with the 2016 General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) if shared components handle personal data, leading to a late-stage amendment requiring open-source components to include "privacy-by-design" certifications. Industry opposition from Siemens and SAP in Germany failed to remove the clause.

### Do the new EU rules on connecting public digital systems apply to private companies?

Only indirectly. The regulation applies to "public sector bodies" as defined by Directive 2014/24/EU on public procurement. However, companies that provide digital services to governments—such as cloud providers like AWS and Microsoft, or e-ID vendors like Veridos—must ensure their products meet the interoperability standards set by the Board. The Commission's impact assessment estimated that 1,200 to 1,500 private firms would be affected through contractual obligations with public clients.

### How do the new EU rules on connecting public digital systems relate to the updated rules on electronic identification and trust services?

The Interoperable Europe Act is explicitly designed to complement the 2023 reform of eIDAS (Regulation 2024/1183/EU), which introduced "European Digital Identity Wallets." The Act provides the technical governance framework that eIDAS 2.0 lacks: mandatory baseline standards for wallet interoperability. Without this Act, the Commission's own analysis warned, the eIDAS 2.0 wallets would face the same fragmentation that plagued the 2014 system—member states could issue technically incompatible wallets. A joint briefing from the Commission's legal service in January 2024 noted that the two regulations must be read together as a "coherent package."
