## What is it?

Directive 2004/113/EC forbids direct and indirect gender discrimination in the access to and supply of goods and services available to the public, covering both the public and private sectors. It applies to housing, banking, insurance, transport, and retail — essentially anywhere goods or services are offered to consumers without a close personal relationship. It does not cover the content of media or advertising, nor education.

The most politically explosive provision was Article 5(2), which originally allowed Member States to permit "proportionate" gender-based differences in insurance premiums and benefits where sex was a "determining factor" in risk assessment. The European Court of Justice's 2011 *Test-Achats* ruling struck this exemption down, forcing a uniform ban on gender-based pricing in insurance contracts concluded after 21 December 2012. This made the EU the first jurisdiction in the world to prohibit insurers from charging men and women different premiums based solely on their sex.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's story begins not in 2004 but in 1996, with a failed proposal. The European Commission under Jacques Santer had tried to extend sex equality to goods and services, but Member States could not agree on whether insurance pricing should be exempted. The proposal languished for years.

By the late 1990s, a structural tension had become untenable: the Treaty of Amsterdam (1999) gave the EU explicit competence to combat discrimination based on sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation. The Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) was adopted quickly in June 2000, covering goods and services for racial discrimination. The Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) followed in November 2000, but only covered employment. The gap was obvious: you could sue a landlord for race discrimination in housing but not for sex discrimination. This asymmetry could not survive a legal challenge on equality grounds.

The coalition that drove the directive through was a classic EU social-policy alliance: the European Parliament's Women's Rights Committee, led by German MEP to Elena Valenciano; the Belgian Presidency of 2001 which made gender mainstreaming a priority; and the European Commission's Directorate-General for Employment under Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (a Greek socialist). Diamantopoulou relaunched the proposal in November 2003, framing it as closing the "legal vacuum" left by the Racial Equality Directive.

Opposition came from a predictable triplet: the UK insurance industry, which argued that actuarial tables showing women live longer and have fewer car accidents were "scientific fact" not discrimination; the German government, concerned about pensions linked to sex-differentiated life insurance; and the European insurance federation CEA (now Insurance Europe), which warned of market disruption. A July 2004 Commission impact assessment noted that UK insurers alone derived roughly €10 billion annually from gender-differentiated car insurance premiums (with young women paying significantly less than young men).

The timing was propitious: the 2004 enlargement brought ten new Member States with weaker social-policy traditions, but the directive was already drafted and the existing Member States wanted to lock in standards before the newcomers could water them down. The European Parliament adopted the directive in March 2004 by 376 votes to 97, with 73 abstentions — a comfortable majority but with significant conservative and liberal dissent over the insurance exemption.

The breakthrough that unblocked the decade-old deadlock was the compromise on Article 5(2). The Commission proposed allowing gender-based differences "where sex is a determining factor in the assessment of risk" — a vague phrase that gave Member States an opt-out. This was deliberately ambiguous: it let the insurance industry claim victory while giving the Court of Justice a hook to later strike it down. And strike it down the Court did, in *Association Belge des Consommateurs Test-Achats ASBL v. Conseil des ministres* (C-236/09), ruling in March 2011 that the exemption violated Article 21 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights. The Court gave insurers 21 months to adjust — a remarkably short transition period that sparked panic in the industry.

## Timeline

- **1996** — Commission proposes a directive on sex equality in goods and services; proposal stalls in Council
- **1999** — Treaty of Amsterdam enters into force, expanding EU anti-discrimination competence
- **2000** — Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) adopted, covering goods and services for race but not sex; gap becomes politically untenable
- **2003, November** — Commissioner Diamantopoulou relaunches the proposal with the controversial Article 5(2) exemption
- **2004, March** — European Parliament adopts directive by 376-97-73
- **2004, December 13** — Council formally adopts Directive 2004/113/EC
- **2005, December 21** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law
- **2009** — Belgian consumer association Test-Achats challenges the insurance exemption before the European Court of Justice
- **2011, March 1** — ECJ ruling in *Test-Achats* declares Article 5(2) invalid; bans gender-based insurance pricing for contracts after 21 December 2012
- **2012, December 21** — Unisex pricing for new insurance contracts becomes mandatory across the EU
- **2023** — European Commission's 2023 report on implementation notes uneven enforcement, with complaints rising in car and life insurance markets

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was a dramatic reshaping of European insurance markets. Young male drivers in the UK saw their car insurance premiums rise by an average of 25-30% within months of the 2012 unisex pricing rule, according to figures cited by the UK's Association of British Insurers. Women over 60 saw their life insurance premiums increase by similar margins. The Commission's 2015 report on the directive found that five Member States — including the UK, Germany, and Austria — had to amend national legislation after the *Test-Achats* ruling to comply.

Industry adaptation was rapid but painful. Some insurers exited specific product lines; others introduced "lifestyle-based" pricing (occupation, mileage, health habits) that effectively replicated gendered outcomes through indirect discrimination. The European Commission's 2020 evaluation noted this risk, warning that "the use of proxy variables correlated with sex may undermine the directive's effectiveness."

The broader cultural impact was significant. The directive forced a philosophical shift: EU law now treated sex as a prohibited category for commercial risk assessment, elevating equality over actuarial tradition. This principle has since influenced debates on using genetic data, age, and disability in insurance pricing, and contributed to the framing of the 2019 General Data Protection Regulation's restrictions on profiling.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU have a single rule on gender-based insurance pricing?

Before 2004, EU law only banned sex discrimination in employment. The Racial Equality Directive (2000) then banned race discrimination in goods and services, creating an embarrassing legal asymmetry. The 2004 directive closed that gap for sex, but allowed Member States to keep gender-differentiated insurance premiums. The European Court of Justice struck down that exemption in 2011, making unisex insurance pricing mandatory across the EU.

### Can an EU country still allow insurers to charge men and women differently?

No. Since 21 December 2012, all new insurance contracts in the EU must be priced without direct reference to the customer's sex. The ECJ's *Test-Achats* ruling (2011) invalidated the only legal basis for such differentiation. However, insurers can use factors statistically correlated with sex — like occupation, mileage, or smoking status — provided these are not obvious proxies.

### Does this mean women pay the same as men for everything?

No. The directive applies only to goods and services "available to the public without a personal relationship." It does not cover housing loans (which fall under separate credit rules), insurance linked to employment (covered by the Recast Directive 2006/54/EC), or private arrangements. Gender-based pricing also remains legal in narrow exceptions such as wedding services or single-sex gyms where the service's character requires single-sex provision.

### What happens if an insurer breaks the rule?

Individuals can file complaints with national equality bodies, which Member States were required to establish under the directive. The Commission can also launch infringement proceedings against a Member State that fails to enforce the rules. The 2011 *Test-Achats* case was brought by a consumer association, not the Commission, showing the directive's reliance on private enforcement. Ten years after the ban, the Commission's 2023 implementation report noted that most complaints involved car and travel insurance pricing, but few resulted in formal penalties.
