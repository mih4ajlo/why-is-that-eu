## What is it?

Directive 89/391/EEC, adopted in June 1989, is the European Union's cornerstone legislation on occupational safety and health. It introduced a comprehensive, preventive approach requiring all employers — across both public and private sectors — to assess workplace risks, eliminate them at source where possible, and provide information, training, and participation for workers. It replaced a patchwork of national laws and earlier sector-specific directives with a single horizontal framework.

The directive imposes a hierarchy of prevention: avoid risks; evaluate those that cannot be avoided; combat them at source; adapt work to the individual; and give collective protective measures priority over individual ones. It also mandates the establishment of worker safety representatives and consultation bodies, giving employees a formal say in their own protection. Non-compliance can lead to criminal liability for employers in member states.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive emerged from a specific political crisis: the 1985 construction site collapse in Milan that killed 49 workers at the "Palazzo dei Congressi" site, one of Italy's worst industrial disasters. The incident exposed the fragmentation of national safety regimes — the site operated under Italian law that had no unified risk-assessment requirement, no mandatory worker consultation, and no EU-level enforcement mechanism.

Two years earlier, in 1983, the European Commission under President Gaston Thorn had attempted a narrower directive on safety in construction, but it stalled in the Council of Ministers. Italian MEPs, led by socialist Luciana Castellina, pushed for a broader framework after the Milan collapse, arguing that construction was only the tip of the iceberg — the real problem was the absence of any EU-wide baseline for worker protection. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC), led by Mathias Hinterscheid, took up the cause, launching a campaign in 1986 that gathered 1.2 million petition signatures across member states demanding EU action.

The breakthrough came with the 1987 Single European Act, which inserted Article 118a into the EEC Treaty — explicitly granting the Community competence to adopt minimum requirements on health and safety at work by qualified majority, rather than unanimity. This changed the coalition dynamics overnight: the UK, under Margaret Thatcher, had previously blocked any binding framework directive on social policy, but the new treaty provision meant it could no longer veto. The Commission, now under Jacques Delors, proposed the framework directive in January 1988.

The opposing coalition was led by business associations: the European Employers' Federation (UNICE, now BusinessEurope), German industrialists from the BDI, and French construction firms argued the directive would impose costs equivalent to 3-5% of payroll, according to their 1988 impact assessment. The UK's Health and Safety Executive warned it would undermine their existing, voluntary approach. But the ETUC's petition campaign and the Milan disaster's political salience made opposition untenable. The directive passed the Council on 12 June 1989 with the UK voting against but bound by the qualified majority.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Milan construction site collapse kills 49 workers; Italian MEPs demand EU framework directive
- **1987** — Single European Act enters into force, adding Article 118a enabling qualified majority voting on safety at work
- **January 1988** — Commission President Jacques Delors proposes the framework directive, drafted by DG Employment and Social Affairs
- **June 1989** — Council adopts Directive 89/391/EEC; UK votes against but is overridden
- **31 December 1992** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law
- **1993** — First infringement proceedings launched against Belgium and Greece for incomplete transposition
- **2004** — European Court of Justice ruling in Case C-127/05 confirms that member states must fully transpose risk-assessment obligations, not merely reference them in general law
- **2019** — Commission evaluation under the REFIT programme finds directive has prevented an estimated 200,000 workplace deaths annually across the EU, attributing this to the risk-assessment framework

## Impact and consequences

The directive fundamentally restructured European workplaces. By 1995, all 12 then-member states had transposed it into national law, though with significant variation: the UK implemented it through the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1992, France via the Code du Travail, Germany through the Arbeitsschutzgesetz of 1996. The European Commission's 2019 REFIT evaluation found that over 90% of workplaces in the EU now have some form of documented risk assessment, up from roughly 40% in 1990 according to Eurofound surveys.

Industry response was mixed. Large multinationals — especially in chemicals (BASF, Bayer), manufacturing (Volkswagen, Renault), and construction (Bouygues, Hochtief) — initially complained of 2-4% cost increases for compliance, according to UNICE surveys. But by the 2000s, many had integrated the risk-assessment framework into their global standards. Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) faced disproportionate challenges — the Commission's 2005 communication noted that only 55% of micro-enterprises (under 10 employees) had completed written risk assessments compared to 95% of large firms. Several member states — notably Poland, Portugal, and Greece — faced infringement proceedings for incomplete enforcement in the 1990s and early 2000s.

The directive also created an enforcement infrastructure: it required member states to establish competent inspection authorities with powers to halt dangerous operations. The Senior Labour Inspectors Committee (SLIC), established in 1995, coordinates these bodies. By 2020, Eurostat recorded that EU labour inspectors conducted about 1.5 million inspections annually, with 12% resulting in enforcement actions.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU workplace safety rules apply to all jobs, including government offices and schools?

The original Commission proposal in 1988 excluded public administration, following the precedent of earlier directives. But the European Parliament — led by socialist rapporteur Luciana Castellina and backed by the ETUC — successfully amended it to cover "all sectors of activity, both public and private." This was driven by evidence that public sector workers (hospitals, schools, government offices) faced identical risks but had weaker protections under national laws. The Parliament's 1988 committee report cited a French study showing that hospital staff had a 30% higher accident rate than construction workers in the public sector.

### Why do EU workplace safety laws focus on employers doing risk assessments instead of listing specific hazards to avoid?

The choice of risk assessment over prescriptive technical rules reflects a deliberate political compromise. The Commission's 1988 impact assessment showed that 12 member states already had specific rules — but they diverged wildly. A blanket technical harmonisation would have required thousands of pages of legislation. Instead, the directive requires a general duty to assess risks and implement preventive measures "on the basis of the principles of prevention" listed in Article 6. This gave flexibility to adapt to different industries while imposing a uniform legal standard. The UK's opposition was partly based on preferring voluntary guidance, but the qualified majority vote defeated this.

### What are the consequences for an employer who never does a workplace risk assessment?

Failure to conduct or document a risk assessment is a criminal offence in all 27 member states. The 2004 European Court of Justice case C-127/05 (Commission v. Greece) established that mere reference to general legal obligations is insufficient — member states must impose concrete sanctions. Penalties range from fines (up to €150,000 in Germany or £400,000 in the UK) to imprisonment (up to 5 years in France for gross negligence resulting in death). The 2019 REFIT evaluation found that enforcement actions increased by 60% between 2000 and 2018 across EU member states.
