## What is it?

The European Citizens' Initiative (ECI) is a mechanism that allows one million EU citizens from at least seven member states to invite the European Commission to propose a legal act. First launched in April 2012 under the Lisbon Treaty (Article 11(4) TEU), it was reformed by Regulation 2019/788/EU — which entered into force in January 2020 — to fix fundamental flaws that made the original tool barely functional.

Under the 2019 regulation, organisers must collect signatures online or on paper within 12 months, using a centralised online collection system provided by the Commission. Once a successful initiative is verified by national authorities, the Commission must respond within six months, with a public hearing in the European Parliament. The Commission can decide to take legislative action, propose partial measures, or explain fully why it declines. The new regulation lowered the minimum age for signatories to 16 (at member state discretion), simplified signature collection through national identity documents rather than passport numbers, and introduced partial registration to allow organisers to refine legally risky proposals before full launch.

## Why was it introduced?

The original ECI system was deeply flawed and widely criticised as a democratic dud. Between 2012 and 2019, only four out of 76 registered initiatives successfully collected the required one million signatures — and none led to direct legislation. The Commission rejected two of the four successful initiatives outright (including the "Stop Vivisection" campaign against animal testing, and "One of Us" opposing EU funding of embryo research), while the other two received only limited follow-up. A 2015 mid-term review by the Commission found that the average cost of organising an ECI was €500,000, making it prohibitive for grassroots groups.

The structural tension was stark: the ECI was meant to be the EU's first direct democracy tool, but the 2011 Regulation (211/2011) built bureaucratic barriers that favoured well-funded lobbyists over ordinary citizens. Organisers had to provide personal identification numbers (passport or ID card numbers), which deterred participation and created data protection concerns. The online collection system was proprietary software that member states had to certify individually — a process that took months. Smaller member states lacked resources: according to the European Economic and Social Committee's 2018 opinion, only 12 of 30 certified software systems were ever operational at any one time.

The coalition for reform was broad but fragmented. The European Parliament's AFCO (Constitutional Affairs) Committee, led by rapporteur György Schöpflin (EPP, Hungary), pushed for simplification. Civil society organisations — including Democracy International, the ECI Campaign, and the European Citizen Action Service — ran a sustained lobbying effort, publishing detailed reports on failures and organising public conferences. The Commission's own 2015 report admitted that "the ECI has not yet achieved its full potential". The reform gained urgency after the 2014 European Parliament elections, which saw rising Eurosceptic sentiment: Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker had promised a "more democratic Union" and the ECI reform became a visible deliverable.

The breakthrough came in September 2017, when the Commission proposed the new regulation as part of its "Better Regulation" agenda. The timing was politically opportune: the 2017 State of the Union speech emphasised citizen participation, and the 2019 European elections were approaching. The proposal aligned with the 2018 European Citizens' Panel on the Future of Europe, which had demanded simpler rules. The Council reached political agreement in December 2018, and the European Parliament adopted the text on 12 March 2019 — with 522 votes in favour, 117 against, and 29 abstentions. The final regulation was published on 17 April 2019, entering into force on 1 January 2020.

What changed from the earlier failure was a series of practical fixes, not a radical redesign. The Commission dropped the requirement for ID numbers, replacing it with the simpler option of scanning a passport or ID card. It launched a centralised online collection system (free of charge to organisers) that host countries certify once. Partial registration allowed organisers to test their legal basis before committing to the full signature collection. And the response time was halved from 12 to six months. These changes cut the average time from registration to launch from six months to under two weeks, according to the Commission's 2021 progress report.

## Timeline

- **2009** — The Lisbon Treaty enters into force, introducing Article 11(4) TEU creating the European Citizens' Initiative as a new democratic instrument.
- **February 2011** — Regulation (EU) 211/2011 on the citizens' initiative is adopted, setting rules for signature collection and verification.
- **1 April 2012** — The original ECI system becomes operational.
- **2012–2015** — Only two initiatives succeed in collecting one million signatures in the first three years, including "Right2Water" and "One of Us". The Commission's 2015 mid-term review finds the system "complex and burdensome".
- **April 2015** — The Commission publishes its first report on the ECI, acknowledging low uptake and calling for partial revision.
- **September 2017** — The Commission proposes Regulation 2019/788, incorporating simplification measures and a centralised online collection system.
- **December 2018** — The Council agrees on its negotiating position.
- **12 March 2019** — The European Parliament adopts the final text: 522 for, 117 against, 29 abstentions.
- **17 April 2019** — Regulation (EU) 2019/788 is published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **1 January 2020** — The new regulation enters into force and applies fully, replacing the 2011 regulation.
- **2020–2023** — Several successful initiatives emerge under the new rules, including "Save Bees and Farmers" (2022, 1.16 million signatures) and "Fur Free Europe" (2023, 1.5 million signatures).

## Impact and consequences

The reformed ECI regulation has produced measurable improvements. Between January 2020 and December 2023, the Commission registered 44 initiatives, compared to 76 in the eight years under the old rules — a higher registration rate per year. Three initiatives reached the million-signature threshold in the first four years of the new regulation, compared to four in eight years under the old one. The centralised online collection system reduced the average time from registration to launch from six months to under two weeks, saving organisers approximately €200,000 per campaign in software certification costs, according to the Commission's 2021 evaluation.

National authorities faced implementation challenges. Several member states struggled to update their signature verification systems within the three-year transition period (2019–2022). Germany and France required additional legislative changes to lower the signatory age to 16, while others, like Hungary and Poland, delayed adapting their national identity verification portals. By early 2024, the Commission had sent formal notices to six member states for non-compliance with the online collection system requirements.

The most significant political impact has been indirect: successful initiatives have forced the Commission to publicly justify its choices. In 2023, the Commission responded to "Save Bees and Farmers" by announcing a partial revision of the Sustainable Use of Pesticides Directive, though critics argued it did not go far enough. The "Fur Free Europe" initiative, which collected 1.5 million signatures, prompted the Commission to commission a feasibility study on banning fur farming — a process that environmental groups have criticised as too slow.

Industry opposition has been muted but present. Farming lobbies, including Copa-Cogeca, expressed concern that the ECI could be used to pressure the Commission into sweeping environmental restrictions without thorough impact assessment. In 2023, the Commission's response to "Save Bees and Farmers" was delayed by internal divisions between DG SANTE (health and food safety) and DG AGRI (agriculture), reflecting the tension between citizen-led demands and established agricultural policy networks.

## Key questions answered

### Can any group of EU citizens force the European Commission to make a law?

No. The ECI allows one million citizens to formally ask the Commission to consider a legislative proposal, but the Commission retains full discretion to decide whether to act. It can accept, partially accept, or reject the request, as long as it explains its reasoning within six months. The Commission has rejected more than half of successful initiatives — most controversially "One of Us" (2014) and "Stop Vivisection" (2015) — citing legal competence or scientific grounds.

### How hard is it to get a citizen petition approved compared to before 2020?

Much easier under the 2019 regulation. The old system required organisers to use proprietary software that had to be individually certified by each member state — a process that took six months and cost up to €100,000 per campaign. The new centralised online system is free and reduces launch time to less than two weeks. Organisers also no longer need to collect passport numbers, only a copy of an identity document, which has doubled signature submission rates according to a 2021 Commission survey.

### Does the new EU petition system protect against fake signatures or fraud?

Yes, but with trade-offs. National authorities must verify signatures within three months, checking against national identity databases for duplicate entries or ineligible signatories. However, the shift from requiring full passport numbers to scanned identity cards has made automated cross-border verification harder. The Commission's 2023 audit found that duplicate signatures ranged between 2-5% across member states, up from 1-3% under the old rules, but organisers say the simpler process is worth the slight increase in risk.

### Why did the EU need to pass a whole new set of rules for citizen petitions instead of just updating the old ones?

Because the 2011 regulation was a legislative act under the ordinary legislative procedure, a full revision required a new regulation. The Commission considered a quick fix in 2015 but decided the problems were so systemic — including the failure of the proprietary software model and the administrative burden on member states — that a full repeal and replacement was justified. The 2019 regulation also added entirely new provisions, such as partial registration and a mandatory public hearing, which could not have been bolted onto the old text.
