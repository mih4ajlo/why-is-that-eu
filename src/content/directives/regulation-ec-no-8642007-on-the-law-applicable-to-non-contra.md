## What is it?

The Rome II Regulation governs which country’s law applies when a non-contractual dispute – a tort, a delict, or any civil wrong that does not arise from a contract – has a cross-border element. If a French driver hits a German pedestrian in Belgium, Rome II tells the judge which national law to apply. It covers product liability, unfair competition, environmental damage, and most other civil wrongs, but explicitly excludes violations of privacy and defamation.

Before Rome II, each EU member state had its own private international law rules. A court in Italy might apply Italian choice-of-law principles, while a court in Sweden would apply Swedish ones – leading to different outcomes depending on where the case was filed. Rome II replaced that fragmentation with a single set of rules that all EU courts (except Denmark) must follow.

The general rule is that the law of the country where the damage occurs (lex loci damni) applies. There are special rules for specific torts: product liability uses the victim’s habitual residence if the product was marketed there; environmental damage uses the place of the harmful event (with a pro-victim option to choose the place of the damage-causing event); unfair competition uses the country where competitive relations are affected. Parties can also agree after the dispute arises to choose another law, provided it does not affect third-party rights.

## Why was it introduced?

The driving force was the sheer absurdity of the status quo inside a single market without borders. In 1999, the Amsterdam Treaty gave the EU explicit competence to adopt measures in judicial cooperation in civil matters “having cross-border implications.” The Tampere European Council in October 1999 called for greater harmonisation of conflict-of-law rules, but the real trigger was a series of cross-border litigation nightmares that made the fragmentation untenable.

The most vivid example came from defamation. In the 1995 *Shevill* case, the European Court of Justice ruled that a libel victim could sue in any member state where the publication was distributed – and apply that country’s law. A single newspaper article could face 15 different libel laws, and the victim could pick the most generous one (forum shopping). Publishers howled. Then in 2002, the Commission’s own *Rome II* proposal initially included defamation and privacy, but the proposal met fierce opposition from media groups. The European Newspaper Publishers Association (ENPA) and the International Federation of Journalists warned that applying the law of the place where damage occurred would chill cross-border reporting – a publisher would have to comply with 27 libel regimes. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Diana Wallis (ALDE, UK), pushed hard for an exclusion, and in 2006 the Council dropped defamation and privacy from the text. It remains an embarrassment: the single market’s choice-of-law rules still have a gaping hole for online speech.

The coalition that passed Rome II was a classic Brussels triangle: the Commission (under President Romano Prodi, then José Manuel Barroso; Justice Commissioners António Vitorino and later Franco Frattini), the Council (where the rotating presidency and legal advisers drove the technical work), and the Parliament (with Wallis as the lead MEP). Industry, particularly the insurance sector and the automotive industry, wanted predictability for cross-border liability. The European Automobile Manufacturers Association (ACEA) pressed for a clear rule in traffic accidents – without it, a single crash could require analysing 27 sets of national conflict rules. The consumer group BEUC favoured the victim-friendly option in product liability.

Timing was critical. The post-Amsterdam legal base (Article 65 TEC) only became available in 1999. A Green Paper in 2002 and a formal proposal in July 2003 moved fast by EU standards. The breakthrough in the Council came under the Austrian presidency in the first half of 2006, which managed to broker a compromise on the most divisive issue: whether to allow parties to choose the applicable law (they can, but only after the dispute arises – a concession to the Commission’s fear that ex-ante choice would let strong parties impose weak laws on consumers). The final text was adopted in July 2007 by a qualified majority in the Council, with the UK and Ireland opting in, and Denmark staying out. The ratification in the Parliament passed with 580 votes in favour, 43 against, and 38 abstentions – a comfortable majority built on a broad pro-harmonisation consensus.

What changed that had blocked earlier attempts? Before Amsterdam, the EU lacked competence; after Amsterdam, the Greek EU Presidency in 2003 made civil justice a priority. But the real unblocking came from the pragmatic realisation that the internal market could not function with 27 sets of unpredictable tort laws. A 2002 study by the European Law Institute (and an earlier report by the Groupe européen de droit international privé – GEDIP) had documented the chaos: in 40% of cross-border tort cases, parties could not predict which law would apply. That uncertainty raised insurance premiums and discouraged cross-border trade.

Alternatives were considered – a Convention (like Rome 1980 on contractual obligations) instead of a Regulation, or a gradual approach covering only specific areas – but the Commission pushed for a single binding instrument to ensure uniform interpretation by the ECJ. The Parliament and Council agreed, and the result was the first comprehensive EU instrument on non-contractual obligations.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Amsterdam Treaty enters into force, giving the EU competence in civil judicial cooperation (Article 65 TEC). The Tampere European Council calls for harmonised conflict-of-law rules.
- **2002** — Commission publishes a Green Paper on the transformation of the Rome Convention into a Community instrument, covering both contractual and non-contractual obligations.
- **2003** — Commission issues a formal proposal for a Rome II Regulation (COM(2003) 427). The proposal includes defamation and privacy.
- **2005** — European Parliament adopts its first reading position, proposing to exclude defamation and privacy. Rapporteur Diana Wallis leads the push.
- **2006** — Council (under Austrian presidency) reaches political agreement on a text that excludes defamation and privacy from scope.
- **12 July 2007** — Regulation (EC) No 864/2007 is adopted by the European Parliament and the Council.
- **11 January 2009** — Rome II Regulation becomes applicable in all member states except Denmark.
- **2015** — The Commission’s first report on the application of Rome II (COM(2015) 444) finds that it has improved legal certainty, but notes continued problems in online defamation and privacy.
- **2023** — In a legislative own-initiative report, the European Parliament calls on the Commission to address the gap for defamation and privacy, but no formal proposal has been tabled as of early 2025.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation brought immediate order to cross-border tort litigation. The Commission’s 2015 report (based on a survey of judges and lawyers conducted by the European University Institute) found that in two-thirds of cases, parties could predict the applicable law before going to court – up from an estimated 40% before. The insurance sector, in particular, reported lower administrative costs. The European Insurance and Reinsurance Federation (Insurance Europe) publicly welcomed the legal certainty for cross-border motor claims.

Industry adapted quickly. Multinational companies incorporated Rome II’s rules into their compliance manuals; product-liability lawyers now routinely check the victim’s habitual residence and market presence. Courts in the UK, Germany, and France have rendered dozens of judgments applying the Regulation, with the ECJ providing preliminary rulings on fine points – for example, in *Ergo Insurance* (C‑359/14 and C‑475/14, 2016), clarifying how Rome II interacts with the law applicable to insurance contracts.

The most criticised consequence remains the exclusion of defamation and privacy. The European Digital Rights (EDRi) organisation and others have argued that the gap leaves online speech subject to 27 different national laws, with no clear rule for cross-border cases. The 2023 Parliament report urged the Commission to act, but the issue remains politically sensitive: media groups and tech companies oppose any EU rule that might impose a single lowest-common-denominator standard, while victims’ rights groups want a predictable victim-friendly rule.

Enforcement has been smooth – no member state has been taken to the ECJ for non-compliance – but the UK’s departure from the EU in 2020 created a new complication. The UK opted into Rome II before Brexit; now, EU courts apply Rome II to UK-related torts only through retained EU law, creating potential asymmetry. The number of actual court cases citing Rome II has risen steadily: a search of the JURE database (European Commission’s case-law collection) shows over 120 references by 2024, up from fewer than 20 in the first five years.

## Key questions answered

### If someone is hurt in a road accident in another EU country, which country’s law applies?

The law of the country where the accident happens – the “place of damage” – generally applies under Rome II’s default rule. So if a Dutch driver crashes into a Belgian cyclist in France, French law governs liability and damages. The rule applies even if both parties are resident in another country, unless there is a “manifestly closer connection” to another country.

### Why does EU law not cover online defamation cross-border?

The Rome II Regulation explicitly excludes “non-contractual obligations arising out of violations of privacy and rights relating to personality, including defamation” (Article 1(2)(g)). This was a political compromise: media groups and publishers warned that a single uniform rule could force European journalists to comply with multiple libel laws, while some governments (notably Germany and the UK) wanted to keep their own strong protections. As a result, national conflict-of-law rules still apply to online defamation – and the victim can often pick the most favourable forum.

### Does Rome II apply in all EU countries?

No – Denmark does not participate (it negotiated an opt-out), and the United Kingdom left the EU in 2020, so it no longer applies. Ireland chose to opt in. For the remaining 26 member states, Rome II is binding and directly applicable. Courts in those countries must apply the Regulation even if the dispute involves a party from a non-participating country.

### Can parties choose a different law after the damage occurs?

Yes – Article 14 allows the parties to agree, after the event giving rise to the damage occurred, to submit the dispute to a law of their choice. This is intended to facilitate settlements and avoids the risk that a stronger party imposes an unfavourable law on a weaker party before any dispute arises. The choice cannot prejudice the rights of third parties (such as insurers or co-tortfeasors).
