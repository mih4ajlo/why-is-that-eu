## What is it?

Rome II is a uniform set of choice-of-law rules that every EU court (except Denmark's) must apply when a non-contractual dispute crosses borders. If a Polish truck hits a German cyclist in Italy, or a French website defames a Dutch businessman, or a Spanish company is unjustly enriched at the expense of a Belgian counterparty, Rome II tells the seized court which national substantive law governs the claim — regardless of where the case is filed.

The regulation's default rule is *lex loci damni*: the law of the country where the direct damage occurs, not where the tortious act was committed or where any indirect consequences are felt. It then layers exceptions for specific torts — product liability, environmental damage, intellectual property infringement, unfair competition, industrial action — and for unjust enrichment, negotiorum gestio and culpa in contrahendo. Parties can also agree, in most commercial contexts, to choose the applicable law themselves.

Crucially, Rome II is *universal*: the law it designates applies even if it is the law of a non-EU country. The regulation does not deal with which court has jurisdiction (that is the Brussels I Regulation's job) or with how to enforce judgments — only with the substantive law that decides the merits.

## Why was it introduced?

For decades, a French court hearing a cross-border car-crash claim and a German court hearing the same dispute could reach opposite results because they applied different conflict-of-laws rules. The Hague Convention of 1971 on traffic accidents tied courts to the law of the place of the accident; German case law leaned toward *lex loci delicti commissi*; English common law used a double-actionability test; Dutch courts had developed their own approach. The Commission's 2003 proposal openly argued that this fragmentation undermined the predictability that a single market for goods, services and people required, and encouraged forum shopping by claimants hunting for the most generous national tort regime.

The legal base only became available with the Treaty of Amsterdam in 1999, which moved judicial cooperation in civil matters into the Community pillar, and with the Tampere European Council that October, where heads of state and government set out an ambitious programme to build an EU "area of freedom, security and justice." Choice-of-law harmonisation for contracts was already on the table (the 1980 Rome Convention, later converted into the Rome I Regulation); a parallel instrument for non-contractual obligations was the obvious next step. The Commission had been consulting on a draft "Rome II" since 1998, when the European Group for Private International Law circulated a preliminary text.

The negotiations were unusually bruising for a technical instrument. Three issues nearly derailed the file. First, **defamation and privacy**: the European Parliament, led by rapporteur Diana Wallis, pushed hard to include press torts under a special rule favourable to publishers; the UK media industry feared London being forced to apply restrictive continental privacy laws, while French and German legislators were unwilling to expose their citizens to looser English standards. The deadlock was so sharp that defamation was simply *excluded* from the final text — a gap that survives today. Second, **traffic accidents**: several Member States were parties to the 1971 Hague Convention and refused to abandon it, so Rome II preserves those prior treaty obligations. Third, **product liability and environmental damage**: industry groups, particularly the chemicals and pharmaceuticals sectors represented through bodies like CEFIC, lobbied for predictability based on the manufacturer's habitual residence, while consumer and environmental NGOs wanted victims to benefit from the law most protective to them. The compromise — a cascade of connecting factors for product liability (Article 5) and a victim-favouring election right for environmental damage (Article 7) — bears the marks of that fight.

The file went to conciliation between Parliament and Council in 2007, one of the few co-decision dossiers in justice policy to require that procedure. Commissioner Franco Frattini brokered the final compromise; the regulation was adopted in July 2007 and applied from 11 January 2009.

## Timeline

- **1980** — Rome Convention on contractual obligations signed; parallel work on non-contractual obligations stalls for lack of legal base.
- **1999** — Treaty of Amsterdam communitarises judicial cooperation in civil matters; Tampere Council prioritises civil-justice harmonisation.
- **2002** — Commission publishes preliminary draft for public consultation.
- **2003** — Commission formally proposes Rome II (COM(2003) 427).
- **2005** — European Parliament first reading; Wallis report seeks major changes, especially on defamation.
- **2007** — Conciliation committee reaches compromise; defamation excluded; Regulation (EC) 864/2007 adopted on 11 July.
- **2009** — Regulation applies from 11 January across all Member States except Denmark.
- **2012** — Commission report on the application of Article 7 (environmental damage) and review clause findings.
- **2011–present** — CJEU builds a substantial case law, including *Homawoo* (temporal scope), *Kainz* (product liability), *Lazar* (indirect damage to relatives), *Nikiforidis* and *flyLAL* on overriding mandatory rules.

## Impact and consequences

Rome II rewired cross-border civil litigation in Europe. Before 2009, a claimant injured in a road accident abroad faced a lottery: the damages calculation might follow the law of the accident state, the home state, or somewhere in between depending on where suit was filed. After 2009, the *lex loci damni* rule produced far greater predictability — insurers in particular reorganised reserving and pricing around it, and motor-insurance bureaux across Europe restructured claims handling.

The regulation also reshaped corporate practice. Multinational groups began inserting Rome II choice-of-law clauses into commercial framework agreements alongside Rome I clauses, recognising that disputes often straddle contractual and tortious claims. Competition lawyers paid particular attention to Article 6, which makes the law of the affected market applicable to unfair competition and antitrust damages claims — a rule that became central to the wave of follow-on cartel damages litigation after the Damages Directive (2014/104/EU). The CJEU's 2014 *flyLAL* and later judgments built an entire architecture for cross-border competition claims on this provision.

The defamation gap remains the regulation's most visible failure. With no harmonised rule, English courts continued to attract cross-border libel claims — the so-called "libel tourism" phenomenon that prompted the UK's own Defamation Act 2013. The European Parliament has repeatedly called on the Commission to fill the gap, most recently in connection with the 2024 anti-SLAPP Directive, but no proposal has yet materialised. Denmark's opt-out from EU justice cooperation also means Danish courts continue to apply their own conflict-of-laws rules, creating a small but persistent island of fragmentation.

## Key questions answered

### If I'm injured in a car crash abroad in the EU, which country's law decides my compensation?

As a rule, the law of the country where the accident — and the direct injury — occurred. So a Belgian tourist hit by a car in Spain will, in most cases, have damages calculated under Spanish law even if they sue in a Belgian court. The exception is when both driver and victim habitually reside in the same country, in which case that shared home-country law applies. Several older Member States are also still bound by a 1971 Hague Convention that can lead to slightly different outcomes.

### Why can you still sue British tabloids for libel under English law over articles read across Europe?

Because defamation was deliberately left out of the EU's choice-of-law rules. The Parliament and Council could not agree on how to balance press freedom against privacy protection, so each Member State applies its own national conflict rules to libel and privacy claims. That allowed "libel tourism" to flourish in London until the UK's own 2013 reform, and the gap has fed into the EU's more recent debate over strategic lawsuits against journalists (SLAPPs).

### Can companies pick which country's tort law applies to their disputes in advance?

Yes, in most commercial settings. Businesses dealing with each other can agree before or after a dispute arises which national law will govern non-contractual claims between them — a useful tool for supply contracts where claims may sound in both contract and tort. The freedom does not extend to disputes over competition law or intellectual property, and weaker parties such as consumers cannot be bound by pre-dispute choice-of-law agreements.

### Does this apply if the law chosen is from outside the EU?

Yes. The regulation is "universal" — when its rules point to the law of, say, Switzerland, Brazil or the United States, the EU court must apply that foreign law to the merits. Courts can refuse only on narrow grounds, mainly public-policy exceptions or where a Member State's overriding mandatory rules demand otherwise.
