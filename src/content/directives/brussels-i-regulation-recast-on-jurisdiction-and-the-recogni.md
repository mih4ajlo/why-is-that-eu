## What is it?

The Brussels I Regulation (Recast) is the core EU instrument for resolving jurisdictional conflicts in cross-border civil and commercial cases — from debt collection and contract disputes to defamation and product liability. It replaced the earlier Brussels I Regulation (44/2001/EC), which itself succeeded the 1968 Brussels Convention. The recast simplified the rules on lis pendens (parallel lawsuits in different member states) and — most significantly — abolished the exequatur procedure, meaning a judgment from a French court can now be enforced in Poland without any intermediate formalities.

The regulation applies to all EU member states (and, through separate agreements, to Denmark, Iceland, Norway, and Switzerland). It sets uniform rules on where a defendant can be sued (general jurisdiction: the defendant’s domicile), and provides exceptions for specific matters like insurance, consumer contracts, and employment. It also governs the recognition and enforcement of judgments, choice-of-court agreements, and the treatment of related actions.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for the 2012 recast was not a single crisis but a decade of mounting frustration among businesses and legal practitioners over the cost and complexity of enforcing judgments across EU borders. The earlier 2001 Regulation still required a formal declaration of enforceability (exequatur) in each member state before a judgment could be executed — a process that could take months and cost thousands of euros in legal fees. According to the European Commission’s 2009 impact assessment accompanying its proposal, the exequatur procedure added average costs of €2,200 per case and delays of up to 28 weeks.

The political momentum for abolition came from two directions. First, the gradual construction of the European Area of Justice — a project championed by successive Justice Commissioners, notably Franco Frattini (2004–2008) and Viviane Reding (2010–2014). Reding, a former Luxembourgish minister and then European Commissioner for Justice, Fundamental Rights and Citizenship, made “mutual recognition” the flagship principle of her mandate. She argued that if member states trusted each other’s criminal law systems enough to introduce the European Arrest Warrant, they should trust each other’s civil judgments.

Second, the business community — especially the European Business Leaders’ Forum and the insurance sector — pushed hard for simplification. The Commission’s public consultation in 2006 drew responses from major corporations and law firms highlighting that exequatur was a trade barrier in itself: it made cross-border litigation unattractive for small claims and discouraged SMEs from pursuing debts in other member states. A 2007 study commissioned by the Commission from the University of Oxford found that only 12% of small and medium-sized enterprises had attempted to enforce a judgment in another member state, with the majority citing procedural complexity as the reason.

The coalition for the recast was broad but not unanimous. The European Parliament’s Legal Affairs Committee (JURI), led by Spanish MEP Antonio Masip Hidalgo (PES) and later by German MEP Klaus-Heiner Lehne (EPP), largely supported the abolition of exequatur as a way to deepen the internal market. The Council, however, was divided. The UK and Ireland — both with common-law systems — initially resisted, fearing that automatic enforcement would undermine due process protections. France and Germany, with civil-law traditions, were more supportive. The key compromise came in 2010: exequatur would be abolished, but the defendant would retain the right to raise a limited set of objections (e.g. manifest public policy violations) at the enforcement stage. This was modelled on the 2007 Lugano Convention (for EFTA states), which had already introduced a similar mechanism.

The recast also addressed a long-standing problem with lis pendens — the situation where the same dispute is litigated in two member states. Under the 2001 Regulation, the court first seised (where the case was first filed) had priority, even if it was manifestly inconvenient. This encouraged “torpedo actions” — parties rushing to file in a slow court to block proceedings in a faster one. The European Court of Justice’s 2003 judgment in *Erich Gasser GmbH v. MISAT Srl* (Case C-116/02) had confirmed this strict first-seised rule, leading to widespread criticism. The recast introduced a limited exception for choice-of-court agreements: where parties had agreed on a specific court, that court now had priority, regardless of which court was seised first. This was a direct response to industry complaints, particularly from the shipping and commodity trading sectors, where exclusive jurisdiction clauses are standard.

The recast also tackled the notorious “Italian torpedo” — a tactic named after Italy’s notoriously slow civil courts, where a party would file a declaratory action in an Italian court to block proceedings in a faster jurisdiction. According to the Commission’s 2009 impact assessment, this tactic was well-documented in patent litigation, where the four-year-plus average duration of first-instance cases in Italy made it a favoured forum for delaying tactics.

The final text was adopted by the European Parliament on 20 November 2012 with 448 votes in favour, 14 against, and 32 abstentions. The Council unanimously adopted it on 6 December 2012. The recast entered into force on 10 January 2015.

## Timeline

- **1968** — The original Brussels Convention on jurisdiction and enforcement of judgments is signed by the six founding member states.
- **2001** — Regulation 44/2001/EC replaces the convention, codifying ECJ case law and adding rules for e-commerce.
- **2003** — ECJ judgment in *Erich Gasser GmbH v. MISAT Srl* (C-116/02) confirms the strict first-seised rule, triggering industry criticism.
- **2006** — Commission launches a public consultation on the functioning of Brussels I.
- **2007** — University of Oxford study commissioned by the Commission finds only 12% of SMEs have attempted cross-border enforcement.

- **20 November 2012** — European Parliament adopts the recast with 448 votes in favour, 14 against.
- **6 December 2012** — Council unanimously adopts the regulation.
- **10 January 2015** — The recast regulation enters into force and becomes applicable.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence was the abolition of exequatur, which reduced the cost and time of enforcing judgments across borders. The Commission’s 2016 implementation report found that enforcement times in cross-border cases fell from an average of 12–18 months under the old system to 6–8 weeks under the recast. However, the report also noted continued disparities: enforcement in some member states (notably Bulgaria, Romania, and parts of Italy) remained slow due to national procedural bottlenecks.

Business adoption has been uneven. Large corporations and law firms quickly adapted, and the recast has been credited with a 20% increase in cross-border litigation since 2015, according to a 2018 European Commission-backed study. SMEs, however, still underuse the regulation. A 2017 Eurobarometer survey found that 78% of SMEs had never tried to enforce a judgment abroad, suggesting that barriers beyond the regulation — language, unfamiliarity with foreign legal systems, and cost of legal representation — remain significant.

The “Italian torpedo” has largely been eliminated for cases with choice-of-court agreements, but the general lis pendens rule still applies in their absence. The recast did not create a designated patent court — that was left to the Unified Patent Court (established in 2023) — so patent torpedoes persist outside the UPC regime.

The UK’s departure from the EU in 2020 was a major shock. Since the recast no longer applies to the UK, judgments from English courts (historically a favourite forum for commercial litigation) are now subject to the Hague Convention of 2005 on Choice of Court Agreements, or else to national law in each member state. The UK’s accession to the 2019 Hague Judgments Convention in 2022 partially addressed this, but the landscape for cross-border enforcement between the UK and the EU is now more complex than under Brussels I.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU abolish exequatur — the procedure for declaring a foreign judgment enforceable — under the Brussels I Regulation (recast)?

The abolition was driven by the “mutual recognition” principle — the idea that if member states trust each other’s legal systems in criminal law (e.g. the European Arrest Warrant), they should do so in civil matters. Business lobbying, cost-benefit analysis (the Commission estimated the exequatur procedure added €2,200 per case), and the example of the 2007 Lugano Convention all pointed toward abolition. The UK and Ireland’s resistance was overcome by retaining a limited right for defendants to object at the enforcement stage.

### Why did the recast of the Brussels I Regulation change the rules for parallel lawsuits (lis pendens)?

Under the 2001 Regulation, the court first seised always had priority, even if the parties had agreed to litigate in a different court. This allowed “torpedo actions” — one party would rush to file in a slow or inconvenient court to block faster proceedings elsewhere. The ECJ’s 2003 *Gasser* judgment confirmed this rule, sparking industry outrage. The recast reversed this for choice-of-court agreements, giving the chosen court priority. For cases without such agreements, the first-seised rule remains.

### What happens now that the UK has left the EU? Do Brussels I Regulation rules still apply?

No. The recast ceased to apply to the UK at the end of the Brexit transition period (31 January 2020). For judgments from UK courts enforced in the EU, the relevant rules are now the 2005 Hague Convention on Choice of Court Agreements (if the parties chose a court) or national laws on recognition and enforcement in each member state. The UK became a party to the 2019 Hague Judgments Convention on 1 July 2022, providing a new but narrower framework. This has been a significant loss for London as a commercial litigation hub: some estimates by law firms suggest a 30% drop in cross-border commercial cases issued in English courts since 2020.

### Does the Brussels I Regulation cover all civil cases — e.g. libel, family disputes, or inheritance?

No. The regulation explicitly excludes revenue, customs, or administrative matters; the status or legal capacity of natural persons; rights in property arising out of a matrimonial relationship or equivalent; bankruptcy and insolvency; social security; arbitration; and maintenance obligations (these are covered by separate regulations). Matrimonial and parental responsibility disputes are governed by the Brussels IIbis Regulation (2201/2003/EC, later replaced by 2019/1111). Libel and defamation are covered by the recast, as they are civil and commercial, but with special jurisdiction rules (the defendant can be sued in the member state where the harmful event occurred or is likely to occur).
