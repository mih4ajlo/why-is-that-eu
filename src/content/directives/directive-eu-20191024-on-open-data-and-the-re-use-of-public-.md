## What is it?

The Open Data Directive (2019/1024/EU) is the third iteration of EU rules on re-using public sector information (PSI), replacing the 2013 PSI Directive. It requires EU member states to make government-held data—from weather and transport data to statistics and cultural heritage—available for re-use, free of charge, in machine-readable formats, and via application programming interfaces (APIs) where feasible. The directive also introduces a new category of "high-value datasets" that must be made freely available across the EU.

The directive entered into force on 16 July 2019, with member states required to transpose it into national law by 18 July 2021. It applies to public sector bodies, public undertakings in certain sectors (transport and utilities), and research-funding bodies.

## Why was it introduced?

The Open Data Directive was driven by a structural tension: while the 2013 PSI Directive had expanded the scope of re-usable data, the EU's data economy was growing far faster than the public sector's willingness to share. By 2017, the European data economy was valued at €300 billion by the Commission, but public sector information remained locked behind fees, incompatible formats, and restrictive licensing. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment found that only 7% of high-value datasets were available for free re-use across all member states.

The immediate trigger was the Commission's ambitious Digital Single Market strategy, launched under President Jean-Claude Juncker in 2015. Digital Commissioner Günther Oettinger (2014-2016) and his successor Mariya Gabriel (2017-2019) made open data a pillar of the strategy. The key coalition driving the directive comprised the Commission, the European Parliament's ITRE committee (led by rapporteur Neena Gill, S&D UK), and a coalition of open-data start-ups and transparency NGOs, including Open Knowledge International and the Open Data Institute.

The timing mattered: the UK's vote to leave the EU in 2016 created a political vacuum that the Commission filled with a "digital sovereignty" narrative, arguing that European companies needed EU-produced data to compete with US platforms and Chinese state-driven data sharing. The leak of the Panama Papers in 2016 and the Cambridge Analytica scandal in 2018 also boosted momentum for transparency mandates.

The directive's most contentious element—mandating free re-use of high-value datasets—faced opposition from several member states and public-sector bodies that relied on data sales for revenue. The Commission estimated that data-sales revenue across the EU was roughly €1.5 billion annually, while the economic value of free re-use was estimated at €40 billion. The European Parliament's final vote on 4 April 2019 saw 532 votes in favour, 94 against, and 22 abstentions, with the main opposition coming from MEPs from countries like Austria, Belgium, and Germany, where meteorological and land-registry agencies had the highest earnings from data sales.

A critical change from earlier failed attempts: the 2013 PSI Directive had compromised by setting maximum charging limits but never enforcing them. The 2019 directive instead banned charging outright for high-value datasets and limited other charges to marginal cost of reproduction, a red line that the Commission's legal service argued was justified under Article 114 TFEU (internal market harmonisation), not just digital policy.

## Timeline

- **2003** — First PSI Directive (2003/98/EC) adopted, establishing minimum rules for re-use of public sector documents
- **2013** — Revised PSI Directive (2013/37/EU) expands scope to libraries, museums, and archives; introduces charging principles
- **2015** — Commission launches Digital Single Market strategy, identifies open data as priority
- **2017** — Public consultation on PSI reform receives 600+ responses; Commission found 80% of respondents supported free re-use of high-value datasets
- **25 April 2018** — Commission proposal for Open Data Directive published, with Commissioner Gabriel as lead
- **4 April 2019** — European Parliament adopts directive (532-94-22)
- **6 June 2019** — Council formally adopts the directive
- **16 July 2019** — Directive enters into force
- **18 July 2021** — Transposition deadline for member states
- **21 January 2022** — Commission adopts Implementing Regulation on high-value datasets (6 categories: geospatial, earth observation, meteorology, statistics, companies, mobility)
- **2023** — Commission infringement proceedings launched against 6 member states for late or incorrect transposition
- **June 2024** — First evaluation report due (delayed at time of writing)

## Impact and consequences

The directive's primary impact has been a dramatic reduction in fees for high-value datasets. By 2023, the Commission reported that all member states had made meteorological and statistics data freely available, while geospatial and earth observation data saw near-universal free access by early 2024. The Open Data Institute estimated in 2022 that the directive had unlocked datasets worth at least €10 billion in potential economic value, though actual uptake lagged in southern and eastern Europe.

The timeline for compliance varied starkly. The Netherlands, Finland, and Estonia had already adopted open-by-default policies and transposed the directive ahead of the July 2021 deadline. At the other end, Greece, Romania, Croatia, and Bulgaria failed to notify full transposition until late 2022, prompting Commission infringement letters. In Greece, the government estimated that its land registry was losing €12 million in annual fees; to compensate, the Greek Parliament passed a law in December 2021 allowing the land registry to charge for commercial licences on non-high-value data, a practice the Commission later challenged.

Industry response was split: large tech companies like Google and Microsoft pushed for maximum openness, while European SMEs in data-driven sectors—weather analytics firms, logistics companies—reported significant cost savings. The European Association of Meteorological Services (EUMETNET) estimated that free re-use of weather data saved the aviation industry across Europe roughly €200 million annually in data-acquisition costs alone.

One unintended consequence: the mandate for API-based access forced many smaller public authorities, particularly rural councils and local libraries, to invest in technical upgrades they lacked the expertise or budget for. The Commission's 2022 implementation report noted that only 14 member states had fully met the API requirement, with Portugal, Latvia, and Luxembourg struggling most.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a third iteration of PSI rules?

Because the 2013 directive allowed member states to charge above marginal cost for most data, creating a fragmented market where the same meteorological data cost €500 in one country and €50,000 in another. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment found that this patchwork suppressed the data economy by roughly 60% of its potential. The new directive closed that loophole for high-value datasets and harmonised charging to marginal cost for the rest, making cross-border re-use commercially viable.

### Why are cultural heritage institutions exempt from mandatory free re-use?

Libraries, museums, and archives have digitisation and preservation costs the Commission considered disproportionate. The directive allows these institutions to charge cost-plus (actual costs plus a reasonable return on investment) for commercial re-use of cultural data, while non-commercial re-use must be free. This compromise came after intense lobbying from the European Museum Association and the European Bureau of Library, Information and Documentation Associations, which argued in their 2017 joint submission that mandatory free re-use would bankrupt small museums.

### Why were public undertakings included for the first time?

The 2013 directive only covered public sector bodies. The Commission found that transport and utility companies (publicly owned or controlled) generated massive, commercially valuable datasets—train timetables, energy grid loads, port traffic—that were closed to re-use. Including them under the directive was championed by the European Parliament's rapporteur Neena Gill, who argued in a 2018 parliamentary working document that "publicly funded infrastructure data must be a common resource." The inclusion faced strong opposition from Deutsche Bahn and SNCF, but the Commission maintained that Article 114 TFEU gave it the power to regulate public undertakings in the internal market.
