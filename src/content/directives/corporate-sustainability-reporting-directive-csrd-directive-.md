## What is it?

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) replaces the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) and requires all large companies, and listed small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), to publish detailed, audited reports on their environmental, social, and governance (ESG) performance using mandatory European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). Reports must apply the principle of "double materiality" — disclosing both how sustainability issues affect the company (financial materiality) and how the company affects people and the planet (impact materiality).

The directive covers roughly 50,000 companies in the EU, up from approximately 11,700 under the NFRD. It applies to all large companies meeting two of three criteria: more than 250 employees, turnover above €40 million, or balance sheet total above €20 million. Non-EU companies with a €150 million+ turnover in the EU must also report. Reports must be digitally tagged for machine readability (using the European Single Electronic Format) and subject to mandatory assurance — initially "limited assurance" moving toward "reasonable assurance".

## Why was it introduced?

The CSRD was born from a scandal in plain sight: the NFRD of 2014 had become a wild west of voluntary, inconsistent, and often misleading corporate sustainability reporting. The European Commission’s own 2020 Fitness Check on the NFRD found that 82% of companies reported under the directive, but the quality was "highly variable" — many reports lacked comparability, omitted key metrics, and were never audited. The Commission’s impact assessment noted that investors managing €17 trillion in assets had publicly demanded mandatory, standardised ESG data through the 2020 "Statement of Investors' Support for the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive".

The political moment mattered. Ursula von der Leyen, elected Commission President in July 2019, made the European Green Deal her flagship policy within her first 100 days. Internal documents later showed that the Commission’s DG FISMA (Financial Stability, Financial Services and Capital Markets Union) — led by Commissioner Mairead McGuinness — saw sustainability reporting as the "critical infrastructure" for both the Green Deal and the Capital Markets Union. Without reliable ESG data, the EU could neither enforce its own environmental laws nor channel private capital into green investments under the Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852/EU).

The coalition that passed the CSRD was a classic Green Deal alliance: the European Parliament’s centre-right EPP (who wanted to unlock green finance for industry), the Socialists & Democrats (who demanded worker and human rights reporting), and the Greens/EFA (who insisted on double materiality and mandatory assurance). The opposition came primarily from business lobbies. BusinessEurope, the main employer group, warned in February 2022 that the CSRD would create "disproportionate administrative burdens" for SMEs. The European Federation of Accountants (FEE) was split: large accounting firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) backed mandatory assurance as a new revenue stream, while smaller firms warned they lacked capacity. In the Council, the final vote in November 2022 passed with a qualified majority — the Czech presidency secured approval with all 27 member states in favour except a handful of abstentions (notably Germany and Malta, who had concerns about administrative burden).

What broke the previous deadlock was the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic. The Commission’s 2021 proposal for the CSRD was drafted during the first lockdown. Internal Commission documents obtained by NGO ClientEarth show that the crisis "accelerated consensus" that the pre-2020 voluntary system was unworkable. A pivotal moment came in June 2020 when the European Court of Auditors published a special report titled "EU action on non-financial reporting: slow and patchy", concluding that the NFRD had "failed to achieve its objectives" and that the "large majority of companies' reports did not cover all key non-financial issues". This gave the Commission the political cover to propose a radical expansion rather than incremental reform.

The directive also owes its structure to the work of the European Financial Reporting Advisory Group (EFRAG), a private-sector body. In March 2021, the Commission formally mandated EFRAG to draft the first set of ESRS, drawing on existing frameworks like the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), the Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB), and the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD). The first ESRS were adopted as delegated regulations in July 2023 after a heavily lobbied consultation period — environmental NGOs and institutional investors fought for mandatory sector-specific standards, while industry groups succeeded in reducing the number of data points from an initial proposal of over 1,000 to around 800.

## Timeline

- **2014** — The Non-Financial Reporting Directive (2014/95/EU) comes into force, requiring roughly 11,000 large "public interest entities" (listed companies, banks, insurers) to report on environmental, social, and employee matters.
- **2019** — European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen pledges to "revise the non-financial reporting directive" as part of her European Green Deal, launched in December.
- **March 2020** — The European Court of Auditors publishes a special report concluding that the NFRD "failed to achieve its objectives".
- **June 2020** — The Commission’s Fitness Check on the NFRD finds that 82% of companies report under the directive, but quality is "highly variable" and reports are rarely audited.
- **October 2020** — The European Parliament’s JURI Committee (Legal Affairs) adopts a report calling for mandatory, audited ESG reporting for all large companies.
- **April 2021** — The Commission publishes its CSRD proposal as part of a "Sustainable Finance Package", with Commissioner Mairead McGuinness leading the presentation.
- **February 2022** — BusinessEurope warns that the CSRD would create "disproportionate administrative burdens" for SMEs.
- **June 2022** — The European Parliament adopts its negotiating position with 416 votes in favour, 159 against, and 48 abstentions, supporting mandatory double materiality and assurance.
- **November 2022** — The Council formally adopts the CSRD; the Czech presidency secures unanimous support.
- **January 2023** — The CSRD enters into force following publication in the Official Journal on 16 December 2022.
- **July 2023** — The Commission adopts the first set of European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) as delegated regulations, covering climate, pollution, water, biodiversity, circular economy, own workforce, value chain workers, affected communities, and consumers.
- **January 2024** — First reporting entities (large public-interest companies already subject to NFRD) must apply the CSRD rules for the 2024 financial year, with first reports due in 2025.
- **January 2025** — Other large companies (not previously subject to NFRD) must begin reporting for the 2025 financial year.
- **January 2026** — Listed SMEs must begin reporting (with an opt-out until 2028).

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate impact is the sheer scale of companies now covered: from roughly 11,700 under the NFRD to an estimated 50,000 under the CSRD, according to the Commission’s 2021 impact assessment. This includes many mid-cap companies that had never produced a sustainability report before. A 2023 survey by accountancy firm PwC found that 73% of affected companies lacked the internal systems, data collection processes, or ESG expertise to comply by the first deadline.

The auditing profession is the biggest winner. The Big Four accounting firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) spent heavily lobbying for mandatory assurance. A 2022 study by the European Federation of Accountants estimated the CSRD would create a new €2-3 billion per year market for sustainability assurance services in the EU. However, the requirement for "limited assurance" (a moderate level of checking) rather than full "reasonable assurance" was a significant compromise forced by industry — full assurance was deferred to a future Commission review by 2028.

Member states are struggling to implement the directive. By early 2024, only a handful of EU countries had transposed the CSRD into national law; the Commission launched infringement proceedings against several states for delays. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) warned in November 2023 that diverging national transpositions risked fragmenting the single market for ESG data.

The most controversial element is the digital tagging requirement. Companies must mark up their sustainability data using the XBRL digital reporting format, a complex standard that few European firms have experience with. A 2023 pilot by the German Federal Ministry of Justice found that even large DAX-listed companies struggled to produce machine-readable ESRS reports within budget.

## Key questions answered

### Which companies have to report on sustainability in the EU?

All large companies — those with more than 250 employees, €40 million+ turnover, or €20 million+ balance sheet — must produce audited sustainability reports. This covers roughly 50,000 companies, up from 11,700 under the old rules. Listed small and medium-sized enterprises get a phased-in deadline, starting in 2026. Non-EU companies with €150 million+ EU turnover must also report on their EU operations.

### Why does the law require companies to report on both profit and planet impact?

The CSRD uses "double materiality" — a concept pioneered by the Global Reporting Initiative. Companies must report both on financial risks that climate change or social issues pose to their business (financial materiality), and on how their operations affect the environment, workers, and communities (impact materiality). The Commission reasoned in its 2021 proposal that without the impact side, reports would only capture "what matters to the company, not what matters to society".

### Can regulators fine companies for inaccurate sustainability reports?

Yes, but indirectly. The CSRD does not set its own penalties — it amends the Accounting Directive (2013/34/EU), meaning member states must enforce it through existing national company law. Penalties must be "effective, proportionate and dissuasive". For example, Germany’s transposition law (2023) allows fines of up to €10 million or 5% of annual turnover for serious breaches. However, the first enforcement actions are expected to focus on missing reports rather than inaccurate data — the European Securities and Markets Authority said in 2023 that "limited assurance" means auditors only check processes, not every figure.

### How does this law differ from the US Securities and Exchange Commission's climate rules?

The CSRD is much broader in scope and ambition. While the SEC’s 2024 climate disclosure rule (currently stayed by courts) covers only Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions for large US-listed companies, the CSRD mandates reporting on all three scopes (including value chain Scope 3), plus biodiversity, water, circular economy, labour rights, human rights, and governance — roughly 800 data points. The CSRD also requires third-party assurance from day one; the SEC rule phases in assurance only for Scope 1 and 2 after several years.
