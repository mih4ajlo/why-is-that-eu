## What is it?

Regulation (EU) No 236/2012 (the Short Selling Regulation, or SSR) creates a common EU framework for short selling of shares, sovereign debt instruments, and certain credit default swaps (CDS). It requires anyone holding a net short position in shares above a specified threshold to notify the relevant national competent authority, and for higher thresholds also to disclose publicly. For sovereign debt, only private notification to the competent authority is required above a threshold.

The regulation also bans naked short sales of shares (i.e., selling shares that the seller has not borrowed or secured for borrowing), subject to narrow exceptions, and prohibits naked credit default swaps on sovereign debt where the buyer has no underlying exposure to the sovereign entity. It grants national regulators and the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) emergency powers to temporarily restrict short selling in exceptional circumstances.

## Why was it introduced?

The single most dramatic trigger was the Financial Stability Board’s September 2008 decision, following the collapse of Lehman Brothers, to ban short selling of financial stocks in the US and UK. Within days, France, Germany, Italy, and Spain imposed similar bans. The European Commission’s 2010 impact assessment noted that these uncoordinated national measures — applied and lifted at different times — fragmented the single market and undermined regulatory credibility.

But the deeper structural tension was between two competing views of short selling. The European Central Bank and the French government, led by President Nicolas Sarkozy, saw aggressive short selling of sovereign bonds and bank stocks as a destabilising force during the eurozone sovereign debt crisis. In March 2010, Greece’s sovereign CDS spreads surged to 400 basis points; by May 2010 the EU-IMF bailout of Greece was agreed. Hedge funds like Paulson & Co. and Oaktree Capital had publicly taken short positions in Greek debt and Southern European banks. Conversely, the UK’s Financial Services Authority (FSA) and the London-based hedge fund industry argued that short selling provided market liquidity and price discovery.

The timing was driven by the sovereign debt crisis reaching its peak. In August 2011, as Italian and Spanish bond yields spiked above 6%, France, Italy, Spain, and Belgium again imposed temporary short-selling bans on financial stocks. On 12 September 2011, German Chancellor Angela Merkel and President Sarkozy publicly called for a coordinated EU ban. This broke the deadlock: the European Parliament had already voted in July 2011 on a draft regulation, but the Council had stalled. The second wave of national bans shifted the political calculus, and the Commission pushed through a revised text that gave ESMA binding powers to impose emergency bans across the EU.

The key coalition driving the regulation was France, Germany, Italy, and Spain — all countries that had imposed unilateral bans — versus the UK, which consistently voted against the final text. In the Council vote on 22 February 2012, the UK was isolated, its finance minister George Osborne arguing the rules would harm London’s competitiveness. The regulation was adopted by qualified majority with 25 member states in favour; the UK’s opposition did not block it.

Alternatives considered included mandatory buy-in mechanisms (requiring settlement within a specific timeframe), a pure disclosure regime without trading restrictions, and a voluntary industry code. The Commission rejected the voluntary approach after the 2009 CESR report found that “abusive short selling” had contributed to market disorder in financial stocks. The final compromise banned naked short sales but allowed some exemptions for market makers.

## Timeline

- **September 2008** — FSA, SEC, and multiple EU member states impose temporary short-selling bans on financial stocks after Lehman Brothers collapse
- **March 2009** — CESR publishes report finding “abusive short selling” contributed to financial stock volatility
- **April 2010** — European Commission launches consultation on short selling regulation
- **12 September 2011** — Merkel and Sarkozy call for EU-wide ban; Commission accelerates legislative process
- **15 November 2011** — European Parliament votes 510-87 to adopt the regulation
- **22 February 2012** — Council adopts regulation by qualified majority (UK opposed)
- **1 November 2012** — SSR enters into force and applies
- **2013-2015** — ESMA issues regulatory technical standards on threshold calculations and exemptions
- **2020** — During COVID-19 crisis, ESMA does not impose an EU-wide ban but several national regulators (France, Italy, Spain) temporarily restrict short selling under SSR emergency powers
- **2021** — Commission evaluation report finds SSR “largely functioning well” but notes compliance costs

## Impact and consequences

The regulation ended the patchwork of 27 different national rules. A 2014 ESMA review found that after the SSR came into force, net short position disclosures increased 40% on what had been voluntary national regimes. But the cost of compliance fell disproportionately on smaller asset managers — the Association for Financial Markets in Europe (AFME) estimated in 2013 that compliance systems cost each firm between €50,000 and €500,000.

The ban on naked short selling of shares had limited enforcement: between 2013 and 2018, ESMA reported only three cases of serious breaches referred to national authorities, with the largest fine being €500,000 imposed by the Italian CONSOB in 2017. The ban on naked sovereign CDS, however, was more contentious. A 2016 European Systemic Risk Board study found that the ban reduced liquidity in sovereign CDS markets by 15-20% but had no measurable effect on sovereign borrowing costs.

In 2020, when COVID-19 triggered market panic, ESMA declined to impose an EU-wide short-selling ban, unlike in 2008. The Commission’s 2021 evaluation report concluded this showed “market discipline and transparency have increased,” though it noted that national regulators invoked emergency powers more frequently than expected. The UK, after leaving the EU, repealed the SSR equivalent in 2021 and returned to a lighter disclosure-only regime, a move criticised by French authorities as regulatory arbitrage.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU ban naked CDS on sovereign debt under Regulation (EU) No 236/2012 on short selling and certain aspects of credit default swaps when the G20 had not agreed to do so?

The ban was driven by French fears in 2011 that speculators were “betting against” eurozone sovereigns. Germany and France saw the ban as a way to protect Italy and Spain from CDS-driven contagion. The UK argued that CDS pricing reflects fundamental risk, not manipulation. The Commission’s 2011 impact assessment admitted that “evidence of manipulation is limited,” but the political imperative to stabilise sovereign debt markets overrode economic analysis. The ban was unprecedented globally — no major jurisdiction had banned naked sovereign CDS before.

### Why did the UK oppose Regulation (EU) No 236/2012 on short selling and certain aspects of credit default swaps so strongly?

The City of London dominates European short selling: the FSA’s 2012 estimate was that 70-80% of EU short positions were executed through London-based entities. The UK government and hedge fund lobby (backed by the Alternative Investment Management Association) argued that disclosure thresholds were too low, that the sovereign CDS ban would push trading to New York, and that ESMA’s emergency powers duplicated national regulators. The UK’s opposition was the most significant political barrier, but it was unable to attract other member states after the sovereign debt crisis shifted the balance.

### Why didn’t ESMA use its emergency powers under Regulation (EU) No 236/2012 on short selling and certain aspects of credit default swaps during the COVID-19 crash in March 2020?

ESMA decided that the transparency regime (mandatory position disclosures) was sufficient, and that a ban could destabilise markets by removing liquidity. The Commission’s 2021 evaluation report explicitly cited this as a “learning effect” — that the 2008 bans had been panicked actions, while the SSR’s design allowed a more measured response. However, France, Italy, and Spain still imposed national 15-day bans on financial stocks under SSR Article 20, showing that national authorities remained more skeptical than ESMA.
