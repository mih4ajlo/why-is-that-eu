## What is it?

The Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR III) is the EU's legislative package that transposes the final components of the Basel III international banking standards into EU law. It amends the original CRR (Regulation (EU) No 575/2013), alongside the Capital Requirements Directive VI (CRD VI), and was adopted in May 2024. The regulation aims to strengthen the resilience of EU banks by increasing the quality and quantity of capital they must hold against risk-weighted assets, and by introducing a binding "output floor" that prevents banks from using their own internal models to reduce capital requirements below 72.5% of what standardised models would require.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for CRR III was the European Commission's legislative proposal in October 2021, but its roots lie in the global financial crisis of 2007–2008, which revealed catastrophic weaknesses in bank capital buffers. During the crisis, several major EU banks — including Dexia (Belgium/France), Fortis (Belgium/Netherlands), and Anglo Irish Bank — required taxpayer bailouts exceeding hundreds of billions of euros. In response, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision developed the Basel III framework, first published in December 2010 and revised through 2017 to include the "Basel III finalisation" package.

The structural tension driving CRR III was the persistent gap between the EU's initial implementation of Basel III via CRR I (2013) and CRD IV (2013) and the stricter international standards agreed later. The 2017 Basel III finalisation introduced a binding output floor, which EU banks — particularly German Landesbanken and French mutual banks — had successfully lobbied against during the initial transposition. According to the European Banking Authority's 2022 impact assessment, some banks were using internal models to reduce risk-weighted assets by up to 40% compared to standardised approaches, undermining the comparability and credibility of capital ratios.

The coalition that pushed CRR III through was led by the European Parliament's economic committee rapporteur, Markus Ferber (EPP, Germany), and the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen, with strong support from the European Central Bank (ECB). The ECB's 2022 supervisory report argued that without an output floor, the EU's banking system would remain structurally undercapitalised relative to international peers. Opponents included the Association of German Banks (BdB), the French Banking Federation (FBF), and the German Savings Banks Association (DSGV), which warned — in letters to EU institutions during 2019–2020 — that the output floor would increase capital requirements by 10–15% for mid-sized banks focused on lending to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). The German government, under Finance Minister Olaf Scholz, initially opposed a binding floor in 2021, but shifted its position after the EU's 2021 stress test of 50 major banks found that nine would breach capital requirements under adverse scenarios.

Timing was crucial: the package moved forward under the German EU Council presidency (July–December 2020), which prioritised finalising the Basel III implementation. Political momentum accelerated after the collapse of Credit Suisse in March 2023, which, though not an EU institution, demonstrated that even large banks with low capital requirements could fail. The Commission's impact assessment, published alongside the legislative proposal in October 2021, estimated that the reforms would increase EU banks' capital requirements by an aggregate of €37 billion to €97 billion over four years, but that benefits from reduced crisis probability would outweigh costs by a factor of three to one.

## Timeline

- **2017** — The Basel Committee finalises the Basel III reforms, including the 72.5% output floor, subsequent to the 2010–2011 original Basel III framework.
- **2019** — The European Commission launches a public consultation on implementing the final Basel III standards, receiving over 60 responses from banks, associations, and public authorities.
- **October 2021** — The Commission publishes the CRR III and CRD VI legislative proposals, alongside an impact assessment estimating €37–97 billion additional capital needs.
- **June 2022** — The European Parliament adopts its negotiating position with 485 votes in favour, 93 against, and 25 abstentions, broadly supporting the output floor.
- **October 2022** — The Council of the EU adopts its general approach, after intense negotiations among member states over calibration of the output floor and SME risk weights.
- **March 2023** — The collapse of Credit Suisse reinforces urgency for finalising prudential rules.
- **November 2023** — Trilogue negotiations conclude, with a political agreement reached on the final text including phased implementation of the output floor (phased-in over five years starting 2025).
- **May 2024** — CRR III and CRD VI are formally adopted by the European Parliament (with a large majority) and the Council, and published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **January 2025** — Date of application of most provisions, with the output floor phased in from 2025 to 2030.

## Impact and consequences

CRR III imposes the most significant increase in EU bank capital requirements since 2013. The binding output floor directly affects approximately 40–50 of the largest EU banks that use internal ratings-based (IRB) models for credit risk, according to the EBA's 2023 assessment. The European Banking Authority projects that the output floor alone will increase risk-weighted assets for these banks by 15–25%, particularly impacting German, French, and Italian institutions with extensive use of internal models.

Industry response has been mixed. Large banks such as Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas, and UniCredit have publicly stated they will adjust by optimising their internal models rather than reducing lending, but the Association of German Banks has warned of reduced capacity to finance the green transition. The ECB, however, noted in its 2024 financial stability review that the reforms address a "long-standing source of regulatory arbitrage" and that the phase-in period allows banks to adjust without credit contraction.

Countries with concentrated banking systems — such as Estonia, Luxembourg, and Malta — have raised concerns about disproportionate impact on smaller banks, but the final text includes exemptions for institutions below certain thresholds. The first CRR III reporting deadlines (June 2025) will show whether banks have met capital targets; non-compliance will trigger supervisory measures from the ECB and national authorities, including restrictions on dividend distributions and variable remuneration.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU banks oppose the output floor in CRR III so strongly?

EU banks, particularly in Germany and France, argued that the output floor penalises their extensive use of internal models, which they claimed better reflected their actual risk profiles. The BdB and FBF lobbied the Commission repeatedly between 2017 and 2021, warning that a binding floor would raise capital requirements for SME loans by 10–20%, potentially reducing lending to the real economy. They also argued it disadvantaged European banks competing with US institutions that faced less stringent rules under the Federal Reserve's Basel III implementation.

### Why was the final text of CRR III adopted after years of delay despite the 2017 Basel agreement?

The delay stemmed from sharp divisions within the Council of the EU: Germany, France, and other countries with large banking sectors initially pushed for a more gradual, non-binding approach, while the ECB, the European Parliament, and smaller member states like the Netherlands and Sweden demanded stricter implementation. The collapse of Credit Suisse in March 2023 broke the deadlock, providing a concrete demonstration of the risks from low capital buffers. Under the Swedish EU Council presidency (first half of 2023), negotiators reached a compromise: a binding output floor but phased in over five years, with some flexibility for mortgage and SME exposures.

### How does CRR III affect SME lending in practice?

The regulation includes a specific "SME supporting factor" — first introduced in CRR II (2019) — which allows banks to apply a reduced risk weight of 76.19% to SME exposures under the standardised approach. CRR III maintains this factor, but the output floor limits its benefit for banks using internal models. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment estimated that the overall effect on SME lending would be "modest" — a reduction of 0.3–0.5% — while the ECB argued that improved capital quality would reduce funding costs for banks, partially offsetting any credit contraction.

### Why did the European Parliament support stricter rules than the Council for CRR III?

The Parliament's economic committee, led by rapporteur Markus Ferber (EPP), viewed the output floor as essential to restoring confidence in EU bank capital ratios, which had been criticised by the IMF and the Basel Committee as being inflated by internal models. The Parliament's 2022 plenary vote (485 in favour) reflected a political consensus that EU banks needed to align with international standards, particularly after the ECB's 2021 stress test exposed vulnerabilities. Green and left-wing groups also supported stricter rules, arguing that capital adequacy is a precondition for prudential oversight of climate risks.
