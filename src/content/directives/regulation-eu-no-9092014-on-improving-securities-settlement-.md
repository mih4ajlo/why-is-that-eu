## What is it?

A central securities depository (CSD) is the institution that keeps a central record of who owns which securities – shares, bonds, ETFs – and ensures that when a trade is executed, the securities are delivered against payment on the agreed date. Before the CSDR, CSDs were regulated at national level with widely diverging rules on settlement periods, penalty regimes and risk management.

The CSDR sets a single rulebook for all EU CSDs. It requires CSDs to be authorised by their home regulator under common conditions, to segregate client assets, to offer a harmonised settlement cycle (T+2, i.e. trade date plus two days) and to participate in mandatory buy-in and penalty mechanisms if a trade fails to settle on time. The regulation also imposes stronger capital and operational risk requirements on CSDs themselves.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was born from a specific failure exposed by the 2008 financial crisis: when Lehman Brothers collapsed, settlement fails surged across European markets, and the lack of a harmonised penalty framework meant that some counterparties could delay settlement almost cost-free, creating systemic risk. A 2009 report by the European System of Central Banks (ESCB) and the Committee of European Securities Regulators (CESR, now ESMA) concluded that diverging national rules on settlement discipline were a major source of inefficiency and risk.

Commissioner Michel Barnier, then in charge of internal market and services, made post-trade harmonisation a priority. The Commission’s 2012 proposal was explicitly linked to the simultaneous build-out of Target2-Securities (T2S), the European Central Bank’s platform for settling securities in central bank money. Without a unified CSD regulation, T2S would have operated alongside 35 different national penalty regimes, undermining the project’s goal of a single European securities settlement system.

The push did not go uncontested. Large investment banks and asset managers – including the Association for Financial Markets in Europe (AFME) and the International Securities Lending Association (ISLA) – argued that mandatory buy-ins would harm liquidity and drive up costs for end-investors. They succeeded in softening the original proposal: the final text includes a two-year phase‑in of buy-ins, an optional ceiling on cash penalties (set by each CSD), and a carve‑out for market‑making activity. Even so, the European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Kay Swinburne (ECR, UK), pushed for a stronger discipline framework than the Council initially wanted. The final compromise was adopted on 23 July 2014 by a comfortable majority, though several member states (notably the UK and Germany) expressed concerns about the cost of implementation for their large CSDs (Euroclear UK & Ireland and Clearstream).

## Timeline

- **2009** – The ESCB-CESR report on securities settlement identifies the absence of harmonised penalties and settlement cycles as a key vulnerability.
- **7 March 2012** – The European Commission publishes its proposal for a CSD regulation and a regulation on settlement discipline.
- **2013** – The European Parliament and Council reach a political agreement after intense lobbying over mandatory buy‑ins.
- **23 July 2014** – The CSDR is formally adopted as Regulation (EU) 909/2014.
- **17 September 2014** – Most provisions enter into force; CSDs must apply for authorisation under the new rules within six months.
- **2017–2018** – ESMA develops and publishes the Regulatory Technical Standards (RTS) on settlement discipline, including the mandatory buy‑in framework.
- **13 September 2020** – The settlement discipline provisions were originally due to apply, but the Commission, at the request of industry and ESMA, postponed the date by six months, then to February 2022, after market participants warned that the COVID‑19 pandemic had prevented necessary IT testing.
- **1 February 2022** – The settlement discipline rules (cash penalties and mandatory buy‑ins) finally become applicable across the EU.
- **2023–2024** – ESMA begins publishing aggregated fail‑rate data; several national competent authorities start inspecting compliance with the buy‑in obligation.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate effect of the CSDR was regulatory consolidation: the number of authorised CSDs in the EU contracted from over 30 pre‑2014 to around 24 by 2023, as smaller national CSDs merged or outsourced services. For market participants, the introduction of mandatory buy‑ins forced trading desks and repo desks to re‑engineer settlement processes. A 2023 ESMA report noted that the average fail rate across EU equity markets declined by roughly one‑third in the first year after the discipline rules became binding, although it cautioned that the drop could partly reflect changes in market conditions.

Industry reaction has been mixed. The buy‑in requirement, in particular, remains controversial: a 2024 study by the Alternative Investment Management Association (AIMA) found that the cost of executing buy‑ins in fixed‑income markets had been passed through to end investors, and some smaller actors – such as pension funds – have reported difficulties in sourcing replacement bonds. The European Commission launched a targeted review of the settlement discipline framework in 2023, promising to assess whether the mandatory buy‑in threshold (set at 0.5% of the transaction value) should be recalibrated.

National regulators have begun issuing fines for non‑compliance. In 2023, the Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) fined two broker‑dealers for repeatedly failing to report settlement fails on time, though most enforcement has so far taken the form of informal warnings.

## Key questions answered

### What happens if a trade fails to settle in the EU? 
Under CSDR, when a securities trade does not settle by the intended date, the selling party must pay a daily cash penalty calculated as a percentage of the transaction’s value. If the failure persists beyond a set number of days (typically four), a mandatory buy‑in is triggered: the buyer – or a designated agent – must purchase the securities in the open market, and the failing seller covers any price difference as well as all costs. Failures are reported to the relevant CSD and, aggregated, to ESMA.

### Are all central securities depositories in the EU regulated the same way? 
Yes, since the CSDR entered into force, every CSD operating in the EU must obtain authorisation under the same rules on capital, governance, risk management and client asset protection. However, there are still practical differences: some CSDs (notably in the Nordic countries) function as the sole CSD for their market, while others (like Euroclear and Clearstream) operate across multiple jurisdictions under passporting rights. All are supervised by their home national regulator, with ESMA coordinating oversight for cross‑border CSDs.

### Why did the EU’s rules on settlement penalties take so long to implement? 
The settlement discipline measures were originally scheduled to apply in September 2020, but industry participants – led by AFME and the European Central Securities Depositories Association (ECSDA) – argued that the technical integration with Target2‑Securities and internal systems required more time. The COVID‑19 pandemic further delayed testing. The Commission accepted a two‑step deferral: first to February 2021, then to February 2022. The protracted implementation reflected the complexity of automating buy‑in processes across different asset classes and legal environments, as well as the political sensitivity of forcing market‑makers to abide by strict timelines.
