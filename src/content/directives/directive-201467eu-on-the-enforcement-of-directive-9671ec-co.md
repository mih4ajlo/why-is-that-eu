## What is it?

The Posted Workers Enforcement Directive (2014/67/EU) was an EU law designed to crack down on companies that post workers to other EU countries while underpaying them, misclassifying them, or failing to comply with local labour laws. It did not change the underlying conditions of posting (that came in 2018 with the revision of the Posting of Workers Directive), but it gave national authorities stronger tools to detect and penalise abuse.

The directive required posting companies to notify host Member States of their workers’ details, hold a designated contact person in the host country, and retain pay records for at least two years. It also introduced joint liability rules making subcontractors and main contractors liable for unpaid wages, and created a mechanism for administrative cooperation and cross-border exchange of information between labour inspectorates.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for the Enforcement Directive was not a single scandal but a slow-burning political crisis over “social dumping” in the EU’s single market — a crisis that erupted into full public view after the 2004 and 2007 enlargements.

The original Posting of Workers Directive (96/71/EC) was designed in an era when posted workers were a niche phenomenon: engineers temporarily servicing equipment in another Member State. By the late 2000s, that had changed dramatically. The European Commission’s 2012 impact assessment cited a leaked 2011 study estimating that 1.9 million workers were posted in the EU in 2008 and 2010, with numbers rising as much as 10–15% annually. The vast majority were from lower-wage Member States like Poland, Romania, and Lithuania working on construction sites, in meatpacking plants, or as truck drivers in France, Germany, Belgium, and Austria.

The structural tension was an asymmetric shock: post-2004, the EU’s transition rules on labour mobility gradually ended, but companies from new Member States could post workers indefinitely under 96/71/EC using “letterbox” companies registered in low-tax jurisdictions. A German construction company, for instance, could subcontract to a firm registered in the UK but employing Polish workers who paid no social security in Germany and earned far below German collective agreements. The Commission’s 2012 impact assessment noted that in 2011, Germany’s Federal Labour Court (BAG) had ruled that the 96/71/EC framework was “inadequate to prevent abuse” — a rare judicial rebuke of EU law by a national top court.

Political coalition dynamics were explosive. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) had been campaigning for enforcement reform since 2006. The German, Austrian, and French governments — all with powerful construction lobbies and strong unions — pushed hardest. The German Bundestag’s 2011 report on posted worker abuse cited 40,000 cases of detected social security fraud in the construction sector alone between 2006 and 2010. In France, the 2010 report by Depute Jean-Michel Clément found that 40% of posted workers on French construction sites were paid below the legal minimum.

The opposition came from the European Services Forum (ESF) — the lobbying arm of large service companies — and from the governments of the United Kingdom, Poland, Hungary, and the Baltic states. They argued the directive was protectionist and would fragment the single market. The British government, in a 2012 submission to the Commission, warned the rules would “restrict the legitimate use of posted workers” and “disproportionately burden small businesses.” Poland’s Ministry of Economy estimated compliance costs at €2.5 billion annually for Polish firms.

The timing was broken by a specific event: the 2011-2012 economic crisis in the Eurozone periphery. The sovereign debt crisis shifted political attention to the single market’s imbalances. In September 2012, European Commission President José Manuel Barroso announced the “Single Market Act II” package, which explicitly identified “improving the enforcement of posting rules” as a priority. The impetus came from Employment Commissioner László Andor, a Hungarian social democrat who made social dumping his signature issue. Andor’s March 2012 speech at the European Parliament directly blamed “letterbox companies and bogus self-employment” for “undermining the very idea of a social market economy in Europe.”

The directive passed on 15 May 2014 by qualified majority in the Council. The UK, Denmark, and Poland voted against. The European Parliament approved it on 16 April 2014 with a final vote of 476 in favour, 98 against, and 39 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **1996** — Original Posting of Workers Directive (96/71/EC) enters into force, setting minimum conditions for posted workers.
- **2004-2007** — EU enlargements bring in 12 new Member States, dramatically increasing the scale and complexity of posting.
- **2010** — French Depute Jean-Michel Clément publishes report documenting widespread underpayment of posted workers in French construction.
- **2011** — German Federal Labour Court rules 96/71/EC inadequate to prevent abuse in a case involving a Romanian construction company.
- **March 2012** — Commissioner László Andor first signals the Commission will propose an enforcement directive, citing “painful evidence of exploitation.”
- **21 March 2012** — European Commission publishes proposal for the Posted Workers Enforcement Directive (COM(2012) 131).
- **April 2014** — European Parliament approves the directive (476-98-39).
- **15 May 2014** — Council adopts the directive (QMV; UK, Denmark, Poland vote against).
- **18 June 2014** — Directive published in the Official Journal.
- **1 July 2014** — Directive enters into force.
- **18 June 2016** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s immediate effect was uneven. The Commission’s 2019 implementation report found that 22 Member States had fully transposed the directive by the June 2016 deadline, but Belgium and Hungary were late, and the Commission launched infringement proceedings against Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic.

The most concrete impact was on joint liability. France and Germany, which already had national joint liability laws in construction, upheld the standard. The Czech Republic and Slovakia introduced joint liability for the first time in 2016-2017. According to the Commission’s 2019 report, the number of cross-border information requests between national labour inspectorates increased from 1,264 in 2016 to over 5,200 in 2018 — a 300% jump.

The construction sector was most affected. In France, the number of on-site inspections of posted workers rose from 8,000 in 2015 to 12,500 in 2018, according to the French labour inspectorate (DGTIMA). Fines for non-compliance totalled €47.6 million in France between 2016 and 2019.

However, enforcement remained patchy. The Commission’s 2020 evaluation acknowledged that “letterbox companies” remained hard to detect — a 2019 Eurofound study estimated that 10-15% of posted workers in the EU were still working for companies with no real economic activity. The directive did not address the core problem that poster companies could register in one Member State while working entirely in another — a loophole only partially closed by the 2018 revision of the Posted Workers Directive (2018/957/EU).

## Key questions answered

### When do EU companies have to pay posted workers the local minimum wage instead of their home country's wage?

European companies posting workers to another EU country are required to comply with that country’s minimum wage and working conditions — but only for the duration of the posting. If a Polish construction company sends workers to Germany for six months, those workers must be paid at German wage rates, not Polish ones. This obligation was strengthened by the 2014 Enforcement Directive, which demanded that host countries be able to verify this through mandatory prior notification and records retention.

### Can a company that hires a subcontractor be held responsible if that subcontractor doesn't pay workers properly?

Yes, under the 2014 Enforcement Directive, EU countries must introduce joint liability rules making main contractors and subcontractors liable for unpaid wages to posted workers in construction. This means a German general contractor can be required by a French labour inspector to pay back wages owed by a Romanian subcontractor. France, Germany, and Austria already had such laws; the directive pushed the Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Portugal to adopt them for the first time.

### What happens if a Polish truck driver delivers goods inside France for several weeks?

Under the 2014 Enforcement Directive, the Polish company must notify French authorities of the driver’s posting, designate a contact person in France, and keep records of pay and working time for two years. In practice, truckers present a massive enforcement problem — the Commission’s 2019 report found that over 70% of all posted worker declarations in Germany and France related to road transport workers. The issue was only partially resolved by the 2020 Mobility Package that introduced specific posting rules for the transport sector.

### Why did the UK oppose rules that make it easier to enforce pay and conditions for posted workers?

The UK government argued the directive would impose excessive administrative burdens on businesses and undermine the single market’s flexibility. The UK’s 2012 submission to the Commission said the rules would “restrict the legitimate use of posted workers” and “disproportionately burden small businesses.” At the time, the UK had a large number of companies posting workers from other EU countries — especially in construction, hospitality, and transport — and the UK government feared higher compliance costs would push these companies to leave the market.
