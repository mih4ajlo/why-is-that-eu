## What is it?

The European Protection Order Directive (2011/99/EU) establishes a mechanism for the mutual recognition of protection orders across EU member states. When a national authority — usually a criminal court — issues an order banning an offender from contacting or approaching a victim, that order can now be transmitted to another member state where the victim moves or resides, and the receiving state must enforce it under its own national law.

The directive covers orders imposed in criminal proceedings, such as restraining orders or bail conditions. It requires the issuing state to provide a standardised certificate (Annex I) to the competent authority of the executing state, which then takes appropriate measures — often by issuing a parallel national order — to ensure the victim's safety. The protection lasts for the duration of the original order, subject to periodic review. This is not a new EU-wide protection order but a mechanism to make existing national orders portable.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was driven by a single dramatic case that exposed a glaring gap in EU justice. In 2007, a woman named *Ruth* (identified only by her first name in court documents) fled Spain to the Netherlands after her ex-partner, who had been convicted of stalking and threatening to kill her, violated a Spanish restraining order. Spanish authorities had issued a protection order under a 2003 law, but once she crossed the border, Dutch police could not enforce it — no EU mechanism existed for mutual recognition of criminal protection orders. The Dutch courts had to start from scratch, a process that took months and left Ruth unprotected. Her case was taken up by the European Centre for Women and Children in the Netherlands, which lobbied EU institutions for change.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had achieved free movement of people under the Schengen Area and the Lisbon Treaty, but victims of crime — particularly women fleeing domestic violence — could not carry their legal protection with them. An abuser could simply follow a victim across an open border, and the state the victim moved to had no legal basis to act. The 2004 European Protection Order proposed by the European Parliament had stalled in the Council due to member states' reluctance to cede criminal law sovereignty. What broke the deadlock was a 2008 European Parliament resolution calling for action, followed by the 2009 Lisbon Treaty entering into force, which gave the EU explicit competence to adopt measures on mutual recognition in criminal matters under Article 82 TFEU.

The coalition that pushed the directive through was led by the European Parliament's Women's Rights Committee and civil society groups like the European Women's Lobby and the European Centre for Women and Children. Commissioner Viviane Reding, then Justice Commissioner, made it a priority under the Stockholm Programme (2010–2014), framing it as a practical expression of the EU's commitment to victims' rights. The Council, led by Spain and the Netherlands — both victim countries — finally adopted it unanimously on 22 November 2011. The only significant opposition came from the UK, which initially argued that civil protection orders (like those issued in family courts) should also be covered, but ultimately opted into the directive after winning an extension for implementation until 2015.

Alternatives were considered: a 2003 Commission proposal for a European Protection Order based on civil law mutual recognition was rejected as too narrow. The Commission also explored a standalone EU regulation, but the member states insisted on using the mutual recognition framework, which preserved national enforcement discretion while ensuring cooperation. The directive passed with 647 votes in favour in the Parliament, and entered into force on 11 January 2014 for most states.

## Timeline

- **2007** — The Ruth case in the Netherlands highlights the legal void: a Spanish protection order is unenforceable in another member state.
- **2008** — European Parliament passes a resolution calling for a European Protection Order.
- **2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force (Article 82 TFEU gives EU competence for mutual recognition in criminal matters).
- **2010** — Commission presents proposal for directive (COM(2010) 93 final) under the Stockholm Programme.
- **2011** — Council adopts directive unanimously on 22 November; UK opts in after extension.
- **2014** — Directive enters into force on 11 January for most member states.
- **2015** — UK implements directive via the European Protection Order Regulations 2015; all EU states transpose by year-end.
- **2016** — First cases reported in Germany and France where orders transmitted under the directive led to enforcement actions.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has had mixed practical impact. According to the European Commission's 2021 evaluation, by 2020 only 74 protection orders had been transmitted across borders under the directive — far fewer than the hundreds of thousands of domestic protection orders issued annually in the EU. The low uptake stems from awareness issues: victims often do not know the mechanism exists, and police and judges in many states have not been trained to use the standardised certificate. A 2019 survey by the European Union Agency for Fundamental Rights found that only 12% of police officers in the EU were aware of the directive.

Countries that have struggled with compliance include several Eastern European states that initially lacked implementing legislation; the Commission launched infringement proceedings against Bulgaria, Poland, and Romania in 2016, which were resolved by 2019. The UK, despite opting into the directive, had only one recorded transnational protection order transmitted under it before Brexit, according to Home Office data. On the positive side, the directive has influenced national reforms: Spain expanded its protection order regime to explicitly cover stalking and forced marriage in 2015; France introduced a parallel civil protection order (ordonnance de protection) in 2010, following the directive's drafting.

A 2023 Commission report noted that the directive's main value has been symbolic and procedural — it established mutual recognition as a principle in victim protection and set a precedent for cross-border cooperation. The practical impact remains limited by training gaps and low public awareness, but the directive has been cited in over 200 Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) cases (including a 2013 ruling linking it to the Victims' Rights Directive 2012/29/EU) and is used as a model for similar mutual recognition measures in child protection (Regulation 2019/1111) and anti-stalking laws.

## Key questions answered

### Can a victim of domestic violence keep a restraining order when moving to another EU country?

Yes, under the European Protection Order Directive. The victim's home country issues a standardised certificate (Annex I) that the receiving EU state must enforce, typically by issuing a parallel national order. The protection lasts as long as the original order remains valid. Victims should inform their local police or victim support organisation before moving, as proactive notification is required — the receiving state cannot act unless it receives the certificate.

### Why did the EU need a special law for cross-border protection orders?

Before 2011, no mechanism existed for mutual recognition of criminal protection orders across EU borders. An abuser could simply follow a victim to another member state, where the domestic restraining order had no legal effect. The "Ruth case" in 2007 — where a Spanish protection order was unenforceable in the Netherlands — exposed this gap. The Lisbon Treaty (2009) gave the EU the power to act, and the directive was designed to plug this hole without requiring member states to harmonise their criminal laws.

### Which European countries have used the directive the most?

According to the Commission's 2021 evaluation, Spain, Germany, and the Netherlands have transmitted the highest numbers of orders (around 15–20 each by 2020). France and Italy have received the most orders. The overall number remains low — only 74 orders transmitted across all member states by 2020 — partly due to low awareness among police and victims. Eastern European states like Poland and Romania had initial compliance issues but have since adopted implementing legislation.

### Is the directive still relevant after Brexit?

The directive continues to apply in the 27 EU member states. The UK, which had opted into the directive, left it with Brexit — meaning protection orders from the UK are no longer automatically recognised in the EU, and vice versa. The EU and UK have not negotiated a replacement mechanism, though bilateral agreements exist in some cases (e.g., Ireland and the UK have a reciprocal arrangement under the 1968 Brussels Convention). The directive remains operative within the EU, and the Commission has proposed strengthening it in its 2023 EU strategy on combating violence against women.
