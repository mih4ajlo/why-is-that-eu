## What is it?

CRR III is the third major revision of the EU’s Capital Requirements Regulation, the rulebook governing how much capital EU banks must hold against their loans and investments. It completes the EU’s transposition of the Basel III framework agreed by international banking supervisors in 2017. The regulation, adopted together with the Capital Requirements Directive VI (CRD VI), replaces the patchwork of earlier Basel implementations with a single, binding rule across all 27 member states from 1 January 2025.

At its core, CRR III increases the quantity and quality of capital banks must hold, particularly for complex trading activities and mortgage lending. It introduces an “output floor” — a backstop preventing banks from using their own internal risk models to reduce capital requirements below 72.5% of what standardised models would demand. It also tightens requirements for exposures to sovereign debt and central counterparties, and introduces new rules for crypto-asset exposures.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a new scandal but the slowest-moving train wreck in financial regulation: the global Basel III agreement of 2017. After the 2008 financial crisis forced taxpayers to bail out failing banks from Ireland to Greece, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision — the global standard-setter — spent a decade rewriting rules. The problem was that the 2010-11 Basel III agreement had been implemented unevenly across the EU, with national regulators delaying or watering down key provisions. The UK’s Prudential Regulation Authority pushed for early compliance, while Germany’s BaFin and France’s ACPR resisted tighter rules on mortgage lending and sovereign exposures.

The specific driver from 2019 was the European Commission’s recognition that the EU’s 2013 CRR/CRD IV package had left banks with too much discretion to game the system. In a 2019 impact assessment, the Commission found that internal risk models allowed large banks to hold 30-50% less capital than standardised models would require — a disparity it called “unjustified”. The banking lobby fought back hard. The European Banking Federation warned in 2020 that the output floor would raise capital requirements by €70-100 billion, forcing banks to cut lending to SMEs. The German Banking Industry Committee (Deutsche Kreditwirtschaft) specifically argued that the floor would penalise the German *Hausbank* model of relationship-based lending.

The proposal emerged in October 2021 under Commissioner Mairead McGuinness (financial services), who inherited a file that had stalled under her predecessor Valdis Dombrovskis. The timing was politically critical. The COVID-19 pandemic had already forced the ECB to pump €1.85 trillion into bank liquidity through TLTRO-III operations, and the 2022 energy price shock following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine was squeezing bank borrowers. The Commission argued that stronger capital buffers were essential to withstand the coming wave of non-performing loans — which Eurostat data later showed reaching 3.5% of total loans in Greece and 2.8% in Cyprus by mid-2023.

The coalition that passed CRR III was unusual. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, Jonás Fernández (S&D, Spain), brokered a deal between centre-right MEPs who wanted to protect mortgage lending and Greens/Left MEPs who demanded stricter crypto rules. The German government of Olaf Scholz (SPD) eventually backed the output floor after securing a 3-year phase-in for smaller banks. The final text passed the Parliament with 437 votes in favour, 97 against, and 43 abstentions — a comfortable majority but with significant opposition from the far-right Identity and Democracy group and some German liberals (FDP) who argued it would damage Europe’s competitiveness against US banks.

The biggest last-minute fight was over sovereign exposures. Spain and Italy — with debt-to-GDP ratios of 112% and 144% respectively — faced massive capital increases if banks had to hold equity against their government bond holdings. The compromise was a “prudential backstop” requiring extra capital only when sovereign debt exceeded 300% of a bank’s Tier 1 capital, a threshold that only a handful of eurozone banks would breach.

## Timeline

- **2017** — The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision publishes the final Basel III reform package, known as “Basel III: Finalising post-crisis reforms”
- **October 2021** — The European Commission publishes the legislative package for implementing Basel III in the EU, including CRR III and CRD VI proposals
- **April 2023** — The European Parliament’s Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs (ECON) agrees its negotiating position, led by rapporteur Jonás Fernández (S&D)
- **June 2023** — The Council of the EU reaches a general approach on the package, after prolonged disputes between Germany, France, and Italy over the output floor and sovereign exposures
- **December 2023** — Trilogue negotiations between Parliament, Council, and Commission conclude with a provisional political agreement
- **April 2024** — The European Parliament votes to adopt CRR III (437-97-43) and CRD VI
- **May 2024** — The Council of the EU formally adopts the package
- **19 June 2024** — CRR III is published in the Official Journal of the European Union
- **1 January 2025** — CRR III enters into application for most provisions
- **1 January 2028** — Full application of the output floor and phase-in of sovereign exposure rules

## Impact and consequences

For the 110-odd systemically important EU banks directly affected, CRR III represents the most significant capital increase since the original CRR in 2013. The ECB’s 2024 SREP (Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process) found that Tier 1 capital ratios for the largest eurozone banks averaged 15.2% — comfortably above the new minimum, but stress tests showed that full implementation of the output floor could reduce this by 1-2 percentage points for some banks. The biggest hits were expected for Deutsche Bank (which relied heavily on internal models for its trading book) and BNP Paribas (with large sovereign holdings).

The regulation transformed the EU’s treatment of crypto-assets. Banks holding Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other “unbacked” crypto-assets must now hold a full 100% capital charge — meaning every euro of volatile crypto exposure must be backed by a euro of capital. The European Banking Authority, which wrote the implementing technical standards in 2023, said this effectively forced EU banks with retail crypto exposure to either spin off those businesses or raise capital.

The biggest structural change was the elimination of the “internal models” advantage. Under the old CRR/CRD IV framework, the 30 largest EU banks saved an estimated €20 billion annually in capital costs by using proprietary risk models. The output floor eliminated about 70% of these savings by 2028. The Dutch bank ING estimated in 2024 that its capital requirements would rise by €2.5 billion under CRR III, leading it to restructure its corporate loan book.

Smaller banks — those with assets below €5 billion — benefited from a simplified regime, known as the “standardised approach”, which allowed them to avoid the complex internal models entirely. The German Sparkassen and Volksbanken sector, representing 1,200 small institutions, praised this as protecting their business model.

## Key questions answered

### Why do European banks have to hold more capital after the 2008 crisis anyway?

The 2008 financial crisis exposed a fatal design flaw: banks globally were holding so little capital that when US subprime mortgages collapsed, the system nearly died. In the EU, governments bailed out banks with €1.6 trillion in state aid between 2008 and 2014 according to the Commission. Basel III raised capital requirements from 2% of risk-weighted assets to a 4.5% minimum plus various buffers, but left loopholes (internal models, sovereign exemptions) that CRR III finally closes.

### Did the banking industry really oppose these new rules?

Yes, intensely. The European Banking Federation, the Association of German Banks, and France’s Fédération Bancaire Française all lobbied against the output floor in particular, arguing it would reduce SME lending by 1-2% and harm competitiveness against US and Chinese banks. The German banking association warned in a 2022 position paper that the floor would create “competitive disadvantages” for European banks. The final compromise gave them a 5-year phase-in.

### What does the new regulation mean for someone with a mortgage?

Very little directly, but indirectly it may make mortgages slightly more expensive. Under the old rules, many EU banks used internal models to reduce capital requirements on residential mortgages to 10-15% of the standardised amount. CRR III’s output floor limits this advantage. The ECB’s 2023 Financial Stability Review estimated that the new floor could raise mortgage pricing by 5-15 basis points for specialised mortgage lenders, though the effect on diversified retail banks is minimal.

### Can European banks still use their own risk models to decide how much capital to hold?

Technically yes — the regulation allows internal models — but the output floor now sets a minimum. If a bank’s internal model says it needs €5 billion in capital for a loan portfolio, but the standardised model says €7 billion, the bank must hold at least 72.5% of the standardised amount: €5.075 billion. This effectively caps the capital savings banks can generate from internal models.
