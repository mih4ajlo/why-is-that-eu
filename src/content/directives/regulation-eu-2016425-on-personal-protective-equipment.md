## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2016/425 governs the design, manufacture, and conformity assessment of personal protective equipment (PPE) sold in the European Union. It covers everything from construction helmets and welding gloves to life jackets, respirators, and high-visibility workwear. The regulation requires manufacturers to ensure their products meet minimum health and safety requirements, affix the CE marking, and maintain a technical file that allows authorities to trace how compliance was demonstrated.

Unlike the earlier directive it replaced (89/686/EEC), this regulation is directly applicable in all member states without national transposition. It created three risk categories — simple (I), intermediate (II), and complex (III) — with correspondingly stricter conformity assessment procedures for higher-risk PPE. For complex items such as fall-arrest harnesses or filtering respirators, manufacturers must submit their design to a notified body for EU-type examination before placing the product on the market.

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst for revision was a growing gap between the 1989 directive and the reality of the 2010s global PPE market. By 2013, the European Commission’s impact assessment found that counterfeit and mislabelled PPE — particularly imported from Asia — was circulating widely, while national market surveillance authorities lacked the tools and coordination to stop it. A single incident crystallised the problem: between 2010 and 2013, European hospitals reported multiple failures of surgical gloves that had been falsely CE-marked, leading to contamination risks during operations. In the construction sector, tests by German authorities in 2013 showed that over 30% of hard hats sold as certified failed basic impact tests.

Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier launched a reform proposal in March 2013 as part of the “New Legislative Framework” package. The proposal aimed to harmonise conformity assessment procedures, sharpen obligations on importers and distributors, and give market surveillance authorities explicit powers to recall non-compliant products. Industry associations such as the European Safety Federation and the European Committee for Standardisation (CEN) supported stronger rules, but small manufacturers warned that requiring notified-body involvement for more products would raise costs. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, Czech MEP Pavel Svoboda, pushed for a tighter definition of “complex PPE” to avoid loopholes.

The 2014 European elections and the arrival of Jean-Claude Juncker’s Commission did not derail the file. Juncker’s Commissioner for Internal Market, Industry and SMEs, Elżbieta Bieńkowska, kept the reform as a priority. In December 2015, Parliament and Council reached a political agreement after a single trilogue session — unusually fast for product safety legislation. The lack of serious opposition reflected a broad consensus that the old rules were no longer fit for purpose. The regulation was formally adopted in March 2016 and became applicable on 21 April 2018.

## Timeline

- **1989** — Directive 89/686/EEC on PPE adopted, harmonising basic safety requirements across the EU.
- **2008** — New Legislative Framework (Decision 768/2008/EC) sets a template for updating product safety directives; Commission begins reviewing 89/686/EEC.
- **March 2013** — Commission proposes a new PPE Regulation (COM/2013/022 final) under Commissioner Barnier.
- **December 2015** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement after one trilogue meeting.
- **March 2016** — Regulation (EU) 2016/425 adopted by Council and Parliament.
- **21 April 2018** — Regulation becomes applicable, replacing directive 89/686/EEC.
- **2019–2020** — First market surveillance actions: German authorities seize 50,000 non-compliant helmets; a European alert system (RAPEX) records over 1,200 PPE-related alerts per year.
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic triggers temporary Commission recommendation (2020/403) allowing manufacturers to place pandemic-critical PPE on the market without full re-certification, provided they follow alternative procedures.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation significantly tightened the market entry requirements for high-risk PPE. Notified bodies — testing and certification organisations operating in the EU — must now reassess their competence every five years, and the Commission can suspend a notified body that fails to perform. By 2022, the number of notified bodies for PPE had dropped from around 60 under the old directive to roughly 45, as smaller bodies struggled with the cost of reaccreditation.

Small and medium-sized enterprises faced the biggest adjustment. The requirement to submit a technical file and sometimes a design prototype for EU-type examination added months to product development cycles. The European Association for the Protection of Workers (Eur-Proteggi) estimated in 2019 that compliance costs increased by 15–25% for category III PPE.

Consumer and worker groups generally welcomed the changes. The European Trade Union Institute noted that the regulation closed a loophole that had allowed “self-certification” for complex products such as dust masks. However, critics — including some national industry federations — argued that the regulation did not go far enough on market surveillance, because responsibility remained with individual member states rather than a central EU agency. A 2021 European Court of Auditors report confirmed that cross-border enforcement was still weak, with less than 10% of non-compliant products found in one country being flagged to authorities in other member states.

## Key questions answered

### Do all safety helmets and gloves sold in the EU have to meet the same safety standards?

Yes — whether they are made in Germany or imported from China, all PPE sold in the EU must comply with the essential health and safety requirements in the regulation. Manufacturers or importers must carry out the required conformity assessment and affix a CE mark. The regulation sets three risk categories, with the strictest rules — including testing by an independent notified body — applying to items like respirators, fall harnesses, and protective gloves for chemicals.

### How are PPE products tested before they can be sold in the EU?

Manufacturers must first classify the product into one of the three risk categories. For category I (e.g. simple gardening gloves), the manufacturer self-declares conformity using a technical file. For categories II and III, the manufacturer must involve a notified body, which reviews design specifications and may test prototypes. Category III also requires ongoing surveillance of production through random checks or a quality-assurance audit of the factory. All steps must be documented in a technical dossier that authorities can request at any time.

### What happens if a PPE product is found to be unsafe after it is on the market?

National market surveillance authorities can order the recall or withdrawal of non-compliant PPE. They also notify the rest of the EU through the Rapid Alert System (RAPEX). The regulation gives authorities the power to impose penalties, which vary by member state but can include fines and, in serious cases, criminal prosecution. Under the regulation, an importer or distributor who discovers a safety problem must notify the authorities within 48 hours.

### Why did the EU update its PPE rules in 2016, decades after the original directive?

The old 1989 directive was written when PPE was simpler and mostly produced in Europe. By 2010, global supply chains, new materials (e.g. nanocomposite coatings), and a surge of low-cost imports had exposed weaknesses. The Commission’s 2013 impact assessment identified widespread fake CE marks, inconsistent national enforcement, and undefined roles for importers. The 2016 regulation aligned PPE with the EU’s modern product safety framework, giving clearer obligations to every actor in the supply chain and sharper tools to market surveillance authorities.
