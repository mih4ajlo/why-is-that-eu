## What is it?

The SoHO Regulation (2024/1832/EU) replaces the previous patchwork of EU directives governing blood, tissues, and cells — known collectively as substances of human origin (SoHO) — with a single, directly applicable regulation. While the earlier directives focused narrowly on transfusion and transplantation, the new rules cast a far wider net, covering any substance of human origin intended for human application, including use in the manufacturing of advanced therapy medicinal products and medical devices.

The regulation establishes a new framework for authorising SoHO preparations and SoHO establishments (the facilities that collect, process, store, and distribute these substances). It creates an EU-wide electronic registry for SoHO establishments, introduces a "SoHO coordination board" to harmonise enforcement across member states, and sets updated rules for donor protection, traceability, and adverse event reporting. Critically, it also extends oversight to SoHO used in research and manufacturing — areas previously unregulated under EU health law.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for the SoHO Regulation was not a single scandal but a systemic failure exposed by a series of incidents and structural gaps. In 2017, the European Court of Auditors published a damning report finding that the existing blood and tissue directives had been applied inconsistently across member states, with some countries conducting no inspections at all. The auditors found that between 2012 and 2015, only 11 member states had fully implemented the 2004 tissue directive, and eight had not transposed the 2002 blood directive correctly.

The real pressure came from the changing shape of the industry. By 2019, the European Commission's impact assessment estimated that over 25 million units of blood were donated annually in the EU, alongside 35,000 organ transplants and hundreds of thousands of tissue grafts. But an entirely new category of SoHO use had emerged since the original directives were written: the extraction of plasma, platelets, and other components for manufacturing into medicines. The market for plasma-derived medicinal products alone was growing at 8% annually, driven by shortages of immunoglobulins and clotting factors. Yet these manufacturing activities fell into a regulatory grey zone — the blood directive applied only to transfusion, not to industrial use.

Commissioner Stella Kyriakides, then Commissioner for Health and Food Safety, made the SoHO reform a centrepiece of her mandate, warning in 2020 that "the current legal framework no longer reflects the scientific, societal, and economic realities." The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the timeline dramatically, as member states scrambled to secure plasma supplies for convalescent plasma therapy and blood donations dropped by 30-40% during lockdowns, according to the European Blood Alliance.

The European Blood Alliance and the European Tissue and Cell Society were early advocates for reform, lobbying for harmonised standards after the 2017 Court of Auditors report. The pharmaceutical industry — particularly companies like Grifols, CSL Behring, and Takeda, which rely on European plasma for fractionation — pushed for clearer rules on industrial use. The European Parliament's rapporteur, German MEP Virginie Joron, was a vocal proponent of stronger donor protections after reports emerged of unethical plasma collection practices in some Eastern European countries.

The Council adopted the regulation with a qualified majority on May 27, 2024, with Austria, Poland, and Slovakia voting against — citing concerns over national sovereignty in health systems and potential costs for smaller member states. The Parliament approved it 477 votes to 93 on April 24, 2024.

## Timeline

- **2002** — Blood Directive (2002/98/EC) sets minimum standards for blood collection and testing
- **2004** — Tissue and Cells Directive (2004/23/EC) establishes quality and safety standards for tissues and cells
- **2017** — European Court of Auditors report reveals inconsistent implementation: only 11 member states fully comply with tissue directive
- **2019** — European Commission publishes impact assessment estimating 80,000 adverse reactions to blood transfusions annually in the EU
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic begins; plasma shortages and blood donation drops reach crisis levels across Europe
- **2022** — Commission formally proposes the SoHO Regulation as part of the EU Pharmaceutical Strategy
- **2024, April** — European Parliament approves the regulation with 477 votes in favour, 93 against
- **2024, May** — Council adopts the regulation with Austria, Poland, and Slovakia voting against
- **2024, July** — Regulation published in the Official Journal
- **2027** — Full application date for most provisions; five-year transition period for SoHO preparations and establishments

## Impact and consequences

The regulation makes three consequential changes to the European health landscape. First, it shifts oversight from transposed national laws to directly binding EU rules, eliminating the variation that allowed some member states to operate de facto unregulated plasma collection systems. Second, it closes the manufacturing loophole: SoHO used in medicines must now meet traceability and quality standards identical to those for transplant use. Third, it creates an EU-level enforcement infrastructure.

Industry has responded unevenly. The plasma fractionation sector — dominated by about a dozen large facilities — faces the most significant adjustment costs, estimated by the Commission at €16,000-€270,000 per establishment depending on size. Smaller blood banks and tissue establishments, many operating at national level, are likely to struggle with the new electronic registry and authorisation requirements. The European Blood Alliance has warned that "the administrative burden on small establishments may be disproportionate."

The regulation's adoption forced a spike in compliance activity: as of late 2024, according to the Commission's public registry, 12 member states had begun updating their national SoHO establishment licences to meet the new standards. The SoHO Coordination Board, which began operating in early 2025, has already flagged potential shortages in immunoglobulins in seven countries due to new plasma fractionation oversight.

## Key questions answered

### What body parts and fluids can now be legally used for medical treatments in Europe?

EU law now covers blood, blood components, tissues, cells — including stem cells, reproductive cells, and fetal cells — and any substance extracted from a human body intended for medical application. It also covers SoHO used in manufacturing medicines, which was previously unregulated at EU level. Organs for transplantation are covered by a separate 2010 directive.

### Why did Europe change the rules for blood, tissue, and cell donations?

The old rules from 2002 and 2004 were written when most SoHO were used directly in transfusion or transplant. Since then, the plasma-derived medicine industry has exploded — producing everything from immunoglobulins to clotting factors — but these industrial activities fell into a regulatory gap. A 2017 European Court of Auditors report showed the rules were applied so unevenly across countries that patients in some member states had essentially no protection.

### Who pushed back against the updated European health rules and what were their concerns?

Austria, Poland, and Slovakia voted against the regulation in the Council, arguing it infringed on national health system autonomy. The Polish government specifically stated it would increase costs for smaller member states without improving safety. The European Blood Alliance warned that the administrative burden could overwhelm small blood banks, while the pharmaceutical lobby supported the regulation but pushed for longer transition periods to avoid supply disruptions.

### Will the new European rules stop donors from being taken advantage of?

Yes, and this was a major driver of the regulation. The new rules ban financial incentives for SoHO donations, prohibit misleading marketing of donation procedures, and require specific informed consent for all SoHO uses — including in research and manufacturing. These provisions came after reports of unethical plasma collection in Eastern Europe and after the European Parliament received petitions from donor advocacy groups.
