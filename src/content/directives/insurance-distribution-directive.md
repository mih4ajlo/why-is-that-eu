## What is it?

The Insurance Distribution Directive (IDD) is an EU directive that sets out the regulatory framework for the selling of insurance products across the European Union. It replaced the earlier Insurance Mediation Directive (IMD) from 2002, expanding the scope to cover all "distributors" — not just brokers and agents, but also insurance companies selling directly and ancillary sellers like car rental firms or travel agencies.

The IDD imposes harmonised rules on conduct of business, disclosure of information, professional qualifications, and conflicts of interest. It introduced mandatory suitability and appropriateness assessments for certain products, pre-contractual disclosure documents like the Insurance Product Information Document (IPID) for non-life insurance, and a ban on commission payments that distort consumer choice. For life insurance with investment elements, the directive aligned rules with the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID II).

## Why was it introduced?

The IDD was a direct response to the PPI mis-selling scandal in the United Kingdom, which by 2014 had cost UK banks over £27 billion in compensation — the largest financial scandal in EU history. Payment Protection Insurance (PPI) was sold alongside loans and credit cards, often to people who were ineligible to claim, with sales staff incentivised by high commissions. The scandal exposed a fundamental structural tension: the IMD allowed intermediaries to operate under national regimes with minimal harmonisation, enabling commission-driven mis-selling without adequate consumer safeguards.

The European Commission’s 2012 impact assessment found widespread consumer detriment across member states. According to the Commission, 17% of insurance claims investigated by national authorities between 2008 and 2010 involved mis-selling or inadequate advice, and over half of consumers surveyed did not understand key policy terms. The IMD’s minimal-harmonisation approach meant that rules varied dramatically — Germany banned commissions for some products, while Greece had no suitability assessment at all.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. Consumer organisations and regulators — notably the UK’s Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), which had been at the forefront of PPI enforcement — pushed for strong conduct rules. The European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Burkhard Balz (EPP, Germany), initially proposed extending MiFID-style ban on commissions for all investment products. The European insurance industry, represented by Insurance Europe, fought this fiercely, arguing that commissions were essential for independent intermediaries and that a full ban would push consumers to unregulated markets. A compromise was reached: the IDD applied MiFID-style rules only to investment-related life insurance, with enhanced disclosure and conflict-of-interest rules for other products.

Timing was critical. The PPI scandal peaked in 2012-2013, with the UK’s Supreme Court ruling in *Plevin v. Paragon Personal Finance* (2014) that undisclosed commissions could make insurance relationships unfair. Meanwhile, the 2008 financial crisis had eroded public trust in financial services, and the EU was in the midst of a wave of consumer-protection legislation under the Barroso II Commission. Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier, who had overseen the revision of the Consumer Credit Directive and the Mortgage Credit Directive, made insurance distribution a priority.

What changed the legislative dynamic was the UK’s experience. Earlier attempts to revise the IMD had stalled in 2007-2009 because member states could not agree on scope or commission rules. The sheer scale of PPI compensation — by 2017, UK banks had paid out £32 billion — made inaction politically untenable. The UK’s Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 amendments in 2012, banning commission-driven sales of PPI, created a template that EU negotiators could cite. The Irish Central Bank’s 2014 report on tracker mortgage mis-selling added further pressure.

The European Commission proposed the IDD in July 2012. The European Parliament adopted its position in February 2014, and after trilogue negotiations, the final text was adopted in January 2016. Member states had until February 2018 to transpose the directive into national law, with application from October 2018.

## Timeline

- **2002** — The original Insurance Mediation Directive (2002/92/EC) enters force, harmonising registration requirements but leaving conduct rules to member states
- **2005-2010** — PPI mis-selling escalates in the UK; banks like Lloyds, Barclays, and RBS face growing complaints
- **2011** — UK High Court dismisses banks' challenge to FSA rules banning PPI sales without customer initiative; compensation costs begin to spiral
- **July 2012** — European Commission publishes proposal for the IDD, citing PPI mis-selling and inconsistent consumer protection across member states
- **February 2014** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with strong commission-disclosure requirements
- **January 2016** — The IDD is formally adopted by the European Parliament and Council as Directive (EU) 2016/97
- **February 2018** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law
- **October 2018** — The IDD applies across all EU member states
- **2019-2022** — National authorities begin enforcement; the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) issues guidelines on product oversight and governance

## Impact and consequences

The IDD forced fundamental changes in how insurance is sold across the EU. Member states with previously lax regimes — such as Bulgaria, Romania, and Poland — had to introduce mandatory qualification requirements for insurance sales staff. Countries like Germany and the Netherlands, which already had strong consumer protection, faced relatively minor adjustments but benefited from the harmonised EU passport for distributors.

Industry compliance costs were significant. A 2020 study commissioned by Insurance Europe estimated that one-off adaptation costs totalled €1.2 billion across the EU, with ongoing compliance costs around €400 million annually. Smaller intermediaries — often sole traders or family businesses — struggled most, with some national associations reporting 15-20% of agents leaving the sector rather than meeting new qualification and disclosure requirements.

The IPID — a standardised two-page document explaining key features of non-life insurance — became a consumer-facing success story. EIOPA’s 2021 consumer survey found that 68% of customers who received an IPID said it helped them compare products, though only 44% said they fully understood it.

Enforcement has been uneven. In 2020, the Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) fined several intermediaries for failing to comply with IDD suitability assessments. The Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF) revoked 23 licences in 2019 for non-compliance with qualification requirements. The IDD’s ban on commission structures that create conflicts of interest led to a shift from upfront to trail commissions, particularly in the UK (which transposed the IDD despite Brexit, as it applied before withdrawal).

## Key questions answered

### Why did the IDD expand the definition of "insurance distributor"?

The original IMD only regulated insurance intermediaries — brokers and agents. The PPI scandal showed that banks, car dealers, and travel agencies selling insurance as ancillary products often lacked oversight. The IDD brought all distributors under the same rules, recognising that the sales channel — not just the product — determined consumer risk.

### Why didn't the IDD ban commissions entirely?

The European Parliament proposed a full ban for investment-related products, but industry opposition from Insurance Europe and some member states (notably Germany and Austria, where commission-based models dominate) forced a compromise. The IDD instead required commissions to be disclosed, banned volume-based remuneration that could distort advice, and applied MiFID-like bans only to investment life insurance with savings elements.

### Why did the UK's PPI scandal drive the IDD?

The UK's PPI mis-selling was the largest consumer financial scandal in EU history, with over £32 billion in compensation paid by 2017. It demonstrated the systemic harm from commission-driven sales without suitability checks. The UK's regulatory response — banning PPI sales without customer request, forcing firms to disclose commission — provided a practical template that the European Commission adopted in its 2012 proposal.
