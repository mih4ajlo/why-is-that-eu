## What is it?

The Landfill Directive (1999/31/EC) establishes uniform technical and operational requirements for landfill sites across the European Union. It mandates that all landfills be classified into three categories — hazardous waste, non-hazardous waste, and inert waste — and requires that only treated waste be accepted for disposal. Operators must obtain permits, monitor leachate and gas emissions for decades after closure, and provide financial guarantees to cover post-closure care.

The directive's most consequential provision is the progressive reduction target for biodegradable municipal waste (BMW) going to landfill: Member States were required to cut BMW landfilling to 75% of 1995 levels by 2006, 50% by 2009, and 35% by 2016. This effectively forced a European-wide shift from dumping waste to composting, recycling, incinerating, or otherwise treating it before disposal.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to a series of environmental scandals across Europe in the 1980s and 1990s. The most dramatic trigger was the **1989 Prüm disaster** in West Germany, where the illegally operated "Monte Scherbelino" landfill — a former quarry that had become Germany's largest waste dump — contaminated groundwater and emitted explosive methane gas, forcing the evacuation of nearby villages. A leaked internal German environment ministry report in 1990 revealed that over 40% of the country's 4,000 active landfills had no groundwater protection lining.

The structural tension was clear: the EU's post-war economic boom had created a waste explosion, but landfill regulation remained a national patchwork. Countries like Greece, Ireland, and Italy sent over 90% of their municipal waste to unlined dumps, while Denmark, with scarce land, had already banned landfilling of combustible waste in 1985. This created both an environmental hazard and an economic distortion — waste-intensive industries relocated to less regulated states.

Commissioner for the Environment **Ritt Bjerregaard** led the push from 1994, but faced fierce opposition. The waste management industry — led by **Veolia**, **Suez**, and **Remondis** — warned of "massive job losses" and €20 billion in compliance costs, according to their 1997 joint submission. The Council was split: the UK, Spain, and Greece argued that the 1995 baseline unfairly penalized countries that had already reduced landfilling (e.g., Denmark at 37% BMW landfilled) while demanding huge investments from laggards (the UK at 84%). The European Parliament's environment committee, chaired by **Ken Collins** (UK, PES), pushed for even stricter targets.

What broke the deadlock was **two events in 1998**. First, a catastrophic landslide at the **Aznalcóllar mine dump** in Spain released toxic sludge into the Guadiamar River — though technically a mining waste incident, it dominated Spanish headlines and made the conservative Aznar government suddenly pro-regulation. Second, the **Leozinho landfill disaster** near Lisbon in August 1998, where a waste pile collapsed and killed two workers, triggered Portugal to drop its opposition. The Environmental Council of June 1999 passed the directive by qualified majority, with only Greece still voting no.

## Timeline

- **1992** — European Commission publishes first draft proposal under Commissioner **Carlo Ripa di Meana** (DG Environment)
- **1994** — Revised proposal under Commissioner **Ritt Bjerregaard** introduces binding BMW reduction targets
- **24 September 1996** — European Parliament adopts its first reading position, demanding targets of 10% reduction of 1995 levels by 2010
- **18 December 1997** — Council reaches a common position; Parliament criticises the "watered-down" 35% target by 2016
- **April 1999** — Conciliation committee reaches compromise; Parliament approves on 5 May 1999 (395 votes for, 41 against)
- **20 June 1999** — Council formally adopts the directive
- **16 July 1999** — Directive enters into force
- **16 July 2001** — Member States required to transpose into national law
- **16 July 2009** — Deadline for BMW reduction to 50% of 1995 levels
- **16 July 2016** — Deadline for BMW reduction to 35% of 1995 levels
- **30 May 2018** — Amending Directive (EU) 2018/850 enters into force, setting landfill restriction target of maximum 10% of municipal waste by 2035

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate effect was the closure of thousands of illegal or non-compliant landfills. According to the **European Environment Agency's 2019 "The European environment — state and outlook" report**, the number of landfill sites in the EU-27 fell from roughly 23,000 in 1995 to fewer than 2,500 by 2018. In Italy alone, **ANPA (the national environmental protection agency)** recorded a decline from 4,200 dumps in 1999 to 650 by 2010.

The BMW targets reshaped waste management entirely. Eurostat data shows that in 1995 61% of EU municipal waste was landfilled, falling to 23% by 2020. Countries like Germany, Austria, and the Netherlands now landfill less than 1% of municipal waste — effectively a ban achieved through the directive. Conversely, **Malta and Cyprus** were granted derogations and still landfilled over 70% as of 2021, prompting the Commission to open infringement proceedings.

The directive's requirement for leachate and methane monitoring created a new industry. By 2015, landfill gas capture — extracting methane for energy — provided electricity for roughly 1.5 million European households annually, according to the **European Biogas Association**.

However, unintended consequences emerged. The sharp reduction in landfill capacity created a landfill "gap" in countries like the UK, where permit delays for new landfills drove up illegal dumping — **Flycapture**, the UK national database, recorded a 42% increase in fly-tipping incidents between 2004 and 2008, according to Defra's 2009 report.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU ban dumping raw, untreated trash in landfills?

The 1989 Prüm landfill gas explosion in Germany and the 1998 Leozinho collapse in Portugal showed that treating waste as a simple "dump" created deadly environmental and public health risks. The directive responded by requiring all waste to be treated — shredded, composted, incinerated, or chemically stabilized — before landfilling, to reduce volume, prevent gas generation, and stop groundwater contamination.

### Are any landfills still allowed in Europe?

Yes, but only lined, permitted, and monitored sites classified into three categories — inert waste (e.g., construction rubble), non-hazardous waste, and hazardous waste. Mixed dumping is banned. Operators must maintain insurance and provide a €100-200 million typical financial guarantee per large site to cover 30+ years of post-closure monitoring.

### Why do some EU countries still bury most of their trash despite the rules?

The directive sets reduction targets but not absolute bans. Countries like Malta, Cyprus, and Greece received longer transitional periods. Failure to meet the 2016 BMW target of 35% of 1995 levels led the Commission to open infringement cases — Greece was referred to the CJEU in 2023 for still landfilling 80% of its waste.

### What is the 2035 limit on landfilling and how does it connect to these waste rules?

The 2018 amendment (Directive (EU) 2018/850) set a strict limit: by 2035, no Member State may landfill more than 10% of its municipal waste. This is an absolute cap, unlike the original 1999 directive's relative targets. Countries that still landfill heavily — like Romania (69% in 2020) and Croatia (54%) — face massive investment needs to build treatment infrastructure and will likely miss the deadline without derogations.
