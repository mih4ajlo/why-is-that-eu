## What is it?

The Work-Life Balance Directive (2019/1158/EU) sets EU-wide minimum requirements for leave policies related to parenting and caregiving. It mandates at least 10 working days of paid paternity leave, 4 months of parental leave per parent (with 2 months non-transferable and paid at a level set by each member state), and 5 working days of unpaid carers' leave per year. It also introduces the right to request flexible working arrangements for parents and carers.

The directive replaces the weaker 2010 Parental Leave Directive (which required no payment for parental leave) and goes beyond the 1996 Parental Leave Framework Agreement, marking the first time the EU has required paid paternity leave across all member states.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origins lie in a political crisis of low birth rates and stubborn gender inequality that the European Commission's 2017 "Pillars of Social Rights" package identified as structural failures. By 2017, the EU birth rate had fallen to 1.6 children per woman, far below replacement levels, while women's employment rates in 25-to-54-year-olds hovered around 70% compared to 86% for men, according to Eurostat. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment on the directive showed that 64% of women cited family responsibilities as their main reason for being out of the labour force, compared to just 30% of men.

The specific trigger was the Commission's decision in April 2017 to withdraw the stalled 2008 Maternity Leave Directive proposal — which had aimed to extend maternity leave from 14 to 20 weeks fully paid — after a decade of deadlock in the Council, blocked primarily by the UK, Germany, and Malta. Commissioner for Employment Marianne Thyssen (2014-2019) pivoted to a broader work-life balance approach that included fathers and carers, framing it as a growth issue rather than just a women's issue. Thyssen argued publicly that "if we want to increase the labour market participation of women, we need to involve men in care."

The coalition supporting the directive was unusual: left-wing MEPs in the European Parliament's Committee on Employment (EMPL) worked with the European Women's Lobby and business associations like BusinessEurope, who wanted predictable EU-wide rules rather than a patchwork of national laws. The main opposition came from the European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) group and the UK's Conservative MEPs, who argued that leave policies should remain national. In the final Parliament vote on April 4, 2019, the directive passed with 490 in favour, 82 against, and 78 abstentions.

The timing was crucial. The directive was fast-tracked through the legislative process in the final year of the Juncker Commission (2014-2019), taking just 2 years from proposal to adoption, unusually quick for social legislation. This was possible because of the post-2015 migration crisis, which had pushed work-life balance down the agenda but then created urgency around female labour market integration as a solution to integration challenges. The Commission also leveraged the 2017 European Pillar of Social Rights proclamation, which gave political cover for new social standards.

What changed from earlier attempts was the inclusion of paid paternity leave. Earlier efforts had focused exclusively on maternity leave, which pitted member states with generous existing provisions (like Sweden's 480 days) against laggards without. By framing paternity leave as a tool for both gender equality and labour market flexibility, the Commission broke the deadlock.

## Timeline

- **1996** — EU adopts Parental Leave Directive (96/34/EC) under the Social Policy Agreement, requiring 3 months unpaid parental leave, but fails to address gender take-up gap
- **2010** — Revised Parental Leave Directive (2010/18/EU) extends leave to 4 months but still allows it to be unpaid, leading to men taking only 2% of parental leave in some member states according to a 2017 Commission report
- **2008-2015** — Commission proposal for 20-week fully-paid maternity leave stalls in Council, blocked by UK, Germany, and Malta; formally withdrawn in 2017
- **April 26, 2017** — Commission publishes the European Pillar of Social Rights, listing work-life balance as a key principle
- **May 2017** — Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker announces new work-life balance proposal in his State of the Union speech
- **April 2018** — Commissioner Thyssen publishes the formal legislative proposal for the Work-Life Balance Directive
- **April 4, 2019** — European Parliament adopts the directive by 490-82; Council adopts it officially on June 13, 2019
- **July 1, 2019** — Directive enters into force
- **August 2, 2022** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law (extended due to COVID-19, but most countries met the extended deadline)
- **2023-2024** — Commission begins infringement proceedings against several member states (e.g., Poland, Hungary, Czechia) for incomplete transposition, particularly on paternity leave payment levels

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact was forcing 4 member states — Poland, Czechia, Slovakia, and Lithuania — to introduce paid paternity leave for the first time. In Poland, a 2019 government report showed that before transposition, only 1% of eligible Polish fathers took parental leave because it was unpaid; after introducing the minimum pay level (70% of salary), take-up rose to 12% by 2022, according to a European Commission 2023 evaluation. In Czechia, the Constitutional Court in October 2022 struck down the government's initial transposition law as insufficient, ordering payment of at least 60% of salary.

According to the Commission's 2023 report on transposition, 24 of the 27 member states met the August 2022 deadline, with Belgium, Ireland, and the Netherlands late by several months. The Commission has launched formal infringement procedures against Hungary and Poland for failing to ensure paternity leave is paid at a level that effectively promotes take-up; as of mid-2024, no fines had been issued.

Industry response was mixed. BusinessEurope formally supported the directive based on its flexibility provisions (allowing leave to be taken flexibly or part-time) but warned of administrative burdens for small businesses. The European Trade Union Confederation pushed for higher pay levels, arguing the directive's floor of payment "at a level adequate to encourage take-up" was too vague; a 2022 ETUC study found that only Sweden, Denmark, and Finland actually paid full salary during paternity leave, while Germany paid 67% and France 26%.

The COVID-19 pandemic distorted the directive's early impact. In 2020, Eurofound's COVID-19 survey found that women in the EU took on 71% of additional unpaid care work during lockdowns, reversing some work-life balance gains. However, the directive's flexible working rights became valuable tools: in the Netherlands, a 2023 evaluation by the Social and Economic Council found that 45% of employees exercised the new right to request flexible working arrangements within 6 months of transposition.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU act on work-life balance rather than leave it to member states?

Because the 2008-2015 deadlock on maternity leave proved that member states would not voluntarily harmonise leave policies. The Commission saw the issue as structural: without EU action to make paternity leave paid and non-transferable, the gender employment gap would persist. The 2017 European Pillar of Social Rights gave the Commission the political mandate to act, framing it as a competitiveness issue rather than just a social one.

### Why is paternity leave paid in the directive but parental leave payment is vague?

The compromise between member states in the Council negotiations split the difference. Paternity leave had to be paid because Commission data showed that only paid leave achieves significant male take-up (Eurofound 2018 report found take-up rates of 2-5% for unpaid paternity leave vs 44-80% for paid in countries like Sweden). Parental leave payment levels were left to member states because richer countries like Sweden (with full pay for 480 days) and poorer countries like Bulgaria (with minimal pay) could not agree on a common floor.

### How did this directive affect the employment of women?

By making fathers' leave paid and non-transferable, the directive aimed to reduce employers' hidden preference for hiring men over women (the "motherhood penalty"). A 2022 study by the European Institute for Gender Equality (EIGE) projected that closing the gender employment gap by 50% through such policies could boost EU GDP by up to 3.5% annually by 2050. Early evidence from Spain (which introduced 16 weeks paid paternity leave in 2021 under the directive) showed that the gender employment gap for parents with children under 6 shrank from 18.3% in 2019 to 14.1% in 2022, according to the Spanish Ministry of Labour.
