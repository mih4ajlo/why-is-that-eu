## What is it?

The Farm to Fork Strategy is a non-binding communication from the European Commission, published on 20 May 2020, outlining a blueprint for transitioning the EU’s food system to become "fair, healthy, and environmentally-friendly." It is not a single regulation but a policy framework that drives subsequent legislative proposals, including binding targets for pesticide reduction, nutrient loss, organic farming expansion, and antibiotic use in livestock.

The Strategy targets the entire food chain — from production and processing to retail and consumption — seeking to halve the use of chemical pesticides, reduce fertilizer use by at least 20% by 2030, increase organic farmland to 25% of total agricultural area, and cut food waste by 50% by 2030. It also proposes mandatory front-of-pack nutrition labeling (eventually the Nutri-Score system gained prominence, despite resistance) and stricter animal welfare rules.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was not a single scandal, but a convergence of structural pressures, a political window opened by the European Green Deal, and a global pandemic. The most specific, dramatic element was the COVID-19 pandemic itself, which hit in early 2020 just as the Commission was finalizing the Green Deal legislative package. The lockdowns exposed the fragility of global food supply chains — supermarket shelves in Italy and Spain emptied of fresh produce, while farmers dumped milk and ploughed under vegetables due to disrupted logistics. Commissioner for Health and Food Safety, Stella Kyriakides, stated in the Strategy’s launch: "The current pandemic has shown us how fragile our society is and how important it is to restore the balance between nature and human activity." This crisis broke previous deadlocks in EU food policy.

The **structural tension** lay in the contradiction between the EU’s Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), which for decades had subsidized high-intensity production (by 2019, over €58 billion annually, according to the Commission’s 2020 budget), and growing scientific evidence of ecological damage. The European Environment Agency’s 2019 "State of Nature" report showed that only 23% of EU agricultural habitats were in favorable conservation status, and that farmland bird populations had declined by 57% since 1980. Meanwhile, the EU’s own Joint Research Centre found that food systems accounted for nearly 30% of total EU greenhouse gas emissions. The CAP’s "decoupling" reforms (starting 2003) had failed to reverse environmental degradation because subsidies remained linked to land area, not outcomes.

Major **corporate opposition** came from agri-business giants like Bayer (following its 2018 acquisition of Monsanto) and the European Crop Protection Association (ECPA). Bayer warned of "significant yield losses" if pesticide targets were met, while the European farmers' union Copa-Cogeca claimed the Strategy would cut EU food production by up to 15%, according to their 2020 impact study. In response, the Commission’s own 2020 impact assessment argued that healthier diets would reduce healthcare costs by an estimated €1.6 billion annually.

The **coalition that got this passed** was an unusual alliance: the European Parliament’s Green group (led by Ska Keller and Bas Eickhout), the Socialists & Democrats pushing for farm income support reforms, and centrist Renew Europe (including French President Emmanuel Macron’s allies, who saw it as a way to boost French organic agriculture). The opposition came from the European People’s Party (EPP), which dominated agriculture committees and defended large-scale farming — a key political force in Germany, Poland, and Hungary. The 2020 German EU presidency, under Chancellor Angela Merkel, brokered compromises that kept the Strategy alive, notably by agreeing to higher CAP direct payments for "eco-schemes" in June 2021.

**Timing** was crucial: incoming Commission President Ursula von der Leyen had made the Green Deal her flagship policy in 2019, and the Strategy was a key deliverable. The COVID-19 pandemic created a "rally-round-the-flag" effect that allowed the Commission to frame food system reform as a matter of public health and security, not just environmentalism. This was the moment that broke the previous deadlock — earlier attempts, like the 2007 "Strategy on the Sustainable Use of Pesticides," had lacked binding targets and were weakened by industry lobbying. The failure of that earlier directive (Directive 2009/128/EC) to achieve significant pesticide reduction — the Commission’s 2017 audit found it led to only a 10% reduction in risk, not use — provided empirical evidence that voluntary measures failed.

## Timeline

- **2019** — European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen commits to a "Farm to Fork Strategy" in her political guidelines, promising to "ensure a fair, healthy and environmentally-friendly food system."
- **20 May 2020** — The Commission publishes the Communication "A Farm to Fork Strategy for a fair, healthy and environmentally-friendly food system" (COM/2020/381 final), alongside the Biodiversity Strategy.
- **June 2021** — The Commission proposes a reform of the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) for 2023-2027, requiring 25% of direct payments for eco-schemes aligned with Farm to Fork goals.
- **14 July 2022** — The Commission proposes binding national targets to reduce pesticide use by 50% by 2030 under revision of the Sustainable Use of Pesticides Directive (SUR). The proposal faced deadlock in the Council.
- **November 2023** — The European Parliament’s ENVI committee votes to reject the SUR proposal due to splits among the EPP and far-right groups, effectively stalling mandatory pesticide targets.
- **December 2023** — The Commission tables the "Nature Restoration Law" (COM/2022/304), which includes legally binding restoration targets for agricultural ecosystems, including rewetting drained peatlands.
- **February 2024** — The EU’s Farm to Fork-related push for mandatory "front-of-pack" nutrition labeling fails to gain consensus among member states after Italy, led by Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, led a blocking minority against the Nutri-Score system, warning it would harm Italian exports like Parmigiano Reggiano.

## Impact and consequences

**Who was affected**: Large industrial farms in France, Germany, and Poland faced the most direct pressure. For example, the cap-and-trade-like pesticide reduction targets (if implemented) would have required German cereal farmers to cut inputs by up to 50%; the German Farmers' Association (DBV) warned of a 30% yield loss in their 2021 position paper. Small organic farmers benefited — the Strategy’s goal of 25% organic land by 2030 led to increased subsidy support in CAP national strategic plans, with Ireland, for instance, raising its organic budget from €12 million to €60 million annually by 2023.

**Industry response**: The food processing sector adapted by accelerating reformulation. Nestlé and Danone announced in 2021-2022 "climate-smart agriculture" programs for their supply chains, with Danone committing to reduce fertilizer use by 30% by 2030. However, the European Food and Drink Federation (FoodDrinkEurope) lobbied heavily against mandatory labeling and sugar reduction targets, arguing these would cost €6 billion in consumer price increases, citing a 2021 McKinsey study they commissioned.

**First enforcement actions**: Given it is a strategy, not a regulation, "first enforcement" is indirect. The CAP’s 2023-2027 plans included "conditionality" rules: farmers receiving CAP payments must comply with expanded Good Agricultural and Environmental Conditions (GAEC) standards, including GAEC 9 on buffer strips to protect water quality. In 2024, the Commission withheld approval of the Polish and Hungarian CAP plans initially, citing insufficient ambition on eco-schemes — a first of its kind enforcement signal.

**Consequences for consumers**: The Strategy sparked debates about EU food imports — demanding equivalent standards on imported food (e.g., banning chlorinated chicken and hormone-treated beef under EU-South America trade negotiations). The Commission’s 2021 impact assessment estimated that meeting pesticide targets would increase EU food prices by 0.7-1.1%, but reduce diet-related health costs (estimated €280 billion annually in the EU, per the 2018 EU Health at a Glance report) by €5-10 billion over a decade.

## Key questions answered

### Why are pesticide reduction targets alone?

Because Directive 2009/128/EC, which required Member States to adopt National Action Plans against pesticides, failed to drive real reduction — the Commission’s 2017 evaluation found that between 2011 and 2016, EU pesticide sales actually rose by 4% despite the directive. The Strategy’s 50% target was chosen to force actual cuts, but industry pushback (from Bayer, Syngenta, etc.) resulted in the SUR proposal’s defeat in 2023, leaving the target effectively voluntary again.

### Why did the COVID-19 pandemic matter?

The lockdowns created a literal "food chain crisis": in April 2020, Dutch farmers ploughed under millions of tulip bulbs and dumped milk; Spanish fruit workers were stranded without transport. This gave Commissioner Kyriakides and von der Leyen the political cover to argue that food system resilience requires shorter supply chains, reducing import dependency, and healthier diets to avert future pandemics (zoonotic disease risk). The crisis turned the Strategy from a Green Deal aspiration into a crisis-response document.

### Why did the European People’s Party oppose it?

The EPP represents the dominant farming lobby across the EU — specifically the large-scale commodity farmers in France (FNSEA), Germany (DBV), and Poland (KOWR). These farmers, facing low commodity prices, argued the Strategy would export production to Brazil or Ukraine, where environmental standards are weaker. The EPP’s lead candidate for the 2023 European Parliament election, Manfred Weber, personally urged the Commission to shelve binding targets, stating at the March 2023 EPP Congress: "We cannot make Europe a food museum that only organic farmers can afford." The opposition reflected the structural tension: high-export countries like Poland (pork, apples) feared losing their comparative advantage.

### Why did mandatory nutrition labeling fail?

Because labeling rules require a qualified majority in the Council, and Italy, with backing from Spain and Portugal, built a blocking minority. The Nutri-Score system, designed to rank packaged foods from A (healthy) to E (unhealthy), rated traditional Mediterranean foods like olive oil and Parmigiano Reggiano as C or D due to fat content, triggering a fierce "protezione del made in Italy" campaign. The Italian government, led by Prime Minister Mario Draghi in 2022 and later Giorgia Meloni, argued this was cultural discrimination, not science. The Commission’s Secretary-General, Ilze Juhansone, appointed a working group in 2023 that recommended shelving the mandatory label until 2025, effectively kicking the issue.
