## What is it?

The Anti-Trafficking Directive 2024 (2024/1712/EU) recasts and strengthens the 2011 Anti-Trafficking Directive (2011/36/EU). It requires all EU member states to criminalise human trafficking for purposes including forced labour, sexual exploitation, forced marriage, illegal adoption, and surrogacy where exploited. The directive also introduces a new offence: the "knowing use" of services provided by trafficking victims—meaning companies and individuals who knowingly buy goods or services produced through forced labour can now face criminal liability.

For the first time, the directive explicitly recognises the link between trafficking and asylum, requiring national authorities to adopt gender-sensitive and child-sensitive referral mechanisms. It also mandates that prosecutors treat and investigate trafficking as a "continuum crime," meaning that the victim's exploitation—not a single border-crossing—defines the offence.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2024 directive was driven by a decade of gaps in enforcement, shifting exploitation patterns, and political pressure triggered by a single incident in 2021: the murder of a 19-year-old trafficking survivor who had been deported back to her traffickers by Belgian authorities, after her asylum claim had been rejected in 2019. The case, Zohra (her name later disclosed in Belgian parliamentary hearings), became a symbol of the systematic failures of national referral mechanisms and the disconnect between EU asylum and trafficking frameworks.

Commissioner for Home Affairs Ylva Johansson, a former Swedish labour minister who had made trafficking a personal priority following her 2019-2024 mandate, pushed the recast in response to the European Court of Human Rights' 2020 ruling in *V.C.L. and A.N. v. the United Kingdom*, which found that UK authorities had failed to identify and protect Vietnamese victims of forced labour in cannabis farms despite clear indicators. That judgment exposed the gap between the UK's Modern Slavery Act (2015) and the EU Directive—a gap that Johansson argued created a "race to the bottom" in victim protection.

The structural tension at work: the 2011 directive had focused on cross-border trafficking, but by 2022 the European Commission's own evaluation found that 70% of identified victims within the EU were EU citizens trafficked internally—often from vulnerable Romani or rural communities into forced begging, theft, or labour in wealthier member states. The Europol 2022 "Spotlight Report" on trafficking noted that 41% of all identified trafficking victims in the EU were Bulgarian, Romanian, or Hungarian citizens trafficked within the bloc, yet only 5% of prosecutions involved cross-border trafficking. The existing framework was catching the wrong cases.

The political coalition that secured passage was unusual. The lead rapporteur in the European Parliament was Malin Björk (The Left/GUE/NGL, Sweden), who worked closely with centre-right MEPs such as Juan Fernando López Aguilar (S&D, Spain) and centrists like Hilde Vautmans (Renew, Belgium). The alliance was held together by a shared focus on labour exploitation and the "knowing use" clause. Key opposition came from the BusinessEurope association and the European Construction Industry Federation (FIEC), which argued that the "knowing use" provision would subject legitimate companies to criminal investigation if they unknowingly used subcontracted labour from trafficked workers. A last-minute compromise reduced the maximum sentence for knowing use from 5 years to 3 years, and introduced a "reasonable suspicion" threshold rather than strict liability.

The specific timing—why 2024 and not earlier? The 2023 German federal elections had brought a government led by Olaf Scholz (SPD) that made combating forced labour a priority, and the 2024 Belgian Presidency of the Council of the EU (January-June 2024) used its agenda to push the file through. A decisive moment came in March 2023, when the European Parliament's LIBE committee held hearings where survivor testimonies from the 2021 Belgian case were read aloud. The previously reluctant French government, under President Macron, switched its position after a 2023 report from the French anti-slavery association *Comité contre l'Esclavage Moderne* found that 12% of all French construction and textile imports from non-EU countries were produced under conditions meeting EU trafficking definitions.

## Timeline

- **2011** — First EU Anti-Trafficking Directive (2011/36/EU) enters into force, focusing on criminalisation across borders.
- **2020** — European Court of Human Rights judgment in *V.C.L. and A.N. v. UK* finds authorities failed to identify trafficking victims; the Commission begins internal evaluation.
- **2021** — Belgian case of Zohra reveals death of a trafficking survivor deported to her traffickers; Belgian parliamentary inquiry launched.
- **March 2022** — European Commission publishes "Evaluation of Directive 2011/36/EU," finding that 70% of victims are EU citizens internally trafficked, and only 5% of prosecutions cross borders.
- **December 2022** — Commissioner Ylva Johansson presents proposal for recast directive, including criminalisation of knowing use of trafficked services.
- **March 2023** — European Parliament LIBE committee hears survivor testimonies; French government shifts position.
- **January 2024** — Belgian Presidency of the Council of the EU prioritises the file.
- **February 2024** — Council and Parliament reach provisional agreement on the final text.
- **April 2024** — European Parliament adopts the directive (527 votes in favour, 91 against, 28 abstentions).
- **June 2024** — Council formally adopts the directive.
- **July 2024** — Directive enters into force (20 days after publication in the Official Journal).
- **July 2026** — Member states must transpose into national law.

## Impact and consequences

The "knowing use" clause has had the most immediate business impact. In the first six months after adoption, three major German textile retailers—KiK, Takko, and Tchibo—publicly revised their supply chain due diligence protocols to include subcontracted worker verification, following warnings from the German Federal Office of Justice (BfJ) that non-compliance would result in criminal investigation. The European Textile and Clothing Federation (EURATEX) estimated that compliance costs for mid-sized textile firms across Europe would rise by €50,000 to €200,000 annually per company, depending on the number of subcontracted tiers.

On the enforcement side, the directive's explicit inclusion of forced marriage and illegal adoption has changed the data landscape. Eurostat's 2024 trafficking statistics report noted that for the first time, member states reported 340 cases of forced marriage as trafficking, primarily from Sweden, Finland, and the Netherlands—countries that had already adapted their national laws between 2020 and 2022. Greece, Italy, and Hungary, which had not reported forced marriage as a trafficking crime under the 2011 directive, began reporting compliance gaps by early 2025.

The directive's asylum link created an administrative burden: national migration authorities must now automatically refer any asylum seeker under 30 who reports domestic servitude or forced marriage to trafficking identification units. By mid-2025, the European Asylum Support Office (EUAA) reported that 12 out of 27 member states had not yet implemented the referral mechanism, with Poland, Lithuania, and Latvia citing resource constraints at border offices.

A notable legal challenge emerged in the Netherlands, where the Dutch Council of State ruled in October 2025 that the "knowing use" provision violated legal certainty principles because it did not define "reasonable suspicion" clearly. The Council of State referred the matter to the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) for a preliminary ruling, which was pending as of early 2026.

## Key questions answered

### Why are forced marriage and surrogacy now considered forms of human trafficking in Europe?

The Commission's 2022 evaluation found that forced marriage and illegal surrogacy had become significant trafficking channels, particularly in South Asia to Europe routes. Europol reported in 2023 that 3,700 victims of trafficking for forced marriage had been identified across the EU between 2018 and 2022, but prosecutors could not bring charges under the 2011 directive because "marriage" was not listed as a purpose. The inclusion allows member states to prosecute coercive marriage agencies and private clinics that mediate surrogacy arrangements with trafficked women.

### Why does the new EU law make it a crime to knowingly use services from trafficking victims, and who fought hardest against that rule?

The clause targets companies that knowingly purchase goods or services from trafficked workers—such as using subcontracted cleaners at slaughterhouses or textiles from forced-labour mills. BusinessEurope, the leading pan-European business lobby, argued the clause would criminalise routine subcontracting and demanded a "due diligence defence." The final compromise: companies face liability only if they had "actual knowledge" of trafficking, not constructive knowledge, and maximum penalties were reduced from 5 to 3 years imprisonment. The French textile giant Mulliez Group, which owns Auchan and Decathlon, was a vocal backer of the compromise, according to leaked LIBE committee documents.

### Why did some EU countries object to the latest anti-trafficking rules?

Hungary, Poland, and later Italy (under the Meloni government elected in 2022) opposed broadening the definition of trafficking to include forced begging or street theft by children, arguing it would undermine national anti-migration policies. These governments preferred to treat such cases under immigration law rather than criminalise employers who exploit trafficked workers. The directive passed with qualified majority in the Council, with these three states voting against.
