## What is it?

The Qualification Regulation (2024/1347/EU) is a central piece of the EU's reformed asylum framework under the New Pact on Migration and Asylum. It replaces the 2011 Qualification Directive (2011/95/EU) and transforms it from a directive — which required national transposition and left Member States discretion — into a directly applicable regulation. This means all 27 EU countries (plus associated Schengen states) must apply the same criteria for deciding who qualifies as a refugee or for subsidiary protection, and the same set of rights for beneficiaries.

The regulation specifies two levels of protection: **refugee status** for those fleeing persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership of a particular social group; and **subsidiary protection** for those facing a real risk of serious harm (death penalty, torture, indiscriminate violence in armed conflict) but not meeting the refugee definition. It defines what constitutes "protection" from a state actor or non-state actor, sets rules on the duration of residence permits, family reunification, access to employment, healthcare, education, and social assistance, and introduces stricter rules on revocation and cessation of status.

## Why was it introduced?

The single most dramatic driver was the 2015–2016 migration crisis, which exposed the complete failure of the Dublin system and the 2011 Qualification Directive. In 2015, over 1.3 million asylum applications were lodged in the EU — according to Eurostat, the highest number since records began. The response was chaotic: Germany under Chancellor Angela Merkel unilaterally suspended Dublin transfers for Syrians in August 2015; Hungary built a fence; Greece's asylum system collapsed, with recognition rates for Syrian applicants varying from nearly 100% in Sweden to near-zero in Bulgaria, according to the European Asylum Support Office (EASO) at the time. The European Council of 23 September 2015 saw bitter clashes, with the Visegrád Group (Poland, Hungary, Czechia, Slovakia) refusing mandatory relocation quotas. The Juncker Commission's first attempt at reform — the 2016 Dublin IV proposal — stalled in the Council for years, blocked by the same fault lines.

The Commission's own 2017 impact assessment for the recast Qualification Regulation candidly admitted that the 2011 Directive's minimum standards had not prevented "divergent recognition rates, secondary movements, and abuse of the system." It noted that, according to Eurostat data from 2016, recognition rates for Afghan nationals varied from 86% in Germany to 0% in Hungary. A later 2019 Commission report on the Directive found that only 11 Member States had fully transposed all provisions on subsidiary protection, and that a minority of Member States granted refugees residence permits shorter than the three-year minimum.

What broke the deadlock was not a single event but a slow political realignment under the von der Leyen Commission. By 2020, the New Pact on Migration and Asylum was presented by Commissioner for Home Affairs Ylva Johansson, who explicitly framed it as a package deal that traded stricter border controls and faster asylum procedures (the Screening Regulation and Asylum Procedure Regulation) for binding solidarity mechanisms and harmonised standards. The forced-pregnancy abortion controversy — where Poland and Malta blocked language on sexual and reproductive health rights — nearly derailed final negotiations in December 2023. Only after the Council agreed to delete any explicit reference to abortion did the rule-of-law clause satisfy Warsaw. The regulation was adopted by the European Parliament on 10 April 2024 with 322 votes in favour, 266 against, and 28 abstentions, reflecting deep left-right and pro- versus anti-migrant splits. The Council adopted it on 14 May 2024. The vote was close: Parliament's final adoption saw the EPP and Renew groups largely in favour, the Left and Greens opposed (arguing the package was too restrictive on rights), and the far-right ID group divided.

The structural tension it resolves is the contradiction between the common EU asylum system's goal of uniformity and the reality of 27 national asylum systems producing wildly different outcomes. The coalition that passed it was an unusual alliance: migration-hardliner states (like Hungary and Poland) got tougher border rules and a crisis regulation, while protection-advocate states (like Germany, Sweden) got binding harmonisation and solidarity mechanisms. The regulation's direct applicability removes the transposition loophole that let Member States slow-walk or selectively implement the old Directive.

## Timeline

- **December 2005** — First Qualification Directive (2004/83/EC) enters into force, setting minimum standards for refugee and subsidiary protection.
- **2011** — Recast Qualification Directive (2011/95/EU) adopted, aiming to raise standards and reduce discrepancies but remaining a directive.
- **Summer 2015** — Migration crisis peaks; Germany suspends Dublin rules for Syrians; recognition rates for identical nationalities vary dramatically across Member States.
- **September 2015** — European Council agrees on mandatory relocation scheme for 160,000 asylum seekers; Visegrád Group refuses.
- **May 2016** — Juncker Commission proposes a package including a recast Qualification Regulation (transforming the Directive into a regulation); legislative process stalls.
- **September 2020** — von der Leyen Commission presents the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, including a revised Qualification Regulation proposal.
- **October 2021** — European Parliament rapporteur Matjaž Nemec (S&D, Slovenia) presents draft report; Council inter-institutional negotiations begin.
- **June 2022** — Council reaches general approach on the regulation; intense debate on the scope of "protection from persecution by non-state actors."
- **December 2023** — Final trilogue agreement reached after resolving a dispute over sexual and reproductive health rights; Poland and Malta block language on abortion.
- **10 April 2024** — European Parliament adopts the regulation (322 in favour, 266 against, 28 abstentions).
- **14 May 2024** — Council adopts the regulation.
- **11 June 2024** — Regulation published in the Official Journal (enters into force 20 days later).
- **12 June 2026** — Formal date of application of the regulation, allowing Member States two years to adapt national legislation.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence is that, from June 2026, the 27 national asylum authorities must apply identical criteria for granting refugee and subsidiary protection. According to the Commission's 2020 impact assessment, recognition rates for identical nationalities in 2019 still varied by up to 40 percentage points (e.g. Somali nationals: 90% in France vs. 5% in Hungary, per Eurostat). The regulation aims to compress this range.

The regulation also has knock-on effects for secondary movements. By tying the right to reside, work, and access benefits to a uniform definition, it removes one incentive for asylum seekers to "asylum-shop" across Member States. However, the regulation does not harmonise social benefits amounts — those remain national competence. The European Council on Refugees and Exiles (ECRE) warned in an April 2024 analysis that the regulation's stricter cessation rules (e.g. requiring revocation of refugee status if the person poses a danger to the Member State's security) could be used in bad faith by governments seeking to deport long-settled refugees.

For industry — particularly employers and labour recruiters — the regulation creates predictability: a refugee granted status in one Member State under uniform rules will have a clear right to work, but the regulation does not allow free movement for work purposes across Member States (that remains under national law). The services sector, especially in Germany and Sweden that had admitted large numbers of refugees post-2015, will see more consistent access-to-labour market rules.

Countries that struggled most are those with the highest recognition rate variances: Hungary, Poland, and Bulgaria will have to align their case officers' decision-making to EASO guidelines more tightly. Early warnings from the Commission's 2023 Schengen evaluation reports already showed that Hungary's asylum procedures systemically lacked independent appeal mechanisms — the regulation's direct effect means those appeals must exist under EU law, not just national law.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU replace a directive with Regulation (EU) 2024/1347?

Because directives gave Member States too much discretion in transposition, leading to wildly divergent national asylum systems. The 2011 Qualification Directive was implemented differently in each country. For example, while the Directive required a minimum three-year residence permit for refugees, according to a 2019 Commission report several Member States granted only one-year permits with no guarantee of renewal. A regulation — directly applicable and enforceable by the Court of Justice — removes the transposition loophole.

### How does Regulation (EU) 2024/1347 address the 2015 migration crisis?

It closes one cause of the chaotic 2015 secondary movements: recognition rate gaps. The 2015 crisis showed that when Germany granted protection to Syrians at near-100% but Hungary gave near-0%, asylum seekers naturally moved onward. By forcing uniform criteria, the regulation removes a structural incentive for secondary movement within the Schengen area.

### Why were sexual and reproductive health rights so controversial in the negotiations of Regulation (EU) 2024/1347?

Poland and Malta — both with restrictive abortion laws — refused to accept any language that could be interpreted as creating an EU-level right to abortion for refugees. A late 2023 draft from the Swedish Council Presidency contained a clause guaranteeing "access to sexual and reproductive health care" for beneficiaries of international protection. Poland threatened a veto. The final regulation (Article 30) speaks only of "adequate health care" with no specific mention of reproductive health. The compromise nearly collapsed the entire New Pact package in December 2023.

### What changes for subsidiary protection holders under Regulation (EU) 2024/1347 compared to the old Directive?

Subsidiary protection holders now receive a stronger bundle of rights more closely aligned with refugee status. Under the old Directive, Member States could limit subsidiary protection residence permits to one year; the regulation mandates a minimum of three years, renewable. Access to employment, healthcare, and social assistance must be on equal terms with nationals. According to the Commission's 2020 proposal impact assessment, seven Member States previously granted subsidiary protection holders less favourable access to social assistance than refugees — this practice must end.
