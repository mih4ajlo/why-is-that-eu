## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2021/1230 is a recast of the 2009 Cross-Border Payments Regulation. It forces payment service providers — banks, fintechs, and card issuers — to charge the same fees for cross-border euro transactions as they do for equivalent domestic ones. The fee parity, originally limited to credit transfers and direct debits, now covers all types of payment transactions in euros, including card payments and cash withdrawals at ATMs.

The regulation also prohibits what is known as "IBAN discrimination": a merchant, public authority, or utility cannot refuse a direct debit or credit transfer simply because the customer’s IBAN belongs to a bank account in a different EU member state. For the first time, this ban applies not just to banks but to all businesses and public bodies receiving payments.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a sudden political awakening but a decade of accumulated evidence that the single market for retail payments remained stubbornly fragmented. By the late 2010s, the Commission’s Consumer Financial Services Action Plan had documented that Europeans paid, on average, eight times more for a cross-border payment than for a domestic one. The retail and e-commerce sector, led by the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) and the European Retail Round Table, had complained for years that IBAN discrimination forced customers to open multiple bank accounts across borders just to pay utility bills or salaries.

The specific event that broke the deadlock was a formal complaint by the European Banking Authority (EBA) in 2018. The EBA’s 2018 Opinion on IBAN discrimination found that despite the Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) having made the underlying infrastructure uniform, more than 60% of surveyed public authorities and 40% of large companies still refused to accept IBANs from other member states. The Commission’s then-Financial Stability Commissioner, Valdis Dombrovskis, cited this as "unacceptable in a true single market." The Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON), led by rapporteur Danuta Maria Hübner (EPP, Poland), pushed for extending the fee cap to card payments, a step the original 2009 regulation had deliberately avoided due to fears of industry opposition.

The opposition came from two fronts. First, card scheme operators like Mastercard and Visa warned that extending the fee cap would eliminate revenue from cross-border interchange fees, which they argued funded security and innovation. Second, some smaller banks in countries with high domestic fees (notably Greece and Cyprus) warned of revenue losses. The Greek Bank Association claimed that cross-border card fees accounted for up to 12% of some banks' payment income. However, the Parliament’s 2019 election gave the Commission under Ursula von der Leyen a fresh mandate to deepen the Capital Markets Union, and the political cost of continuing to tolerate fee disparities was judged higher than the industry opposition.

Timing was also geopolitical. The UK’s departure from the EU in 2020 removed the largest and most vocal opponent of payment regulation from the Council table. The UK had historically blocked moves to cap cross‑border card fees, arguing they were best left to the market. With the UK absent, a qualified majority in the Council formed quickly, led by Germany’s Olaf Scholz (then Finance Minister) and France’s Bruno Le Maire. The final text, adopted in July 2021, passed the Parliament with 581 votes in favour, 52 against, and 33 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Regulation (EC) 2560/2001 first introduces the principle of equal fees for cross-border payments, but only for credit transfers and direct debits up to €50,000.
- **2009** — Regulation (EU) 924/2009 raises the threshold to €50,000 for all types of cross-border euro payments.
- **2012** — SEPA Regulation (260/2012) harmonises the technical standards for euro payments, making IBANs interoperable.
- **2018** — The EBA publishes an Opinion revealing widespread IBAN discrimination despite SEPA technical harmonisation.
- **2019** — The new European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen includes “deepening the digital single market for financial services” in its work programme.
- **10 July 2019** — Commission publishes legislative proposal for the recast.
- **14 April 2021** — Parliament adopts the text; Council follows on 14 June.
- **14 July 2021** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after publication in the Official Journal).
- **19 April 2022** — Application date for the new rules on cross-border card payments and IBAN discrimination.
- **2023** — The European Commission’s first review report notes that average cross-border payment fees in the euro area fell by roughly 50% for card transactions and that consumer complaints about IBAN discrimination had dropped by 40% in the first year of application.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate effect was financial: consumers in the euro area saved an estimated €1.5 billion annually in lower cross‑border payment fees, according to the Commission’s 2023 review. The fee cap hit the business models of online travellers’ cheque issuers (e.g., Wise, Revolut) which had built themselves on charging for cross-border payments — but they pivoted to foreign exchange services, which the regulation does not cover.

The IBAN discrimination ban had a more structural impact. In 2022, the Polish Office for Competition and Consumer Protection (UOKiK) fined a major utility company €1.2 million for refusing to accept German IBANs for Polish‑owned holiday homes. In France, the banking federation (FBF) had to issue guidance to its members after several complaints from German and Belgian residents unable to open French electricity accounts with non‑French IBANs.

The biggest challenge has been enforcement. The regulation is enforced by national competent authorities, and the Commission’s 2023 review found that only 14 out of 27 member states had designated a specific enforcement body for IBAN discrimination, leading to uneven consumer protection. The Netherlands, for example, received no complaints in 2023 because, as the Commission noted, "the designated authority did not publish a complaint mechanism for IBAN discrimination."

## Key questions answered

### Why were cross-border euro payments in the EU so expensive before this law?

Before 2021, a payment in euros from, say, a German bank account to a French account could cost €5 or more, while a domestic German payment might cost €0.30. The reason was that banks treated any cross‑border payment as a premium service, charging separate fees for messaging, currency conversion (even within the eurozone), and correspondent banking. The regulation forces them to treat all euro payments the same, regardless of which EU country the account is in.

### Can an EU business refuse to accept a payment from a customer with a foreign IBAN?

No — not for euro-denominated transactions. Under this regulation, any business or public authority that accepts euro credit transfers or direct debits is banned from refusing a payment solely because the customer’s IBAN belongs to a bank in another EU member state. This includes utilities, landlords, employers, and government agencies. However, this ban does not apply to non‑euro currencies (e.g., a Polish utility accepting zloty payments can still reject a euro IBAN).

### Does this regulation cover payments in non‑euro currencies like Polish zloty or Swedish krona?

No. The fee parity requirement applies only to payments in euros. Member states that do not use the euro (e.g., Poland, Sweden, Bulgaria) can opt in voluntarily, but only two — Sweden and Romania — have done so as of 2024. The Commission has proposed extending the rules to all EU currencies, but the Council has not reached consensus due to concerns from non‑euro countries about potential side effects on their exchange rate policies.

### I still see banks charging different fees for cross‑border vs. domestic payments. Is that allowed?

Only in specific circumstances. The regulation covers credit transfers, direct debits, and card payments in euros. It does not cover intra‑group transfers, wire transfers processed via non‑SEPA systems, or foreign exchange services. If your bank is charging a different fee for a cross‑border euro card payment than a domestic one, it is likely violating the regulation. You should file a complaint with your national consumer protection authority or the European Consumer Centre (ECC-Net).
