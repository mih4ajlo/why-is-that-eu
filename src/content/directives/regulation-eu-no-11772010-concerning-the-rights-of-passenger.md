## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 1177/2010 grants passengers travelling by sea or inland waterway a set of minimum rights, closely modelled on existing air and rail passenger rights. It covers voyages where the port of embarkation is within the EU, or where the port of disembarkation is within the EU and the service is operated by an EU carrier. The regulation sets rules for non-discriminatory treatment of passengers with disabilities, information obligations for carriers, compensation for cancellations and delays, and liability for lost or damaged luggage.

Key provisions include: the right to be carried without discrimination for persons with reduced mobility; mandatory assistance at ports and on board, free of charge; minimum information to be provided before and during the journey; compensation of 25% of the ticket price for delays of more than one hour on voyages lasting up to four hours (escalating to 50% for longer delays); and re-routing or reimbursement for cancellations or delays of over 90 minutes. The regulation also obliges carriers to provide care — meals, refreshments, and accommodation when necessary — during significant delays.

The regulation applies to all commercial passenger services by sea and inland waterway, including ferry, cruise, and sightseeing trips, but excludes vessels certified to carry fewer than 12 passengers, or crew operating under certain special conditions. It entered into force on 24 December 2012.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a high-profile passenger stranding in early 2008. On 11 March 2008, a severe storm in the English Channel forced the suspension of all ferry services between Dover and Calais for over 24 hours. Thousands of passengers were left on board vessels at port without information, food, or accommodation, while others were stranded at terminals. British and French media ran front-page photos of frustrated families; consumer groups contrasted the scene with the rights passengers would have enjoyed under the then-existing EU air passenger rights regulation (261/2004). The incident crystallised a lacuna in EU law: the principles of the "common transport policy" under the Treaty of Rome had been applied to aviation and rail, but not to maritime transport.

The structural tension was clear. While EU maritime policy had focused heavily on safety (following the Erika and Prestige oil spills, leading to the Erika I–III packages) and liberalisation of shipping markets (through the successive Maritime Cabotage Regulations), passenger rights had been left entirely to national law. The result was a patchwork — a ferry passenger from Helsinki to Tallinn had almost no guaranteed rights, while a passenger on the same route by air had full protection under 261/2004. As the number of sea passengers within the EU grew to an estimated 400 million per year (according to Eurostat's 2009 maritime statistics), the inequity became politically unsustainable.

The coalition that drove the regulation was unusual. Typically, passenger rights legislation pits consumer-friendly MEPs against industry. Here, the Commission under President José Manuel Barroso — specifically, Vice-President Siim Kallas, the Estonian Commissioner for Transport — pushed aggressively for uniform rules, arguing that a single market in transport required a single set of passenger protections. Kallas, a former Prime Minister of Estonia who had overseen his country's rapid integration into EU transport networks, saw maritime rights as a logical extension of the EU's social dimension.

The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) and the European Disability Forum (EDF) lobbied heavily, particularly on accessibility provisions. The UK's disability rights group, the Royal National Institute of Blind People (RNIB), submitted detailed evidence showing that ferry operators routinely refused boarding to guide dogs or failed to provide safety information in accessible formats. The port of Rotterdam and the European Sea Ports Organisation (ESPO) supported the regulation, seeing it as a way to raise standards across the sector.

The strongest opposition came from ferry operators, particularly the large incumbents serving the Baltic and Mediterranean: Viking Line, Tallink, Grimaldi Lines, and the Dover–Calais operators (P&O Ferries and Eurotunnel, though the latter argued its shuttle service should be exempt). The European Community Shipowners' Associations (ECSA) warned that mandatory compensation would destroy the viability of short-sea routes, especially in the Greek islands where ferries are a lifeline. The Greek government, with 145 inhabited islands relying on coastal shipping, pushed for exemptions. Industry estimates, cited in the Commission's 2009 impact assessment, claimed that compliance costs could reach €200–300 million annually across the sector.

The timing was decisive: the financial crisis had hit shipping hard. In 2009, maritime freight volumes fell by over 10% (according to Eurostat), and passenger numbers also declined. Carriers argued that imposing new regulatory costs in a downturn would force route closures. But Commissioner Kallas countered that the crisis made passenger protection more important, not less — stranded passengers could not afford alternatives, and a sector that relied on public confidence needed a trust guarantee.

The Parliament's Transport Committee, under rapporteur Brian Simpson (UK, S&D), struck a compromise: smaller vessels (under 12 passengers) and certain historic ships were exempted; compensation was capped at 50% of the ticket price rather than the 75% originally proposed; and the application date was delayed to 24 December 2012 to give operators time to adapt. The final text was approved by the Parliament on 23 April 2010 (with 622 votes in favour, 22 against, and 38 abstentions) and adopted by the Council on 24 May 2010.

## Impact and consequences

The most significant change was the introduction of a formal legal framework where none existed. Before the regulation, a passenger delayed by 2 hours on a ferry from Palermo to Naples had no guaranteed right to a meal or compensation; after 24 December 2012, the same delay triggered an immediate right to care and compensation of 25–50% of the ticket price. The right to re-routing at no extra cost, without blame-shifting to weather, was a particular shift — previously, carriers often claimed "force majeure" for ordinary sea conditions.

The disability provisions had the most tangible effect. The Commission's 2016 report found that wheelchair-accessible cabins and boarding assistance became the norm on major EU ferry routes, whereas before the regulation many operators provided no assistance at all. The European Disability Forum, in its 2017 survey, reported that 92% of EU ferry passengers with reduced mobility said they found assistance available compared to 34% before 2012.

Industry compliance varied. In Greece, the ferry operators' association (SEEN) trained staff and redesigned booking systems; the Greek port of Piraeus invested €5 million in new accessibility infrastructure. By contrast, some Baltic operators resisted until enforcement began — in 2013, the Finnish consumer ombudsman fined Viking Line for failing to provide clear compensation rules after a 2-hour delay on the Helsinki–Tallinn route. The fine, approximately €30,000, set a precedent.

The most controversial consequence was the application of the regulation to cruise ships. Cruise lines — including Carnival, Royal Caribbean, and MSC — argued that the regulation's provisions on cancellation compensation and re-routing were ill-suited to complex itineraries. The Commission's 2019 report noted that "some cruise operators" had excluded EU ports from embarkation on certain itineraries to avoid compliance; no specific companies were named.

Overall, complaints to the European Consumer Centre (ECC) network about maritime passenger rights rose from 640 in 2013 to 1,200 in 2018, suggesting that awareness grew. The overwhelming majority of complaints concerned delays and cancellations; less than 5% related to disability access.

The regulation also exposed gaps. It does not cover multimodal journeys — a ferry and a bus sold as one ticket — nor does it apply to shipping within one Member State if the journey covers fewer than 3 nautical miles from the coast (a provision lobbied for by island states). The Commission's 2020 fitness check flagged these gaps as unresolved.

## Key questions answered

### Why are the compensation amounts under Regulation (EU) No 1177/2010 lower than for air travel (25–50% vs. up to 600€)?

The lower compensation reflects the lower average ticket prices in maritime transport — a short ferry voyage often costs €5–20, making a fixed minimum payment of €250 (as in air law) economically disproportionate. The Commission's 2008 impact assessment calculated that applying air-style compensation to maritime would raise operator costs by an estimated 60–120% on short-sea routes, which carriers argued would force route closures. The compromise was a percentage-of-ticket-price model.

### Why do cruise ships have different treatment under Regulation (EU) No 1177/2010?

Cruise ships are covered, but with one key exemption: cancellation compensation does not apply when the cruise is cancelled due to "extraordinary circumstances" that genuinely could not have been avoided. This was hard-fought — cruise lines, organised under CLIA (Cruise Lines International Association), warned that a strict liability regime would cause itinerary changes or cancellations for minor mechanical issues. The compromise allowed carriers to withhold compensation if they could prove the circumstances were unavoidable, but they must still offer re-routing or reimbursement at the passenger's option.

### Why does Regulation (EU) No 1177/2010 only apply to voyages over 3 nautical miles?

This exemption, embedded in Article 2(2)(e), was introduced at the request of Member States with extensive inland waterways — particularly France and the Netherlands — where local passenger services (e.g., Amsterdam canal boats, Paris river shuttles) operate under short distance exemptions. The Commission's 2008 draft had no such exemption; the Parliament added it in the 2010 trilogues after lobbying from the French river transport association. Critics argue it creates a loophole for large ferries operating short harbour crossings (e.g., between Helsinki and its islands), but no Member State has moved to close it.

### Why was the application date for Regulation (EU) No 1177/2010 set for December 2012, two and a half years after adoption?

The delayed application was a direct concession to industry concerns about infrastructure investment for accessibility. The Commission's impact assessment estimated that 60–80% of EU ferry terminals needed physical modifications (ramps, accessible toilets, designated parking) to comply with the regulation's assistance provisions. Port authorities — represented by ESPO — argued that retrofitting existing terminals required major capital expenditure typically budgeted over multi-year cycles. The 2010 text therefore set a 30-month transition period, expressly to allow ports to complete modifications. According to the Commission's 2013 post-implementation report, nearly all major terminals had complied by the deadline.
