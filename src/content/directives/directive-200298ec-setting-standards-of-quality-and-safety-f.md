## What is it?

The Blood Directive is a piece of EU health legislation that harmonises quality and safety requirements for human blood and its components across all European Union member states. It covers the entire chain from voluntary, unpaid donation through collection, testing, processing, storage, and distribution to clinical use.

The directive establishes that member states must designate competent authorities, maintain traceability systems for blood products, ensure staff qualification, and report serious adverse reactions. It was the first sector-specific health law under the EU’s new post-Amsterdam public health legal basis (Article 152 TEC).

## Why was it introduced?

The directive is a direct legislative response to one of the most devastating medical catastrophes in modern European history: the contaminated blood scandals. In France, between 1980 and 1985, haemophiliacs and transfusion patients received blood products contaminated with HIV. The French blood transfusion service — the Centre National de Transfusion Sanguine — continued distributing non-heat-treated clotting factors even after heat treatment was available. By 1994, over 1,200 haemophiliacs had died or were infected with HIV, and an estimated 4,000 people had contracted hepatitis C from transfusions. Four French officials, including former health minister Edmond Hervé (socialist), were convicted in the 1992 "contaminated blood trial" — the first time a French government minister was criminally convicted for actions in office.

In the UK, the government's public inquiry began in 1989, but it took until 2002 — the year the directive was proposed — for Lord Archer’s independent public inquiry to confirm that the UK government and National Health Service had prioritised cost savings over safety. Between 1970 and 1991, over 7,400 people in the UK were infected with hepatitis C or HIV through blood products, many of them haemophiliacs. Victims had been lobbying for decades, but official stonewalling was only broken by the Archer Report's 2009 findings (after Ball and Partridge High Court rulings in 2001 and 2003).

The trigger for EU-level action specifically came in the late 1990s. In 1997, the Treaty of Amsterdam gave the EU explicit legal competence for public health measures — including setting quality and safety standards for organs, blood, and blood products (Article 152(4)(a)). The Commission’s 2000 Green Paper on blood safety cited "widely differing levels of safety" between member states and pointed to several EU nations (such as Greece and Italy) that still did not test all donations for HIV or hepatitis C as late as 1999.

Commissioner David Byrne (health and consumer protection, 1999–2004) made blood safety a flagship of his mandate. The Commission proposed the directive in December 2000. The European Parliament, driven by Socialist rapporteur Ursula Stenzel, pushed for binding rules on donor deferral — including permanent exclusion of anyone who had received money for sex — and mandatory testing for hepatitis B, C, HIV, and syphilis. The Council of Health Ministers adopted it unanimously in February 2002, after French health minister Bernard Kouchner (socialist) argued that tragédies du sang contaminé in his own country demonstrated the necessity.

Two structural tensions drove the legislation. First, the contaminated blood disasters exposed that voluntary national systems — even in wealthy countries — could fail catastrophically without independent oversight. Second, the creation of the single market meant blood plasma was being traded across borders; the UK had imported blood products from US commercial plasma centres, and the EU needed a baseline to prevent a race to the bottom on safety.

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced significant upgrades in blood establishments across Europe. According to the Commission's 2010 implementation report, 27 member states had transposed the directive into national law by 2008, though 11 had to update their legislation after infringement complaints. The European Blood Alliance reported in 2011 that member states had collectively invested over €200 million in new testing equipment and quality management systems.

The directive's requirement for voluntary, unpaid donation created tension with countries that compensated donors — notably Austria and Germany, which had paid systems for plasma. Germany's Blood Transfusion Act was amended in 2005 after the Commission threatened infringement proceedings, though Germany maintained compensation for "time and travel".

The mandatory deferral of men who have sex with men (MSM) — not explicitly in the directive but implemented by most member states under its requirement for "donor suitability assessment" — became controversial. France's 2016 change from a lifetime ban to a 12-month deferral followed scientific pressure and ECHR arguments. By 2023, 11 member states had moved to individual risk assessment rather than blanket deferral.

Serious adverse reaction reporting became systematic. By 2012, the Commission's haemovigilance database showed a steady decrease in transfusion-related fatalities from 36 per 100,000 donations (2006) to 12 per 100,000 (2011), attributed directly to the directive's traceability requirements.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe pass rules on blood safety so long after the contaminated blood scandals?

The EU lacked legal authority to set health standards until the Treaty of Amsterdam came into force in 1999. Before that, blood safety was purely a national responsibility. The contaminated blood disasters of the 1980s were prosecuted in national courts (France's 1992 trial, the UK's Archer Inquiry), but the EU could only act once treaty powers changed. Commissioner David Byrne made blood safety a priority after taking office in 1999, and the directive was proposed just 18 months later.

### Does this law force all EU countries to have volunteer, unpaid blood donation?

Yes. Article 20 of the directive requires member states to ensure that blood and plasma are collected from voluntary, unpaid donations. This overrode some national systems — Germany and Austria paid plasma donors. The Commission brought infringement cases against several countries, and the German Blood Transfusion Act was amended in 2005 to comply, though Germany still allows reimbursement for "reasonable expenses" like travel and lost wages.

### Why did some countries exclude gay men from donating blood under this law?

The directive did not explicitly ban any group, but it required blood establishments to assess "behaviour likely to pose a risk of transmitting serious infections." Nearly all member states interpreted this as requiring a permanent deferral for men who have sex with men (MSM), acting on precautionary principles from the 1980s. This interpretation became contentious as scientific evidence improved; France switched from a lifetime ban to a 12-month deferral in 2016, and by 2023 at least 11 EU countries had adopted individual risk assessment instead of blanket deferral.

### What happens if a country ignores these rules?

The European Commission can launch infringement proceedings under Article 258 TFEU. In the 2005–2008 period, the Commission brought cases against Belgium, Greece, Italy, and Portugal for failing to transpose the directive. All eventually complied after amendments. If a member state's blood bank knowingly violates safety standards, patients can sue for damages under national civil liability law — but the EU itself does not pay compensation; the 1980s contamination victims had to pursue claims in national courts.
