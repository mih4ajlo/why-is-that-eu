## What is it?

The European Defence Industry Reinforcement Act (EDIRPA) is a regulation adopted in 2023 that provides €300 million from the EU budget to co-finance joint procurement of defence products by member states. It reimburses up to 20% of the contract value for purchases made by consortia of at least three member states, provided the equipment is produced in the EU or associated countries. The act is designed as a demand-side instrument: rather than subsidising production directly, it lowers the financial risk for member states to pool their orders, thereby creating larger, more predictable demand that allows European defence manufacturers to invest in capacity.

Unlike the European Defence Fund (EDF), which funds research and development, EDIRPA targets urgent procurement of existing or modified off-the-shelf systems. It runs until the end of 2024, with the possibility of limited extensions. The regulation was proposed in July 2022 and adopted in July 2023.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine on 24 February 2022. Within weeks, EU member states had transferred significant quantities of military equipment — including artillery shells, armoured vehicles, and air defence systems — to Ukraine from their own national stocks. By mid-2022, several member states faced critical gaps in their inventories, particularly in ammunition and heavy weapons. The European External Action Service assessed in a May 2022 internal note that, without rapid replenishment, the ability of European armies to meet NATO collective defence obligations would be compromised.

Structural tension existed long before the invasion: EU defence spending had been fragmented across 27 national procurement systems, producing duplication and small production runs that kept unit costs high. A 2021 European Court of Auditors special report found that only 2% of EU defence equipment spending was coordinated at EU level. A 2020 Commission communication on the European Defence Fund had already identified that low interoperability and small-scale orders hurt the competitiveness of the European defence technological and industrial base.

Coalition dynamics: The proposal was championed by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen and Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton, both of whom argued that the invasion revealed a strategic vulnerability — Europe’s defence industry was incapable of scaling up production quickly. Breton held a series of meetings with CEOs of major defence companies in spring 2022, including Dassault Aviation, Rheinmetall, KNDS, and MBDA. The companies warned that they could not increase production without guaranteed long-term orders. France and Germany supported the initiative, but for different reasons: France wanted to strengthen strategic autonomy by favouring EU-based suppliers; Germany wanted to accelerate procurement to replenish Bundeswehr stocks after its Zeitenwende (historic shift in defence policy announced by Chancellor Olaf Scholz on 27 February 2022).

Opposition came primarily from Poland and the Baltic states, which argued the process was too slow and the budget too small to address immediate needs. Poland’s defence minister Mariusz Błaszczak stated in September 2022 that EU joint procurement was “bureaucratic and slow” and that Warsaw would continue buying American and South Korean equipment on bilateral terms. The European Parliament’s Industry Committee approved the proposal in October 2022 with 48 votes in favour, 33 against, and 2 abstentions — a narrower margin than the Commission had hoped for.

Timing: The act succeeded in 2023 because the ammunition crisis of early 2023 broke the deadlock. In March 2023, the Council agreed to a three-track approach to supply Ukraine with 1 million artillery shells within 12 months: Track 1 involved immediate transfers from national stocks; Track 2 was a dedicated joint procurement programme; Track 3 used EDIRPA as the legal instrument to reimburse joint purchases. Without the ammunition urgency, EDIRPA might have languished in the Council for months.

What changed: Previous attempts to encourage joint procurement — notably the 2017 Permanent Structured Cooperation (PESCO) — had produced only 68 projects by 2022, most of which were small-scale research initiatives rather than actual procurement. PESCO had no dedicated budget and no incentive for member states to pool their orders. EDIRPA introduced financial incentives for the first time, but the sums remained modest relative to total EU defence spending of €240 billion in 2022.

## Timeline

- **2017** — PESCO launched by 25 member states to deepen defence cooperation, but lacks financial mechanisms for joint procurement.
- **24 February 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine. EU member states begin transferring military stocks to Kyiv.
- **May 2022** — European External Action Service warns of stockpile depletion in internal note.
- **July 2022** — Commission proposes EDIRPA with a €500 million budget envelope, later reduced to €300 million during Council negotiations.
- **October 2022** — European Parliament approves its negotiating mandate; Council adopts general approach in December.
- **March 2023** — Council agrees to the ammunition track plan, using EDIRPA as the legal vehicle for Track 2 joint procurement.
- **July 2023** — EDIRPA adopted by the European Parliament (396 votes in favour, 173 against, 66 abstentions) and Council. Entered into force on 27 July 2023.
- **October 2023** — First call for proposals launched; deadline for applications set for February 2024.
- **December 2023** — Only one consortium (for air defence systems) had submitted an application by the deadline, reflecting slow take-up.
- **2024 (expected)** — First reimbursements to member states; programme ends by 31 December 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate impact has been modest in scale. By March 2024, only three consortia had submitted applications under EDIRPA: one for air defence systems (led by Germany), one for ammunition (led by France), and one for armoured vehicles (led by Italy). The total requested reimbursement claims amounted to roughly €180 million of the €300 million available. Industry associations, such as the Aerospace, Security and Defence Industries Association of Europe (ASD), noted that the 20% reimbursement rate was insufficient to offset the higher transaction costs of multinational procurement compared to bilateral purchases.

The act revealed the persistent fragmentation of European defence markets. According to the European Defence Agency’s 2022 defence data report, 78% of EU member states’ defence procurement between 2018 and 2022 was spent on non-EU suppliers, primarily the United States. EDIRPA’s eligibility requirement that equipment be produced in the EU or associated countries (including Norway but excluding the UK) caused tension with Denmark, which had historically procured most of its major weapon systems from the US.

A secondary consequence was political: EDIRPA became the template for the expected European Defence Investment Programme (EDIP) proposed by the Commission in March 2024, which would expand joint procurement to cover large-scale projects with a €1.5 billion budget under the next Multiannual Financial Framework (2028-2034). The European Court of Auditors announced in February 2024 that it would audit EDIRPA’s effectiveness, given the slow take-up.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EDIRPA provide only €300 million when EU defence spending is €240 billion annually?

The budget was constrained by the existing Multiannual Financial Framework (2021-2027), which had no dedicated line for defence procurement. The €300 million was reallocated from the European Defence Fund’s remaining flexibility margin and from unused cohesion funds. Member states in the Council, particularly the Netherlands and Sweden, opposed a larger budget, arguing that defence procurement should remain a national competence funded by national budgets, not the EU.

### Why did the Commission favour joint procurement over direct subsidies to manufacturers?

Commissioner Breton argued in a September 2022 speech to the European Defence Agency that demand-side intervention was more urgent than supply-side subsidies, because the bottleneck was not production capacity but guaranteed orders. He cited the example of the French Caesar howitzer: Nexter could have doubled production if France had placed a firm order for 200 units instead of 76. EDIRPA reimburses the buyer, not the seller, to incentivise pooling of demand.

### Why is participation limited to EU-based suppliers?

The regulation includes an “associated countries” clause (Article 2(2)) allowing Norway and other EEA members but explicitly excludes third-country entities unless they have a security agreement with the EU. This reflects the geopolitical logic of the post-2022 environment: the Commission argued that joint procurement must strengthen European autonomy and reduce dependency on non-EU suppliers. Poland objected strongly, as it had signed separate contracts with South Korea for K2 tanks and K9 howitzers, but the requirement remained.

### Will EDIRPA be renewed after 2024?

The regulation is explicitly temporary. The Commission’s March 2024 proposal for the European Defence Investment Programme (EDIP) would replace EDIRPA with a longer-term framework offering up to 40% co-financing for joint procurement, with a proposed budget of €1.5 billion for 2025-2027. However, as of mid-2024, the EDIP proposal remains under negotiation, and its adoption is uncertain given the budgetary constraints and political disagreements over whether the EU should take on debt for defence spending.
