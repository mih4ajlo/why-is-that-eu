## What is it?

The EU Chips Act is a regulation and accompanying funding framework that mobilises around €43 billion in public and private investment to boost European semiconductor manufacturing, design, and research. It rests on three pillars: the "Chips for Europe Initiative" (€3.3 billion of EU money for pilot lines, design platforms, and quantum chips), a state aid framework that lets member states subsidise "first-of-a-kind" fabrication plants, and a crisis-response mechanism giving the Commission powers to monitor supply chains, prioritise orders, and impose export controls during shortages.

The headline target — capturing 20% of global chip production by 2030, up from roughly 9% — is politically symbolic more than legally binding. The legally significant change is that Brussels now treats chip fabs as projects of common European interest, allowing Berlin, Paris, Rome, and Warsaw to write subsidy cheques to TSMC, Intel, Infineon, and STMicroelectronics that would normally fall foul of EU competition rules.

## Why was it introduced?

When Volkswagen halted production at its Wolfsburg plant in December 2020 because it could not get enough automotive microcontrollers from Bosch and Continental's suppliers, the chip shortage stopped being a technical curiosity and became a political emergency. By 2021, European carmakers produced roughly 11 million fewer vehicles than planned, and Stellantis, Renault, and BMW were rotating workers onto short-time schemes. Auto industry losses ran to an estimated €100 billion globally that year. The shortage exposed an uncomfortable fact: although companies like ASML in Veldhoven and Infineon in Munich were European champions, the EU had let cutting-edge fabrication migrate to Taiwan (TSMC) and South Korea (Samsung), with TSMC alone producing more than 90% of the world's most advanced logic chips.

Commission President Ursula von der Leyen used her September 2021 State of the Union speech to call for a "European Chips Act," explicitly framing it as the answer to "Taiwan dependence." Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton, a former CEO of Atos and France Télécom, drove the file with a dirigiste enthusiasm that alarmed Nordic and Dutch officials worried about subsidy races. Breton's pitch landed in a transformed political environment: the United States had passed its own CHIPS and Science Act in August 2022 with $52 billion in subsidies, and Beijing was pouring more than $150 billion into its domestic semiconductor industry through the "Big Fund."

The Commission tabled its proposal on 8 February 2022. Negotiations were unusually fraught. Germany, France, and Italy pushed for generous state aid; the Netherlands, Sweden, Finland, and Ireland resisted, fearing the rules would mainly subsidise large member states. The crisis-response pillar — particularly the Commission's power to force fabs to prioritise certain orders — drew sharp pushback from SEMI Europe and the European Semiconductor Industry Association, who warned in 2022 position papers that mandatory order prioritisation would deter investment. The final text watered these powers down considerably. Parliament approved the regulation on 11 July 2023 with 587 votes to 10; the Council followed on 25 July.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation unlocked the largest wave of semiconductor state aid in European history. Germany alone committed nearly €20 billion, including €9.9 billion approved for Intel's Magdeburg complex and €5 billion for the TSMC-led ESMC joint venture in Dresden. France authorised €2.9 billion for the STMicroelectronics-GlobalFoundries fab in Crolles. Italy negotiated €2 billion for STMicroelectronics in Catania. Poland approved support for an Intel assembly and test facility near Wrocław.

Then the strategy stumbled. Intel's August 2024 financial collapse — a $1.6 billion quarterly loss and 15,000 layoffs — led CEO Pat Gelsinger to pause the Magdeburg project, and his successor confirmed its cancellation in 2025. The Wrocław facility was also shelved. The European Court of Auditors, in an April 2025 special report, concluded bluntly that the 20% market share target was "highly unlikely" to be met and that the Commission had set the goal without a credible roadmap. Production capacity may reach roughly 11–12% of global output by 2030 on optimistic assumptions.

The crisis-response pillar has not been triggered. The design and research pillar, run through the Chips Joint Undertaking based in Brussels, has begun funding pilot lines at imec (Leuven), CEA-Leti (Grenoble), Fraunhofer (Dresden), and VTT (Espoo) for sub-2nm and FD-SOI technologies. ASML, the regulation's quiet beneficiary, saw its market capitalisation surpass €400 billion in 2024 as its EUV lithography monopoly became central to every fab announcement on either side of the Atlantic.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU loosen state aid rules instead of funding fabs directly from the EU budget?

The EU budget simply does not have the firepower — the entire seven-year Multiannual Financial Framework is around €1.2 trillion, while a single leading-edge fab costs €10–20 billion. Member states, particularly Germany, hold the purse strings for industrial subsidies, so Brussels' realistic option was to relax Article 107 TFEU state aid restrictions and let national capitals write the cheques. The "first-of-a-kind" designation gave legal cover for what would otherwise have been illegal subsidies to a single company.

### Why is the 20% target considered unrealistic?

Global chip demand is growing roughly 8% annually, so even doubling absolute European output leaves market share roughly flat. To actually hit 20%, Europe would need to roughly quadruple capacity by 2030 — requiring perhaps €500 billion in cumulative investment, far beyond what has been mobilised. The April 2025 European Court of Auditors report flagged this arithmetic gap explicitly and criticised the Commission for not modelling it before announcing the figure.

### Why did Intel walk away from Magdeburg?

Intel's foundry business burned through cash as it struggled to compete with TSMC on advanced nodes, and the company posted catastrophic results in mid-2024. Even with €9.9 billion in German subsidies, Magdeburg required Intel to commit roughly €20 billion of its own capital at a moment when activist investors were demanding spending cuts. The pause exposed a structural weakness of the Chips Act: subsidies cannot compensate for a company's underlying competitive position.
