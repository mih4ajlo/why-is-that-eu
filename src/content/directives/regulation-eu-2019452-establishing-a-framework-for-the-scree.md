## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2019/452 establishes a framework for member states to screen foreign direct investments (FDI) on grounds of security or public order – and a cooperation mechanism between member states and the European Commission. It does not create a single EU screening body; instead, it requires member states to notify each other and the Commission when screening a foreign investment that could affect security or public order, and allows the Commission to issue non-binding opinions on certain transactions.

The regulation covers investments from any non-EU country, but its focus is on sectors deemed critical: energy, transport, water, health, communications, media, data processing, defence, dual-use items, financial infrastructure, and sensitive technologies including artificial intelligence, robotics, semiconductors, and quantum computing. It entered into force in April 2019 and became fully applicable in October 2020.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was a series of high-profile Chinese acquisitions of European technology companies that exposed a regulatory vacuum. In 2016, the German semiconductor equipment maker Aixtron faced a takeover bid from a Chinese investor. The deal was cleared by German authorities, but the U.S. Committee on Foreign Investment (CFIUS) blocked it on national security grounds because Aixtron had a U.S. subsidiary. Germany lacked any screening mechanism to act. That same year, Chinese appliance maker Midea acquired German robotics company Kuka, a leader in industrial automation used in car manufacturing and aerospace. The German government approved it but later introduced stricter screening rules in 2017.

These cases crystallised a structural tension: the EU had free movement of capital and a single market, but member states had vastly different abilities to vet foreign takeovers. According to the Commission’s 2017 impact assessment on the proposal, only 11 of then-28 member states had any screening mechanism. The others relied on general competition or sectoral rules that could not assess security risks. Meanwhile, Chinese FDI into Europe had surged – the Commission’s own 2017 communication noted a five-fold increase in Chinese investment in EU strategic sectors between 2013 and 2016.

The coalition that pushed the regulation was spearheaded by France, Germany, and Italy. In February 2017, Trade Commissioner Cecilia Malmström announced a proposal as part of a broader trade defence package. The European Parliament was broadly supportive, with the rapporteurs from the centre-right EPP and Socialists & Democrats groups. Opposition came from member states that feared deterring investment – particularly Greece, Portugal, Ireland, and several Nordic countries – and from business associations. BusinessEurope, the main EU lobby group, warned in a 2017 position paper that screening could be used for “protectionist purposes” and harm the EU’s openness. However, the opposition lacked the numbers to block: in the Council, qualified majority voting applied (the regulation was based on Article 207 TFEU – common commercial policy), so the holdout states were outvoted.

Timing was crucial. The Juncker Commission (2014–2019) had been under pressure to show the EU could defend its strategic interests without renouncing openness. The Aixtron and Kuka cases, combined with unease about Chinese state-owned enterprises bidding for European infrastructure, created a window. The 2016 referendum on the EU-China investment deal also heightened awareness. By early 2017, France and Germany had already tightened domestic rules, and they pushed for an EU-level framework. The Commission’s proposal was published in September 2017, and Parliament and Council agreed in a fast 18-month process – unusually speedy for trade legislation.

What changed that allowed success where earlier attempts failed? The EU had considered FDI screening in the 1990s and 2000s but never acted. The difference was the sheer volume and visibility of Chinese deals, and the fact that major member states like Germany switched positions – from opposing any EU intervention to demanding one. The 2016 U.S. blocking of Aixtron also embarrassed Berlin into action. Once Germany, France, and Italy united, the rest followed.

## Timeline

- **2016** — Chinese bids for Aixtron and Kuka prompt German debate; U.S. blocks Aixtron deal, highlighting lack of German screening.
- **February 2017** — Germany and France issue a joint statement calling for an EU-level screening mechanism.
- **September 2017** — European Commission proposes the FDI screening regulation as part of a trade defence package.
- **November 2017** — European Parliament’s International Trade Committee adopts its report; the text is broadly supported.
- **March 2019** — European Parliament (550 votes in favour, 73 against) and Council adopt the final text.
- **11 April 2019** — Regulation enters into force.
- **11 October 2020** — Regulation becomes applicable.
- **March 2020** — Commission issues guidance on FDI screening during the COVID-19 crisis, urging member states to protect healthcare and research assets from opportunistic takeovers.
- **2023** — Commission publishes first implementation report: 22 member states now have screening mechanisms (up from 11 in 2017). The Commission had issued over 200 opinions in the first two years of application.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was a wave of legislative activity. By 2023, 22 member states had set up or strengthened national screening mechanisms, compared to 11 before the regulation. Countries that previously had none – such as Greece, Portugal, and the Czech Republic – introduced new laws. The regulation itself does not require member states to create screening systems, but the cooperation mechanism incentivises them: a country without a mechanism cannot block investments that other member states flag as risky.

In practice, the Commission has used the cooperation mechanism to issue opinions in cases involving Chinese acquisitions of European chipmakers, battery producers, and critical infrastructure. For example, in 2022, the Commission received 400 notifications (according to its 2023 report). It issued around 200 opinions, most of which were followed by member states. However, the regulation’s main value has been information-sharing and early warning, rather than blocking deals. The Commission cannot veto investments; decisions remain national.

Industry adapted by submitting earlier and more detailed notifications. Law firms report that transaction timelines now routinely include a Brussels step for EU-level consultation, even if only one member state is involved. The regulation has also been cited in broader discussions about EU economic security, feeding into the 2023 European Economic Security Strategy and the 2024 foreign subsidies regulation.

Critics, particularly Chinese investors and some EU business groups, argue that the regulation creates uncertainty and delays. The Commission’s 2023 report noted that the average notification took 35 days to process, though complex cases took longer. Supporters counter that the framework is a light-touch coordination tool, not a protectionist barrier.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU block a foreign company from buying a European firm for security reasons?

No, not directly. The regulation does not give the Commission a veto. Member states retain the final decision on each investment. But the regulation creates a cooperation mechanism where other member states and the Commission can raise objections and issue opinions. In practice, a negative opinion from the Commission often leads a member state to block or condition the deal, though it is not legally obliged to do so.

### Which sectors are covered by the FDI screening rules?

The regulation lists critical infrastructure (energy, transport, water, health, communications, data storage); critical technologies (AI, robotics, semiconductors, dual-use goods); security of essential inputs (including food and energy); and access to sensitive information or media platforms. It also explicitly covers investments involving foreign state-owned enterprises, where the risk of state-directed acquisitions is considered higher.

### Why did the EU need a law if member states already screen investments?

Before 2019, only about a third of EU member states had any screening mechanism. Even those that did had no obligation to tell each other about risky takeovers. This meant a Chinese investor acquiring a German robotics firm could also buy a French chipmaker without either country knowing the full picture. The regulation plugs that information gap and creates a floor of cooperation, while leaving member states free to keep stricter national rules.

### Has the regulation stopped any foreign takeovers?

The regulation itself has not formally blocked a deal, but it has influenced outcomes. For example, in 2021, the Commission issued an opinion on a proposed acquisition of a Dutch semiconductor company by a Chinese entity; the Dutch government subsequently prohibited the deal under its own new screening law, citing the Commission’s analysis. More often, the regulation has led to conditions being imposed – such as requiring the acquirer to keep R&D in Europe – or to investors withdrawing early to avoid a public veto.
