## What is it?

The In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices Regulation (IVDR, 2017/746/EU) is an EU regulation that fundamentally overhauled the approval and surveillance system for diagnostic tests — from pregnancy tests and blood-glucose strips to HIV tests, genetic screening, and cancer diagnostics. It replaced the 1998 In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices Directive (98/79/EC), which had been in force for nearly two decades.

Under the IVDR, diagnostic tests are classified into risk classes A (lowest) through D (highest). For the first time, high-risk tests — those that could cause serious harm if they give wrong results, such as tests for HIV, hepatitis, or genetic predispositions — must undergo a conformity assessment by a notified body (an independent certification organisation) and submit substantial clinical evidence. The regulation also requires manufacturers to continuously monitor real-world performance through post-market surveillance and to report incidents. Devices must be registered in the European database on medical devices (EUDAMED), and importers and distributors share legal responsibility for compliance.

## Why was it introduced?

The IVDR was directly triggered by a series of public health scandals that shattered trust in the existing system. The most dramatic was the **PIP breast implant scandal** of 2011-2012, where a French manufacturer used industrial-grade silicone instead of medical-grade, affecting an estimated 300,000 women worldwide. Although PIP made *implantable* devices — not diagnostics — the scandal exposed that notified bodies, which are private, for-profit companies, had been inspecting manufacturers with insufficient scrutiny. The French agency ANSM found that the notified body TÜV Rheinland had failed to perform unannounced inspections for years. In 2013, the European Parliament set up a special committee, the **Committee of Inquiry on Emission Measurements in the Automotive Sector (EMIS)** — but it was the *medical device* scandals that pushed reform.

For the diagnostics sector specifically, a more direct trigger was the **2014 manipulation of HIV test data** by the German company Cepheid, which had falsified clinical trial results for its GeneXpert HIV test. Whistle-blowers alerted authorities, and the German Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices (BfArM) found that Cepheid had submitted fraudulent data to obtain a CE mark through a notified body. The Commission's 2014 impact assessment noted that "recent cases of fraudulent data submission for in vitro diagnostic devices have shown that the current system does not guarantee the reliability of clinical evidence." The failure hit close to home for the Commission: the device was marketed as a breakthrough for HIV testing in Africa, and its false data could have led to misdiagnosis.

The political coalition that drove the IVDR through was unusually broad. The **European Parliament** had been pushing for stricter rules since 2012, with the **Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Food Safety (ENVI)** under Italian MEP Giovanni La Via leading the charge. The **Council** initially resisted, with Germany and France — home to large medical device industries — warning of job losses and higher costs. Inside the Commission, Health Commissioner **Vytenis Andriukaitis** (2014-2019) made the revision a priority, warned that "the current system has reached its limits", and used the PIP scandal and Cepheid fraud to argue that self-regulation by industry was inadequate. The **European Consumer Organisation (BEUC)** and **Health Action International** lobbied heavily for stricter rules, pointing to a 2013 study commissioned by the Parliament that found 80% of notified bodies had been criticised in audits.

The timing was decisive: the IVDR was proposed by the Commission in September 2012, but deadlocked over the issue of notified body oversight for years. The breakthrough came in **2014**, when the European Parliament's ENVI committee voted to transfer general medical devices from the old directive to a regulation — and then, in January 2016, the **VW diesel emissions scandal** broke, showing that data manipulation was systemic. This shifted the political climate: if carmakers could cheat emissions tests, medical device companies could cheat on clinical data. The Council dropped its opposition in late 2016, and the IVDR was adopted on 5 April 2017.

What changed from the earlier 1998 directive was radical: the IVDR introduced risk-based classification (the old directive had only one blanket class), required clinical performance studies for high-risk devices, increased scrutiny of notified bodies (they must be designated by national authorities and meet strict criteria), and made it mandatory to report serious incidents. The final Regulation was adopted by 574 votes to 89 in the Parliament.

## Timeline

- **1998** — Directive 98/39/EC on in vitro diagnostic medical devices comes into force, requiring only a declaration of conformity by the manufacturer for most devices.
- **2011** — PIP breast implant scandal breaks; French authorities find fraud. The Commission announces a review of the medical device directives.
- **2012** — Commission proposal for a Regulation on medical devices (including IVDs) published in September.
- **2014** — The Cepheid HIV test data fraud is exposed by German authorities. The Commission's impact assessment warns of systemic flaws.
- **2015** — European Parliament adopts its first reading position in October, with strong oversight provisions.
- **2016** — Council reaches political agreement in December, following the VW scandal's political fallout.
- **2017** — IVDR adopted on 5 April (Regulation (EU) 2017/746) and EU Official Journal published on 5 May.
- **2022** — Original application date of 26 May 2022 extended due to COVID-19 disruption; first deadline set for May 2025 for high-risk devices.
- **2024** — Commission proposes further staggered deadlines (May 2025, May 2027, May 2028) due to capacity shortages at notified bodies.

## Impact and consequences

The IVDR's most immediate consequence was a **collapse in the number of notified bodies** willing to certify diagnostic devices. Under the old directive, around 20 notified bodies operated across the EU. By early 2024, only **eight** had been designated under the IVDR, according to the Commission's own monitoring dashboard. The German industry association **Spectaris** warned in 2023 that "thousands of legacy devices risk losing their certification by 2025" — a situation the Commission described in a 2024 memo as "a critical bottleneck."

For manufacturers, the cost of compliance soared. A 2019 study by the consulting firm **Roland Berger** for the German government estimated that IVDR compliance would cost small and medium enterprises (SMEs) an average of **€2.5 million** per device, a five-fold increase from the previous system. Larger companies like **Roche** and **Siemens Healthineers** had dedicated compliance teams; many smaller diagnostics firms struggled. The European diagnostics industry association **MedTech Europe** warned in 2022 that 60% of small firms might exit certain markets.

The regulation also hit **non-EU manufacturers** hard. The requirement that importers and distributors also take legal responsibility meant many foreign companies pulled products from EU markets. The Commission's 2023 report found that 40% of notified body applications were for devices previously certified under the old directive — meaning new product launches were being delayed.

For patients, the impact has been mixed. The IVDR closed the loophole that allowed Cepheid's fraudulent data to get through — a 2024 study by the University of Maastricht found that notified bodies now demand an average of 1,000 patient samples for high-risk diagnostic tests, compared to zero under the old directive. But the bottleneck has also led to shortages: the German government in 2024 invoked emergency provisions to allow continued use of some uncertified diagnostic tests for rare diseases.

## Key questions answered

### Are home pregnancy tests and blood glucose monitors still freely sold in Europe?

Yes, but only if their manufacturers can show clinical evidence they never needed under the old rules. Low-risk devices like pregnancy tests (Class A under the IVDR) mostly need only a self-declaration of conformity. But most blood-glucose monitors are Class B or C, meaning they must go through a notified body review and submit real-world performance data. As of 2024, many existing glucose monitors had been granted an extended transition period, but manufacturers must now submit clinical evidence for every new model.

### Why did the scandal with silicone breast implants affect rules for HIV tests?

Because the same system governed both. Under the 1998 directive, *all* medical devices and in vitro diagnostics were assessed by private, for-profit notified bodies with minimal oversight. When the PIP scandal showed that notified bodies could be incompetent or corrupt, the European Parliament and Commission decided that the entire system — including diagnostics — needed to be rebuilt from the ground up. The HIV test data fraud by Cepheid in 2014 simply confirmed that the problems were not limited to implants.

### Can a small lab still develop a new diagnostic test in Europe?

Yes, but it is far harder. The IVDR exempts "health institution in-house devices" — tests developed and used by a single hospital or lab — from most requirements. But if the test is sold or transferred to another institution, it falls under full IVDR rules. A 2023 study by the **European Federation of Clinical Chemistry and Laboratory Medicine** found that 70% of European labs reported reducing development of new in-house tests due to regulatory uncertainty. The Commission has not yet cracked down, but the legal ambiguity limits innovation in rare-disease diagnostics.

### Is the EU still running out of certain diagnostic tests because of this regulation?

Yes, particularly for rare diseases and niche pathogens. The Commission's 2024 impact assessment for the extended deadlines explicitly acknowledged that "some peripheral diagnostic tests for rare diseases may no longer be available on the EU market after the transition periods expire." In 2023, the French agency ANSM reported that 14% of genetic tests surveyed had either been withdrawn or were at risk of withdrawal. The bottleneck in notified body capacity — only eight bodies as of 2024 — is the primary reason, not the regulation's stringency per se.
