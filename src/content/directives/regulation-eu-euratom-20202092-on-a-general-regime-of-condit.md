## What is it?

Regulation 2020/2092 establishes a "general regime of conditionality" that links disbursements from the EU budget to respect for rule-of-law principles. When the Commission determines that breaches of the rule of law in a member state affect — or seriously risk affecting — the sound financial management of EU funds or the protection of the Union's financial interests, it can propose measures including the suspension of payments, the suspension of commitments, or a reduction in pre-financing. The Council then decides by qualified majority within a defined timeframe.

The regulation covers all parts of the EU budget, including cohesion funds, agricultural payments, and the NextGenerationEU recovery instrument. Crucially, it requires the Commission to demonstrate a link between the rule-of-law breach and budgetary risk — meaning it is a financial protection tool, not a generic sanctions mechanism. It operates in parallel with, not in place of, the Article 7 procedure for systemic violations of EU values.

Final beneficiaries — farmers, students, researchers, regional authorities — remain legally entitled to payments even when a government's access is frozen, although the member state must continue to fund them from its own resources.

## Why was it introduced?

By the time MFF negotiations reached their endgame in July 2020, the Commission had spent nearly a decade watching Viktor Orbán's Fidesz government and Poland's Law and Justice (PiS) party dismantle judicial independence while continuing to draw billions in EU funds. The Article 7 procedures — triggered against Poland by the Commission in December 2017 and against Hungary by the European Parliament in September 2018 — were paralysed by the unanimity requirement, with Warsaw and Budapest openly pledging to veto sanctions against each other.

The structural tension was acute: OLAF, the EU's anti-fraud office, had repeatedly flagged irregularities in Hungarian procurement, and the Court of Justice had ruled against Polish judicial reforms in cases including C-619/18 (Polish Supreme Court judges, June 2019) and C-192/18 (judicial retirement age, November 2019). Yet the EU continued transferring funds — Hungary and Poland were among the largest net recipients per capita — to governments the Commission itself accused of breaching EU values. Budget Commissioner Günther Oettinger had floated rule-of-law conditionality in May 2018, and Commission President Ursula von der Leyen made it a centrepiece of her 2019 mandate.

The unblocking event was the pandemic. In July 2020, member states agreed to the €750 billion NextGenerationEU recovery package, financed by joint borrowing. The Frugal Four (Netherlands, Austria, Denmark, Sweden), led by Mark Rutte, refused to authorise unprecedented common debt without rule-of-law guarantees. The European Parliament — with rapporteurs including Eider Gardiazabal Rubial and Petri Sarvamaa — pushed hard, threatening to block the entire MFF.

Orbán and Polish Prime Minister Mateusz Morawiecki vetoed the budget package in November 2020. The deadlock broke in December under German Council presidency: Angela Merkel brokered a compromise European Council conclusion stating that the Commission would develop guidelines and wait for a CJEU ruling before applying the regulation. Hungary and Poland nonetheless challenged the regulation at the Court of Justice. On 16 February 2022, the Grand Chamber dismissed both actions (C-156/21 and C-157/21), confirming the regulation's legal basis in Article 322 TFEU.

## Timeline

- **2017** — Commission triggers Article 7(1) against Poland over judicial reforms.
- **May 2018** — Commission proposes rule-of-law conditionality as part of the 2021–2027 MFF package.
- **September 2018** — European Parliament triggers Article 7(1) against Hungary (Sargentini report).
- **July 2020** — European Council agrees on NextGenerationEU; Frugals demand rule-of-law conditionality.
- **November 2020** — Hungary and Poland veto the MFF and Own Resources Decision.
- **December 2020** — European Council compromise unblocks the package; regulation adopted.
- **1 January 2021** — Regulation enters into force.
- **March 2021** — Hungary and Poland file annulment actions at the CJEU.
- **16 February 2022** — CJEU Grand Chamber dismisses both challenges.
- **April 2022** — Commission formally triggers the mechanism against Hungary.
- **December 2022** — Council suspends approximately €6.3 billion in cohesion funds to Hungary.
- **December 2023** — Commission unlocks roughly €10.2 billion for Hungary after judicial reforms; Parliament sues the Commission over the decision in March 2024.

## Impact and consequences

Hungary became the first and so far only target of the mechanism. In December 2022, the Council froze about 55% of the cohesion programme allocations the Commission had identified as at risk — around €6.3 billion according to the Commission's communication — citing problems in public procurement, conflicts of interest, and the prosecution of corruption. Hungary's entire Recovery and Resilience Plan (€5.8 billion in grants) and additional REPowerEU funds were also held back under linked horizontal enabling conditions, particularly on judicial independence.

Budapest responded by passing an "integrity authority" law in 2022 and amending judicial governance rules in 2023. In December 2023, the Commission concluded that Hungary had satisfied the judicial-independence "super-milestone" and released approximately €10.2 billion in cohesion funds — a decision that coincided with Orbán's threat to veto €50 billion in aid to Ukraine. The European Parliament voted to sue the Commission for unlocking the funds, arguing the reforms were cosmetic.

Poland, despite being the principal political target during drafting, was never formally subjected to the conditionality regulation. After Donald Tusk's coalition replaced PiS in December 2023, the Commission moved quickly to unlock approximately €137 billion in cohesion and recovery funds in early 2024, and closed the Article 7 procedure in May 2024.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU cut off funding to countries that undermine democracy?

Yes, but only when the democratic backsliding directly threatens EU financial interests — for instance through corrupt procurement, weakened prosecution of fraud, or judges unable to adjudicate EU-funds cases independently. Pure violations of media freedom or minority rights, without a budgetary link, must go through the slower Article 7 procedure, which requires unanimity to impose sanctions and has never reached that stage.

### Why did it take Hungary getting billions frozen before EU rules on democracy had real teeth?

For two decades, EU treaties offered only Article 7, designed assuming no member state would seriously violate EU values — and rendered useless once Hungary and Poland pledged to veto each other's sanctions. The COVID recovery fund changed the politics: northern net contributors refused to authorise joint borrowing without an enforcement tool that bypassed unanimity. Conditionality works by qualified majority, which is why it succeeded where Article 7 failed.

### Did Hungary actually fix its rule-of-law problems to get the money back?

The Commission says Hungary met the specific judicial-independence milestones it set, including reforms to the National Judicial Council and limits on Supreme Court interference. Critics — including the European Parliament, which is suing the Commission — argue the reforms left Orbán's broader system of clientelism and politicised prosecution intact, and that the December 2023 release was timed to buy Hungarian acquiescence on Ukraine aid.

### Do farmers and students lose their EU payments when their government breaks the rules?

No. The regulation explicitly protects final beneficiaries: the member state remains obliged to pay them even when Brussels has suspended the corresponding reimbursements. In practice this means the national treasury, not the farmer or researcher, absorbs the financial penalty — though enforcement of that obligation has proven difficult.
