## What is it?

The Gender Equality Directive (2006/54/EC) is a consolidating EU law that brings together and strengthens seven earlier directives on equal treatment between men and women in employment and occupation. It covers access to employment, vocational training, promotion, working conditions, and social security. It explicitly prohibits direct and indirect sex discrimination, sexual harassment, and less favourable treatment related to pregnancy or maternity leave.

The directive also shifts the burden of proof in discrimination cases, so that once a worker establishes facts from which discrimination can be presumed, the employer must prove no breach occurred. It requires Member States to designate bodies for the promotion, analysis, monitoring, and support of equal treatment, and to ensure effective remedies, including compensation.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was a legal and political mess. By the early 2000s, the EU had accumulated a patchwork of seven separate equal treatment directives adopted between 1975 and 2002, each with slightly different definitions, exceptions, and enforcement mechanisms. The European Court of Justice had delivered dozens of rulings that revealed gaps and inconsistencies, including the famous *Dekker* case of 1990 (C-177/88), where the Court found that refusal to hire a pregnant woman was direct sex discrimination — a principle not uniformly reflected in all national laws. The Commission’s 2003 report on the implementation of the Equal Pay Directive (75/117/EEC) found that the gender pay gap across the EU remained stubbornly at around 15-17%, with little improvement since the 1990s, and that legal fragmentation was partly to blame.

The political drive came from Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (European Commissioner for Employment and Social Affairs, 1999–2004), who made gender mainstreaming a central pillar of the Lisbon Strategy for jobs and growth. The Commission’s 2004 proposal explicitly stated that the recast was necessary “to clarify the scope of the principle of equal treatment and to simplify, modernise and improve the existing legal framework.” The European Parliament’s Women’s Rights Committee, led by rapporteur Anna Záborská (EPP, Slovakia), pushed for stronger provisions on sexual harassment and for the directive to cover self-employed workers — though the latter was eventually handled separately in Directive 2010/41/EU.

Coalition dynamics were unusual: the directive had broad support from centre-left and liberal MEPs, while employer federations like BUSINESSEUROPE warned that shifting the burden of proof would create “unnecessary litigation” and increase compliance costs for SMEs. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) campaigned for a stronger directive that would require employers to publish gender pay audits, but this was dropped after resistance from the Council, led by Germany and the UK. The final text was adopted by the Council on 5 July 2006 by qualified majority, with no member state formally opposing — but several, including the UK and Poland, issued statements expressing reservations about the extent of the burden-of-proof provisions.

Timing mattered: the directive was proposed in 2004, the year after the European Year of People with Disabilities, which had demonstrated how EU framework directives could drive national legislative change. The 2004 enlargement brought 10 new Member States, many with weaker gender equality laws, making a consolidation directive a practical necessity to prevent a race to the bottom in labour standards.

## Timeline

- **1975** — First Equal Pay Directive (75/117/EEC) adopted, requiring equal pay for equal work.
- **1976** — Equal Treatment Directive (76/207/EEC) adopted, covering access to employment, training, and working conditions.
- **1990** — ECJ ruling in *Dekker* (C-177/88) establishes that discrimination based on pregnancy is direct sex discrimination.
- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam introduces Article 13 (now Article 19 TFEU), giving the EU explicit competence to combat discrimination based on sex, race, religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation.
- **2002** — Equal Treatment Directive amended by Directive 2002/73/EC to include definitions of harassment and sexual harassment.
- **2003** — Commission report finds gender pay gap remains at 15-17% and calls for consolidation.
- **April 2004** — Commission proposes recast directive under Commissioner Diamantopoulou.
- **July 2006** — Council adopts Directive 2006/54/EC by qualified majority.
- **August 2008** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2010** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against 12 Member States for late or incomplete transposition.
- **2014** — Commission report notes that while all Member States have transposed the directive, enforcement remains uneven.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s most concrete impact was legal clarity: it reduced the number of separate transposition instruments Member States needed to maintain, and it forced several countries — notably Greece, Poland, and the Czech Republic — to update definitions of harassment and shift the burden of proof. By 2010, the Commission reported that all 27 Member States had transposed the directive, though infringement procedures were opened against 12 for incomplete or incorrect transposition.

The practical effect on the gender pay gap has been modest. According to Eurostat, the unadjusted gender pay gap in the EU-27 stood at 17.1% in 2008, the year the directive took effect, and at 14.1% in 2019 — a decline of only 3 percentage points over a decade. The Commission’s 2013 evaluation acknowledged that the directive alone was insufficient to close the gap, and that additional measures such as pay transparency and work-life balance policies were needed — leading to later initiatives like the Pay Transparency Directive (2023) and the Work-Life Balance Directive (2019).

Sexual harassment reporting increased in some Member States after the directive required employers to define and prohibit harassment, though most cases continued to go unreported. The directive also strengthened the role of equality bodies: national equality authorities in France, Sweden, and the Netherlands saw their caseloads increase significantly after 2008, with the French Défenseur des Droits reporting a 40% rise in sex discrimination complaints between 2009 and 2012.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU sex discrimination cases sometimes require the employer to prove they didn't discriminate?

Because victims of discrimination rarely have access to the internal documents and salary data needed to prove their case. The CJEU recognized this in its 1989 *Danfoss* ruling (C-109/88), where it noted that requiring the worker to prove pay discrimination by showing "objective evidence" of the employer’s pay structure was practically impossible. The directive codifies this principle, making it easier for workers to establish a prima facie case.

### Why didn't EU equal pay rules force companies to regularly check if they pay men and women the same?

The Commission initially considered mandatory pay transparency and auditing but abandoned it after fierce opposition from BUSINESSEUROPE and from Germany, the UK, and several Nordic countries. The UK government argued that mandatory audits would impose "unnecessary administrative burdens" on business, particularly SMEs. The ETUC pushed for the measure but lacked sufficient political leverage in the Council. The idea was revived two decades later in the Pay Transparency Directive (2023), which introduced mandatory pay reporting for companies with over 100 employees.

### Why did it take until 2006 to combine all the separate EU laws on sex discrimination into one set of rules?

The original seven directives were adopted piecemeal over 31 years (1975–2006), reflecting different political priorities and institutional contexts. Consolidation became possible only after the Treaty of Amsterdam (1997) gave the EU a clear legal base for anti-discrimination law (Article 13, now Article 19 TFEU), and after the 2004 enlargement created an urgent need for a uniform framework across 25 Member States with vastly different legal traditions and enforcement capacities.
