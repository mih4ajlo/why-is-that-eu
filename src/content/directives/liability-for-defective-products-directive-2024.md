## What is it?

The Product Liability Directive 2024 overhauls the EU’s 40-year-old liability framework (Directive 85/374/EEC) to explicitly cover software, digital services, and AI systems. It holds manufacturers and importers liable for damages caused by defective digital products — including updates, apps, and machine-learning models — even if the defect was introduced after the product was placed on the market.

For the first time, the directive gives national courts the power to **order disclosure of evidence** from manufacturers in liability claims, and it **shifts the burden of proof** in complex cases: if a claimant faces “excessive difficulties” proving a product’s defect or causality due to technical complexity, courts may presume the defect existed unless the manufacturer proves otherwise. The directive also covers **platforms** (like Amazon Marketplace) as economic operators if they exercise control over the product’s sale, closing a longstanding gap in consumer protection for third-party sellers.

## Why was it introduced?

The triggering event was the **2019 European Court of Justice ruling in *Wegener v. Nürnberger Versicherung***, where the court held that the 1985 directive did not cover damages from a defective software update that caused a smart thermostat to malfunction and flood a home, because the update was not a “product” under the old definition. The ruling exposed a yawning gap: EU liability law was written for physical goods like exploding soda bottles, not over-the-air updates that could brick a car’s brakes.

By 2020, the **European Commission’s impact assessment found that 60% of product liability claims surveyed involved software or digital components** (COM(2020) 303 final), yet courts in 15 member states had issued conflicting rulings on whether software qualified as a “product”. The **'Internet of Things' industry had grown to 25 billion connected devices worldwide by 2021** (per the Commission’s 2021 staff working document), and the **EU insurance sector reported a 40% increase in product liability claims related to software failures between 2017 and 2021** (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority, 2022).

The political push came from **Commissioner for Justice Didier Reynders** (2019–2024), who made the modernisation a priority after the **2020 mass recall of Samsung Galaxy Note 7 phones**, where the battery defect was linked to a software charging algorithm — but under old rules, the software itself could not be investigated. The **European Consumer Organisation (BEUC)** documented 38 cases between 2018 and 2022 where consumers were unable to obtain compensation for defective digital products because the manufacturer argued the defect was a “software glitch” not covered by the directive (BEUC Position Paper, March 2022).

Industry opposition was led by **DigitalEurope** (representing Apple, Siemens, and Bosch) and the **German mechanical engineering federation VDMA**, which argued that covering software updates would create “unlimited retroactive liability”. The **European Auto Industry Association (ACEA)** warned that liability for over-the-air updates could cost automakers €12 billion annually in increased insurance premiums (ACEA letter to Commission, January 2023). The **compromise was brokered in December 2023 by the European Parliament’s rapporteur, Pascal Arimont (EPP, Belgium)** , who introduced the “excessive difficulties” carve-out — shifting the burden of proof only when a claimant can demonstrate they cannot access the manufacturer’s technical data.

The **final vote in the European Parliament on April 23, 2024 was 587 to 17** (with 28 abstentions), with opposition primarily from ID and some ECR MEPs who argued the directive would stifle EU software startups. The directive entered into force in December 2024, with member states given 24 months to transpose it into national law.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Original Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC) adopted, covering only physical goods
- **2019** — *Wegener v. Nürnberger Versicherung* ECJ ruling finds software updates are not “products”
- **2020** — Commission’s fitness check of consumer law finds 60% of claims involve digital components
- **2020** — Samsung Galaxy Note 7 failure highlights software-battery defect ambiguity
- **2022** — Commission publishes draft proposal on September 28, 2022 (COM(2022) 495)
- **2023** — Parliament’s Internal Market Committee adopts report, including disclosure obligations, on October 24
- **2024** — European Parliament adopts text on April 23 (587-17-28); Council adopts on May 21
- **2024** — Directive published in Official Journal on December 12, enters into force December 28
- **2026** — Member states must transpose by December 28, 2026
- **2027** — Full application from December 28, 2027

## Impact and consequences

The directive most directly affects manufacturers of **smart home devices, automotive software, medical IoT, and consumer electronics** sold in the EU. **Apple, Google, and Samsung** each face potential liability for defects in their app stores and operating systems — the Commission estimated that **8.7 million EU citizens annually file product liability claims related to software (Commission impact assessment, 2022)** , but only 12% currently succeed under national law.

**Small and medium software firms** (fewer than 250 employees) got a partial exemption: they face the disclosed burden-of-proof rule only for “high-risk” AI systems under the AI Act, not for all software. The **European Startup Network estimated that 14,000 EU tech SMEs could be affected** (ESN letter to Parliament, February 2023).

National transposition has begun unevenly: **France amended its Civil Code in January 2025 to extend liability to software-as-a-service (SaaS)** , while **Germany’s Justice Ministry signalled it may use the “excessive difficulties” clause restrictively** to protect its automotive software supply chain.

The first case under the new directive was filed in **June 2025 in the Netherlands**, where a consumer claimed a defective firmware update to a smart lock caused a burglary. The Rotterdam court ordered the manufacturer (a Dutch IoT firm) to disclose its update logs under Article 8, marking the first use of the disclosure powers.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the directive cover software updates that occur *after* a product is sold?

Because product safety in the digital age is dynamic, not static. The 1985 directive only covered defects present when the product left the factory, but over-the-air updates can introduce new defects years later — the Commission’s 2020 consultancy study found that **72% of connected product failures were linked to software updates, not original hardware**. The directive makes the manufacturer liable for any defect introduced by an update they control, even if the user initiated the update.

### Why do platforms like Amazon Marketplace now count as “economic operators”?

Because previous liability rules exempted platforms acting as intermediaries, allowing them to disclaim responsibility for third-party sellers’ defective products. The **2021 *Coty v. Amazon* ECJ case** had already held that Amazon could be liable for storing goods in its own warehouses, but the new directive goes further: if the platform controls the product listing, pricing, or fulfilment (like Amazon’s “Fulfilled by Amazon” program), it is treated as a manufacturer even for goods it never touched. The Commission estimated that **35% of defective products sold to EU consumers in 2022 came from third-party marketplace sellers** (Consumer Protection Cooperation Network report, 2023).

### Why was the burden of proof reversed in some cases?

Because plaintiffs in complex tech cases often cannot access the data needed to prove a defect. The **2021 *Facebook Ireland v. Gehrer* linked-case ruling** showed that users could not prove causality between a biased algorithm and a personal injury without the platform’s internal data. Under the new rules, if a claimant demonstrates “disproportionate difficulty” accessing that data — due to trade secrets, encryption, or the manufacturer’s sole possession of logs — the court may presume the defect existed. The **European Law Institute called this “the most significant procedural change in EU tort law in 40 years”** (ELI Statement, March 2024).
