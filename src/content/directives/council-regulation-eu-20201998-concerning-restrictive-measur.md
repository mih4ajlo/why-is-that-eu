## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2020/1998, adopted alongside Council Decision (CFSP) 2020/1999 on 7 December 2020, gave the EU for the first time a standing legal instrument to sanction individuals and entities for grave human rights violations regardless of where they occur or which government they serve. Previously, EU sanctions had to be attached to a country-specific regime — Syria, Belarus, Venezuela — which made it politically awkward to hit, say, a Saudi official without designating Saudi Arabia as a whole.

The regime covers genocide, crimes against humanity, torture, slavery, extrajudicial killings, enforced disappearances, and systematic arbitrary detention. It also reaches "other human rights violations" such as trafficking, sexual violence, or violations of freedom of assembly and expression when widespread or systematic. Listed persons face EU-wide asset freezes and travel bans, and EU operators are forbidden from making funds available to them.

Designations require unanimity in the Council, on a proposal from the High Representative or a Member State. It is the EU's counterpart to the United States' Global Magnitsky Act of 2016 and the United Kingdom's 2020 Global Human Rights Sanctions Regulations.

## Why was it introduced?

The push began in earnest in November 2018, when Dutch Foreign Minister Stef Blok formally proposed an EU Magnitsky-style regime at the Foreign Affairs Council. The murder of Saudi journalist Jamal Khashoggi in the Istanbul consulate weeks earlier had exposed the EU's awkward position: Washington promptly sanctioned 17 Saudi officials under its Global Magnitsky Act, while Brussels could only issue statements, because there was no Saudi Arabia sanctions regime to hang designations on.

The intellectual godfather was the Sergei Magnitsky case — the Russian tax lawyer who died in pre-trial detention in 2009 after exposing a $230 million tax fraud, and whose treatment Bill Browder spent the next decade lobbying parliaments to address. The European Parliament had repeatedly called for an EU version, most prominently in a March 2014 resolution naming 32 Russian officials and a March 2019 resolution demanding a horizontal regime. The Netherlands, with backing from the Baltic states, Germany and the Nordics, became the diplomatic engine.

Three structural problems pushed the file over the line. First, country-by-country sanctions had become geopolitically clumsy: hitting Chinese officials over Xinjiang or Saudi officials over Khashoggi required either designating an entire ally-of-convenience or doing nothing. Second, the United States and Canada (2017) and the United Kingdom (July 2020) had moved ahead, leaving an obvious capability gap and complicating transatlantic coordination. Third, Commission President Ursula von der Leyen had promised a "geopolitical Commission," and High Representative Josep Borrell made the Magnitsky file an early test of that ambition.

What finally unblocked it was the German Council Presidency in the second half of 2020 combined with Borrell's decision to formally table the proposal in October 2020. Member States that had previously been lukewarm — notably Italy, Greece, Cyprus and Hungary, all wary of antagonising specific third countries — accepted the package partly because designations would still require unanimity, giving any capital a veto on individual listings. The regime was adopted by the Council on 7 December 2020.

A significant fight was lost by the Parliament and by NGOs including Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch: corruption was excluded from the scope, despite being central to both the original Magnitsky story and the US Global Magnitsky Act. France and several southern Member States resisted, arguing corruption belonged in a separate instrument. The Commission and Borrell pledged in 2021 to bring forward a complementary anti-corruption sanctions proposal; it was tabled in May 2023.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Sergei Magnitsky dies in Russian custody; Bill Browder begins international lobbying campaign.
- **2012** — US Congress passes the Magnitsky Act targeting Russian officials.
- **2014** — European Parliament resolution calls for EU-level Magnitsky sanctions against named Russian officials.
- **2016** — US Global Magnitsky Act extends regime worldwide.
- **November 2018** — Dutch Foreign Minister Stef Blok formally proposes EU regime at Foreign Affairs Council, weeks after Khashoggi's murder.
- **December 2019** — Foreign Affairs Council, under HR Federica Mogherini, agrees to begin preparatory work.
- **July 2020** — UK launches its Global Human Rights Sanctions Regulations, designating Saudi, Russian, Myanmar and North Korean nationals.
- **October 2020** — HR Josep Borrell tables formal proposal.
- **7 December 2020** — Council adopts Regulation 2020/1998 and Decision (CFSP) 2020/1999.
- **2 March 2021** — First designations: four Russian officials linked to the imprisonment of Alexei Navalny.
- **22 March 2021** — Listings of Chinese officials over Xinjiang abuses; Beijing retaliates by sanctioning MEPs and scholars, freezing the EU-China Comprehensive Agreement on Investment.
- **2023** — Commission proposes complementary anti-corruption sanctions regime.

## Impact and consequences

The first designations on 2 March 2021 targeted four senior Russian officials, including the head of the Investigative Committee and the director of the Federal Penitentiary Service, over Navalny's treatment. Three weeks later, on 22 March 2021, the Council listed four Chinese officials and one entity (the Xinjiang Production and Construction Corps Public Security Bureau) over the mass detention of Uyghurs — coordinated with parallel US, UK and Canadian listings.

Beijing's response was unusually severe and reshaped EU-China relations: it sanctioned five MEPs, the Parliament's Subcommittee on Human Rights, two EU national parliamentarians, several academics and the Mercator Institute for China Studies. The European Parliament responded by freezing ratification of the EU-China Comprehensive Agreement on Investment, which Chancellor Angela Merkel had pushed through politically in December 2020. That deal remains shelved.

Subsequent listings have targeted figures from Myanmar (after the February 2021 coup), Libya, South Sudan, Eritrea, North Korea, the Russian Wagner Group, Iranian "morality police" officials following the 2022 death of Mahsa Amini, and Hamas operatives after October 2023. The regime has become the EU's default tool for symbolic individual punishment when full country sanctions are not feasible.

Critics — including Amnesty International and several MEPs — note that unanimity has slowed listings and produced inconsistent geographic coverage, with notably few designations against officials of EU strategic partners. Browder and others have continued pressing for qualified-majority voting on human rights sanctions, a proposal floated by von der Leyen in her 2020 State of the Union but blocked by Hungary and others.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU sanction individual foreign officials for human rights abuses?

Yes. Since December 2020 the EU has had a standing framework to impose asset freezes and travel bans on individuals and entities responsible for serious human rights violations anywhere in the world, without needing to establish a country-wide sanctions regime first. It has been used against Russian, Chinese, Iranian, Myanmar and other officials.

### Why doesn't the EU sanction corrupt officials the same way?

The 2020 regime deliberately excluded corruption, after France and several southern Member States resisted bundling it with human rights. The Commission proposed a separate anti-corruption sanctions instrument in May 2023, but it requires unanimity in the Council and as of writing has not been adopted.

### Why did the EU's Xinjiang sanctions cause such a big diplomatic crisis with China?

The March 2021 listings of four Chinese officials over Uyghur detention were the first EU sanctions on Chinese individuals since the post-Tiananmen arms embargo of 1989. Beijing retaliated by sanctioning MEPs, academics and a major think tank — which prompted the European Parliament to freeze ratification of the EU-China investment agreement Angela Merkel had concluded months earlier. That deal has never been revived.

### Why does any single EU country get to block a sanctions listing?

EU foreign policy decisions, including individual sanctions designations, require unanimity in the Council under the Treaty on European Union. Commission President von der Leyen proposed moving human rights sanctions to qualified-majority voting in her 2020 State of the Union address, but Hungary and other capitals have blocked any treaty-level or "passerelle" change, meaning a single Member State can still veto a name being added to the list.
