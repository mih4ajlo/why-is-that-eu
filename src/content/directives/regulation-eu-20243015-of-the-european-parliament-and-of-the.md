## What is it?

The Forced Labour Regulation (Regulation 2024/3015/EU), adopted in November 2024, establishes a comprehensive ban on products made wholly or in part with forced labour — including state-imposed forced labour — from entering or remaining on the European Union market. It applies to all products, whether manufactured in the EU or imported, and covers all sectors and all stages of production, from raw material extraction to final assembly.

Under the regulation, national authorities and, in certain cases, the European Commission can investigate products suspected of being tainted by forced labour. If an investigation confirms forced labour, the product must be withdrawn from the market, disposed of, and cannot be re-exported to third countries. Companies face fines and potential exclusion from public procurement. The burden of proof shifts to the economic operator to demonstrate that its supply chain is free of forced labour during investigations.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was triggered by a specific geopolitical scandal and a structural failure of voluntary corporate due diligence. In 2019, the European Parliament received reports from the Uyghur Human Rights Project and other NGOs detailing the systematic use of forced labour of Uyghur Muslims in China’s Xinjiang region for cotton, tomato, and solar panel production. The EU had no legal tool to block such goods; existing trade defence instruments covered dumping and subsidies, but not human rights violations in sourcing.

The structural tension was clear: the EU’s Generalised Scheme of Preferences (GSP) could withdraw trade preferences for countries using forced labour, but this was a blunt instrument affecting entire nations and took years to apply. The EU’s Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD, 2024/1760/EU) was still being negotiated and only applied to large EU companies, not to non-EU producers or to products entering the market through smaller importers. A targeted product ban was needed to close this gap.

The coalition that pushed the regulation through was unusual: it united human rights advocates in the European Parliament (notably MEPs from the Progressive Alliance of Socialists and Democrats and the Greens/European Free Alliance), trade unions (the European Trade Union Confederation), and domestic manufacturing industries in Southern Europe (textile associations in Italy and Portugal) that faced competition from Xinjiang cotton. They were opposed by some European retailers (EuroCommerce) and logistics firms, who warned of supply chain disruption and higher compliance costs, as well as by China’s Ministry of Commerce, which called the regulation "a form of economic coercion."

The timing was decisive. In March 2021, the United States had already enacted the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA), creating a presumption that all goods from Xinjiang were tainted unless proven otherwise. The EU risked becoming a dumping ground for goods blocked by the US. In June 2021, the European Commission’s own impact assessment found that over 50 EU companies had been implicated in Xinjiang supply chains, according to a leaked draft reported by Politico EU. The European Parliament’s vote on the file in February 2024 saw 477 votes in favour, 63 against, and 30 abstentions — a broad majority that included the centre-right European People’s Party (EPP) after they secured compromises on burden of proof and investigation timelines.

The specific event that unblocked the negotiations was the publication in May 2023 of satellite imagery analysis by the Australian Strategic Policy Institute (ASPI) showing forced labour camps in Xinjiang, which the Commission’s own Joint Research Centre validated. This hardened the Council’s position, leading to final adoption in November 2024.

## Timeline

- **2019-2020** — NGOs and human rights groups document forced labour of Uyghurs in Xinjiang; European Parliament holds hearings
- **March 2021** — US passes the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA)
- **September 2022** — European Commission publishes proposal for a Forced Labour Regulation
- **February 2024** — European Parliament votes in favour (477-63) with amendments strengthening investigative powers
- **June 2024** — Council of the EU adopts its position after months of negotiation over burden of proof
- **November 2024** — Regulation adopted by co-legislators
- **December 2024** — Regulation enters into force
- **December 2029** — Regulation fully applicable

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects an estimated €200 billion in annual EU imports from high-risk sectors (textiles, electronics, solar panels, agricultural products), based on European Commission trade statistics. The European Commission estimated that up to 5% of EU imports — or roughly €100 billion — could be subject to investigations initially, though many will be cleared.

Industry responded by accelerating supply chain mapping. The German engineering federation VDMA and the French textile union UFIPQ launched joint due-diligence platforms in early 2025. The Danish toy manufacturer LEGO announced it would cease sourcing from Xinjiang altogether in 2025, according to a company press release.

The main compliance challenge is for SMEs: the regulation does not exempt small companies, but allows them to use simplified investigation procedures. The Commission’s guidance, published in March 2024, recommended that SMEs join collective due-diligence initiatives run by trade associations.

China has warned it will retaliate with export restrictions on rare earths and essential minerals, according to a statement from China’s Ministry of Commerce in November 2024. The first enforcement action is expected in 2026 against a solar panel importer using Xinjiang polysilicon, based on information from a whistleblower complaint already filed with the Commission.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the regulation ban re-export of seized goods?

The ban on re-export (Article 23) prevents companies from simply moving forced-labour-tainted products to third-country markets after EU seizure. This closes a loophole where goods could be repatriated to the country of origin or sold to non-EU buyers. The European Parliament’s committee on international trade (INTA) insisted on this provision after learning that US customs had, in 2022, allowed a shipment of Xinjiang cotton to be re-exported to Vietnam, undermining enforcement.

### Why does the regulation not name specific products or regions directly?

The regulation takes a product-based, risk-based approach rather than a country-ban approach. This was a deliberate choice: a blanket ban on all goods from Xinjiang (as the US UFLPA does) was rejected as illegal under WTO rules, specifically the Most-Favoured-Nation principle and the prohibition on discriminatory trade measures. The EU's legal service advised that a presumption of guilt based solely on geographic origin would be challenged successfully at the WTO. Instead, the regulation creates a rebuttable presumption for products from high-risk areas, but only after an investigation has been opened.

### Why did the regulation take until 2024 when the US acted in 2021?

The EU’s legislative process is slower because it requires agreement between the Commission, Parliament, and Council, with extensive stakeholder consultations. The US UFLPA was passed by simple majority and signed by President Biden within 18 months of proposal. In the EU, early attempts in 2020 to use the existing Regulation (EU) No 995/2010 on conflict minerals as a model failed because it applied only to specific minerals, not all products. A broader initiative required a new legal base under Article 207 TFEU (common commercial policy), which the Commission only proposed in 2022 after legal review. Internal divisions in the Council — with Germany, Sweden, and the Netherlands fearing trade retaliation from China — delayed adoption.

### Why are smaller companies not exempt?

The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, MEP Samira Rafaela (Renew, Netherlands), argued that exempting SMEs would create a dual market: large companies would face strict rules while smaller importers could freely import forced-labour goods, undermining enforcement. However, the regulation (Article 19) allows SMEs to use simplified due-diligence questionnaires and to rely on industry-wide risk assessments, lowering compliance costs. The Commission estimates SME compliance costs at €5,000–€20,000 per investigation, versus €50,000–€200,000 for large companies.
