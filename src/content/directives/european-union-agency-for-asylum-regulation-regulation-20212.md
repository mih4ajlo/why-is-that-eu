## What is it?

The EUAA Regulation, adopted in December 2021, establishes the European Union Agency for Asylum as the successor to EASO, which had operated since 2010. The new agency is designed to strengthen the practical implementation of the Common European Asylum System (CEAS) by granting it powers that EASO lacked — including the ability to monitor national asylum procedures, carry out unannounced inspections, deploy asylum experts directly to member states under strain, and issue binding assessments that member states must address. It entered into force on 19 January 2022.

## Why was it introduced?

The most specific trigger was EASO’s collapse during the 2015–2016 migration crisis. As hundreds of thousands of asylum seekers arrived in Greece, Italy, and the western Balkans, EASO was unable to direct Member States to act. Its mandate was limited to providing support only when requested; it had no authority to intervene if a country’s system broke down. The European Court of Auditors’ 2017 report found that EASO’s inability to enforce standards was a fundamental weakness—Member States were free to ignore its recommendations and frequently did.

The structural tension was clear: the Dublin system placed primary responsibility on frontline states like Greece and Italy, yet the agency meant to relieve their burden had no operational teeth. During the 2015 crisis, Greece faced tens of thousands of asylum applications with minimal EU-level operational back-up because EASO could not force other Member States to lend experts or resources. 

The coalition that pushed for the transformation was led by the European Parliament—particularly the LIBE committee—which from 2016 saw a stronger agency as non-negotiable in any reform of the CEAS. Rapporteur Elena Yoncheva (S&D) argued repeatedly that voluntary cooperation had failed. The European Commission under Jean-Claude Juncker proposed the transformation in the May 2016 CEAS reform package, but negotiations stalled for four years. Member states such as Hungary, Poland, and the Czech Republic opposed giving the agency any supranational enforcement power, arguing it infringed national sovereignty over asylum systems.

What finally unblocked the deadlock was the COVID-19 pandemic and the New Pact on Migration and Asylum presented in September 2020 by the von der Leyen Commission. Home Affairs Commissioner Ylva Johansson made the EUAA a central pillar of the Pact, framing stronger operational capacity as a crisis-prevention measure. The political shift after the 2019 European elections, with a pro-reform majority in Parliament and a Commission determined to show progress, provided the necessary coalition. The EUAA Regulation was voted in the Parliament on 10 December 2020 by a comfortable majority (374 in favour, 121 against, 129 abstentions), and the Council formally adopted it in December 2021. The opposition—led by Hungary and Poland—came close to blocking the final text via a qualified majority veto, but in the end only those two countries voted against.

## Timeline

- **2010** — EASO established (Regulation 439/2010) with a mandate to facilitate cooperation between Member States on asylum.
- **2015–2016** — Migration crisis: over 1.2 million asylum applications submitted in the EU according to Eurostat; EASO’s limited role exposed in Greece and Italy.
- **May 2016** — Commission proposes recast of the CEAS, including transforming EASO into a fully-fledged agency with monitoring powers.
- **2017** — European Court of Auditors publishes a damning report on EASO’s effectiveness, noting it “had little influence over the implementation of the CEAS.”
- **September 2020** — Commission adopts the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, proposing the EUAA as a key operational tool.
- **10 December 2020** — European Parliament adopts the EUAA Regulation as part of a broader asylum reform package.
- **December 2021** — Council adopts the Regulation; Hungary and Poland vote against.
- **19 January 2022** — Regulation enters into force; EUAA formally replaces EASO.
- **2023** — EUAA begins conducting its first monitoring visits and deploying its own staff to member states.

## Impact and consequences

The EUAA now has a mandate to provide operational and technical support with or without a formal request from a Member State. It can launch monitoring reports on a country’s asylum system and issue recommendations that the Member State must respond to within a set deadline. In practice, this means the agency has gained leverage over national procedures—for example, it can evaluate whether border procedures comply with EU standards and press for changes.

The most immediate impact was on frontline states. Greece and Italy, which had been criticised for failing to process asylum applications adequately, now face regular inspections. By 2024, the EUAA had deployed over 800 experts to Member States under strain, according to the agency’s annual activity report. The agency also took over the management of the EU’s asylum common database (Eurodac) and now handles operational coordination for the new Crisis and Force Majeure Regulation (part of the Pact).

Member states that opposed the regulation—Hungary and Poland—have been slow to cooperate. In 2023, the Commission sent a formal letter of notice to Hungary for failing to implement the EUAA Regulation’s provisions on monitoring, a step that could lead to an infringement procedure. Industry (law firms, NGOs, migrant support organisations) overwhelmingly welcomed the change, but some expressed concern that monitoring remains non-binding in terms of sanctions.

Financially, the EUAA’s budget grew from €95 million (EASO in 2020) to €180 million by 2024, according to the EU budget. This increase reflects the agency’s expanded staff and field operations.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU create a new asylum agency instead of just strengthening the old one?

EASO was designed in 2010 as a voluntary cooperation body with no enforcement capacity. During the 2015 crisis, it proved unable to compel Member States to provide experts or to intervene when systems collapsed. The EUAA Regulation fundamentally changes the legal basis: the agency can now act without being invited and can monitor compliance. Creating a new legal entity allowed lawmakers to rewrite the mandate from scratch, including mandatory contributions from Member States and a binding monitoring mechanism.

### Can the EUAA force a country to change its asylum procedures?

No, the EUAA cannot directly override national decisions or impose fines. It can, however, conduct unannounced inspections and issue formal recommendations. If a Member State fails to address those recommendations, the Commission can launch infringement proceedings. In practice, the strong monitoring and publicity powers create political pressure. The 2023 monitoring visit to Hungary’s Röszke transit zone, for example, led to a public report that contributed to the Commission’s infringement case against Hungary on asylum access.

### Does the EUAA replace national asylum authorities?

No. The agency supports and monitors national authorities but does not take over their functions. It trains national staff, provides country-of-origin information, and coordinates relocation and resettlement. Only in a “crisis situation”—defined under the new Crisis Regulation—could the EUAA take a more operational coordinating role, but Member States retain decision-making power over individual applications.

### How did the 2015 migration crisis change the EU’s asylum institutions?

The crisis exposed the gap between the EU’s common rules and its weak enforcement. EASO’s lack of operational capacity was a key lesson. The EUAA Regulation was the first institutional reform resulting from the crisis; it was followed by the broader new Pact on Migration and Asylum in 2024, which overhauled the entire European asylum system. The creation of the EUAA was the necessary first step to give the EU the tools to ensure that common standards are actually applied across all Member States.
