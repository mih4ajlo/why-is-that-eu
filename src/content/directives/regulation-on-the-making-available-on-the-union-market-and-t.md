## What is it?

Regulation 2023/1115 forces any company placing beef, cocoa, coffee, palm oil, soya, rubber, wood or their derivatives (chocolate, leather, furniture, tyres, printed paper) on the EU market to file a due diligence statement proving the goods were not grown on land deforested after 31 December 2020 — and that they comply with the producer country's laws on labour, human rights and indigenous tenure.

The proof must include polygon-level GPS coordinates of every plot of land where the commodity was produced. The Commission classifies countries as low, standard or high risk, which determines the intensity of checks. Penalties reach 4% of EU-wide annual turnover, plus confiscation of goods and revenues, and exclusion from public procurement.

It replaces the 2010 EU Timber Regulation, which only covered illegal logging. The EUDR catches legal deforestation too — the central innovation that made it the most lobbied-against environmental file of the Von der Leyen Commission's first term.

## Why was it introduced?

Brazilian deforestation in the Amazon hit a 12-year high in 2020 under President Jair Bolsonaro, with INPE satellite data showing 11,088 km² cleared — an area larger than Lebanon. Investigations by Greenpeace, Repórter Brasil and the Bureau of Investigative Journalism repeatedly traced soya from cleared Cerrado land and beef from illegally deforested Amazon ranches into the supply chains of JBS, Cargill and Bunge, and onto European supermarket shelves. A 2021 WWF report calculated that EU consumption was responsible for 16% of tropical deforestation embedded in international trade — second only to China and ahead of the US.

The political trigger was the 2019 Amazon fire crisis. President Macron threatened to block the EU-Mercosur trade deal in August 2019, tweeting that "our house is burning." Commissioner-designate Frans Timmermans, preparing the European Green Deal, made deforestation one of its early flagship deliverables. The Commission's 2019 communication "Stepping up EU Action to Protect and Restore the World's Forests" admitted that voluntary corporate pledges — the Consumer Goods Forum had promised zero net deforestation by 2020 and missed it spectacularly — had failed.

The Commission published its proposal on 17 November 2021 under Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius. The European Parliament strengthened it dramatically, adding rubber, pigmeat, poultry, sheep, goat and maize to the scope (the final text kept rubber but dropped the others), and pushing the cut-off date back to 2020. Trilogue concluded on 6 December 2022. MEPs approved the final text on 19 April 2023 by 552 votes to 44 with 43 abstentions.

Opposition was intense and global. Indonesia and Malaysia — together producing 85% of world palm oil — called the regulation "regulatory imperialism" and sent a joint mission to Brussels in May 2023. Brazil, Colombia, Ivory Coast, Ghana and 14 other producer countries signed a joint letter in September 2023 warning of "punitive" effects on smallholders. The US Trade Representative formally objected. Inside the EU, Austria's farmers' union, German auto suppliers worried about leather, and Italy's coffee roasters (Lavazza, Illy) lobbied for delays. In October 2024, after a letter from Germany's Robert Habeck and pressure from the EPP, the Commission proposed postponing application by 12 months — Parliament adopted the delay on 17 December 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation covers an estimated €60 billion of annual EU imports. Mondelez, Nestlé, Ferrero and Mars have built or contracted geolocation tracing systems for cocoa, with traceability rates in Ivory Coast and Ghana climbing from below 50% to above 80% during 2023–2024 according to the World Cocoa Foundation. Cocoa futures hit record highs above $12,000/tonne in April 2024 — partly weather-driven, but EUDR compliance costs were cited by traders Olam and Barry Callebaut as a contributing factor.

Smallholder anxiety is real. Ivory Coast has roughly 1 million cocoa farmers, most cultivating less than 5 hectares, many without formal land titles. Ghana's Cocobod warned in 2023 that 30% of its farmers risked exclusion. The Commission allocated €70 million in Team Europe support and launched country dialogues, but NGOs including Fern and Mighty Earth criticised the pace.

The country benchmarking system, originally due mid-2024, slipped to May 2025, when the Commission classified only four countries as high-risk (Russia, Belarus, North Korea, Myanmar) — a decision Greenpeace called "absurd" given Brazil and Indonesia were rated standard. Germany's auto industry association VDA secured guidance that recycled rubber would face lighter scrutiny. The Commission's Information System (TRACES extension) for filing due diligence statements went live in late 2024 with documented capacity issues that justified the delay.

## Key questions answered

### Why was the cut-off date set at 31 December 2020?

The date aligns with the New York Declaration on Forests and the Glasgow Leaders' Declaration on Forests at COP26, where 145 countries pledged to halt deforestation by 2030. Setting the cut-off in the past — rather than at entry into force — prevents a rush to clear land before the rules apply. It also matches the baseline most major commodity traders had already adopted in their voluntary pledges, so the Commission could argue it was codifying existing industry commitments rather than imposing new ones.

### Why was rubber included but not maize?

Rubber survived trilogue because nearly all natural rubber is produced in Southeast Asia on land with high deforestation pressure, and tyre manufacturers Michelin and Continental did not strongly oppose inclusion. Maize was dropped because it is overwhelmingly produced inside the EU and US, where deforestation links are weak, and including it would have created enormous compliance burdens for animal feed supply chains for limited environmental gain.

### Why did the Commission delay application by a year?

Officially, because the IT system and country benchmarking were not ready, and producer countries — particularly Indonesia, Malaysia and Brazil — had escalated complaints to the WTO and to G20 capitals. Politically, the new Commission under Ursula von der Leyen's second term faced pressure from the EPP, which had won June 2024 elections partly on a deregulatory platform, and from Berlin's coalition crisis. The delay was framed as technical to avoid reopening the substantive text, which the Greens and S&D refused to renegotiate.

### Why does this go further than the US Lacey Act or UK Environment Act?

The Lacey Act (amended 2008) and the UK's schedule 17 forest risk commodities rules only cover illegality in producer countries — if deforestation is legal under local law, it can still enter those markets. The EUDR is the first to ban legal deforestation, applying an EU-defined standard extraterritorially. This is what producer governments object to most strongly, and what NGOs consider its decisive innovation.
