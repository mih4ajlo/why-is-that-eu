## What is it?

Directive 2009/73/EC is the cornerstone of the European Union's rules for the internal natural gas market, adopted as part of the Third Energy Package. It forces vertically integrated gas companies—companies that both produce/sell gas and own the pipelines that transport it—to legally separate (“unbundle”) their transmission network from their supply and production arms. The directive also requires non-discriminatory third-party access to gas storage and LNG terminals, strengthens the independence of national regulators, and establishes the Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER).

In practice, this means a company like Gazprom, which both supplies gas to Europe and controls the pipelines that bring that gas in, could no longer operate both functions without strict separation. The directive sets out three models: full ownership unbundling (sale of the pipeline network), the Independent System Operator (ISO) model (network assets stay with the parent but are operated by an independent company), and the Independent Transmission Operator (ITO) model (a legally separate subsidiary within the same group, with strict governance rules). The ITO was the compromise that allowed the directive to pass.

## Why was it introduced?

The Gas Directive was born from a political crisis and a pipeline war. In January 2006, Russia’s state-owned Gazprom shut off gas to Ukraine—European customers in Poland, Slovakia, Hungary, and Austria lost supply in the middle of winter. A far more severe cutoff followed in January 2009, just as the directive was being debated by the European Parliament. Eighteen EU member states reported supply drops; thousands of households lost heating. The European Commission’s then-Energy Commissioner, Andris Piebalgs (2004–2010), used these outages to argue that Europe’s dependence on a single, vertically integrated supplier—Gazprom—was a fundamental security risk.

The structural tension was clear: the gas market was built on long-term, destination-clause contracts linked to oil prices, and Gazprom’s control over pipelines gave it the power to block competitors from accessing the network. Despite liberalisation efforts from the First and Second Energy Packages (1998 and 2003), incumbents in France, Germany, Austria, and Italy resisted unbundling. Gazprom supplied roughly 25% of EU gas at the time, according to a 2009 Commission staff working document, and its subsidiaries held equity stakes and long-term transport bookings in several new member states.

The coalition dynamics were fraught. The European Commission, backed by the UK, the Netherlands, the Nordic countries, and most new member states (especially Poland, the Baltic states, and Slovakia), pushed for full ownership unbundling—the most radical separation. Germany and France led the opposition, supported by their national energy champions (E.ON, RWE, GDF Suez, EDF). German Chancellor Angela Merkel declared in early 2008 that “ownership unbundling would weaken investment and threaten supply security,” paraphrased from her speech at the European Council. The German and French governments proposed a weaker “effective unbundling” alternative. The tipping point came in 2008: the European Parliament, led by rapporteur Alejo Vidal-Quadras (EPP, Spain), insisted on ownership unbundling as the default. The final text, adopted on 13 July 2009, kept full unbundling as the preferred model but allowed ISO and ITO as compromises. The ITO loophole was crucial: Germany and France accepted the package only after securing it.

Timing was everything. The 2009 gas crisis had shattered the argument that voluntary access and negotiated third-party access were sufficient. The Treaty of Lisbon, which entered into force in December 2009, gave the EU explicit competence in energy policy (Article 194 TFEU), but the directive was adopted before that—using the internal market legal base. The earlier failed attempt—the 2007 Commission proposal for a preference for ownership unbundling—had been stalled by Council opposition. What changed was the January 2009 cutoff, which made non-cooperation politically untenable.

## Timeline

- **1998** — First Gas Directive (98/30/EC) opens national markets to limited competition, but incumbents retain vertical integration.
- **2003** — Second Gas Directive (2003/55/EC) requires legal and functional unbundling for transmission, but ownership remains unchanged allowing de facto control.
- **January 2006** — Russia-Ukraine gas dispute cuts supplies to EU; three days of cold weather outages in Poland and Hungary.
- **September 2007** — European Commission proposes the Third Energy Package with full ownership unbundling as the default.
- **Spring 2008** — Germany and France counter-propose a weaker alternative; Merkel privately pressures Commissioners including President Barroso.
- **6 May 2009** — European Parliament adopts its first reading position, strongly backing ownership unbundling. Final text with ITO compromise agreed.
- **13 July 2009** — Directive 2009/73/EC adopted by the Council and Parliament.
- **3 March 2011** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2012–2013** — European Commission opens infringement proceedings against several member states (including Poland, Bulgaria, and Hungary) for failure to implement unbundling correctly.
- **2015** — Commission initiates an antitrust case against Gazprom for alleged abuse of dominance (overpricing, territorial restrictions). Gazprom’s contractual restrictions such as destination clauses are later explicitly banned.
- **2018** — Gazprom offers commitments to the Commission to end the antitrust case (e.g., removing territorial restrictions, allowing re-export of gas). The Commission makes these commitments legally binding.
- **2022** — Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine leads to a further acceleration of diversification, making the Third Energy Package’s non-discriminatory access provisions central to the EU’s REPowerEU plan.

## Impact and consequences

The Gas Directive fundamentally reshaped Europe’s gas market structure. By 2015, according to the Commission’s own market monitoring, more than 20 member states had adopted full ownership unbundling. Germany and France chose the ITO model, allowing E.ON and Engie (formerly GDF Suez) to keep their networks under strict regulatory oversight.

The most visible direct consequence was the frontal collision with Gazprom. The directive made Gazprom’s control of pipelines in Bulgaria (through the joint venture Bulgartransgaz) and Lithuania (through Lietuvos dujos) illegal. Lithuania, in particular, forced Gazprom to sell its stake in the country’s gas transmission operator by 2016. Bulgaria resisted, leading to an infringement action; in 2018 the Commission fined Bulgaria for failing to implement the directive’s provisions on the Balkan gas hub, though the fine was relatively modest (the Commission did not impose a specific fine but threatened daily penalties).

The 2018 Gazprom antitrust case commitments were a practical enforcement tool. Gazprom agreed to remove territorial restrictions in contracts with eight central and eastern European countries (Bulgaria, Czechia, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Slovakia) and to allow resale of gas across borders. The commitments did not fully unbundle Gazprom—the company still owns pipelines outside the EU, such as Nord Stream—but within the EU, the directive forced Gazprom to restructure its subsidiaries. By 2020, Gazprom had to create a separate “independent operator” for its share of the Opal pipeline (a Yamal-Europe link), though the process was slow and contested.

Smaller countries gained new leverage. The EU’s requirement for independent regulators, backed by ACER, reduced the influence of large producers. However, the ITO loophole allowed some incumbents to maintain significant control, and the effectiveness of unbundling depended on national enforcement. The European Energy Regulators (ACER) later noted in its 2021 annual report that “unbundling compliance varies significantly,” particularly regarding storage access and cross-border capacity allocation.

Overall, the Gas Directive did not break Gazprom’s market power overnight, but it created a legal framework where a single company could no longer control supply, pipelines, and storage in the EU simultaneously. The 2022 energy crisis tested whether that framework could withstand further geopolitical shocks—and the Commission used the same unbundling rules to fast-track joint gas purchasing and emergency redistribution mechanisms.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU force gas pipeline companies to separate from gas suppliers? 

The EU wanted to stop a single company from both selling gas and controlling the pipelines that other gas sellers need to use. If a company owns the pipes, it can block competitors, charge high access fees, or give its own gas priority—especially problematic when the supplier is a foreign state-owned giant like Gazprom. Separation makes the pipeline a neutral highway that any licensed supplier can use, fostering competition and reducing the risk of politically motivated supply cuts.

### How did Russia's Gazprom try to avoid the EU's unbundling rules? 

Gazprom fought the rules in several ways. First, it argued that ownership unbundling violated its pre-existing investment treaties and long-term contracts. Second, it lobbied key member states—especially Germany and France—to water down the requirement during legislative debates. Third, after the directive was passed, it restructured its EU subsidiaries into separate legal entities but kept indirect control through contractual arrangements. The European Commission’s 2015 antitrust case forced it to lift territorial restrictions on gas sales, but Gazprom never fully sold its pipeline stakes in the EU except where forced (e.g., Lithuania).

### Could the EU have secured its energy supply without unbundling? 

Proponents of the directive argued that unbundling was essential to enable the creation of a genuinely competitive internal market. Without it, the gas that reached Europe would have remained in the hands of a few dominant sellers who could price-discriminate across borders. Opponents, especially Germany and France, claimed that alternative models—such as strong regulatory oversight of vertically integrated companies—could have achieved the same result. The 2009 gas crisis convinced the majority that voluntary regulation was insufficient. However, even after unbundling, supply security in 2022 depended less on market structure and more on diversification, new pipelines (Southern Corridor, LNG terminals), and political coordination—showing that unbundling was a necessary but not sufficient condition.
