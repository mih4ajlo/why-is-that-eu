## What is it?

Directive 2001/18/EC governs the deliberate release into the environment of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) in the European Union. It covers both experimental releases (field trials) and the placing on the market of GMOs as products or in products — for example, GM seeds, food ingredients, or feed. The directive replaced the original 1990 GMO legislation (Directive 90/220/EEC), which had become politically untenable after a wave of public protests and a de facto EU-wide moratorium on new GMO approvals.

Under 2001/18/EC, any company seeking to market a GMO must first submit a dossier containing a full environmental risk assessment to the competent authority of the member state where the product will first be placed. That assessment is peer-reviewed by all other member states via the Standing Committee on the Food Chain and Animal Health; if no qualified majority for or against is reached, the European Commission makes a final decision. The directive also introduces mandatory labelling and traceability requirements — the first time EU law explicitly obligated producers to track GMOs through the entire production and supply chain. These rules were later supplemented by Regulation (EC) 1829/2003 (GM food and feed) and Regulation (EC) 1830/2003 (traceability and labelling).

## Why was it introduced?

The story of the 2001 directive cannot be told without the 1999 de facto moratorium. By June of that year, five member states — Austria, France, Greece, Italy, and Luxembourg — had blocked any new GMO approvals under the existing 1990 directive, citing consumer fears and inadequate safety assessments. The immediate trigger was a wave of public outrage in Europe against U.S. biotech giant Monsanto, which had begun marketing glyphosate-tolerant soybeans and Bt maize in the mid-1990s. In 1996, protesters in the UK and France blockaded ships carrying GM soy imports from the United States, and the tabloid press dubbed GM food “Frankenfood.” Eurobarometer surveys from 1999 showed that over 70% of EU citizens considered GM foods “dangerous” — a level of mistrust rivalled only by the BSE crisis.

But the deeper structural failure was in Directive 90/220/EEC itself. It had been drafted before the commercialisation of any GM crop, and its “simplified procedure” — meant to avoid duplicating scientific assessments across member states — collapsed under political pressure. In 1996, the Commission approved Monsanto’s Bt maize (MON810) and GM soybeans under the old rules. France promptly banned the maize anyway, citing new scientific doubts. The United States launched a formal WTO dispute in 2003 (EU – Approval and Marketing of Biotech Products), alleging that the EU’s block constituted an illegal trade barrier. The WTO panel ruling in 2006 found the de facto moratorium had indeed violated trade rules, but the EU had already replaced the legislative framework by then.

The political coalition that got 2001/18/EC through was fragile. The Environment Commissioner, Margot Wallström (Sweden), and the Agriculture Commissioner, Franz Fischler (Austria), worked together over two years to draft a compromise that would satisfy both the anti-GMO camp (led by France, Austria, and Italy) and the pro-trade camp (the UK, the Netherlands, and the biotech industry, represented by EuropaBio). The European Parliament also pushed hard for stronger labelling and traceability. The pivotal moment came in early 2000, when the Commission published a communication on the “precautionary principle,” essentially codifying the argument that member states could block GMO products on the grounds of scientific uncertainty even without unanimous evidence of harm. That principle was woven into Article 4 and Annex II of the directive, which require environmental risk assessment to consider “cumulative long-term effects” — a phrase industry fiercely opposed.

The directive was officially adopted by the Council on 12 March 2001, with the Parliament approving its amendments in February. The final text imposed a case-by-case assessment, compulsory monitoring plans, and a sunset clause of ten years for each authorisation. It also required member states to submit a report every three years on measures taken to avoid the presence of unauthorised GMOs — a provision that later became the legal basis for “national safeguard clauses” under which several member states banned MON810. The U.S. government and Monsanto had lobbied heavily against the traceability provisions, arguing they would cripple global supply chains. But the Commission’s impact assessment, published in 2000, concluded that the economic cost of traceability was manageable compared to the cost of losing consumer confidence altogether — a lesson drawn directly from the BSE scandal, where a decade of denial had destroyed the European beef industry.

## Timeline

- **1990** — Directive 90/220/EEC adopted, the first EU-wide framework for GMO releases.
- **1996** — Monsanto’s Roundup Ready soybean and Bt maize (MON810) approved under 90/220. French and UK protests escalate.
- **1998** — Austria, Luxembourg, and Greece invoke a safeguard clause to ban MON810. The Commission refers them to the European Court of Justice (later upholding their bans).
- **1999** — Five member states (Austria, France, Greece, Italy, Luxembourg) declare a de facto moratorium on new GMO approvals. The Commission vows to rewrite the rules.
- **2000** — Commission publishes a Communication on the Precautionary Principle. Legislative proposal for 2001/18/EC presented in July.
- **2001** — Directive 2001/18/EC adopted by the Council on 12 March, enters into force on 17 October.
- **2003** — Complementary Regulations 1829/2003 (GM food and feed) and 1830/2003 (traceability and labelling) adopted, strengthening the 2001 framework. The United States, Canada, and Argentina launch the WTO dispute.
- **2004** — Member states are required to transpose the directive into national law. Several miss the deadline; the Commission opens infringement proceedings against France, Greece, and the Netherlands.
- **2006** — WTO panel rules that the EU moratorium violated trade rules, but the ruling has limited practical effect because the new directive is already in force.
- **2007** — Commission authorises the first GM product (a pest-resistant maize from Syngenta) under the new regime, ending the de facto moratorium.
- **2015** — Directive (EU) 2015/412 amends 2001/18/EC to allow member states to “opt out” of GMO cultivation in their territory during the authorisation process.

## Impact and consequences

The directive restored legal certainty to the EU’s GMO approval system but did little to increase public acceptance. By 2023, only one GM crop — MON810 maize — was actually cultivated commercially in the EU, concentrated in Spain, Portugal, and a handful of other countries. According to the Commission’s 2021 report on GMO cultivation, the total area planted with GM maize in the EU was roughly 106,000 hectares, less than 2% of the EU’s total maize area. Nineteen member states have invoked the 2015 opt-out to ban all GM crops from their territory.

The traceability and labelling requirements had a profound effect on global supply chains. The U.S. Department of Agriculture estimated in 2005 that the cost of segregating GM from non-GM shipments added 6–15% to the price of exported grain. Several European retailers, including Carrefour and Tesco, adopted “GM-free” private labels, forcing suppliers to guarantee zero tolerance for unauthorised events. The directive also became the template for GMO regulations in other jurisdictions, including Brazil and South Korea.

Legal challenges have been frequent. The European Court of Justice has repeatedly upheld member state safeguard clauses against industry lawsuits, most famously in the 2017 *Confédération paysanne* case, which ruled that organisms obtained by mutagenesis (new genetic techniques) can also fall under the directive. The French consumer association UFC-Que Choisir estimated in 2021 that the cost of GMO testing and compliance for European food companies was between €60 million and €100 million per year, based on Commission data.

## Key questions answered

### Can GM crops be grown freely in the EU?

No. Even after a GM crop is authorised under Directive 2001/18/EC, each member state can restrict or ban its cultivation on its own territory. Nineteen EU countries have done so since an opt-out rule was introduced in 2015. As of 2024, only Spain, Portugal, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic still plant GM maize, and only one approved GM crop — MON810 — is grown at all.

### Why did the EU take so long to regulate GMOs?

The original 1990 directive was drafted before any commercial GM products existed, and it relied on a single-country “lead authority” system that broke down when France refused to accept the approvals granted to the UK. Public panic over “Frankenfood” in the late 1990s — amplified by the BSE crisis — pushed the Commission to rewrite the law from scratch. The legislative process took five years because pro-industry member states (UK, Netherlands) opposed traceability requirements, while anti-GMO states (Austria, France) demanded a full-ban option. The compromise — case-by-case risk assessments plus a strong precautionary principle — satisfied neither side entirely.

### What traceability rules apply to GM food?

Any operator that places a GMO or GM-derived product on the EU market must transmit information about its genetic modification through every step of the supply chain. Labelling must include the unique identifier code for each GMO event. Records must be kept for five years, and the EU maintains a public register of all authorised GMOs. The rules are so strict that even a small amount of unauthorised GM material (above the 0.9% labelling threshold) triggers a mandatory recall and removal from the market.
