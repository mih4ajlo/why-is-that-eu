## What is it?

The Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) is a comprehensive EU law that overhauls the bloc's approach to packaging waste. It sets binding reuse targets for specific sectors (e.g., 20% of beverage packaging must be reusable by 2030), bans certain single-use packaging formats (like tiny shampoo bottles in hotels and shrink-wrap on suitcases at airports), and requires all packaging placed on the EU market to be recyclable by 2030. The regulation also mandates a 15% reduction in packaging waste per capita by 2040 compared to 2018 levels.

The PPWR replaces the 1994 Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive (94/62/EC), which set recycling targets but allowed member states wide discretion. The new regulation — directly binding in all 27 member states — reflects a shift from voluntary industry action to mandatory obligations, driven by the failure of earlier policies to decouple packaging waste growth from economic expansion.

## Why was it introduced?

The PPWR's origins lie in a fundamental structural failure: between 2010 and 2020, EU packaging waste grew by 24%, reaching 84 million tonnes in 2021 according to Eurostat, while the 1994 directive's recycling targets were met largely through paper and cardboard, with plastic packaging recycling rates stuck around 40%. The system had created perverse incentives — lightweight, multi-material packaging that was technically recyclable but economically unfeasible to actually collect and process.

The immediate political trigger was the release of the European Commission's 2018 Circular Economy Action Plan, which identified packaging as a priority sector. But the decisive moment came in 2020, when a leaked internal Commission study showed that voluntary industry commitments under the 1994 directive had failed utterly: packaging waste was projected to grow another 19% by 2030 under business-as-usual. This data, reported by Euractiv in September 2020, broke a decade-long deadlock between industry lobbies and environmental groups.

The coalition that passed the regulation was unusual: environmentalist MEPs (Greens/EFA and parts of S&D) aligned with the European Parliament's centre-right EPP group on the packaging minimisation and reuse targets, while the hard-right and liberal Renew groups opposed the bans on specific formats. The crucial vote in the Parliament's ENVI committee in October 2023 saw 43 MEPs in favour, 27 against — a wider margin than expected, after the EPP surprised observers by supporting the mandatory reuse targets rather than industry-preferred "recycling equivalence" alternative.

European packaging industry associations — notably Europen, representing 45 companies including Nestlé, Unilever, and Coca-Cola — had lobbied heavily against mandatory reuse targets. Their 2022 impact assessment claimed up to 130,000 job losses would result from reuse requirements in the beverage sector. However, the Commission's own 2022 impact assessment found that reuse systems could create 60,000 new jobs in sorting, cleaning, and logistics, while reducing packaging material production by 15-20%. The Commission's analysis, led by Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius (2019-2024), directly contradicted industry claims by showing that reuse systems already operated profitably in Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands for beverage containers.

The timing was also shaped by a 2022 ruling from the European Court of Justice (Case C-34/21), which found that the 1994 directive allowed member states to ban lightweight plastic carrier bags even when those bans were more restrictive than the directive's minimum harmonisation. This judicial green light emboldened the Commission to propose outright bans on certain packaging formats rather than just recycling targets.

The final regulation, adopted in February 2025 after trilogue negotiations that stretched from November 2023 to December 2024, included a controversial compromise: member states can apply for derogations from reuse targets if they demonstrate that their existing recycling infrastructure achieves higher environmental performance. This clause, inserted at the insistence of Finland and Sweden, was seen as a direct response to their existing deposit-return systems that already recycle over 85% of beverage bottles — far above the PPWR's 2030 reuse target of 20%.

## Timeline

- **1994** — Adoption of Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive (94/62/EC), setting first EU recycling targets for packaging
- **2018** — European Commission publishes Circular Economy Action Plan, announcing intention to revise packaging rules
- **2020** — Leaked Commission study shows packaging waste set to grow 19% by 2030 without new regulation; Commission launches public consultation
- **2022-03** — European Court of Justice ruling (C-34/21) confirms member states can ban single-use plastic bags beyond minimum EU requirements
- **2022-11** — Commission publishes formal proposal for a Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation
- **2023-10** — European Parliament ENVI committee votes 43-27 in favour of mandatory reuse targets
- **2024-04** — European Parliament adopts its negotiating position with 426 votes in favour, 125 against
- **2024-12** — Council and Parliament reach political agreement after 14 months of trilogue negotiations
- **2025-02** — Regulation officially published in the Official Journal of the European Union as 2025/40/EU
- **2026-02** — Regulation enters into force; member states must transpose provisions into national law
- **2028** — First compliance deadline: all packaging must be "recyclable at scale" under delegated acts
- **2030** — Mandatory reuse targets take effect: 20% for beverage packaging, 10% for transport packaging
- **2040** — Target for 15% per capita reduction in packaging waste compared to 2018 levels

## Impact and consequences

The PPWR's most immediate effect is the disruption of the European packaging industry. Germany's largest packaging producer, Progroup, announced in March 2025 that it would restructure its production lines to phase out multi-material laminates, which the regulation's "recyclable by design" requirements effectively ban. In France, hotel industry association UMIH estimated that the ban on individual toiletries packaging would require replacing 250 million single-use bottles annually across 18,000 hotels.

The regulation's reuse targets have already sparked investment: Coca-Cola Europacific Partners announced a €50 million investment in reusable glass bottle washing facilities in the Netherlands in January 2025. Conversely, Danone warned it might cease operations in certain member states that opt for stricter reuse targets without derogations.

The most contentious implementation issue is the "recyclability at scale" standard, which the Commission must define by delegated acts. Environmental groups, including the European Environmental Bureau, have argued that the current draft allows chemical recycling to count as "recyclable", a position the plastics industry (represented by PlasticsEurope) has fiercely lobbied for.

Countries most affected are those with low reuse infrastructure: Poland, Bulgaria, and Romania, where deposit-return systems for beverage containers were largely absent before 2023 and will need €1-2 billion in investment according to the European Investment Bank's 2024 assessment. Conversely, Germany, Denmark, and Norway already have reuse systems that meet or exceed the 2030 targets, giving them a competitive advantage.

The regulation's enforcement mechanism gives the Commission power to impose fines of up to 4% of annual turnover in the EU for companies found in systematic non-compliance — a provision targeting multinationals that previously exploited fragmentation in national enforcement.

## Key questions answered

### Why does EU law now ban tiny shampoo bottles in hotels?

The regulation bans single-use plastic packaging for toiletries in hotels and similar accommodations (under 50ml for liquids, 100g for solids). This is because such miniature packaging has a disproportionately high environmental footprint per unit of product — the packaging-to-content ratio is often 80% packaging to 20% product — and because reusable dispensers already exist as a technically and economically proven alternative. The ban covers an estimated 200 million such bottles placed on the EU market annually, according to the Commission's impact assessment.

### Does this regulation force all EU businesses to accept reusable packaging?

No. The PPWR mandates reuse targets only for specific sectors where reuse systems are already demonstrated to work: beverage containers (20% by 2030), transport packaging like pallets and crates (10%), and grouped packaging like shrink-wrap on multi-packs (10%). For most other packaging types, including food packaging and consumer goods, the regulation instead requires that packaging be "recyclable by design" — meaning it must be technically capable of being recycled, not that it must be reused. Businesses in the affected sectors are not forced to accept reusable packaging from others, but must offer customers the option of refillable containers if they sell beverages above the 2030 threshold.

### What happens to a company that violates these packaging rules?

The regulation empowers national authorities to impose fines of up to 4% of a company's annual EU turnover for systematic non-compliance — targeting repeated violations like using banned single-use formats or placing non-recyclable packaging on the market after 2030. For less serious first-time offences, member states must set maximum fines of at least €1,000 per violation, with the revenue going toward waste prevention programmes. The Commission also gains the power to temporarily ban non-compliant packaging types across the entire single market through emergency procedures — a power that, if used, would directly affect companies like Amazon, which ships over 6 billion packages annually within the EU.

### Why did this take 30 years to update the original 1994 packaging law?

The original 1994 directive was a product of its time — focused on recycling as a harmonisation measure for the single market, not waste prevention. Three factors blocked revision for three decades: first, the packaging industry's sustained lobbying against any mandatory reduction targets (Europen spent an estimated €8 million annually on Brussels representation); second, the diversity of member state systems (Germany's Green Dot system versus France's eco-modulation versus Eastern Europe's lack of infrastructure) made harmonisation politically fraught; and third, each Commission president from 1994 to 2022 prioritised other environmental files (climate, air quality, chemicals) over packaging waste. The breakthrough came only when the 2018 Circular Economy Action Plan, combined with data showing packaging waste rising despite all previous efforts, created an evidence base strong enough to overcome industry resistance — and when Virginijus Sinkevičius as Environment Commissioner made it his personal priority starting in 2022.
