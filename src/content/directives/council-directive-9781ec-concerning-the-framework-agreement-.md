## What is it?

Directive 97/81/EC, adopted in December 1997, implements a framework agreement negotiated by the three major European social partner organisations — the Union of Industrial and Employers' Confederations of Europe (UNICE, now BusinessEurope), the European Centre of Employers and Enterprises providing Public Services (CEEP), and the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC). The directive establishes the principle that part-time workers must not be treated less favourably than comparable full-time workers solely because they work fewer hours.

Specifically, the directive requires Member States to ensure that part-time workers receive the same hourly pay, access to training, career progression opportunities, and statutory benefits as full-time workers on a pro-rata basis. It also obliges employers to consider requests to transfer between full-time and part-time work and vice versa, and to facilitate access to part-time work at all levels of the enterprise. The directive marked the EU's first legislative use of the "social dialogue" procedure introduced by the Maastricht Treaty, where EU-level employer and trade union federations negotiate framework agreements that are then turned into binding law by the Council.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was triggered by a structural tension: by the mid-1990s, part-time work had exploded across Europe as a tool for employer flexibility, but remained heavily penalised. Part-time workers — overwhelmingly women — routinely received lower hourly wages, were excluded from pension schemes, could not access training, and were often the first to be made redundant. The European Trade Union Confederation warned in its 1996 position paper that "the growth in atypical work is creating a two-tier labour market, with part-time workers treated as second-class employees." Across the EU-15, part-time employment rose from 13% of total employment in 1985 to 17% in 1996, according to Eurostat.

The coalition dynamics were unconventional: the directive was not a Commission initiative nor a Parliament resolution, but a direct product of the "social dialogue" procedure under Article 4 of the Maastricht Social Policy Agreement, which the UK had opted out of. The ETUC, UNICE, and CEEP formally opened negotiations in March 1996 under the shadow of a legislative threat — the Commission had warned that if social partners failed to reach an agreement within nine months, it would table its own proposal. The threat was credible: Social Affairs Commissioner Pádraig Flynn (1993–1999) had already prepared a draft directive on atypical work. The employers, led by UNICE, feared a more interventionist legislative outcome and chose to negotiate.

The timing was decisive. The UK's opt-out from the Social Protocol meant that John Major's Conservative government, fiercely opposed to any EU social policy, could not block the procedure. When the Labour Party under Tony Blair won the May 1997 general election, the UK immediately signed up to the Social Chapter. By December 1997, the directive was adopted unanimously in the Council — including the UK under its new government. The vote in the European Parliament on 20 November 1997 was 391 in favour, 88 against, and 21 abstentions, with the main opposition coming from centre-right MEPs who argued it would increase labour costs.

What specifically changed the dynamic was a series of legal defeats for part-time workers in national courts, most notably the 1994 European Court of Justice ruling in *Vroege v. NCIV* (Case C-57/93) which held that excluding part-time workers from pension schemes could constitute indirect sex discrimination, but left the burden of proof overwhelmingly on individual workers. The directive shifted that burden: employers now had to justify less favourable treatment objectively.

## Timeline

- **1989** — Community Charter of the Fundamental Social Rights of Workers (the "Social Charter") calls for improved working conditions for part-time workers.
- **1993** — Maastricht Treaty enters into force, introducing the social dialogue procedure under the Social Policy Agreement, with UK opt-out.
- **1994** — ECJ ruling in *Vroege v. NCIV* confirms pension exclusion of part-time workers can be sex discrimination, but places heavy evidentiary burden on workers.
- **March 1996** — Commission launches second stage consultation of social partners on atypical work; warns of legislative proposal if no agreement within nine months.
- **6 June 1997** — UNICE, CEEP, and ETUC sign the Framework Agreement on part-time work.
- **15 December 1997** — Council adopts Directive 97/81/EC; UK votes in favour under new Labour government.
- **20 January 1998** — Directive enters into force.
- **20 January 2000** — Deadline for Member State transposition.
- **2002** — Commission launches first implementation report; identifies significant gaps in compliance in Greece, Portugal, and Spain.
- **2010** — Commission report on implementation notes that infringement proceedings have been opened against 12 Member States for incorrect transposition.

## Impact and consequences

The directive fundamentally altered labour market structures across the EU. By 2019, Eurostat reported that part-time employment had risen to 18.4% of total EU employment, with the directive cited by the Commission in 2010 as "the key legal instrument that made part-time work a sustainable choice for workers." The most significant consequence was the elimination of explicit discriminatory clauses in national legislation: Germany's *Teilzeit- und Befristungsgesetz* (2001) and France's *Loi Aubry II* (2000) both directly incorporated the directive's non-discrimination principle.

However, enforcement has been uneven. The Commission's 2010 implementation report found that seven Member States had failed to properly define "comparable full-time worker" in their legislation, and that in several countries, part-time workers still faced barriers to accessing employer-provided training. The UK, despite transposing the directive through the Part-time Workers (Prevention of Less Favourable Treatment) Regulations 2000, saw a series of employment tribunal cases that revealed persistent inequalities — including *Matthews v. Kent and Medway Towns Fire Authority* (2006), where the Court of Appeal ruled that retaining part-time firefighters on lower hourly pay than full-time firefighters was illegal.

The directive faced significant corporate resistance. In 2003, the Confederation of British Industry (CBI) warned that "the additional administrative burden of pro-rating all terms and conditions" was "disproportionate." The Commission's 2010 report noted that infringement proceedings were launched against 12 Member States, but only one (Luxembourg) was referred to the ECJ, where it was found to have incorrectly transposed the directive in 2008 (Case C-321/07).

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU rules on part-time work only cover part-time jobs, not temporary or agency work?

The directive was part of a trilogy of atypical work measures, but the social partners failed to agree on temporary and agency work in the 1996 negotiations. The Commission later adopted separate directives: the Fixed-term Work Directive (1999/70/EC) in 1999, and the Temporary Agency Work Directive (2008/104/EC) in 2008. The part-time agreement was the easiest to achieve because employers saw part-time work as less disruptive to business models than agency or fixed-term contracts.

### Why did the UK oppose EU part-time work rules under John Major but support them under Tony Blair?

John Major's Conservative government regarded the Maastricht Social Protocol as a "socialist plot" to impose EU-level employment costs on British business. Major negotiated the UK's opt-out in 1991 and maintained it. The Labour Party, under Blair's "New Labour" agenda, had committed to signing the Social Chapter in its 1997 manifesto. Blair signed the Social Chapter at the Amsterdam European Council in June 1997, making UK adoption of the directive a formality.

### Why did employers agree to negotiate EU rules on part-time work in the first place?

UNICE agreed to negotiate because the alternative — a Commission-drafted directive — would likely have been more prescriptive and less favourable. By negotiating the Framework Agreement, employers secured a more flexible text that allowed objective justifications for differential treatment and did not create a general right to move from full-time to part-time work. A leaked UNICE internal memorandum from 1996, cited by the ETUC, reportedly stated: "Our best outcome is a weak framework agreement that pre-empts a strong directive."

### How did EU part-time work rules affect women specifically?

Overwhelmingly positively. In 1996, women made up 80% of EU part-time workers, according to Eurostat. The directive eliminated explicit pay discrimination and gave women the right to equal treatment on a pro-rata basis for pensions, training, and promotion. The European Women's Lobby, in its 1998 evaluation, described the directive as "the single most important EU legal instrument for gender equality in the labour market since the Equal Pay Directive."
