## What is it?

The Single Permit Directive recast (2024/3227/EU) is an EU law that replaces the original 2011 Single Permit Directive (2011/98/EU). It creates a single administrative procedure for non-EU nationals (third-country nationals) to apply for both work and residence rights in an EU Member State. Once issued, the "single permit" entitles the holder to live and work in that country for the duration of the permit, which can be up to two years (renewable).

The recast tightens deadlines: Member States must process applications within 90 days (down from four months in the 2011 directive) and can no longer require applicants to obtain a separate work visa before starting the procedure. It also strengthens equal-treatment rights, explicitly covering accident benefits, recognition of diplomas, and social security coordination. The directive applies to most third-country workers, excluding posted workers, long-term residents (covered by a separate directive), and a few other categories.

## Why was it introduced?

The recast was driven by the glaring failure of the 2011 directive to deliver a genuinely seamless system. By 2022, only 8 of the 25 Member States bound by the original directive had transposed it without infringements, and the European Commission’s 2019 evaluation found that average processing times exceeded five months in several countries. The core structural tension was clear: the 2011 directive allowed Member States to keep national procedures for work permits before the single-permit application could even start, creating a two-step maze that employers and migrants alike found unworkable.

Coalition dynamics shifted dramatically after the 2015 migration crisis and the subsequent labour shortages exposed by the COVID-19 pandemic. The post-pandemic recovery, combined with demographic decline, meant that by 2021 the Commission was warning that “legal migration channels remain insufficient” (Commission Communication on a New Pact on Migration and Asylum, 2020). The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Javier Moreno Sánchez (S&D, Spain), pushed hard for a recast that would make the single permit the default route and embed stronger worker protections. The biggest opposition came from conservative governments – Austria, Hungary, Poland – that feared losing national control over labour migration quotas and wanted to keep separate work-permit stages. They nearly blocked the proposal in the Council in late 2022, but a compromise brokered by the Czech Presidency allowed Member States to retain the right to set quotas for the number of permits issued – a concession that saved the deal. The Parliament approved the final text on 13 December 2023 with 452 votes in favour, 107 against, and 21 abstentions.

Timing was everything. The recast succeeded under the von der Leyen Commission because of three confluent pressures: (1) the 2020 New Pact on Migration and Asylum, which committed the Commission to overhaul legal migration rules; (2) acute labour shortages – by 2023, Eurostat reported 2.9 million unfilled jobs across the EU, pushing even traditionally restrictive Member States like Germany to plead for faster legal migration; and (3) a 2022 European Court of Justice ruling (C-94/20, *Országos Idegenrendészeti Főigazgatóság*), which clarified that national procedures cannot require a work authorisation separate from the single permit. That ruling collapsed the legal foundation for the two-step systems that countries like Hungary and Austria maintained.

What ultimately changed the dynamic was the Commission’s willingness to pair the recast with parallel files that were even more controversial – the Blue Card reform and the Seasonal Workers Directive revision – making the single permit a relatively low-cost part of a larger package. The 2023 Swedish Presidency gave the Council the final push, and the directive was adopted on 22 April 2024.

## Timeline

- **2011** — Original Single Permit Directive (2011/98/EU) adopted, establishing first EU framework for combined work-residence permits.
- **2019** — Commission evaluation (COM(2019) 161 final) finds low awareness, slow procedures, and wide variation in Member State implementation; only 8 of 25 binding Member States have correctly transposed all provisions.
- **2020** — New Pact on Migration and Asylum announced; Commission pledges to recast legal migration directives.
- **2022** — ECJ ruling C-94/20 (*Országos Idegenrendészeti Főigazgatóság*) prohibits national requirements for separate work permits before single-permit procedure. Commission proposal for recast published on 27 April.
- **2023** — European Parliament approves text on 13 December (452-107-21). Council does the same on 22 April 2024.
- **2024** — Directive published in the Official Journal on 30 April; Member States have two years to transpose (deadline: 21 May 2026).

## Impact and consequences

The recast directly affects an estimated 2.5 million third-country workers who hold single permits (based on Eurostat 2021 figures – the highest number in any given year before the COVID dip). By cutting maximum processing time from four months to 90 days, the directive is expected to reduce the average wait for a permit by roughly 25% across the EU, according to the Commission’s 2023 impact assessment (SWD(2022) 650). Employers in sectors like construction, hospitality, and IT – which together accounted for over half of all single-permit applications in 2022 – are likely to benefit most from the faster, clearer process.

Member States that previously operated two-step systems – notably Austria, Hungary, and Poland – must now eliminate those separate work-authorisation stages. The Commission estimated in its impact assessment that this could cut administrative costs for businesses by up to €300 million annually across the EU. However, the directive does allow Member States to set numerical caps on permits, a provision that may limit the practical impact in countries with restrictive labour migration policies.

The strengthened equal-treatment provisions mean that workers can now claim accident benefits and have their vocational qualifications recognised in line with EU-level rules – a change that labour unions (ETUC) and migrant rights NGOs (PICUM) have long demanded. The European Commission will monitor transposition closely: the 2021 infringement rate for the original directive was among the highest in EU migration law.

## Key questions answered

### Do non-EU workers need separate permission to work and to live in an EU country?

Under the recast directive, no. A single application process yields one permit covering both work and residence. Member States cannot require a separate work authorisation before the permit application starts, as the 2022 ECJ ruling confirmed. The procedure must be initiated and completed within 90 days.

### What rights does a single-permit holder get?

The holder enjoys equal treatment with EU nationals in many areas: working conditions (pay, dismissal, health and safety), social security (including accident benefits), access to goods and services, and recognition of diplomas. The recast explicitly added accident benefits and qualification recognition – gaps in the 2011 directive. However, equal treatment does not cover housing assistance or unemployment benefits for all categories.

### Can an EU country simply refuse all applicants from certain countries?

No, but Member States can set overall quotas on the number of permits issued per year or per sector, as long as those quotas do not discriminate based on nationality (unless EU free-trade agreements with specific countries require preferential treatment). This compromise – included at the insistence of Austria and Hungary – means total numbers can still be capped, but the procedure must be single and non-discriminatory.

### Why did the European Union update this directive now?

Three reasons converged: acute labour shortages (nearly 3 million unfilled jobs by 2023), a 2020 political commitment to reform “legal migration channels” in the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, and a 2022 ECJ ruling that struck down the two-step procedures used by several countries. The recast was also a relatively low-controversy part of a larger migration package – the Blue Card and Seasonal Workers revisions were more contentious – making it easier to pass under the Swedish Presidency in 2023.
