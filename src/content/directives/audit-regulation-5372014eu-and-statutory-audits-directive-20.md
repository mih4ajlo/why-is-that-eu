## What is it?

The EU audit reform consists of two pieces of legislation: Directive 2014/56/EU (amending the earlier Eighth Company Law Directive 2006/43/EC on statutory audits) and Regulation 537/2014/EU, which sets specific requirements for audits of “public-interest entities” (PIEs) — mainly listed companies, banks, and insurers. Together they overhauled the way audits are conducted, governed, and overseen across the European Union.

Central to the reform are mandatory audit firm rotation: PIEs must change their external auditor every ten years (with a possible extension to twenty years if a public tender is held); a cap on fees from non-audit services (limited to 70% of the average audit fees over three consecutive years); a blacklist of prohibited non-audit services (such as tax advisory, legal services, and internal audit outsourcing); and strengthened oversight through the creation of the Committee of European Auditing Oversight Bodies (CEAOB), which coordinates national audit regulators.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform was triggered by two waves of scandals that exposed fundamental weaknesses in audit independence. The first was the collapse of the Italian dairy giant Parmalat in 2003, which revealed a €14 billion hole in its accounts. Parmalat’s auditors — first Grant Thornton, later Deloitte — had failed to challenge the company’s fraudulent practices. The scandal echoed the earlier Enron/Arthur Andersen collapse in the United States, but the EU’s initial response was limited: it updated the Eighth Company Law Directive in 2006, requiring audit committees for PIEs and strengthening oversight, but left the Big Four’s (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) market dominance and the core conflict-of-interest problem untouched.

The second wave came with the 2007-2008 financial crisis. The European Commission’s 2010 Green Paper “Audit Policy: Lessons from the Crisis” noted that auditors had failed to warn investors about the fragility of banks such as Northern Rock and Fortis. More structurally, it highlighted that the Big Four audited more than 85% of Europe’s listed companies, creating a market where companies feared challenging their auditor and where audit firms had little incentive to split audit from lucrative consulting work. The Green Paper warned of “systemic risk” from the concentration.

Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier (2010–2014) made audit reform a priority. In November 2011, the Commission proposed a full legislative package. The proposal was deliberately tough: a mandatory rotation period of six years (later extended to ten), a ban on all non-audit services for audit clients, and a cap on market share to force the break-up of the Big Four. The Big Four fought back fiercely, mobilising clients and business organisations such as BusinessEurope and the European Banking Federation. The UK government, worried about competitiveness, opposed mandatory rotation altogether.

The package went through three years of negotiation. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, the German MEP Klaus-Heiner Lehne (EPP), sought a balance. The final compromise, adopted in April 2014, was significantly watered down: the rotation period was extended; the prohibition of non-audit services became a blacklist plus a fee cap; and the market share cap was dropped entirely. Even so, the reform was the most significant shake-up of the audit profession in the EU’s history. The Commission’s 2011 impact assessment had estimated that without action, the Big Four’s market share would continue to rise, further reducing choice and independence.

## Timeline

- **2003** — Parmalat collapses after a multibillion-euro fraud. Auditors are criticised for failing to detect the scheme.
- **2006** — The EU adopts Directive 2006/43/EC on statutory audits, introducing audit committees and public oversight bodies but leaving market structure unchanged.
- **2007‑2008** — The global financial crisis reveals widespread audit failures in the banking sector.
- **2010** — The European Commission publishes its Green Paper “Audit Policy: Lessons from the Crisis”, opening a public consultation.
- **Nov 2011** — Commission proposes the audit reform package (directive and regulation), including mandatory rotation, non-audit service ban, and market share cap.
- **2012‑2013** — European Parliament and Council debate the proposals. Lobbying intensifies. The UK, the Netherlands, and some Nordic member states push back.
- **3 Apr 2014** — European Parliament adopts the final compromise (text approved by 539 votes to 83).
- **16 Apr 2014** — Council adopts the package.
- **17 Jun 2016** — Most provisions apply for PIEs (listed companies, banks, insurers). Member states transpose Directive 2014/56/EU into national law.
- **2020** — First mandatory rotation deadlines for early-cycle PIEs (ten years after entry into force).
- **2022** — European Commission publishes a review report on the functioning of the regulation.

## Impact and consequences

The reform forced the Big Four to restructure their European operations to separate audit from consulting. For example, Deloitte and PwC sold or spun off parts of their advisory practices in several member states. However, the compromise meant that many large companies could delay rotation for up to twenty years by holding a tender, reducing the immediate effect.

The European Commission’s 2022 review report noted that while mandatory rotation was taking place — with around 30% of PIEs in some member states having switched auditor since 2016 — the Big Four’s market share remained above 80% for the largest listed companies. Mid‑tier firms such as BDO and Grant Thornton gained some ground, but the market concentration did not dramatically shift. The Commission also found that the fee cap on non‑audit services had cut revenues for audit firms from consulting, lowering the risk of independence impairment.

National regulators, coordinated through CEAOB, strengthened inspections. Several member states, including Germany and Italy, introduced rotation periods shorter than the EU maximum. A 2019 study by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) found that audit quality had improved in terms of number of deficiencies identified, but warned that the cost of compliance had increased for smaller PIEs.

Opposition from the industry continued: BusinessEurope argued in a 2021 position paper that the rules were overly prescriptive and reduced choice, but the Commission’s review concluded that the overall objectives remained valid and no fundamental revision was needed.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU force large companies to change their auditor every ten years?

Mandatory rotation breaks long-term relationships between a company and its audit firm, which can lead to “audit fatigue” where the auditor becomes too familiar with management and loses objectivity. The Parmalat and financial crisis scandals showed that long tenure contributed to auditors failing to challenge management’s figures. Rotation forces a fresh look and reduces incentives for auditors to suppress criticism in order to retain the contract.

### Do the audit rules apply to all companies in the EU?

No — they apply only to “public-interest entities” (PIEs): companies listed on stock exchanges, banks, insurance undertakings, and other entities designated by member states. Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) that are not listed are covered by the general statutory audit directive (2006/43/EC) with lighter requirements. The reform deliberately targeted the largest and most systemically important audits.

### What services are auditors banned from providing to the same client?

The regulation bans a blacklist of non-audit services that create a direct conflict of interest: tax advisory services that depend on the audit judgment, legal services, internal audit outsourcing, and services related to the client’s financial reporting systems. Beyond that, the fee from any permitted non-audit services cannot exceed 70% of the average of the last three years’ audit fees, calculated as a rolling average.

### Did the reform break the dominance of the Big Four?

Not decisively. The European Commission’s 2022 review acknowledged that market concentration remained high, with the Big Four auditing the vast majority of large listed companies. The originally proposed market share cap — which would have forced mandatory sharing of audit work — was dropped from the final text. However, the reform opened some door for mid‑tier firms; several countries reported an increase in the number of public-interest entities audited by second‑tier networks such as BDO and Grant Thornton.
