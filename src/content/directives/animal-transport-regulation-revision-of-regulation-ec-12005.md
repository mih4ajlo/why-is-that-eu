## What is it?

Regulation EC 1/2005 sets minimum standards for the commercial transport of live vertebrate animals within the EU. It covers journeys over 50 km, specifying maximum journey times, space allowances, temperature controls, feeding/watering intervals, and vehicle standards (ventilation, partitions, navigation systems). It applies to cattle, pigs, sheep, goats, horses, poultry, and fish transported by road, sea, rail, or air.

The regulation requires that any transporter holds a valid authorisation from their Member State, that drivers and attendants complete certified training, and that journeys over 8 hours (or 12 hours for horses) must have detailed transport logs. For long journeys (over 8 hours for most mammals), vehicles must meet stricter standards — including bedding, onboard watering systems, and GPS tracking — and must stop at designated control posts en route. In 2023 the Commission proposed a significant revision to tighten rules, reduce maximum journey times to 12 hours for most slaughter animals, and ban certain live exports from the EU.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a series of animal welfare scandals during live transport in the late 1990s and early 2000s that became politically impossible to ignore. In 1999, Belgian television broadcast footage of cattle being transported from Ireland to Lebanon in conditions described by veterinary inspectors as "systemic cruelty" — animals dying from dehydration, heat stress, and injuries sustained during rough handling on ships. The European Commission's Food and Veterinary Office (FVO, now DG SANTE) published a series of damning reports between 2000 and 2002 documenting frequent violations across Member States: animals transported for over 24 hours without water, calves shipped from Eastern Europe to southern Italy in winter with no heating, and sheep dying in transit from Spain to the Middle East.

The specific trigger for the 2004 proposal was a 2002 report by the European Parliament's Temporary Committee on Foot-and-Mouth Disease (established after the devastating 2001 UK outbreak). The committee found that live animal transport had spread the disease across the continent — infected sheep from the UK had been transported through France, Germany, and the Netherlands before symptoms appeared — and that existing welfare rules (under the 1991 Directive 91/628/EEC) were systematically violated with no effective enforcement. Then-Commissioner for Health and Consumer Protection David Byrne (Ireland, 1999-2004) made animal transport reform a personal priority, stating in a 2003 speech that "the bottom line is clear: if you cannot transport animals humanely, you should not transport them at all."

The political coalition that passed the regulation was unusual: it united animal welfare campaigners (Eurogroup for Animals, Compassion in World Farming) with meat industry actors who complained that bad practices undermined consumer trust in the entire meat sector, and with southern European Member States (Greece, Italy, Spain) whose smaller-scale livestock sectors were being undercut by cheaper, mass-transported animals from northern Europe. The main opposition came from the Netherlands, Denmark, and Poland — major livestock exporters who argued that shorter journey times would destroy their export business to southern Europe and the Middle East. These countries commissioned a 2003 study by the Dutch Animal Sciences Group claiming that reducing maximum journey times from 24 to 12 hours would cost the Dutch veal industry €45 million annually and risk 3,000 jobs.

The final deal was brokered in late 2004 under the Dutch EU Presidency — an ironic twist, as the Netherlands was the most vocal opponent. A compromise was struck: the original Commission proposal for an 8-hour maximum was dropped to 12 hours for slaughter animals, with exceptions allowing up to 24 hours for species like sheep and goats under certain conditions. The Parliament backed the regulation by 478 votes to 90 in January 2004, with the Council adopting it in December. It entered force in January 2007, after an 18-month transition period for Member States to upgrade infrastructure (control posts, vehicle certification systems).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation created enforceable minimum standards but has struggled with enforcement. The Commission's 2011 enforcement report found that over 41% of long-distance journeys inspected had welfare violations — most commonly overcrowding, missing watering systems, and falsified transport logs. Member States varied enormously: Germany issued over 1,750 fines for transport violations in 2019, while Malta and Cyprus issued fewer than 10 each despite similar volumes of trade.

The economic impact was concentrated. Dutch and Danish livestock exporters lost significant long-distance trade to the Middle East, with Dutch live sheep exports to non-EU countries falling by 62% between 2005-2010. The Polish meat industry, however, adapted more successfully by investing in closer slaughterhouses and refrigerated meat transport instead of live animals — Polish beef exports to Germany actually rose 35% in the first three years of the regulation as processors moved to higher-value chilled meat rather than live cattle.

The requirement for vehicles to have GPS tracking systems — a world first — was hailed by regulators but proved difficult for small-scale transporters. In 2019, the European Court of Auditors reported that only 12 of 27 Member States had fully functioning vehicle navigation systems capable of generating the required data, and that many transport logs were handwritten and illegible.

The Commission's 2023 revision proposal reflects the limits of the original regulation. The 2005 framework's central problem — that Member States enforce their own rules along a single market with highly mobile animals and few border checks — remains unresolved. The proposed shift to digital tracking and harmonised enforcement powers represents an attempt to solve the structural tension that animal welfare advocates say has always existed: between national sovereignty over enforcement and the reality of a continental live transport system that crosses borders daily.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU limit how long animals can be transported?

Yes. Since 2007, the maximum journey time for most farm animals (cattle, pigs, sheep, goats) is 12 hours under EU law. After 12 hours, animals must be unloaded, fed, watered and rested for at least 24 hours before continuing. Horses face an 8-hour limit. The proposed 2023 revision would cut the slaughter-bound limit further to 12 hours with no extension. However exemptions exist: journeys under 50 km or within a holding are not covered.

### Why do animal welfare groups say the rules aren't working?

Because enforcement is weak and uneven across member states. The 2019 European Court of Auditors report found that few countries check vehicles adequately at borders, that GPS tracking data is rarely used in court, and that falsified transport logs are common. The Commission admitted in its 2023 impact assessment that the current system depends on "self-certification" by transporters — with limited oversight for domestic journeys. The biggest gap: once an animal leaves its home country, no single authority is responsible for monitoring its welfare across borders.

### What does the 2023 revision change?

The Commission's proposal introduces four major changes: a maximum journey time of 12 hours for all animals destined for slaughter (currently possible exceptions for longer trips); a ban on exporting live animals to countries outside the OECD (which have weaker welfare standards); mandatory digital GPS tracking and real-time data sharing with authorities; and stricter rules for temperature control during summer. The revision also would require all countries to establish designated control posts along major transport routes.

### Which EU countries are most affected by the rules?

The Netherlands, Denmark, Poland, and Spain are the largest exporters of live animals for transport in the EU. Dutch farmers alone sent over 1.5 million animals — mostly calves and pigs — on journeys longer than 8 hours in 2021, according to data submitted to the Commission. These countries have consistently pushed for longer travel times and more flexibility. Southern EU countries — Greece, Italy, Portugal — are net importers of live animals and have generally supported tighter limits, as have Germany and Austria, where public animal welfare concern is high.
