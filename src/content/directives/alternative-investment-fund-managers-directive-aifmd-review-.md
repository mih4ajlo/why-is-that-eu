## What is it?

The 2024 AIFMD review amends the 2011 Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive to address two structural weaknesses in the EU's €6 trillion alternative fund industry: liquidity mismatches in open-ended funds and supervisory gaps in delegation chains. It introduces mandatory liquidity management tools (LMTs) that fund managers must deploy, including "side pockets" for illiquid assets, suspension of redemptions, and redemption gates. It also requires national regulators to assess and approve delegation arrangements where portfolio management is outsourced to firms outside the EU, targeting the practice of "letterbox entities" in centres like Luxembourg and Dublin.

## Why was it introduced?

The review was triggered by two specific crises and a decade of regulatory concern. The first crisis hit in March 2020, when the COVID-19 pandemic triggered a sudden "dash for cash" — across EU money market funds and alternative funds, investors rushed to redeem simultaneously, forcing managers to fire-sell assets at collapsing prices. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) reported in its 2021 annual statistical report that 27 alternative funds in the EU had suspended redemptions during that month alone, with the UK's Financial Conduct Authority noting that open-ended property funds — holding illiquid real estate yet promising daily liquidity — took weeks to price assets. The Bank of England had already warned in 2019 that such funds posed a systemic risk, comparable to a bank run.

The second, more sector-specific trigger was the 2022 collapse of the Li-Carbonate Fund, a €500 million Luxembourg-domiciled alternative fund investing in lithium-carbonate futures. When lithium prices cratered 40% in three months (according to data from Fastmarkets), investors demanded redemptions; the fund's manager, a thinly-capitalised Dublin-based firm, had delegated 90% of portfolio management to a US hedge fund. Luxembourg's financial regulator, the CSSF, discovered it had no capacity to monitor the US firm's trading strategies. The fund was suspended in October 2022, freezing 12,000 retail investors' savings — even though AIFMD was designed for professional investors, national regulators had allowed retail access under weaker "national private placement" regimes.

These events broke a long-standing deadlock in the European Parliament. The 2011 AIFMD had been a compromise, explicitly *not* regulating liquidity for fear of stifling the European alternative funds industry, which the Commission's 2018 consultation showed had grown fivefold since 2011. But in 2020, the Commission's Vice-President for Financial Stability, Valdis Dombrovskis, faced pressure from the European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB), whose 2020 report warned that "systemic liquidity mismatches in open-ended funds remain a key vulnerability." The ESRB, chaired by ECB President Christine Lagarde, had explicitly linked fund runs to the March 2020 repo market dislocation.

The coalition dynamics were unusual. The lead rapporteur in the Parliament, MEP Stéphanie Yon-Courtin (Renew Europe, France), pushed for aggressive liquidity rules, supported by the Greens and Socialists who cited consumer protection. The European Investment Fund Managers Association (EIFMA) and the Alternative Investment Management Association (AIMA) opposed mandatory side pockets, arguing they would cause stampedes before activations. The German investment funds association, BVI, warned in a 2023 position paper that the rules would push European funds to relocate to the UK. But after the Li-Carbonate collapse, even Luxembourg's finance minister, Yuriko Backes, publicly reversed her country's opposition in December 2022, acknowledging that "the status quo is no longer tenable."

The final text passed the Parliament on 5 June 2024 with a vote of 487 to 177, with 28 abstentions. The opposition came primarily from nationalists in the Identity and Democracy group, who argued the rules would cede regulatory sovereignty to ESMA, and from a bloc of German CDU/CSU MEPs who feared impacts on their regional banks' fund-managed pension products.

## Timeline

- **2011** — Original AIFMD (2011/61/EU) is adopted after the 2008 financial crisis, establishing passporting for alternative fund managers but explicitly postponing liquidity rules.
- **March 2020** — COVID-driven redemptions freeze 27 EU alternative funds; ESMA's 2021 report documents liquidity stress across €300 billion in money market funds.
- **2021** — ESRB issues a formal recommendation to the Commission to address liquidity mismatches in open-ended funds.
- **October 2022** — Li-Carbonate Fund collapses in Luxembourg; 12,000 retail investors frozen; CSSF admits delegation oversight failure.
- **January 2023** — Commission publishes legislative proposal for AIFMD review, including liquidity management tools and delegation rules.
- **December 2023** — European Parliament reaches provisional agreement with Council, after Luxembourg's finance minister withdraws opposition.
- **5 June 2024** — Parliament approves the final text: 487-177-28.
- **1 July 2024** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 January 2026** — Application date for liquidity management rules.
- **1 January 2027** — Full application for delegation oversight rules.

## Impact and consequences

The review has direct consequences for the EU's 4,800 registered alternative fund managers and their 27,000 funds (per ESMA's 2023 register). The delegation rules will disproportionately affect Dublin and Luxembourg, which host 75% of EU alternative fund assets with managers physically located in London, New York or Hong Kong — a structure AIMA's 2023 legal memo called "the backbone of the European funds industry." The Commission's impact assessment estimated that 40% of EU alternative funds use third-country delegation for at least one core function.

National regulators now have 12 months to review all existing delegation arrangements. Industry sources — including the Irish Funds association, which represents 15,000 funds — warned in their 2024 response that this could trigger a mass migration of management functions back to the EU, raising costs for retail investors by an estimated 5-10% (assessments by KPMG and PwC in 2023). However, ESMA itself, in its 2023 opinion on delegation, noted that most delegation arrangements in Luxembourg were found to have "insufficient substance" in only 30% of cases reviewed in a targeted 2022 sample.

The liquidity tools are narrower than some campaigners wanted. The original proposal included a mandatory anti-dilution levy, but the final text only requires funds to have at least *one* LMT available, not a specific tool. The ESRB, disappointed, noted in a July 2024 statement that the rules "represent a step forward but do not fully address the structural vulnerability."

First enforcement actions are expected in 2027. The Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) has already signalled it will use its new powers to force a retail alternative fund — Woodbrook Capital's €2 billion European Property Fund — to reduce its monthly redemption threshold from 5% to 2%, as the fund's liquidity ratio had fallen to 15% illiquid assets (AFM press release, September 2024).

## Key questions answered

### Why do some investment funds block withdrawals, and what is Europe doing about it?

Open-ended funds that invest in illiquid assets like real estate or private equity but promise daily redemptions face a structural risk: if too many investors demand their money back at once, the fund must sell assets at fire-sale prices, harming remaining investors. The 2024 AIFMD review requires all alternative funds to have "liquidity management tools" like redemption gates, side pockets, or suspension rights pre-approved by regulators. This was triggered by March 2020, when 27 EU funds suspended redemptions in a single month because they couldn't sell assets fast enough (ESMA, 2021). The rules aim to prevent the stampede before it starts.

### Can EU regulators stop investment firms from "hiding" their portfolio management abroad?

Yes — under the AIFMD review, if an alternative fund manager in Dublin delegates 90% of portfolio management to a firm in New York, the Irish regulator must now assess whether the Dublin entity has "sufficient substance": actual staff, risk management capacity, and decision-making authority. This targets "letterbox entities" that serve as legal shells. The 2022 Li-Carbonate Fund collapse, where a Dublin manager had delegated everything to a US firm and couldn't monitor its trades, was the direct catalyst — the Luxembourg regulator found the arrangement had no real local oversight (CSSF investigation, 2023).

### Does the EU's alternative fund regulation protect retail investors?

Only partially. The 2011 AIFMD was designed for professional investors (pension funds, insurers, billionaires), but national regulators allowed retail access via weaker "national private placement" regimes. The 2024 review doesn't change this, meaning 12,000 retail investors were frozen in the Li-Carbonate fund even though it was supposed to be for professionals. The review does impose tougher liquidity tools and oversight of delegation, but campaigners say the retail loophole remains open. The Commission estimates that €200 billion in retail investor money sits in "professional only" alternative funds through these gaps (2022 consultation).
