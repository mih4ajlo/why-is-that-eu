## What is it?

The Victims Rights Directive sets binding minimum standards for how EU member states must treat victims of crime. It requires that victims receive information about their rights and the case from their first contact with authorities, have access to victim support services, can participate in criminal proceedings (including being heard and requesting review of decisions not to prosecute), and receive protection based on an individual risk assessment. The directive also mandates that professionals interacting with victims — police, prosecutors, judges — receive training on how to treat victims with respect and avoid secondary victimisation.

It replaced the 2001 Framework Decision on the standing of victims in criminal proceedings (2001/220/JHA), which the European Commission considered insufficiently implemented across the bloc. The directive applies to all victims of all crimes, regardless of their legal status in the EU, and covers both EU citizens and third-country nationals.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was triggered by a specific political and institutional failure: the European Commission's 2009 evaluation of the 2001 Framework Decision found that victims' rights were "systematically ignored" in many member states. Victims were not told about trial dates, were not allowed to make statements, and in some countries, were even billed for costs associated with police investigations. The Commission's January 2010 report concluded that the framework decision had "limited effectiveness" and that "the situation of victims has not improved substantially" — a damning verdict ten years after the original measure.

The structural tension was clear: the 2001 Framework Decision relied on weak implementation monitoring, and member states had transposed it unevenly. The Lisbon Treaty, which entered into force in December 2009, gave the EU new competence to adopt binding directives on victims' rights under Article 82 TFEU, allowing the Commission to propose a more enforceable instrument.

The coalition that drove the directive forward was unusual. The European Parliament had long pushed for stronger victims' rights — particularly MEPs from the Progressive Alliance of Socialists and Democrats (S&D) and the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats (ALEP), who saw it as a fundamental rights issue. The European Women's Lobby and victims' support organisations like the European Forum for Victim Services lobbied intensively, arguing that crime victims, especially of domestic violence, were being re-traumatised by judicial systems that treated them as witnesses rather than rights-holders. The Spanish Presidency of the Council in early 2010 made victims' rights a priority, and the Commission — under Viviane Reding (Justice Commissioner) and with strong backing from President José Manuel Barroso — positioned the directive as a flagship of the Stockholm Programme, the EU's five-year justice and home affairs agenda.

The timing was critical. The 2009 Lisbon Treaty had just entered into force, and the EU needed a high-profile demonstration of its new justice powers. The 2010-2012 period also saw several high-profile cross-border crime cases — including the 2011 murder of British student Meredith Kercher in Italy — that highlighted how victims moving between member states faced radically different levels of protection. Meanwhile, the 2011 EU Anti-Trafficking Directive (2011/36/EU) had already established specific rights for trafficking victims, creating pressure to extend similar protections to all victims.

Member states that opposed stronger measures — notably the United Kingdom, Ireland, and Denmark, which obtained opt-outs — and some Eastern European countries concerned about costs — argued that criminal justice was a national competence and that additional training requirements and support services would strain budgets. The UK Justice Ministry estimated in 2011 that implementing the directive would cost £80 million over four years, a figure victims' groups called exaggerated. Germany and France were broadly supportive but pushed for flexibility on how victim support was organised. The directive was adopted in October 2012 after the European Parliament voted in favour by a large majority (648 votes for, 9 against, 35 abstentions), but only after extended negotiations in the Council over whether police and prosecutors could refuse a victim's request for a case review.

What changed? Two specific events broke the earlier deadlock. First, the European Court of Justice's 2011 ruling in *Case C-483/09* (Magatte Gueye) found that the 2001 Framework Decision required member states to ensure victims could claim compensation and be heard, but the Court could not enforce compliance because the framework decision lacked direct effect. That judicial reality — the EU's highest court acknowledging the limits of its own enforcement power — made the case for a directive unanswerable. Second, the 2010 "Stay Safe" campaign by the Spanish Presidency and Commissioner Reding's personal diplomatic push — she travelled to 10 member states in 2011 to urge adoption — kept the issue politically visible despite the eurozone crisis.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Council adopts Framework Decision 2001/220/JHA on the standing of victims in criminal proceedings, setting non-binding standards.
- **2009** — Commission evaluation finds "limited effectiveness" of the framework decision, with victims routinely denied information and support.
- **December 2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force, giving the EU competence to adopt directives on victims' rights under Article 82 TFEU.
- **May 2010** — Commission's Stockholm Programme action plan includes a new victims' rights package.
- **May 2011** — Commission proposes the Victims Rights Directive, alongside a regulation on mutual recognition of protection orders.
- **September 2012** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement after extended negotiations on case review rights and cost provisions.
- **October 2012** — European Parliament approves directive (648-9-35); Council adopts it on 25 October.
- **November 2012** — Directive published in Official Journal.
- **November 2015** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2016** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against several member states (including Bulgaria, Cyprus, and Poland) for late transposition.
- **2017** — Commission reports that most member states have transposed the directive, but identifies gaps in implementation of individual assessments and training requirements.
- **2020** — Commission's evaluation report (COM/2020/188) finds that the directive has improved victims' access to information and support, but notes persistent problems with risk assessments and cross-border cases.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has had a measurable but uneven impact. According to the Commission's 2020 evaluation report, all member states have established national victim support systems, and the European Commission's 2019 Eurobarometer survey found that 56% of EU citizens were aware of victims' rights — up from 44% in 2014. The European Institute for Gender Equality (EIGE) reported in 2021 that the directive's requirement for individual risk assessments had improved protection for victims of domestic violence in countries like Austria and Finland, which introduced standardised assessment tools.

However, implementation gaps persist. The 2020 Commission evaluation found that victim support services remain underfunded in several member states, particularly in Eastern and Southern Europe — for example, Eurostat data indicated that in 2019, Greece, Malta, and Romania had fewer than one victim support centre per 1 million population, compared to six per 1 million in the Netherlands. The Commission also identified that training for judges and prosecutors remained voluntary or inadequate in 14 member states as of 2020.

Victims' organisations have criticised the lack of enforcement. The European Women's Lobby noted in a 2021 report that the directive's provisions on protection orders — intended to protect victims of domestic violence from their abusers during proceedings — were poorly coordinated with national systems, and the Commission's 2020 evaluation confirmed that recognition of protection orders across borders remained problematic.

The directive has been tested in several notable cases. In *Case C-38/13* (G.), the European Court of Justice ruled in 2014 that victims must be able to participate in proceedings even if they have not lodged a formal complaint. The 2018 case of *Case C-205/17* (S.) clarified that victims must be notified of the prisoner's release if they request it, impacting large prison systems like those in Italy and Spain.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU rules treat crime victims differently depending on whether they are from an EU country or not?

The directive applies to all victims regardless of nationality, but the Commission's 2020 evaluation noted that third-country nationals — particularly irregular migrants — often face practical barriers accessing support services due to fear of deportation. The 2011 proposal originally required equal treatment, but some member states (notably Hungary and Slovakia) argued in Council negotiations that irregular migrants should not receive the same level of support as EU citizens. The final text includes all victims within the EU's territory, but implementation varies significantly: the Commission found in 2020 that at least five member states still impose conditions linking access to victim support to immigration status.

### Why were the UK, Ireland, and Denmark allowed to skip these EU victim protection rules?

These three countries have special protocols under the Lisbon Treaty allowing them to opt out of justice and home affairs measures. The UK insisted on an opt-out during negotiations, arguing that its criminal justice system already provided stronger protections (e.g., the 2005 Domestic Violence, Crime and Victims Act). Ireland initially opted in but later withdrew its participation under Protocol 21. This means the directive does not apply in these countries, creating a gap in cross-border protection for victims moving between them and the rest of the EU — for example, a Spanish victim of domestic violence moving to Ireland cannot rely on the directive's guarantee of risk assessment or support.
