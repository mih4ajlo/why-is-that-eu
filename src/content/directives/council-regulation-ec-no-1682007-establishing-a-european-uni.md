## What is it?

Council Regulation (EC) No 168/2007 created the European Union Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA), an independent body based in Vienna. The FRA took over the work of the earlier European Monitoring Centre on Racism and Xenophobia (EUMC) but expanded its mandate significantly. The agency is tasked with collecting and analysing data on fundamental rights across the EU, providing evidence-based advice to EU institutions and member states, and promoting rights awareness.

The regulation defines the FRA's core tasks: collecting information on fundamental rights, formulating conclusions and opinions for EU institutions, cooperating with civil society and international organisations, and communicating on rights issues. Crucially, the regulation explicitly mandates the agency to monitor hate crime, racism, xenophobia, and other forms of intolerance. The work is carried out through a five-year Multi-annual Framework (MAF), approved by the Council, which sets the thematic priorities. The FRA has no enforcement powers — it cannot investigate individual complaints or issue binding rulings — but its reports and recommendations carry political weight and inform EU legislation.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a decade of growing evidence that racism and xenophobia were widespread, underreported, and poorly understood across the EU. The structural tension lay in the gap between the EU's lofty Treaty commitments to fundamental rights (enshrined in Article 6 of the Treaty on European Union) and the lack of reliable, comparable data to measure whether those commitments were being met. Without credible data, national governments could deny the scale of the problem, the European Commission could not effectively propose legislation, and victims had no official record of discrimination.

The coalition that drove the regulation was unusual: it was pushed not by a powerful industry lobby but by civil society, the European Parliament, and a handful of committed member states. The European Parliament had long called for strengthening the EUMC. In 2003, the Parliament adopted a resolution urging the Commission to propose a new agency with a broader mandate. The European Council, meeting in December 2003, asked the Commission to prepare a proposal. The Commission, under President Romano Prodi and Justice Commissioner António Vitorino, formally proposed the regulation in 2004.

The timing was critical. The 1997 Treaty of Amsterdam had given the EU new powers to combat discrimination, but the EUMC — created in 1997 and operational since 2000 — was limited to race and ethnicity. After the September 11 attacks and the subsequent rise in anti-Muslim and anti-immigrant sentiment, the need for broader monitoring became urgent. A series of high-profile incidents — including the murder of Dutch politician Pim Fortuyn in 2002 and growing tensions around immigration — created a political window.

What changed was the direct intervention of the European Council, the EU's highest political body. In 2003, EU leaders agreed that the EUMC's mandate was too narrow and instructed the Commission to expand it. The proposal faced resistance from some member states, notably the United Kingdom and several newer member states, who feared the agency would encroach on national competencies and police their human rights records. Some governments argued that fundamental rights were a national, not EU, matter and that the agency would duplicate the work of the Council of Europe's institutions. The Commission's 2004 impact assessment noted that the Council of Europe had expressed concern about overlapping mandates.

The compromise was a focused mandate: the FRA could only act within the scope of EU law, meaning it could not monitor a member state's domestic human rights record unless it related to the implementation of EU legislation. The final regulation, adopted under the UK's EU Presidency, was a delicate balance. The European Parliament was a key supporter — it had pushed for a stronger mandate — and the regulation was adopted with broad support in both the Council and Parliament. The vote in the Council was unanimous, with the UK, Ireland, and some Nordic states securing specific opt-outs on data collection provisions.

## Timeline

- **1997** — The Treaty of Amsterdam gives the EU new powers to combat discrimination. The European Monitoring Centre on Racism and Xenophobia (EUMC) is established.
- **2003** — The European Council asks the Commission to propose a new, broader fundamental rights agency.
- **December 2003** — The Commission issues a proposal for a new regulation (COM(2003) 693 final).
- **2004** — The Commission formally proposes the regulation. Consultations begin with the Council and Parliament.
- **2005** — The European Parliament adopts its first-reading opinion, calling for a stronger mandate than the Commission proposed.
- **2006** — Political agreement reached in the Council. Key negotiations focus on the agency's independence and its geographic scope.
- **15 February 2007** — The Council adopts Regulation (EC) No 168/2007.
- **1 March 2007** — The regulation enters into force.
- **2007** — The FRA is formally established in Vienna, taking over staff and resources from the EUMC.
- **2008** — First Multi-annual Framework (2007-2012) adopted, prioritising racism, xenophobia, and related intolerance.
- **2013** — Second Multi-annual Framework (2013-2017) adopted, expanding to include data protection, access to justice, and rights of the child.
- **2018** — Third Multi-annual Framework (2018-2022) adopted, adding migration, borders, and asylum.
- **2022** — The European Commission proposes a recast of the regulation to strengthen the FRA's role in monitoring rule of law and hate crime.

## Impact and consequences

The FRA's most consequential impact has been on hate crime and discrimination monitoring. Since its creation, the agency has published annual reports on racism, antisemitism, Islamophobia, and homophobia across the EU, providing the first-ever EU-wide data on these issues. A key consequence was the establishment of a standardised methodology for collecting hate crime data, which previously varied wildly — countries like Finland recorded hundreds of incidents while others recorded near zero. The FRA's reports exposed this discrepancy as underreporting rather than a lack of incidents.

The agency's data directly shaped EU law. The FRA's 2012 report on discrimination against Roma children in education influenced the EU's Framework for National Roma Integration Strategies. Its 2015 report on antisemitism helped push the Council to adopt a Declaration on combating antisemitism in 2018. The Commission's 2019 report on hate crime, which used FRA data, found that member states had failed to implement EU rules, triggering infringement proceedings.

Industry and civil society were both affected. NGOs relied on FRA data for advocacy, while tech companies used the agency's reports to understand hate speech and discrimination in their platforms. Some member states resisted sharing data; the Hungarian government, under Viktor Orbán, accused the FRA of political bias and refused to cooperate with certain investigations, a standoff that escalated in 2021.

The regulation also opened a new avenue for EU action: the FRA's "opinions" have been cited as legal justifications for subsequent directives, including the Audiovisual Media Services Directive's provisions on hate speech. However, the regulation's limitation to EU law scope has been a constant tension, with the agency unable to monitor situations like the 2015 migrant crisis in member states' asylum procedures unless linked to EU rules.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU have its own human rights watchdog?

Yes. The European Union Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA) is an independent body based in Vienna that monitors fundamental rights across the EU. It collects data, provides expert advice to EU institutions and member states, and publishes reports on issues like racism, hate crime, and discrimination. However, it cannot investigate individual complaints or enforce laws — that role belongs to the Council of Europe's European Court of Human Rights.

### How does the EU track hate crime across its 27 countries?

The FRA is the primary body tasked with collecting and standardising hate crime data from all EU member states. It has developed a common methodology to count and compare incidents of racism, antisemitism, Islamophobia, homophobia, and other forms of hatred. The agency's annual reports expose which countries underreport incidents and highlight gaps in national police data. Before the FRA, most EU countries had no comparable data.

### Why can't the EU investigate human rights abuses in all EU countries?

The FRA's mandate is limited to situations that fall within the scope of EU law. This means the agency can only monitor human rights issues when a member state is implementing EU legislation — for example, EU asylum rules or anti-discrimination directives. It cannot monitor a country's purely domestic human rights record (like its criminal justice system or prison conditions) unless those areas are linked to EU law. This limitation was a deliberate compromise to win support from member states that wanted to keep national sovereignty over human rights.

### What did the FRA do that its predecessor could not?

The FRA replaced and expanded the European Monitoring Centre on Racism and Xenophobia (EUMC). While the EUMC was limited to monitoring racism and xenophobia, the FRA covers the full spectrum of fundamental rights in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights — including data protection, access to justice, children's rights, and asylum. It can also issue formal "opinions" to EU institutions during the legislative process, something the EUMC could not do.
