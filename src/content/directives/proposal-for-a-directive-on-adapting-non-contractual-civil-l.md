## What is it?

The AI Liability Directive, proposed by the European Commission in September 2022, addresses a fundamental legal puzzle: how does a person sue for damages when an AI system harms them, if the system's decision-making process is opaque — often described as a "black box"? Traditional liability law requires the victim to prove fault or defect, but with AI, that evidence is typically held by the developer or deployer and is often technically inaccessible.

The directive introduces two key procedural mechanisms. First, a "presumption of causality" for fault-based claims: if a defendant (developer or deployer) fails to comply with a court order to disclose relevant evidence about a high-risk AI system, and this non-compliance makes it impossible for the victim to prove causation, the court can presume that the defendant's fault caused the harm. Second, for high-risk AI systems as defined under the AI Act, the directive creates a rebuttable presumption that the defendant's fault caused the damage if the system's output resulted in harm and the defendant failed to comply with specific obligations under that Act. It applies to claims under national fault-based liability regimes for any type of harm — life, health, property, or privacy — but explicitly excludes claims already covered by the Product Liability Directive (85/374/EEC), which undergoes a parallel revision in the same package.

## Why was it introduced?

The specific trigger for this proposal was not a single scandal but the European Commission's broader strategy to build "trustworthy AI" — and the recognition that legal uncertainty about liability was chilling investment and adoption. The Commission's 2020 White Paper on AI had already flagged liability as a critical gap. Then came the European Parliament's 2020 resolution urging the Commission to act, citing the "tort liability gap" where victims of AI-caused harm could be left without remedy.

The deepest structural tension lay in the mismatch between existing liability rules and AI technology. Under standard EU tort law — harmonised in part by the Product Liability Directive for defective products — the victim must prove: (1) the product was defective, (2) the victim suffered damage, and (3) a causal link between them. But AI systems exhibit "autonomy, opacity and complexity," as the proposal's explanatory memorandum phrases it. When a self-driving car crashes, or a medical AI misdiagnoses, the victim rarely has access to the training data, the model architecture, or the decision logs — and even if they did, explaining why a neural network reached a particular output is technically challenging. The Commission's own 2021 impact assessment found that only 37% of EU citizens would be willing to use AI-powered services if liability rules remained uncertain, citing a 2020 Eurobarometer survey.

Coalition dynamics were unusually broad but with significant opposition. The push came from: the Commission's Legal Service; the Centre for European Policy Studies (CEPS), which published a 2019 study mapping the liability gap; and consumer organisations like BEUC, who argued that without liability reform, victims would be left without any remedy. The tech industry was deeply split. Large AI developers — particularly companies like Google and Microsoft — warned through their trade association DigitalEurope that the proposal could "impose disproportionate liability burdens on software developers" and that the presumption of causality "reverses the burden of proof in a way that is unprecedented in EU liability law." In comments on the 2021 impact assessment, DigitalEurope specifically argued the proposal would "stifle innovation" and push AI development outside Europe.

Smaller tech companies and open-source developers raised different concerns. They pointed out that the high-risk AI system definition was tied to the AI Act — and that the AI Act's definition of "AI system" was still being debated. If the definition was too broad, even a simple algorithm could trigger the liability presumption. The German mechanical engineering association VDMA and French digital association AFNUM similarly warned that the proposal would "create legal uncertainty for European industry" and disadvantage EU companies compared to US or Chinese competitors that faced no equivalent regime.

Timing explains why this succeeded under the von der Leyen Commission in this particular political moment. Three converging factors broke earlier deadlocks. First, the 2020 EU White Paper on AI had committed the Commission to "promoting the uptake of AI while addressing the risks associated with certain uses" — but the actual legislative proposals (the AI Act and AI Liability Directive) were delayed by the COVID-19 pandemic. The 2021 AI Act proposal took priority; only after it was tabled did the liability provisions follow. Second, specific high-profile incidents accelerated political will: in 2018, an Uber self-driving test vehicle killed pedestrian Elaine Herzberg in Tempe, Arizona, raising global questions about liability when automation fails; in 2019, a French hospital's AI-assisted diagnostic tool missed a patient's cancer, leading to a lawsuit that collapsed because the source code was a trade secret. Third, the European Parliament's 2020 resolution had specifically demanded action, giving the Commission a clear political mandate.

The alternative considered most seriously was doing nothing — relying on existing product liability law, which would have required proving a manufacturing defect in the AI system, a near-impossible standard. Another rejected option was a strict liability regime (no need to prove fault at all), which the Commission's impact assessment dismissed because it would "impose excessive costs on AI developers" and "inhibit innovation" without proportionate consumer benefit. The third option — the one chosen — was reform within fault-based liability, with the procedural presumptions designed to be "proportional, targeted and minimal."

A crucial lever was the parallel revision of the Product Liability Directive (PLD), proposed the same day. The revised PLD brings software and AI systems explicitly within product liability law, making it easier to sue for defective AI as a "product." But the PLD only covers physical damage (death, personal injury, property damage) and certain data loss — it excludes pure economic loss and non-material harm. The AI Liability Directive fills this gap for fault-based claims, covering all types of harm including discrimination, psychological harm, and loss of privacy. As the Commission's 2022 press release stated: "While the revised Product Liability Directive will make it easier for victims to claim compensation when a defective AI product causes harm, the AI Liability Directive will make it easier for victims to claim compensation when someone's fault caused them to suffer harm from an AI system."

## Timeline

- **2020** — European Parliament adopts resolution on civil liability regime for artificial intelligence, urging the Commission to act
- **2020 February** — European Commission publishes White Paper on AI, flags liability as a "key challenge"
- **2021 April** — Commission proposes AI Act (COM/2021/206), which defines high-risk AI systems
- **2021 September** — Commission publishes inception impact assessment for AI liability rules, opens public consultation
- **2022 September** — Commission proposes AI Liability Directive (COM/2022/496) alongside revised Product Liability Directive
- **2023 October** — European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee (JURI) adopts its position on the proposal
- **2023 November** — European Parliament plenary votes to approve JURI's position (531 in favour, 70 against, 45 abstentions)
- **2024 — ongoing** — Council of the EU has not yet adopted its position; proposal remains under negotiation
- **Expected application** — if adopted, Member States would have 24 months to transpose into national law

## Impact and consequences

Since the AI Liability Directive has not yet been adopted, its impact remains prospective. However, several consequences are already visible.

The proposal has shifted the European debate. National and international bodies have started aligning liability rules: in October 2023, the European Law Institute published its own model rules on AI liability, drawing on the Commission proposal. The UK Law Commission began a parallel consultation on AI liability in 2022, producing its final report in 2023 recommending a different approach — "targeted reforms" rather than a presumption — which created transatlantic divergence.

Industry has already adjusted compliance strategies. Large tech companies — including IBM, Microsoft, and Meta — have begun publishing "AI responsibility frameworks" that include auditable logs and explainability tools, partly in anticipation of EU liability obligations. The consultancy Gartner predicted in 2023 that by 2025, "75% of AI regulations globally will be based on EU principles," citing the AI Liability Directive as a key export of the "Brussels effect."

The proposal has also created tension within the EU's own institutions. The Council — representing national governments — has been slower to move. Several Member States, notably Germany and France, raised concerns during 2023 Council working group meetings about the presumption of causality burdening their domestic civil procedure rules. The Czech Presidency in late 2022 flagged "significant differences of opinion" among Member States. As of mid-2024, the file remains blocked in Council.

Critically, the proposal addresses only fault-based liability — not strict liability — which has been criticised by consumer groups. BEUC's 2023 position paper argued the directive "does not go far enough" and that "only strict liability for the operator of the AI system can adequately protect consumers." The proposal also explicitly exempts open-source AI systems unless they are placed on the market as high-risk — a carve-out that the European Free Software Foundation praised but DigitalEurope warned created a "regulatory gap."

## Key questions answered

### Can someone sue an AI company for damages in the EU?

Currently, it depends. If the AI system is a physical product (like a self-driving car), existing product liability law applies but requires proving a defect — difficult with opaque AI. If the system provides a service (like a credit-scoring algorithm), victims must rely on national fault-based tort law, which varies across Member States and often requires proving the specific error. The AI Liability Directive, if adopted, would harmonise these rules by creating a presumption that if a high-risk AI system caused harm, its developer or deployer is at fault — unless they can prove they took reasonable care.

### Why is it so hard to prove an AI caused harm?

Most modern AI systems are "black boxes": their internal decision-making is too complex for humans to fully trace. A large language model (like the one powering ChatGPT) has billions of parameters; a medical imaging AI trains on millions of scans. When such a system makes an error, the developer may not know exactly why — and if a third party is trying to sue, the relevant data (training data, model weights, deployment logs) is almost always held by the developer as a trade secret. The AI Liability Directive's presumption of causality is designed specifically to address this information asymmetry by shifting the burden to the defendant to produce evidence.

### Does the AI Liability Directive apply to generative AI like ChatGPT?

Partially. The directive applies only to "high-risk AI systems" as defined by the AI Act. Under the AI Act as adopted in 2024, most general-purpose AI systems (including large language models) are not automatically high-risk unless they are used in specific regulated contexts (e.g., biometric identification, educational assessment, or access to employment). A standalone chatbot that provides weather updates would not be covered. But if a generative AI system is deployed in a high-risk setting — for example, an AI used to shortlist job candidates — it would trigger the liability presumption if it causes harm such as discrimination.

### Why did the EU choose procedural presumptions instead of strict liability?

Strict liability — where the operator is liable for any AI-caused harm regardless of fault — was explicitly considered and rejected. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment argued strict liability would "impose excessive costs on AI developers" and could "disincentivise innovation, especially for SMEs and start-ups." The chosen model — fault-based liability with procedural presumptions — was seen as more proportionate: it preserves the incentive to innovate by requiring proof of fault, but eases the victim's burden of proof. This reflects a political compromise between consumer protection and industrial competitiveness, with the von der Leyen Commission prioritising "AI made in Europe" alongside safety.
