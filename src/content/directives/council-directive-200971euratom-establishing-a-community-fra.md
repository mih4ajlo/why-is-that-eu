## What is it?

Directive 2009/71/Euratom is the EU's binding nuclear safety framework. It obliges all member states — including those without reactors — to establish a national legal framework for nuclear safety, to maintain a competent regulatory authority that is functionally independent from any body promoting nuclear energy, and to make licence holders primarily responsible for the safety of their installations. It was adopted under the Euratom Treaty, which gives the EU competence over civil nuclear matters distinct from the regular EU treaties.

The 2014 amendment, Directive 2014/87/Euratom, was a direct response to the March 2011 Fukushima Daiichi accident in Japan. It introduced a high-level EU safety objective — to prevent accidents and, should one occur, to mitigate consequences and avoid early or large radioactive releases. It mandated topical peer reviews of all EU reactors every six years on specific safety themes, strengthened the independence and resources of national regulators, required on-site emergency response centres, and tightened transparency obligations toward the public.

Together, the two directives cover all civilian nuclear installations: power reactors, research reactors, fuel fabrication plants, enrichment facilities, and spent fuel storage on the same site as a reactor. Compliance is policed by the European Commission and, technically, by the Court of Justice of the EU — which can fine member states that fail to transpose or apply the rules.

## Why was it introduced?

Before 2009, the EU had no binding nuclear safety law — a remarkable gap for a bloc with more than 130 operating reactors. The Euratom Treaty of 1957 created a community for civilian atomic energy, but safety standards were left to national regulators, with the IAEA providing only non-binding guidance. The Commission tried to change this in 2003 under Energy Commissioner Loyola de Palacio with a "nuclear package" proposing binding safety rules, but France and the United Kingdom — the bloc's two largest nuclear operators — blocked it in Council, arguing that their national regulators already exceeded any conceivable EU standard and that Brussels lacked technical competence.

What broke the deadlock was a combination of enlargement and the WENRA process. The 2004 and 2007 enlargements brought Soviet-designed reactors in Bulgaria, Slovakia, Lithuania, the Czech Republic, Hungary, Romania and Slovenia into the EU. The Commission had already forced Lithuania to shut Ignalina's RBMK units (the same design as Chernobyl) and Bulgaria to close four Kozloduy VVER-440 units as a condition of accession. The Western European Nuclear Regulators Association (WENRA), founded in 1999, had spent a decade producing common "reference levels" that national regulators voluntarily endorsed. By 2008, under Energy Commissioner Andris Piebalgs, the Commission could present a directive that essentially codified what WENRA had already agreed — making French and UK opposition harder to sustain. The Council adopted it in June 2009.

Then came Fukushima. On 11 March 2011, the Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami caused triple meltdowns at TEPCO's Fukushima Daiichi plant. Within days, Energy Commissioner Günther Oettinger — who publicly described the situation as an "apocalypse" — secured agreement from the European Council to subject every EU reactor to "stress tests" examining their resilience to earthquakes, flooding, loss of cooling, and station blackout. The tests, run by national regulators and peer-reviewed under ENSREG, identified hundreds of recommended upgrades across the EU fleet. The Commission's 2012 communication on the results noted that no plant needed immediate shutdown but that all required improvements, with estimated costs of €10–25 billion across the bloc.

The 2014 revision translated the political lessons of Fukushima into binding law. Oettinger pushed for tougher regulator independence (Japan's NISA had been embedded in the same ministry that promoted nuclear power), the binding high-level safety objective, and the six-yearly topical peer reviews. Industry — represented by FORATOM — and several member states resisted prescriptive design requirements, and the final text settled on a goal-based safety objective rather than detailed technical mandates. The directive was adopted by Council in July 2014 with transposition required by August 2017.

## Timeline

- **1957** — Euratom Treaty signed; civilian nuclear competence assigned to the Community, but binding safety rules left aside.
- **1999** — WENRA founded to harmonise nuclear safety approaches among Western European regulators.
- **2003** — Commission proposes binding nuclear safety directive; blocked in Council by France and the UK.
- **2004–2007** — EU enlargement brings Soviet-designed reactors into the bloc; accession conditions force closure of Ignalina and parts of Kozloduy.
- **June 2009** — Council adopts Directive 2009/71/Euratom; transposition deadline 22 July 2011.
- **11 March 2011** — Fukushima Daiichi accident begins.
- **March 2011** — European Council mandates EU-wide stress tests.
- **2012** — Commission communication on stress test results identifies billions in needed upgrades.
- **June 2013** — Commission proposes revision of the directive.
- **July 2014** — Council adopts Directive 2014/87/Euratom.
- **August 2017** — Transposition deadline for the revised directive.
- **2018** — First topical peer review cycle launched under ENSREG, focused on ageing management.
- **2023** — Second topical peer review on fire protection.

## Impact and consequences

The most concrete effect was institutional: every member state now has a legally independent nuclear regulator with statutory authority to order shutdowns, even those — like Ireland, Denmark or Austria — that have no reactors but may host transport, research facilities or interim storage. Several member states, including the Czech Republic and Slovakia, restructured their regulators to satisfy the independence test.

The Fukushima stress tests triggered substantial capital investment. France's ASN required EDF to install bunkered "hardened safety cores" (noyaux durs) at every reactor, including diesel generators, emergency control rooms and ultimate heat sinks designed to withstand beyond-design-basis events. EDF's published Grand Carénage programme to extend reactor lifetimes — running into the tens of billions of euros — incorporates many of the post-Fukushima requirements. Belgium's Electrabel undertook extensive upgrades at Doel and Tihange before their planned closures.

Compliance has not been smooth. The Commission has opened infringement proceedings against several member states for late or incomplete transposition of the 2014 amendment, including referrals to the Court of Justice. The Court ruled in 2019 (Case C-411/17, Inter-Environnement Wallonie) that Belgium's life-extension of Doel 1 and 2 required environmental impact assessment under EU law — a separate but related judgement that reinforced the role of EU rules over national nuclear decisions.

The directive does not, however, give the Commission power to order a specific reactor closed on safety grounds. Decisions to operate or shut individual plants remain national. This is why disputes over plants near borders — Belgium's Tihange and Doel as seen from Germany and the Netherlands, France's Fessenheim and Cattenom, Slovakia's Mochovce — have generated political friction but rarely legal remedies.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU have the power to shut down an unsafe nuclear reactor?

No. The directive sets binding safety objectives and requires independent regulators, but the decision to license, operate or close an individual reactor remains with national authorities. The Commission can take a member state to the Court of Justice for failing to apply the rules, and can extract closure commitments as a condition of EU membership (as it did with Ignalina, Kozloduy and Bohunice), but it cannot directly order an existing reactor offline.

### What changed after Fukushima for European nuclear plants?

EU-wide stress tests identified hundreds of safety upgrades, and most operators invested billions in backup power, emergency cooling, bunkered control facilities and on-site emergency response centres. The 2014 revision of the directive then made many of these post-Fukushima practices legally binding, including a high-level objective to avoid early or large radioactive releases, six-yearly topical peer reviews, and stricter regulator independence.

### Why are countries without reactors covered by EU nuclear safety law?

Because they may still host research reactors, fuel cycle facilities, radioactive waste storage, or nuclear transports, and because radiation does not respect borders. The directive requires every member state to maintain a competent independent regulator and a national framework even if the country is non-nuclear, which is why states like Ireland and Austria have nuclear safety authorities despite having no power reactors.

### Are cross-border nuclear safety disputes — like Germany versus Belgium over Tihange — settled by EU law?

Only partly. Neighbouring states have rights to information and consultation under EU law, and environmental impact assessment rules can be enforced through the Court of Justice, as Belgium discovered in the Doel 1/2 case. But the substantive decision on whether a specific reactor is safe enough to operate remains with the host state's regulator, leaving cross-border complaints largely diplomatic rather than legal.
