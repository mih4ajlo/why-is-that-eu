## What is it?

The European Market Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR) is a cornerstone of post-financial-crisis EU financial regulation, requiring all standardised over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives to be cleared through central counterparties (CCPs). It also imposes mandatory reporting of all derivative contracts to trade repositories, risk-mitigation techniques for non-cleared trades, and margin requirements.

EMIR applies to all EU counterparties entering into derivative contracts, from large banks and investment firms to non-financial companies exceeding clearing thresholds. The regulation empowers the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) to supervise trade repositories and enforce compliance. It was amended several times, most notably by EMIR Refit in 2019 (Regulation 2019/834/EU) to reduce costs for small counterparties.

## Why was it introduced?

The collapse of Lehman Brothers in September 2008 revealed a catastrophic regulatory blind spot: the global OTC derivatives market, valued at roughly $600 trillion in notional terms according to the Bank for International Settlements, operated almost entirely in private, bilateral contracts with no central record-keeping or risk management. When Lehman defaulted on hundreds of billions of dollars in derivative positions, counterparties could not determine their exposure, triggering a chain of margin calls that froze credit markets.

The political trigger came at the G20 Pittsburgh summit in September 2009, when world leaders — including EU Commission President José Manuel Barroso — committed that "all standardised OTC derivative contracts should be cleared through central counterparties by end-2012." The EU faced pressure to act quickly or risk fragmentation, as the US had already passed the Dodd-Frank Act in July 2010. Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier led the legislative push, arguing that "what happens in one market affects us all."

The coalition for EMIR was unusual: it brought together Southern European states (particularly France and Italy) that wanted tighter regulation with Northern states (Germany, Netherlands) concerned about competitiveness. The UK — home to London’s dominant derivatives market — initially resisted mandatory clearing, warning it could drive business offshore. A critical compromise allowed EU-based CCPs to be recognised globally, preserving London’s role while extending EU supervision.

Opposition came primarily from the derivatives industry. The International Swaps and Derivatives Association (ISDA) warned that mandatory clearing would increase costs for end-users like airlines and manufacturers hedging fuel or currency risk. The European Central Bank under Jean-Claude Trichet raised concerns about CCP location requirements, but a compromise on "location policy" allowed CCPs established in third countries to operate if recognised by ESMA.

The final text was adopted in July 2012 after nearly two years of negotiation. The European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee rapporteur, Dutch MEP Corien Wortmann-Kool, brokered key agreements. The regulation entered into force on 16 August 2012.

## Timeline

- **2008** — Lehman Brothers collapse exposes systemic risk in OTC derivatives; global regulators begin push for central clearing.
- **2009, September** — G20 Pittsburgh summit commits to mandatory central clearing by end-2012.
- **2010, September** — European Commission publishes proposal for EMIR, authored by Commissioner Barnier.
- **2011, February** — European Parliament's ECON committee approves draft with amendments.
- **2012, March** — Council of the EU adopts its position; trilogue negotiations begin.
- **2012, July 4** — European Parliament approves final text (673 votes in favour, 67 against).
- **2012, July 11** — Council adopts regulation.
- **2012, August 16** — EMIR enters into force; phased-in implementation begins.
- **2014, February** — First mandatory CCP clearing requirement takes effect for interest rate swaps.
- **2017, May** — EMIR Refit proposal published to simplify rules for small firms.
- **2019, June** — EMIR Refit (Regulation 2019/834/EU) enters into force, exempting small financial counterparties from clearing.
- **2021, February** — ESMA imposes first fine for trade repository data breaches: €50,000 against UnaVista Ltd.

## Impact and consequences

EMIR transformed EU derivatives markets. By 2020, the notional amount of OTC derivatives cleared through CCPs reached roughly 75% globally, up from below 20% pre-crisis, according to the Financial Stability Board. In the EU, mandatory reporting generated over 50 billion trade reports per year by 2022, according to ESMA.

Counterparties faced significant compliance costs. A 2019 Commission impact assessment estimated that EMIR compliance costs EU financial firms approximately €1.5 billion annually, driven by IT systems, margin posting, and legal agreements. Smaller firms — particularly pension funds and non-financial companies — struggled with clearing thresholds. The exemption for non-financial counterparties under EMIR Refit affected roughly 95% of all non-financial counterparties, according to the Commission.

Enforcement has been limited. ESMA levied its first fine in February 2021 — €50,000 against UnaVista Ltd for failing to maintain accurate trade repository data. National regulators, such as the UK's Financial Conduct Authority, issued several fines for late reporting, but no major clearing house failures have tested the system's resilience.

The UK’s departure from the EU in 2020 created a major jurisdictional split. London-based CCPs (LCH Ltd, ICE Clear Europe) now operate under equivalence decisions, with ESMA extending temporary recognition repeatedly amid political tensions.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EMIR require central clearing for derivatives?

Central clearing reduces counterparty risk by interposing a CCP that guarantees all trades. If one party defaults, the CCP absorbs losses using pooled margin and default funds — preventing the type of cascade that followed Lehman’s collapse. The 2009 G20 commitment made it a political priority, but the EU chose a phased approach: only standardised contracts (mainly interest rate swaps and credit default swaps) were mandated first.

### Why does EMIR require reporting to trade repositories?

Before EMIR, regulators had no centralised view of OTC derivatives exposure. The 2008 crisis showed that no one — not the US Treasury, not the ECB — could measure systemic risk in derivatives. EMIR’s reporting obligation requires every derivative trade to be reported with 25 data fields to an ESMA-registered trade repository, creating a global data pool. By 2020, ESMA had 6 trade repositories handling over 50 billion reports annually.

### Why did pension funds get an exemption from clearing under EMIR?

Pension funds initially opposed mandatory clearing because they hold long-term, illiquid assets that make it expensive to post daily cash margin. A three-year exemption was granted in 2012, extended multiple times under political pressure from the European Association of Paritarian Institutions (AEIP) and national pension fund groups. EMIR Refit eventually made the exemption indefinite, recognising that pension funds pose lower systemic risk than banks.

### How did Brexit affect EMIR?

Brexit created a regulatory schism: London hosts LCH Ltd, the world’s largest derivatives CCP, clearing over €600 trillion notional annually. Under EMIR, UK CCPs needed equivalence to continue serving EU clients. ESMA granted temporary recognition in 2020, but the EU has since pushed for relocation — threatening to require EU banks to clear through EU-based CCPs. In 2022, ESMA extended recognition to June 2025, with the Commission calling for "reduced reliance" on UK infrastructure.
