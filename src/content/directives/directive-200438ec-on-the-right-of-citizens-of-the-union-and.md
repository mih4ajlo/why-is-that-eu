## What is it?

Directive 2004/38/EC merged a patchwork of nine earlier directives and regulations governing free movement — for workers, students, pensioners, self-employed and their families — into a single instrument anchored in the concept of EU citizenship created by the Maastricht Treaty. It gives every EU national an unconditional right to enter another Member State for up to three months with just an ID card, a right to reside for longer if they are working, studying or self-sufficient, and a right of permanent residence after five years of continuous lawful stay.

Crucially, the directive extends these rights to family members regardless of nationality: a Brazilian spouse, a Moroccan parent, or an unmarried partner in a "durable relationship" gains derivative rights when accompanying an EU citizen exercising free movement. It also limits the grounds on which a host state can expel an EU citizen — public policy, public security or public health — and requires that expulsions be proportionate and individualised, not automatic.

Host states must grant equal access to social benefits, though the directive permits restrictions on social assistance during the first three months and on student maintenance grants before permanent residence is acquired. These caveats became the battleground of the 2010s.

## Why was it introduced?

By the early 2000s, free movement rights were governed by a tangle of legislation dating to 1968 — Regulation 1612/68 on workers, plus separate directives for students (93/96), pensioners (90/365) and the economically inactive (90/364) — each with its own conditions, family definitions and expulsion rules. The Court of Justice had spent two decades patching the gaps through jurisprudence, most notably in *Martínez Sala* (1998) and *Grzelczyk* (2001), where it ruled that Union citizenship was "destined to be the fundamental status" of nationals of Member States and that economically inactive citizens could claim equal treatment for social benefits in their host country.

The Tampere European Council of 1999, under the Finnish Presidency, called for a comprehensive overhaul. Commissioner António Vitorino, the Portuguese Justice and Home Affairs Commissioner in the Prodi Commission, tabled the proposal in May 2001, explicitly framing it as a step toward making Union citizenship a substantive status rather than a symbolic one. The timing was deliberate: the 2004 "big bang" enlargement to ten new Member States, mostly Central and Eastern European, was looming, and the Commission wanted a clean, consolidated rulebook before 75 million new EU citizens gained free movement rights.

The structural tension was that the original 1968 rules had been written for a community of six broadly comparable economies, where labour mobility was modest and welfare states roughly aligned. Applying them to a Union of 25 with sharp wage differentials between Germany and Lithuania was a different proposition entirely. The UK, Ireland and Sweden were the only old Member States that opened their labour markets immediately to the 2004 entrants; others imposed transitional restrictions of up to seven years. The Blair government's prediction — drawing on a 2003 Home Office study — that net migration from the new Member States would be in the low tens of thousands per year proved dramatically wrong, with later ONS figures showing hundreds of thousands of Polish, Lithuanian and other A8 nationals settling in Britain.

The directive's most contested feature emerged from Parliament rather than the Commission: MEPs led by Italian rapporteur Giacomo Santini pushed to extend family rights to registered partners and unmarried partners in durable relationships, and to weaken the conditions states could impose on residence beyond three months. Council, dominated at the time by centre-right governments, pushed back on the welfare provisions. The compromise — allowing benefit restrictions in the first three months but requiring equal treatment thereafter — would become the focus of the Cameron renegotiation a decade later.

By the mid-2010s, the directive sat at the centre of British politics. UKIP campaigned explicitly against its in-work benefit rules; Prime Minister David Cameron's February 2016 renegotiation with Council President Donald Tusk secured an "emergency brake" allowing the UK to restrict in-work benefits for new EU arrivals for up to seven years, but only after a finding of exceptional pressure. The deal was ratified at the European Council of 18–19 February 2016 and lapsed when the UK voted to leave on 23 June. The directive's free movement principle, more than any other single piece of EU law, defined the Leave campaign's central argument about "control of borders".

## Timeline

- **1990–1993** — Three "residence directives" (90/364, 90/365, 93/96) extend free movement to non-workers: pensioners, students and the self-sufficient.
- **1998–2001** — Court of Justice rulings in *Martínez Sala*, *Grzelczyk* and *Baumbast* progressively expand citizenship-based rights to equal treatment.
- **May 2001** — Commissioner António Vitorino tables the consolidation proposal.
- **April 2004** — Directive adopted by Parliament and Council under co-decision, weeks before the ten-country enlargement on 1 May.
- **April 2006** — Transposition deadline; several Member States, including Germany and Italy, miss it and face infringement proceedings.
- **2007–2008** — Romanian and Bulgarian accession triggers tensions in Italy and France over Roma communities; France launches mass expulsions of Roma EU citizens in 2010, prompting a Commission rebuke from Justice Commissioner Viviane Reding.
- **2014** — UK Prime Minister Cameron announces intention to renegotiate free movement rules; CJEU rules in *Dano* that economically inactive citizens with no real prospect of work can be denied social assistance.
- **February 2016** — Cameron secures "emergency brake" deal at European Council.
- **23 June 2016** — UK votes to leave the EU, 51.9% to 48.1%.
- **31 January 2020** — UK formally leaves the EU; Withdrawal Agreement preserves Directive 2004/38 rights for citizens already exercising free movement before 31 December 2020.

## Impact and consequences

According to Eurostat, several million EU citizens live in a Member State other than their own, with the largest flows historically running from Poland, Romania and Italy to Germany, the UK (pre-Brexit), Spain and France. The directive made cross-border family formation routine: thousands of British, German and Dutch citizens used it to live with non-EU spouses in conditions more favourable than national immigration law would have permitted, the so-called "Surinder Singh route" derived from a 1992 CJEU ruling.

National administrations struggled with implementation. The Commission's 2008 evaluation report found that not a single Member State had transposed the directive fully and correctly; common failings included overly restrictive treatment of non-EU family members, excessive documentation demands, and unlawful expulsion procedures. Infringement proceedings followed against the UK, Belgium, Cyprus and others.

The political consequences were larger still. The directive supplied the legal mechanism for the post-2004 wave of intra-EU migration that reshaped British, Irish and German labour markets, and it became the proximate cause of the most consequential rupture in the Union's history. Post-Brexit, the EU Settlement Scheme processed several million applications from EU nationals seeking to preserve their UK residence rights — itself a measure of how deeply the directive had embedded EU citizens in national societies.

## Key questions answered

### Can EU citizens really move to any other EU country and claim benefits?

Largely yes, but with conditions. After three months, EU citizens must be working, studying, self-employed or have sufficient resources not to become a burden on the host state's social assistance system. The Court of Justice's *Dano* (2014) and *Alimanovic* (2015) rulings confirmed that Member States can refuse non-contributory benefits to economically inactive EU citizens who fail these conditions — a significant tightening of earlier case law.

### Why did free movement become the central issue in Brexit?

The UK was one of only three pre-2004 Member States to open its labour market immediately to Polish and other Central European workers, and net migration vastly exceeded official forecasts. By the mid-2010s, UKIP and large parts of the Conservative Party argued that free movement made it impossible to control overall immigration numbers. Cameron's February 2016 renegotiation produced only a limited "emergency brake" on in-work benefits, which Leave campaigners called inadequate, and the issue dominated the referendum.

### Does an EU citizen's non-European spouse have the right to move with them?

Yes — this is one of the directive's most important features. A British, French or Spanish citizen exercising free movement in another Member State can bring a non-EU spouse, registered partner, children and dependent parents, regardless of those family members' nationality. The host state must facilitate their entry and residence, and cannot apply ordinary national immigration rules. This has historically been used by EU citizens to circumvent restrictive national rules on family reunification at home.

### What happened to EU citizens in the UK after Brexit?

The Withdrawal Agreement preserved the directive's rights for EU citizens resident in the UK and Britons resident in the EU before the end of the transition on 31 December 2020. The UK established the EU Settlement Scheme, which received millions of applications. Those arriving after the deadline are subject to the UK's points-based immigration system instead.
