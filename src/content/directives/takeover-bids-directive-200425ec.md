## What is it?

The Takeover Bids Directive sets out minimum standards for public takeover bids in the EU. Its core requirement is the mandatory bid rule: anyone acquiring a controlling stake in a listed company must make a fair offer to all remaining shareholders, typically at the highest price paid for shares in the preceding period. This prevents a new controller from squeezing out minority shareholders at an unfair price.

The directive also requires Member States to designate independent supervisory authorities to oversee bids, establishes rules on employee information and consultation, and gives target company boards limited room to take defensive measures without shareholder approval. It does not harmonise the exact threshold for triggering a mandatory bid — Member States set this at between 30% and 50% of voting rights — nor does it impose a single "breakthrough" rule for anti-takeover devices, leaving the latter optional for Member States.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a decade-long political battle over Europe's fragmented takeover landscape, triggered by a series of hostile cross-border bids that exposed deep national fault lines.

The immediate spark came in 1999 with Vodafone's hostile €115 billion takeover of Mannesmann — the largest hostile bid in history at the time. The bid pitted the UK's open market against Germany's tightly controlled corporate structure, protected by supervisory board vetoes and cross-shareholdings. Mannesmann's board fought ferociously, but after weeks of battle, the German government, under pressure from Chancellor Gerhard Schröder, refused to intervene, and the deal succeeded. This shattered the assumption that national champions were immune to foreign takeovers and made clear that Member States had radically different rules on defensive tactics — or none at all.

Internal Market Commissioner Frits Bolkestein, a Dutch liberal, seized on the Mannesmann case to push for harmonised rules. The Commission's 2000 impact assessment found that takeover barriers cost EU companies an estimated 1-2% of market capitalisation annually in reduced efficiency and lost synergies, according to a later study cited in the Commission's 2007 implementation report. But Bolkestein faced a hostile coalition: Germany and Nordic countries, backed by powerful trade unions and industrial holding companies, feared a "wild west" of hostile takeovers; France, led by Finance Minister Laurent Fabius, wanted to preserve national discretion to block bids in strategic sectors.

The European Parliament was the unlikely champion of broader reform. In 2001, MEPs led by British socialist Klaus-Heiner Lehne and German Christian Democrat Karl-Heinz Florenz pushed for a mandatory bid rule that would protect minority shareholders, overriding resistance from some Member States. The Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee voted 27-13 in favour of a strict breakthrough rule that would override all anti-takeover mechanisms — but the Council, under French and German pressure, stripped this out in 2003.

The final compromise was brokered in May 2004 under the Greek presidency. The key trade: the mandatory bid rule became mandatory (Article 5), satisfying minority shareholder advocates, while the breakthrough rule (Article 11) was made optional for Member States. The "reciprocity" clause (Article 12) allowed companies to opt out of the breakthrough rule for two years after defeat of an earlier directive.

What changed? The summer 2002 accounting scandals at Enron and WorldCom had shifted the political climate. Both the Commission and Member States faced mounting pressure from institutional investors — particularly US pension funds — to strengthen minority shareholder protections. The European Corporate Governance Forum, established in 2002, had issued recommendations that aligned with the directive's core. By 2004, the urgency was less about market integration and more about investor confidence after scandals had wiped out billions in shareholder value.

Industry opposition came from two camps: German industrial groups like the Federation of German Industries (BDI) warned that a strong breakthrough rule would allow "locusts" — foreign hedge funds — to dismantle German Mittelstand companies. French companies like Total and LVMH lobbied their government to preserve noyaux durs (hard-core shareholdings). The alternative considered by the Commission was the 13th Company Law Directive, first proposed in 1985 but abandoned after the Mannesmann case; it had been too rigid in prescribing specific thresholds.

The final vote in the Council was not recorded, but the directive passed with a qualified majority — Germany and Sweden were reportedly among the holdouts, while the UK, Netherlands, and the 2004 accession states (e.g., Poland, Hungary) supported it, seeing it as a step toward capital market integration.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Commission proposes 13th Company Law Directive on takeovers; stalls due to Member State divergence.
- **1999** — Vodafone's hostile bid for Mannesmann; first major cross-border hostile takeover exposes regulatory gaps.
- **2000** — Commission issues a new proposal, including mandatory bid rule and breakthrough rule.
- **2001** — European Parliament votes 366-176 in favour of strict breakthrough rule; Council opposes.
- **2003** — Council removes breakthrough rule from draft; compromise negotiations under Irish presidency.
- **2004** — Adopted (21 April) by Parliament in second reading, published in Official Journal (30 April). Member States must transpose by 20 May 2006.
- **2007** — Commission implementation report finds significant divergence in transposition — only 9 Member States fully implemented the directive by deadline.
- **2010** — Commission reviews directive but declines to revise, citing "no clear evidence of systemic failure" in its 2012 report.
- **2014** — Court of Justice rules in *Commission v. Belgium* (Case C-87/12) that Belgium's 8% voting rights threshold for mandatory bids is incompatible with the directive.

## Impact and consequences

The directive reshaped European takeover practice but with uneven results. The mandatory bid rule became a baseline globally — the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO) cited it as a model in 2010. The most significant practical impact was in Member States that lacked such rules: before 2006, Italy had no mandatory bid threshold; after transposition, it set one at 30%, forcing changes for companies like Pirelli and Generali.

Enforcement was initially weak. By the 2007 deadline, only 9 Member States had fully implemented the directive, according to the Commission's 2007 implementation report. Several Member States, including Poland and the Czech Republic, failed to designate independent supervisory authorities for years. The Commission took infringement proceedings against seven Member States — notably Germany, which defended its "opt-out" of the breakthrough rule and was eventually cleared in 2010.

The directive's optional breakthrough rule was a failure: by 2012, only Italy and the Netherlands had adopted it fully, according to the Commission's 2012 report. No major hostile bid since 2004 has invoked breakthrough provisions. This meant that countries like Spain, where several companies held 30%+ stakes that triggered mandatory bids, saw bids collapse because the target's anti-takeover share structures remained intact — as in the 2007 attempted bid for Endesa by E.ON, blocked by Spain's CNMV regulator under national rules.

Industry adaptation was rapid but defensive. German DAX companies, which had average management board turnover of 2.3 years after a successful bid, according to the Commission's 2010 consultation, used the directive's "poison pill" approval requirement (Article 9) to require shareholder votes on defensive measures. France modified its "Loi de Sécurité Financière" to comply, but kept strong state oversight of foreign takeovers in strategic sectors.

The most lasting consequence was jurisdictional: the mandatory bid rule created a floor but not a ceiling. The 2017 Shareholder Rights Directive II (2017/828/EU) extended related protections but left the threshold-setting in Member States' hands. The UK's 30% threshold remained unchanged after Brexit, now governed by the City Code on Takeovers and Mergers — substantively identical to the EU's rule.

## Key questions answered

### Does EU law require someone buying a majority stake in a company to buy out all other shareholders?

Yes. The Takeover Bids Directive's mandatory bid rule (Article 5) requires anyone acquiring a controlling stake — typically 30-50% of voting rights — to make a public offer for all remaining shares at a fair price. This prevents a new owner from forcing minority shareholders to stay in a company they might not want after a change of control.

### Why did it take over a decade for the EU to pass common takeover rules?

The main obstacle was deep disagreement between Member States. Countries like Germany and France wanted to keep the power to block hostile foreign bids to protect "national champions," while the UK and Netherlands pushed for open markets. This deadlock only broke after the 1999 Vodafone-Mannesmann hostile takeover exposed the chaos of 15 different national regimes — plus the 2002 accounting scandals that made minority shareholder protection a political priority.

### Can a company's board block a takeover bid without shareholder approval?

Under the directive, no — at least not without a fight. Article 9 requires that the target company's board get shareholder approval before taking defensive measures that could frustrate a bid. This "non-frustration rule" is mandatory, though Member States can allow companies to opt out. Most countries kept it mandatory, meaning the kind of "poison pills" American companies use are harder in Europe.

### Why did large German companies oppose the directive?

German industrial groups feared it would open the door to foreign hedge funds and private equity firms dismantling Mittelstand companies. The Federation of German Industries (BDI) used the term "locusts" (Heuschrecken) to describe hostile bidders. Germany wanted to preserve the supervisory board system and management-friendly anti-takeover defences — which is why it secured the optional breakthrough rule and a generous five-year transition period.
