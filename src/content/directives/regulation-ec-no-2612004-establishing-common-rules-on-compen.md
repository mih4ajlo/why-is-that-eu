## What is it?

Regulation 261/2004 gives air passengers a legal right to fixed cash compensation when their flight is cancelled, delayed by more than three hours on arrival, or when they are bumped against their will. The amounts — €250 for short-haul, €400 for medium-haul, €600 for long-haul flights over 3,500 km — apply regardless of ticket price. The regulation also requires airlines to provide meals, refreshments, hotel accommodation, and rerouting at no cost, and to refund tickets within seven days for cancelled flights.

The rules cover any flight departing from an EU airport, plus flights into the EU operated by EU-licensed carriers. Airlines can escape compensation only by proving "extraordinary circumstances" — a phrase that has generated more litigation than perhaps any other in European consumer law. Iceland, Norway, and Switzerland apply the same regime through the EEA agreement.

Crucially, the regulation requires no proof of actual damage. A passenger does not need receipts or evidence of lost business; the entitlement is automatic if the flight meets the delay or cancellation threshold and no extraordinary circumstance excuses the airline.

## Why was it introduced?

By the late 1990s, denied boarding had become endemic on European routes. Airlines routinely oversold flights on the assumption that some passengers would not show up, and when too many did, the surplus were bumped — often onto flights the next day, with minimal compensation. An earlier voluntary scheme, Regulation 295/91, set minimum compensation but applied only to denied boarding, excluded charter flights (which carried the bulk of European holiday traffic), and was widely ignored. The Commission's review found that airlines treated the rules as optional and that passengers had no effective remedy.

Transport Commissioner Loyola de Palacio pushed the proposal hard from 2001 onwards, framing it as both a consumer issue and a competition issue: budget carriers like Ryanair and easyJet were transforming European aviation, but passengers had no idea what protections they actually had. The Association of European Airlines lobbied fiercely against binding rules, arguing that voluntary commitments adopted in 2002 were sufficient. The Commission rejected this — the voluntary commitments had no enforcement mechanism and the carriers most likely to strand passengers, the low-cost newcomers, were not signatories.

The September 11 attacks and the collapse of Sabena and Swissair in 2001–2002 sharpened the political mood. Hundreds of thousands of passengers had been left holding worthless tickets with no recourse. The European Parliament, then under co-decision, demanded higher compensation amounts than the Commission had proposed and won that fight in conciliation. The regulation was adopted in February 2004 and took effect on 17 February 2005.

Ryanair and IATA immediately challenged it before the European Court of Justice, arguing it conflicted with the Montreal Convention's liability regime. In its 2006 IATA judgment (C-344/04), the Court upheld the regulation in full, ruling that compensation for delay was distinct from damages and therefore compatible with international law. The decisive expansion came in the 2009 Sturgeon ruling (C-402/07), where the Court held that passengers suffering long delays of three hours or more were entitled to the same compensation as those whose flights were cancelled — a reading the text itself did not obviously require, and which dramatically expanded airline exposure.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Regulation 295/91 introduces minimum denied-boarding compensation; widely seen as toothless.
- **2001** — Commissioner de Palacio publishes proposal for binding compensation across cancellation, delay, and denied boarding.
- **2002** — AEA airlines adopt voluntary commitments; Commission deems them insufficient.
- **February 2004** — Regulation 261/2004 adopted by Parliament and Council.
- **17 February 2005** — Regulation enters into force.
- **10 January 2006** — Court of Justice rejects IATA and Ryanair challenge in Case C-344/04.
- **19 November 2009** — Sturgeon judgment extends compensation right to long delays.
- **2012** — Nelson and TUI judgments (C-581/10 and C-629/10) confirm Sturgeon despite airline pressure to overturn it.
- **2013** — Commission proposes revision to clarify "extraordinary circumstances" and raise delay thresholds; negotiations stall over Gibraltar dispute.
- **2020–2021** — COVID-19 pandemic triggers mass cancellations; Commission warns member states against allowing airlines to issue vouchers instead of cash refunds.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation created an entirely new market: claims management companies such as AirHelp, Flightright, and ClaimCompass take a cut (typically 25–35%) of compensation in return for handling the paperwork and litigation. National courts in Germany, Spain, and Poland are flooded with 261 cases — German courts alone handle tens of thousands annually, according to Commission monitoring reports.

Airlines have responded by aggressively contesting "extraordinary circumstances." Court of Justice rulings have steadily narrowed this defence: bird strikes count as extraordinary (Pešková, C-315/15), but most technical defects do not (Wallentin-Hermann, C-549/07), and wildcat strikes by an airline's own staff do not qualify either (Krüsemann, C-195/17). Ryanair, Lufthansa, and others have at various points been singled out by national enforcement bodies for systematically refusing valid claims.

The 2013 revision proposal — which would have raised the delay threshold for long-haul flights to five hours and clarified the rules — has been frozen in Council for over a decade, primarily because of an unrelated UK–Spain dispute over the application of EU aviation law to Gibraltar airport. After Brexit removed that obstacle, the file remains stalled, with airlines pushing for higher thresholds and consumer groups resisting.

The COVID-19 crisis tested the regulation severely. The Commission and national enforcers reaffirmed in spring 2020 that passengers were entitled to cash refunds within seven days, not vouchers, prompting infringement scrutiny against several member states that had temporarily suspended the rule. According to Commission communications, billions of euros in refunds were owed, and the dispute over voucher schemes reshaped how the EU now thinks about airline insolvency protection.

## Key questions answered

### Can you get cash compensation for a delayed flight in Europe?

Yes. If your flight departs from an EU airport, or arrives in the EU on an EU-based airline, and you reach your destination more than three hours late, you are entitled to between €250 and €600 in cash compensation — independently of any ticket refund. The airline can only refuse if it proves the delay was caused by extraordinary circumstances such as severe weather, air traffic control strikes, or political instability. Routine technical problems do not count.

### Why do airlines fight passenger claims so aggressively?

Because the compensation is a flat sum disconnected from ticket price, a €40 Ryanair fare delayed by four hours triggers €250 in compensation — more than six times the revenue from the seat. The Court of Justice's 2009 Sturgeon ruling extending compensation to long delays, not just cancellations, multiplied airline exposure substantially. Carriers also argue the rules incentivise them to cancel weather-affected flights outright rather than try to operate them late.

### What counts as "extraordinary circumstances"?

The Court of Justice has read this narrowly. Bird strikes, unexpected airport closures, lightning damage, and political unrest qualify. Crew illness, most technical faults, and strikes by the airline's own staff do not, because they are considered inherent to running an airline. This case-by-case approach has frustrated airlines and is one of the main reasons the Commission has tried — unsuccessfully — to revise the regulation since 2013.

### Why has the EU not updated the rules despite all the disputes?

A revision proposed in 2013 has been blocked in Council for over a decade, originally because of a Spanish–British dispute over whether the rules would apply to Gibraltar airport. Brexit removed that obstacle in 2020, but member states remain split: some want higher delay thresholds to relieve airlines, others want stronger enforcement powers. The result is that the framework remains shaped largely by Court of Justice case law rather than fresh legislation.
