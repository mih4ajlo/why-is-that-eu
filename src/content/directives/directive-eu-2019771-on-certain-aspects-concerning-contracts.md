## What is it?

The Sale of Goods Directive (2019/771/EU) sets common EU rules for consumer purchases of physical goods — including those with digital elements like smart TVs or connected fridges — and explicitly covers digital content supplied with goods. It requires sellers to deliver goods that conform to the contract, and when they do not, consumers can demand repair or replacement, a price reduction, or a full refund.

A key innovation is the one-year reversal of the burden of proof: for the first year after delivery, any lack of conformity is presumed to have existed at the time of delivery, shifting the onus to the seller to prove otherwise. The directive also gives national authorities a two-year minimum legal guarantee period, with EU member states free to extend it further. It entered into application on 1 January 2022.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a concrete failure: the fragmentation of consumer contract law across the EU's 28 member states. Before 2019, a consumer buying a faulty washing machine from an online seller in another EU country faced a patchwork of national rules — some offering a two-year guarantee, others one year; some shifting the burden of proof for six months, others for two years. This uncertainty discouraged cross-border e-commerce. According to a 2015 Commission staff working document, only 15% of consumers bought online from another EU country in the previous year, while 55% did so domestically. A 2017 Eurobarometer survey cited in Commission impact assessments showed that 61% of retailers cited legal obstacles, including diverse guarantee rules, as a barrier to cross-border sales.

The trigger came in a courtroom. The 2015 Court of Justice of the European Union ruling in *Faber v. Autohandel* (Case C-497/13) exposed the limits of the existing Consumer Sales Directive (1999/44/EC). Ms. Faber, a Dutch consumer, bought a used car in the Netherlands that later proved defective. The CJEU ruled that national procedural rules could not make it unreasonably difficult for a consumer to enforce the guarantee — but the ruling also highlighted the directive's ambiguous 1999-era language around digital content and smart goods. By 2016, the European Commission under President Jean-Claude Juncker and Commissioner for Justice Věra Jourová decided it was time for a comprehensive rewrite.

The Commission's 2016 impact assessment documented the structural tension: the 1999 directive had been written before the iPhone, before streaming, before "Internet of Things" devices. A "smart" thermostat that failed because its firmware was corrupted did not fit the old legal categories of "goods" or "services." Industry itself was split. Retail associations like EuroCommerce pushed for a consistent single-market rulebook, while manufacturers such as Siemens and Bosch — through DigitalEurope — warned that overly strict liability rules for digital updates could stifle innovation. Consumer groups BEUC and ANEC countered that without clear rules, buyers of connected devices had no legal recourse when software updates broke functionality.

The coalition that passed the directive was unusual: it combined the European Parliament's Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO), led by rapporteur Pascal Arimont (EPP, Belgium), with a Council of member states eager to support e-commerce as a post-financial-crisis growth driver. Germany and France, both with strong consumer protection traditions, formed the core of the Yes coalition. The United Kingdom, still an EU member in 2019, was a key ally — British MEPs helped shape the digital content provisions — and its departure from the EU after Brexit only reinforced the need for a common rulebook.

Opposition came primarily from business lobbies representing durable goods manufacturers. The automotive sector, via ACEA, argued that a two-year guarantee with a one-year reversal of proof would raise costs significantly — though the Commission's impact assessment dismissed the claim, noting that only 2-3% of sales would involve claims in the second year. Eastern European member states worried about the administrative burden on small retailers, and some attempted to negotiate longer transitional periods for micro-enterprises. Ultimately, a qualified majority in the Council prevailed in April 2019 (20 member states in favour, six abstaining — Bulgaria, Croatia, Cyprus, Malta, Portugal, and the UK, for varying reasons).

Timing was critical. The Juncker Commission made the Digital Single Market a top priority, and the Sale of Goods Directive dovetailed with its sibling, the Digital Content Directive (2019/770/EU), which covers purely digital products like streaming and cloud storage. Together, they formed a twin-track approach: one aligning physical goods with digital elements, the other covering standalone digital services. The 2019 European elections added pressure to finalise the file before the May 2019 parliamentary dissolution. The Commission tabled the proposal in December 2015, and after three years of trilogue negotiations — including a 2018 deadlock over whether second-hand sales could be exempted — the final text was agreed in January 2019 and published in May.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Directive 1999/44/EC on consumer sales and guarantees adopted, covering physical goods only
- **2015** — CJEU ruling in *Faber v. Autohandel* (C-497/13) exposes limits of 1999 directive; Commission publishes Digital Single Market Strategy
- **9 December 2015** — Commission proposes new Sale of Goods Directive alongside Digital Content Directive
- **2016-2018** — Trilogue negotiations; key disputes over burden of proof duration, digital content scope, and second-hand goods
- **26 March 2019** — European Parliament adopts directive (573 MEPs in favour, 42 against, 67 abstentions)
- **15 April 2019** — Council adopts directive (qualified majority: 20 member states for, 6 abstentions)
- **20 May 2019** — Directive published in Official Journal
- **1 July 2021** — Member states must transpose into national law
- **1 January 2022** — Directive applies in all EU member states
- **2022-2023** — European Commission launches infringement proceedings against several member states (e.g., Cyprus, Malta, Portugal) for failing to transpose on time

## Impact and consequences

The directive required all 27 EU member states to rewrite parts of their national civil codes, particularly on burden of proof and digital content liability. For consumers, the most visible change is the uniform one-year reversal: if a smart speaker fails within 12 months, the seller must prove it was not defective at delivery — not the buyer. According to the European Commission's 2023 Consumer Protection Cooperation Network data, complaints related to defective goods and guarantees rose 18% in the first year after application, suggesting greater consumer awareness of rights.

Industry adapted unevenly. Large retailers like MediaMarkt and Amazon updated return policies to reflect the new rules, while small hardware stores reported confusion over digital elements — for example, whether a faulty firmware update to a connected fridge constitutes a "lack of conformity." Consumer organisations BEUC reported in 2022 that several member states, including Czechia and Poland, initially transposed the directive with weak enforcement mechanisms.

The directive's most significant long-term impact may be in the smart device market. By treating software updates as integral to the conformity of physical goods, it effectively gave consumers a legal claim when "bricking" updates rendered devices useless — a scenario that had previously left owners of obsolete smart home hubs without remedy. The European Commission's 2023 evaluation noted the directive spurred some manufacturers, notably Philips and Samsung, to extend software support periods for connected home devices.

## Key questions answered

### What happens if a product I bought online in another EU country breaks within a year?

You are entitled to have it repaired or replaced at no cost, or to ask for a price reduction or refund. For the first 12 months after delivery, the seller must prove the fault did not exist at the time of delivery — you don't need to prove it did. This applies equally to a kettle bought locally or a smartphone ordered from a seller in Germany if you live in Spain.

### Do EU rules cover problems with software on my smart device?

Yes, if the digital content (like a fitness app or thermostat firmware) is supplied with the goods and their lack of conformity makes the physical product fail to perform. For example, if your "smart" TV loses streaming functionality due to a faulty update promised at purchase, the seller must remedy it. Purely standalone digital services like Netflix are covered by the separate Digital Content Directive (2019/770/EU).

### Can a seller refuse to repair an expensive item and just offer a refund instead?

No. Under the directive, you have the right to choose between repair or replacement, unless the option you choose is impossible or disproportionate (for example, repair costs being wildly higher than the item's value). The seller cannot force a refund or price reduction on you first — these are remedies you can choose later if repair or replacement fails.

### Why did it take over three years to agree on these EU consumer rights for faulty goods?

The negotiations flagged on three main issues: how long the burden of proof should rest with the seller (the final compromise was one year, not the Commission's proposed two); whether second-hand goods could have a shorter legal guarantee (member states could, but must be at least one year); and how to handle "digital content" embedded in physical goods. The 2018 deadlock was broken when the Parliament accepted a shorter (one-year) burden of proof beyond which the consumer must prove the fault, in exchange for the Council accepting a mandatory two-year legal guarantee period.
