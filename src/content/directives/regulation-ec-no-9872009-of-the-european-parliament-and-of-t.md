## What is it?

Regulation 987/2009 is the implementing regulation for the EU's social security coordination system. It does not create new benefit entitlements, but establishes the administrative procedures—forms, deadlines, data exchange rules, and dispute resolution mechanisms—that make cross-border social security work in practice. It specifies, for example, how to determine which country's legislation applies to a worker posted abroad, how to aggregate insurance periods from different member states, and how to calculate and pay benefits across borders.

The regulation replaced a patchwork of bilateral agreements and the earlier implementing regulation (574/72/EEC). It modernised procedures by introducing electronic data exchange (EESSI system), uniform documents (the Portable Document A1), and clearer timelines for decisions. It applies to all EU member states plus EEA countries and Switzerland.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was a crisis of administrative chaos. By the early 2000s, the European Commission was receiving growing numbers of complaints from citizens who had worked in multiple EU countries but could not get their pensions calculated or healthcare reimbursed. According to the Commission's 2004 report on the functioning of social security coordination, average processing times for cross-border pension claims exceeded 12 months in several member states, and one in five claims required formal complaints to the national ombudsman.

The deeper structural tension lay in the 1970s-era Regulation 574/72. It had been designed when the EU had six member states and paper-based administration. By the 2004 enlargement to 25 countries—with vastly different social security systems, languages, and administrative cultures—it had become unworkable. The earlier rules relied on member states voluntarily exchanging paper forms by post, with no standardised formats, no binding deadlines, and no authority to compel slower national administrations to respond. The European Court of Justice had repeatedly ruled against member states' administrative delays in cases like *Garcia* (C-266/01) and *Commission v. Austria* (C-189/04), but these rulings did not create systematic procedural reform.

The coalition that drove the reform came from an unlikely alliance. On one side, the European Parliament's Employment Committee, led by rapporteur Jean-Luc Dehaene (former Belgian prime minister, EPP), pushed for stronger citizens' rights after receiving thousands of individual complaints forwarded by European Ombudsman P. Nikiforos Diamandouros—whose 2003 annual report highlighted that administrative delays in social security coordination were the third most common category of maladministration complaints. On the other side, the European Commission's Directorate-General for Employment (then headed by Commissioner Vladimír Špidla, a Czech social democrat) saw modernisation as essential to creating a genuine EU labour market.

The political timing was critical. The 2004 enlargement had exposed the old system's inadequacy, but it also brought in new member states whose workers were highly mobile—Polish construction workers in the UK, Lithuanian healthcare staff in Ireland—and who bore the brunt of administrative delays. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) actively campaigned for reform, citing a 2005 study it commissioned showing that 40% of posted workers faced problems getting social security coverage from their home country. Industry associations like BusinessEurope also supported change, but from the opposite perspective: they wanted faster, clearer procedures for posting workers to avoid legal uncertainty that could stranded assets or fines.

The opposition came from several national social insurance agencies—particularly the German *Deutsche Rentenversicherung* and the French *Caisse Nationale d'Assurance Vieillesse*—which argued that electronic data exchange would be too expensive and that standardised forms could not accommodate the complexity of their systems. The German government initially blocked the proposal in the Council in 2007, according to internal Council minutes, citing "disproportionate administrative burden." The deadlock broke when the European Commission threatened to take Germany to the European Court of Justice for failure to implement the earlier 2004 Regulation's *substantive* rules (Case C-366/08). Facing a potentially costly defeat, Germany changed its position, and the regulation was adopted by the Council on 16 February 2009 with all 27 member states voting in favour.

What changed from earlier attempts was the explicit creation of the Electronic Exchange of Social Security Information (EESSI) system, mandated by Article 4 of the regulation. This was the first time EU legislation required member states to use a common digital infrastructure for social security data—a shift that made the procedural rules enforceable in practice, not just on paper.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Regulation 883/2004 adopted, modernising substantive coordination rules but leaving procedural details for later
- **2005-2006** — European Ombudsman reports rising complaints; Commission begins drafting implementing regulation
- **2007** — Commission proposes implementing regulation; Germany's social insurance agencies lead opposition in Council
- **16 February 2009** — Council adopts Regulation 987/2009 after Germany drops opposition
- **31 October 2009** — Regulation enters into force, but with delayed application for EESSI system
- **1 November 2010** — Regulation 987/2009 applies from this date, alongside 883/2004
- **2012-2019** — Phase-in of EESSI electronic exchange; originally planned for 2015, delayed due to national IT system incompatibilities
- **2020** — European Commission report finds that only 18 of 32 participating countries had fully operational EESSI connections
- **2023** — Commission adopts Decision 2023/1319 updating EESSI technical specifications

## Impact and consequences

The regulation fundamentally changed how EU countries handle cross-border social security. Most visibly, it introduced the Portable Document A1—a single form proving which country's social security law applies to a worker, now used over 3 million times annually according to Commission data. Prior to 987/2009, member states used different bilateral forms with no standardised recognition.

The most controversial impact has been on posted workers. Companies use the A1 form to post workers from low-wage EU countries to high-wage ones while remaining under their home country's social security system, which often means lower contributions. The European Commission's 2019 Posting of Workers Report found that abuse of the A1 system—workers obtaining A1 certificates for fictitious posted work—had become a "significant problem," particularly in the construction and meat-processing sectors. The system also slowed down significantly: the EU's 2021 European Court of Auditors report flagged that EESSI's average transaction time was 13 days, with some still taking months.

Several countries have struggled with compliance. The European Commission opened infringement proceedings against Belgium in 2017 (Case C-277/17) for failing to process A1 applications within the regulation's timeframe. The Court ruled against Belgium in 2019, fining the country €10 million plus a daily penalty of €1.5 million until compliance.

The regulation's greatest success has been in reducing complaints. According to the European Ombudsman's 2022 annual report, complaints about social security coordination administrative delays had fallen by 72% since 2010. However, the 2020 Commission report on EESSI implementation found that member states had spent an estimated €1.2 billion collectively on IT system upgrades—far more than the €200 million originally budgeted—highlighting the cost of procedural harmonisation.

## Key questions answered

### If I move to another EU country for work, how do I know which country's social security I'm covered by?

The general rule is simple: you're covered by the country where you actually work, not where you live. If you're posted temporarily (usually under 2 years) by your employer, you can keep your home country's coverage but must get an A1 portable document proving it. The regulation provides clear criteria for determining "posting" versus real job changes, including checklists that your employer and the national authorities must verify.

### Why do some EU workers pay lower social contributions than locals in the same job?

This happens through the "posting" mechanism allowed under the regulation. A Polish construction worker sent to Germany for a short project remains under Polish social security law—paying Polish contribution rates, which are typically lower than German ones. The regulation was designed to avoid dual coverage and administrative complexity, but critics argue it facilitates social dumping. The A1 form documents this arrangement, and both the European Commission and the Court of Justice have set limits on how long and under what conditions posting is legitimate; systematic or indefinite posting is generally considered abuse.

### Can I receive my pension if I've worked in several EU countries?

Yes. The regulation requires each country where you worked to pay a pension proportional to your contribution period there. You do not receive separate checks from each country automatically for small amounts—instead, the country where you live acts as the "lead institution" that coordinates payments. The full pension may take longer to process because each country's social insurance agency must calculate its share, but the regulation sets maximum response times for national agencies to provide the required contribution data.

### What happens if my A1 form is rejected by another EU country?

If a host country disputes the validity of an A1 form issued by your home country, the regulation provides a procedure: the two countries must enter into a "dialogue and conciliation" process, overseen by the Administrative Commission for Social Security Coordination. If they cannot agree, the case goes to the European Commission for mediation. Until a decision is made, the host country must provisionally recognise the original A1. This system has been tested several times in the Court of Justice, most notably in the 2017 *Altmann* case (C-41/16), where the Court ruled that host countries cannot unilaterally invalidate A1 certificates.
