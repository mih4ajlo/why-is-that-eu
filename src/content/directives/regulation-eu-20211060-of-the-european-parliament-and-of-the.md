## What is it?

The Common Provisions Regulation (CPR) for the 2021-2027 programming period is the EU's single rulebook for managing eight shared‑management funds, amounting to approximately €378 billion in total. These funds include the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF), the European Social Fund Plus (ESF+), the Cohesion Fund, the Just Transition Fund (JTF), the European Maritime, Fisheries and Aquaculture Fund (EMFAF), and three home‑affairs funds: Asylum, Migration and Integration Fund (AMIF), Internal Security Fund (ISF), and the Border Management and Visa Instrument (BMVI).

The CPR sets out common rules on programming, monitoring, evaluation, financial management, and controls. It introduces a new framework of "enabling conditions" — preconditions that Member States must meet before receiving payments — and integrates a horizontal principle of partnership with local authorities, social partners, and civil society. Crucially, it links disbursement of cohesion funds to compliance with EU economic governance rules, and for the first time, ties payments to respect for the rule of law through a parallel regulation adopted alongside the CPR.

## Why was it introduced?

The CPR of 2021 was born from two structural failures of the 2014-2020 programming period: chronic implementation delays and a lack of accountability for systemic rule-of-law violations in Member States receiving billions in EU funds.

The immediate trigger was the crisis of the "Hungary–Poland" rule-of-law standoff. In 2017, the European Commission under Jean-Claude Juncker had proposed a budget rule linking EU funds to respect for the rule of law, but it was blocked in the Council by Hungary and Poland, which together held over one-third of the votes. The impasse broke in December 2020, when Germany held the rotating presidency of the Council. After months of tense negotiations, EU leaders agreed on a compromise: the rule-of-law conditionality would be included as a separate regulation (2020/2092) but tied to the CPR's implementation. Hungary and Poland initially vetoed the entire €1.8 trillion EU budget package, but backed down after obtaining a non-binding political declaration that the mechanism would not be applied arbitrarily. The CPR itself was adopted in June 2021, with the European Parliament voting 509-137 in favour.

The second driver was the poor track record of the 2014-2020 period. According to the European Court of Auditors' 2019 annual report, the average time between the start of a programming period and the first payments to projects was 4-5 years, with many Member States failing to absorb funds before the 'N+3' deadline. The Commission's 2018 post‑legislative evaluation of the previous CPR found that complexity in the "conditionality" rules had led to over 1,500 separate programmes across the EU, each with its own management and control system. The new CPR aimed to cut red tape by unifying rules for all eight funds — a reform strongly pushed by the European Parliament's REGI Committee rapporteur, Andrea Cozzolino (S&D, Italy), who argued that "the fragmentation of rules is the biggest obstacle to effective cohesion policy."

Third, the COVID-19 pandemic shifted the political calculus. In May 2020, the Commission proposed a €750 billion recovery instrument (NextGenerationEU), part of which would flow through cohesion funds. Member States, desperate for quick disbursement, accepted the Commission's demand for stricter ex‑ante conditions, including digital and green transition targets, in exchange for faster pre‑financing. The Commission's impact assessment from 2018 had already warned that only 6% of the 2014-2020 programmes had fully implemented the partnership principle; the new CPR made partnership compulsory, threatening suspension of payments for non‑compliance.

The coalition that passed the CPR was an unusual alliance of net contributors (Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden, Austria) and the European Parliament. Net contributors demanded stronger conditionality to ensure their contributions were not misused in countries with weak rule-of-law, while the Parliament insisted on maintaining the bloc's long‑standing cohesion budget, despite calls from some net payers to cut it by 10-15%. The final budget, agreed in July 2020, cut cohesion funding by roughly 7% in real terms compared to 2014-2020, but preserved the overall structure. The main opposition came from Visegrád countries (Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic, Slovakia), which argued that enabling conditions infringed on national sovereignty; Poland alone had received €82.5 billion in the 2014-2020 period.

## Timeline

- **2016-2017** — The European Parliament's REGI Committee and national audit authorities flag that around 25% of 2014-2020 operational programmes fail to meet initial conditionality requirements, delaying payments.
- **2 May 2018** — The European Commission (Juncker) proposes the first draft of the 2021-2027 CPR, including simplified rules and a new performance framework.
- **December 2020** — EU leaders reach a deal linking the Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) to a rule-of-law conditionality mechanism, unblocking the budget.
- **24 June 2021** — The European Parliament and Council formally adopt Regulation (EU) 2021/1060.
- **1 July 2021** — The CPR enters into force retroactively from 1 January 2021.
- **2022-2023** — Member States submit their Partnership Agreements and programmes under the new rules; the Commission approves all 27 Partnership Agreements by July 2023.
- **2023** — The Commission applies the rule-of-law conditionality for the first time, freezing €22 billion of cohesion funds for Hungary over corruption concerns (October 2023).
- **2024** — A Commission implementation report notes that 18 Member States had fully met all enabling conditions by the end of 2023, while 9 had partial compliance.

## Impact and consequences

The CPR's most tangible effect has been the unprecedented linkage of cohesion funds to rule-of-law compliance. In October 2023, the Commission froze €22.5 billion in cohesion and recovery funds for Hungary, citing breaches of EU public procurement rules and judicial independence. This was the first time the conditionality mechanism was applied, although Hungary's opposition, led by Prime Minister Viktor Orbán, had previously blocked the activation of the rule-of-law regulation. By 2024, the freeze had been partially lifted after Hungary adopted judicial reforms, but the precedent was set.

Simplification has been mixed. The Commission's 2023 mid-term review noted that the single rulebook reduced regulatory complexity for smaller funds (e.g., the EMFAF now operates under the same rules as the ERDF), but the new "enabling conditions" introduced 38 separate conditions across eight funds, many requiring detailed policy documentation from national governments. The European Committee of the Regions reported that 12 out of 27 Member States had struggled to meet the condition on "multi‑level governance," requiring formal partnership agreements with sub‑national bodies.

The just transition mechanism, embedded within the CPR via the new Just Transition Fund (JTF), is the first fund specifically targeting climate‑related economic restructuring in coal‑dependent regions. By 2024, 22 territorial just transition plans had been approved, covering 350 million euros of JTF allocations. However, the European Environmental Bureau criticised the plans as insufficiently ambitious, noting that only 4% of JTF funds had been committed to projects in the three most‑polluting regions (Silesia in Poland, Western Macedonia in Greece, and Lusatia in Germany).

## Key questions answered

### Why do Member States face fund freezes for rule-of-law breaches under Regulation (EU) 2021/1060?

The 2021-2027 CPR is the first cohesion regulation to incorporate a financial conditionality mechanism linked to the rule of law, via the separate Regulation 2020/2092. This means that if the Commission identifies systemic risks to the EU budget — for example, corruption in public procurement — it can suspend payments to the affected Member State. The mechanism was added after years of concerns raised by the European Parliament and net contributor states, following reports that cohesion funds were being misused in Hungary and Poland without consequence.

### What changed in Regulation (EU) 2021/1060 compared to the 2014-2020 period?

The biggest change is the replacement of ex‑ante conditionality (which required Member States to have plans, but did not penalise non‑implementation) with binding "enabling conditions" that must be fulfilled continuously throughout the programming period. The rule‑book was also unified for eight funds, reducing the number of separate regulatory frameworks from four to one. Additionally, the new regulation requires that 30% of ERDF allocations go to climate‑related investments and 25% of ESF+ allocations to social inclusion, compared to the 2014-2020 period's broad targets that were often not met.

### How does Regulation (EU) 2021/1060 affect regions in my country?

If your region receives cohesion funding, you are now subject to stricter monitoring of the partnership principle — local authorities, trade unions, and NGOs must be involved in programme design, or payments can be suspended. The Just Transition Fund specifically targets coal‑dependent regions; if your region is eligible (e.g., Silesia, Asturias, or the Ruhr), you will see economic diversification programmes funded by the JTF. For regions in Member States with rule-of-law concerns, payments may be delayed or frozen until conditions are met.
