## What is it?

Dublin III is the EU regulation that decides which single member state must examine an asylum seeker's application. The criteria are hierarchical — family unity comes first, then prior visas or residence permits — but in practice the dominant rule is the "first country of entry": whichever state the applicant first set foot in is responsible. If an asylum seeker moves on to another EU country, that second country can request a "Dublin transfer" back to the responsible state.

The regulation replaced Dublin II (Regulation 343/2003), which had itself replaced the 1990 Dublin Convention. It applies across all 27 EU states plus Norway, Iceland, Switzerland and Liechtenstein. Alongside it sits Eurodac, the EU-wide fingerprint database that allows authorities to detect whether an applicant has already been registered elsewhere.

Dublin III added stronger procedural safeguards than its predecessor — the right to an effective remedy against transfer decisions, limits on the detention of applicants, special protections for unaccompanied minors, and an "early warning" mechanism for member states under pressure. But its core logic — that responsibility falls on the periphery — remained intact, and that is what broke in 2015.

## Why was it introduced?

The European Court of Human Rights had already declared the Dublin system partially broken before Dublin III was even adopted. In its January 2011 *M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece* judgment, the Strasbourg court ruled that Belgium had violated the European Convention on Human Rights by transferring an Afghan asylum seeker back to Greece, where reception conditions amounted to inhuman and degrading treatment. The Court of Justice of the EU followed in December 2011 with *N.S. v. Secretary of State for the Home Department*, prohibiting Dublin transfers to any member state with "systemic deficiencies" in its asylum procedures. Overnight, transfers to Greece stopped across the EU.

Dublin III, negotiated under Cecilia Malmström as Home Affairs Commissioner and adopted in June 2013, was the legislative answer to these rulings. It codified the prohibition on transfers where there is a real risk of inhuman treatment, added the early-warning and crisis-management mechanism, and tightened deadlines. But the Commission and the Council — led by member states that benefited from the geography of first entry, particularly Germany, France and the Netherlands — refused to abandon the "country of first entry" principle. Italy, Greece, Malta and Cyprus had argued throughout the negotiations that this rule was unsustainable for states on the EU's southern maritime border, but they were outvoted.

Two years later their warnings became reality. According to UNHCR data, more than one million people crossed the Mediterranean in 2015, the vast majority landing in Greece. Frontex registered record numbers of irregular border crossings. Greece's asylum system buckled; Italy's reception facilities overflowed. Germany's then-Chancellor Angela Merkel announced in August 2015 that Berlin would no longer apply Dublin returns to Syrian nationals — a de facto suspension of the regulation by its most powerful supporter. The Commission under Jean-Claude Juncker responded with emergency relocation decisions in September 2015 (Council Decisions 2015/1523 and 2015/1601) to move 160,000 asylum seekers out of Greece and Italy. Hungary, Slovakia, the Czech Republic and Poland refused to participate, and the Court of Justice ruled in 2020 (Cases C-715/17, C-718/17, C-720/17) that they had breached EU law.

The structural tension was now impossible to ignore: a regulation built on the assumption that arrivals were manageable and reception conditions broadly equivalent had been designed without solidarity mechanisms strong enough to survive a real crisis. The Commission proposed a "Dublin IV" reform in May 2016, including a corrective allocation mechanism, but it deadlocked in the Council for years over Visegrád opposition to any mandatory relocation. Dublin III was finally replaced by the Asylum and Migration Management Regulation as part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, adopted in May 2024 and applying from mid-2026.

## Timeline

- **1990** — Dublin Convention signed in Dublin, establishing the first-country-of-entry principle outside EU law.
- **2003** — Dublin II Regulation (343/2003) brings the system into EU law.
- **January 2011** — ECtHR rules in *M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece* that transfers to Greece breach human rights.
- **December 2011** — CJEU rules in *N.S.* that transfers cannot proceed where systemic deficiencies exist.
- **June 2013** — Dublin III Regulation adopted; enters into force in July, applies from January 2014.
- **2015** — Mediterranean crisis; UNHCR records over one million arrivals; Germany suspends Dublin returns for Syrians.
- **September 2015** — Council adopts emergency relocation decisions over Hungarian, Czech, Slovak and Romanian opposition.
- **May 2016** — Commission proposes Dublin IV reform; negotiations stall.
- **April 2020** — CJEU rules Poland, Hungary and Czechia breached EU law by refusing to take relocated asylum seekers.
- **September 2020** — Commission's New Pact on Migration and Asylum proposed under Commissioner Ylva Johansson.
- **May 2024** — Asylum and Migration Management Regulation adopted, repealing Dublin III with effect from June 2026.

## Impact and consequences

Dublin III's practical effect was uneven and largely dysfunctional. According to Eurostat data on Dublin procedures, member states issued hundreds of thousands of "take charge" or "take back" requests each year, but only a small fraction — typically under 30% — resulted in actual transfers. The reasons were familiar: applicants absconded, deadlines expired, courts blocked transfers, or receiving states like Greece were legally off-limits.

Frontline states bore the most visible cost. Greece's asylum service, even after reforms, struggled with backlogs that the European Asylum Support Office repeatedly flagged. Reception facilities on the Aegean islands — Moria on Lesbos became the most notorious — were repeatedly condemned by the Council of Europe's human rights commissioner and by NGOs including MSF and the Greek Council for Refugees. Moria burned down in September 2020.

Northern states bore a different cost: secondary movements. Germany received over 440,000 asylum applications in 2015 and again in 2016, according to BAMF figures, and complained that Dublin returns to southern states were impractical. The mutual recriminations — Berlin accusing Rome and Athens of waving migrants north, Rome and Athens accusing Berlin and Vienna of refusing solidarity — poisoned EU migration politics for nearly a decade and contributed directly to the rise of anti-immigration parties across the bloc.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the country where a refugee first arrives in Europe have to handle their asylum claim?

The rule dates back to the 1990 Dublin Convention, when EU governments wanted to prevent "asylum shopping" — applicants lodging claims in multiple countries — and to assign clear responsibility within the emerging Schengen area without internal borders. The logic was that whichever country let someone enter the common area should be accountable for processing them. The unintended consequence was to load almost all responsibility onto the states with external sea or land borders, which is why Italy, Greece, Spain and Malta have spent over three decades trying to renegotiate it.

### Why did the EU asylum system collapse in 2015?

Dublin III had no mechanism for sharing applicants when one country was overwhelmed. When more than a million people crossed the Mediterranean in 2015 — most landing in Greece — the "first country of entry" rule became physically impossible to apply. Greece could not register, house, or process them all, and other member states could not legally send people back to Greece because EU courts had already ruled conditions there were inhuman. The emergency relocation scheme the Commission pushed through was rejected by Hungary, Poland, Czechia and Slovakia, and the system effectively ceased to function as designed.

### What replaces Dublin?

The Asylum and Migration Management Regulation, part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum adopted in May 2024, replaces Dublin III from June 2026. It keeps the first-country-of-entry rule but adds a "mandatory solidarity" mechanism: when a frontline state is under pressure, other member states must either accept relocated asylum seekers, pay a financial contribution (the Commission proposed €20,000 per person not accepted), or provide operational support. Hungary and Poland voted against the package; Italy, France and Germany backed it.

### Did refugees actually get sent back under these rules?

Rarely, in proportion to the requests made. Eurostat data has consistently shown that member states made hundreds of thousands of Dublin transfer requests each year but completed only a small fraction. Applicants disappeared before transfer, statutory deadlines elapsed, courts intervened, or receiving states refused. The gap between the regulation on paper and what happened in practice was one of the main arguments for replacing it.
