## What is it?

The Interreg Regulation (EU 2021/1059) sets the legal framework for the European Territorial Cooperation goal, commonly known as Interreg, under the 2021–2027 EU budget. It determines which cross-border, transnational, and interregional projects can receive funding from the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) and external EU instruments. The regulation defines three strands: cross-border cooperation between adjacent regions (Interreg A), transnational cooperation over larger areas (Interreg B), and interregional cooperation across the whole EU (Interreg C), plus dedicated strands for external borders and outermost regions.

In practice, Interreg finances projects that bring together public authorities, universities, businesses, and civil society from different member states to solve shared problems — from building a cross-border hospital to tackling river pollution or improving public transport links. The 2021 version streamlined rules, increased the co-financing rate for certain strands, and introduced a stronger focus on green transition, digital transformation, and post-pandemic recovery.

## Why was it introduced?

The most direct trigger for the 2021 regulation was the expiry of the 2014–2020 Interreg programmes and the need to align with the new Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) 2021–2027. But the deeper structural tension had been building for decades: EU cohesion policy, while successful in reducing regional disparities within countries, struggled to address problems that do not respect national borders. A polluting factory on one side of a border affects air quality on the other; a missing bridge costs commuters hours; a public hospital in one country cannot serve patients from the next. The existing Interreg programmes (in place since 1990) had proven their worth, but stakeholders — especially border regions and their associations — complained that administrative complexity, low co-financing rates, and rigid project boundaries were holding back impact.

The political coalition that pushed for the new regulation was broad but not uncontested. The European Committee of the Regions and the Association of European Border Regions lobbied heavily for simplification and more money. The European Parliament’s REGI committee, led by rapporteurs such as Pascal Arimont (EPP), repeatedly called for a dedicated Interreg regulation separate from the general cohesion rules. On the other side, some net-contributor member states (notably the Netherlands, Sweden, and Austria) argued for tighter budget discipline and resisted increases. The key breakthrough came in the 2018 Commission proposal under Commissioner Corina Crețu (Cohesion Policy), which proposed a standalone Interreg regulation for the first time — carving it out of the overarching Common Provisions Regulation (CPR). The timing was critical: the Brexit referendum in 2016, the migration crisis of 2015–2016, and the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 each highlighted how urgent cross-border cooperation had become. In March 2020, as EU internal borders snapped shut overnight, the European Commission published a communication on cross-border health crisis management — exposing the legal and operational gaps that Interreg had long tried to address.

What changed between earlier iterations and 2021 was primarily procedural. The new regulation allowed programmes to cover a broader geographical scope (including third countries via external instruments), introduced an ‘external component’ for the first time, raised the ERDF co-financing rate for cross-border strands to 80% (and 85% for outermost regions), and simplified the approval process for small-scale projects. The Commission’s 2018 impact assessment estimated that administrative costs could be cut by up to 20% compared to 2014–2020 rules. The regulation was adopted by the Council on 24 June 2021 and entered into force three days later.

## Timeline

- **1990** — First Interreg Community Initiative launched to support cross-border cooperation.
- **2007** — Interreg becomes a mainstream part of EU cohesion policy for 2007–2013.
- **2015** — European Court of Auditors report criticises complexity of Interreg management and calls for simplification.
- **May 2018** — European Commission proposes the 2021–2027 Interreg Regulation as part of the MFF package, under Commissioner Corina Crețu.
- **February 2020** — European Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement; COVID-19 pandemic underscores urgency.
- **24 June 2021** — Regulation adopted by the Council of the European Union.
- **27 June 2021** — Regulation enters into force (date of publication in the Official Journal).
- **1 January 2022** — New Interreg programmes begin under 2021–2027 framework.

## Impact and consequences

The 2021 Interreg Regulation affects all 27 member states and around 100 cross-border and transnational cooperation programmes. The total EU budget for Interreg 2021–2027 is €8.05 billion (current prices, according to the Commission’s 2021 budget statement). This represents a modest increase in nominal terms over the 2014–2020 envelope of €6.6 billion, though in real terms (adjusted for inflation) it is roughly flat. The regulation’s main impact has been administrative: by removing the need for a separate ‘partner component’ and allowing lump-sum payments for small projects, the Commission estimates that project preparation time has been cut by 30–40%, based on pilot programmes in the Benelux region. Border regions, such as those in the Upper Rhine area, have reported faster approval of joint investments in cross-border health facilities and public transport.

Industry and local authorities have responded positively overall, though some national managing authorities struggled with the new external component (involving third countries like Ukraine, Moldova, and the Western Balkans). For example, the Interreg NEXT programmes for the Mediterranean and Black Sea basins faced delays because of differing procurement rules. A 2023 mid-term evaluation published by the Commission noted that 85% of programme managers found the new regulation ‘significantly simpler’ than its predecessor.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU help regions across borders cooperate?

The EU runs the Interreg programme, which provides funding for projects that involve at least two countries (or regions from different countries) working together on shared challenges — like connecting rural roads, improving flood protection across a river, or setting up cross-border job-training centres. The regulation sets the rules for how this money is spent and which regions are eligible.

### Why is cross-border cooperation important after COVID-19?

The pandemic exposed how quickly internal EU borders can close and how unprepared member states were to manage cross-border health crises, commuting workers, and supply chains. Interreg projects — for example, one that created a cross-border emergency medical coordination network in the EUREGIO region — demonstrated their value during the crisis. The 2021 regulation specifically prioritises health resilience and digital connectivity as funding themes.

### What is the budget for EU cross-border cooperation?

For the 2021–2027 period, the total EU budget for Interreg is about €8 billion, funded from the European Regional Development Fund and external instruments. This is roughly 2.5% of the total EU cohesion budget. Each programme (e.g., Interreg France–Belgium–Netherlands) sets its own budget within this overall envelope, co-financed by national and regional contributions.
