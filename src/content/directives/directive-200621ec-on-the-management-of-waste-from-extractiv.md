## What is it?

The Mining Waste Directive (2006/21/EC) is the EU's primary law governing the management of waste from mining and quarrying operations — a sector that generates approximately 400 million tonnes of waste annually in the EU, according to Eurostat. Its most consequential provisions target "tailings dams," the large earthen embankments used to store the fine-grained, often toxic slurry left after minerals are extracted from ore.

The directive requires operators to obtain permits for all waste facilities, submit financial guarantees for closure and rehabilitation, and implement detailed waste management plans. For "high-risk" facilities — primarily tailings dams — it mandates independent inspections every year, emergency response plans, and a prohibition on constructing new dams that could cause "major accidents" upstream of populated areas. It also requires operators to notify the public and national authorities immediately following any accident with transboundary effects.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct legislative response to two catastrophic tailings dam failures that shocked Europe and revealed a complete regulatory vacuum.

On 30 January 2000, a dam at Aurul SA's gold mine near Baia Mare in northern Romania collapsed, releasing approximately 100,000 cubic metres of cyanide-contaminated tailings — enough to fill 40 Olympic swimming pools — into the Someş River. The toxic plume, carrying an estimated 50-100 tonnes of cyanide, killed nearly all aquatic life in the Someş, Tisza, and Danube rivers, spreading across Romania, Hungary, Serbia, and Bulgaria before reaching the Black Sea. The UN Environment Programme described it as the worst environmental disaster in Central Europe since Chernobyl. Hungarian authorities reported finding dead fish 700 kilometres downstream. The Baia Mare accident killed at least one person directly and devastated the livelihoods of thousands of fishermen and farmers.

Just six weeks later, on 10 March 2000, a second collapse occurred at a different tailings dam at the same Aurul complex, releasing another large volume of waste — this time without cyanide but still heavily contaminated with heavy metals. The operator, Aurul SA, was a joint venture between Australia's Esmeralda Exploration and Romania's state-owned mining company.

The political fallout was immediate and severe. The Romanian government faced furious protests from Hungary and Serbia, both of which threatened to sue at the International Court of Justice. The European Parliament, which had no direct legislative power over mining waste at the time, passed a resolution in March 2000 condemning the accident and calling for EU-wide safety rules. Then-Environment Commissioner Margot Wallström of Sweden personally visited the Baia Mare site and declared that the disaster exposed "a gaping hole in EU environmental law."

The problem was structural: mining waste was excluded from the 1975 Waste Framework Directive (75/442/EEC) and the 1999 Landfill Directive (99/31/EC), on the ground that mining waste was "different" and would be regulated separately — but no separate rules ever materialised. Industry had thus operated for decades in a regulatory vacuum, with tailings dams designed and monitored according to voluntary guidelines at best.

The coalition that pushed through the directive was led by the European Commission's Environment Directorate-General under Wallström, supported by the European Parliament's Environment Committee, whose rapporteur was Danish MEP Lone Dybkjær. The Parliament repeatedly pushed for stronger provisions, including mandatory third-party inspections and liability rules. The mining industry, represented by Euromines and national associations, opposed mandatory financial guarantees and argued that existing national laws in countries like Sweden and Finland already worked well — a position echoed by mining-dependent member states such as Finland and Poland.

The key political battle was over liability and enforcement. The Commission's original 2003 proposal included provisions for operator civil liability for environmental damage — a direct response to Baia Mare, where the joint-venture partners had limited financial responsibility. But the mining industry and several member states successfully blocked these provisions, arguing they would discourage investment. The final directive instead requires only financial guarantees for closure, not for accident damages. The Parliament failed in its last-ditch attempt to reintroduce liability rules in 2006.

Timing was critical. The 2004 EU enlargement brought in Romania and Bulgaria — countries with hundreds of Soviet-era tailings dams in poor condition. The Commission needed rules in place before their accession to prevent the EU from inheriting a ticking regulatory time bomb. The Spanish Aznalcóllar disaster in 1998, where a dam collapse near Seville released toxic sludge that killed fish and contaminated wetlands, had already shown that no country was immune — even though that accident involved a non-cyanide tailings dam. The Baia Mare disaster broke the political deadlock by demonstrating that tailings failures had cross-border consequences the EU's existing water laws could not address.

What changed: the Baia Mare disaster added a transboundary, military-scale threat to the EU's internal environment that forced even mining-friendly member states to accept regulation. Before 2000, mining waste was simply not on the EU legislative agenda — it had been classified as "non-hazardous" by default.

## Timeline

- **1998** — Dam collapse at Aznalcóllar, Spain, releases 4.5 million cubic metres of toxic sludge; no EU legislation results
- **30 January 2000** — Baia Mare dam failure releases cyanide-contaminated tailings into the Someş River, killing fish across four countries
- **10 March 2000** — Second collapse at the same Aurul SA site
- **March 2000** — European Parliament resolution calls for EU mining waste rules; Environment Commissioner Margot Wallström visits Baia Mare
- **2003** — European Commission publishes its first proposal for a Mining Waste Directive, including operator liability clauses
- **2004** — EU enlargement to 25 member states; Romania and Bulgaria begin accession negotiations
- **2005** — Council of Ministers reaches political agreement on a diluted version without liability provisions
- **15 March 2006** — European Parliament votes in final reading to adopt the directive, 439-38, after rejecting liability amendment
- **15 March 2006** — Directive 2006/21/EC enters into force
- **1 May 2008** — Member states required to transpose the directive into national law
- **2010** — Romania and Bulgaria fully subject to the directive after accession
- **2014** — European Commission issues guidance document on tailings dam safety following repeated inspections showing non-compliance
- **2015** — Brazil's Fundão dam disaster at Mariana kills 19 people and destroys villages; prompts EU to review its own rules but no revision follows
- **2019** — Brumadinho dam collapse in Brazil kills 270; European Parliament resolution calls for stronger EU tailings dam rules
- **2020** — Commission's fitness check of the directive finds it "broadly fit for purpose" but notes implementation gaps in older dams

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most visible impact has been on tailings dam inspections and financial guarantees. According to the Commission's 2020 fitness check, 98% of high-risk waste facilities in the EU now have mandatory financial guarantees — up from virtually zero before 2008. The number of major tailings dam failures in the EU has dropped sharply, with no catastrophic collapse on the scale of Baia Mare since 2000.

Industry critics, particularly in Finland and Sweden, argue the directive imposes disproportionate costs on small mining operations. The Commission's own impact assessment acknowledged that compliance costs range from €50,000 for small quarries to over €10 million for large tailings dams, though these figures were contested by environmental NGOs who argued they reflected poor industry estimates.

The directive gave national authorities new powers. Spain's mining inspectorate conducted over 1,500 inspections in the first five years following transposition. Hungary, still traumatised by the Baia Mare spill, implemented particularly strict rules, requiring tailings dams to be licensed as "major hazard" facilities — a status usually reserved for chemical plants.

But the directive has significant gaps. It does not apply retroactively to dams built before 2008 — and many of the EU's oldest, most unstable tailings dams date from the Soviet era or earlier. A 2018 report by the Austrian Environment Agency found that of approximately 600 tailings dams in the EU, at least 80 at "severe" or "critical" risk status had been operating without permits for years. The Commission's 2020 fitness check noted that "the directive's preventive measures are less effective for pre-existing facilities," and that emergency planning requirements "vary significantly across member states."

The Baia Mare disaster's legal aftermath also proved limited. The Romanian government, under EU pressure, prosecuted several Aurul managers — but the company itself was declared insolvent and paid no cleanup costs. The European Commission sued Romania in the European Court of Justice in 2007 for failing to clean up the site, but the case dragged on for years. The Baia Mare site itself was only remediated in 2010-2012 using EU structural funds — at a total cost estimated at €45 million by Romanian authorities, most of which came from European taxpayers rather than the mining company.

## Key questions answered

### Does EU law prevent another cyanide spill from a mine?

The directive now requires financial guarantees and annual inspections for high-risk mining waste facilities, including those storing cyanide-laced tailings, but does NOT require cyanide-free processing methods. A formal ban on cyanide mining — proposed by the European Parliament in 2006 and again in 2010 — was defeated after industry lobbying and opposition from Bulgaria and Romania. The directive instead sets strict limits on cyanide concentrations in new tailings dams and requires immediate notification for any accident with transboundary effects. Several EU countries, including Hungary and the Czech Republic, have since adopted national bans on cyanide mining.

### Can an EU country still get away with a tailings dam disaster today?

Legally, no — the directive requires operators to have financial guarantees for closure and rehabilitation, and national authorities must inspect high-risk dams yearly. But the Commission's 2020 fitness check found that enforcement varies widely: Finland and Sweden have near-complete compliance, while Greece, Portugal, and several Eastern European member states have significant backlogs of unpermitted old dams. The directive's biggest loophole is that it does not apply retroactively to dams built before 2008, and there is no EU-wide liability regime for catastrophic accidents — meaning victims in another member state can only sue under the host country's national law, which may be weak.

### Why didn't the EU ban cyanide mining entirely?

The European Parliament tried. In 2006, MEPs voted to include a ban on using cyanide in the Mining Waste Directive, but the European Council — representing member states — refused, citing the economic importance of gold mining to countries like Romania, Bulgaria, and Greece. The mining industry, through Euromines, argued that cyanide was essential for processing low-grade gold ore and that alternative methods did not exist at commercial scale. A 2010 citizens' initiative (the "Ban Cyanide" campaign) collected over 1 million signatures, but the Commission declined to propose legislation, citing a 2013 industry-funded study that claimed a ban would cost 5,000 jobs in Bulgaria alone. Instead, the Commission has focused on stricter monitoring and enforcement of existing limits.
