## What is it?

The Rule of Law Conditionality Regulation is a legal mechanism that ties member states' access to EU budget funds — worth over €1 trillion in the 2021-2027 multi-annual financial framework — to their compliance with rule of law principles. It empowers the European Commission to propose, and the Council to adopt by qualified majority, measures including suspension of payments, reduction of commitments, or prohibitions on new legal commitments if a member state is found to be breaching rule of law standards that jeopardise the EU's financial interests.

The regulation covers all EU funds managed under shared management, including cohesion policy funds, agricultural subsidies, and the Recovery and Resilience Facility. It defines "rule of law" through Article 2 of the Treaty on European Union, referencing principles including legality, legal certainty, prohibition of arbitrary executive power, effective judicial protection, separation of powers, and non-discrimination. Measures can be applied only where there is a "sufficiently direct link" between the rule of law breach and the impact on the budget — a key threshold that has shaped its enforcement.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to the political crisis over the rule of law in Hungary and Poland that had festered since 2015. The trigger was not a single incident but a growing recognition that existing tools — Article 7 TEU proceedings and infringement actions — were incapable of protecting EU funds from systematic violations. By early 2020, the European Commission had already launched 12 infringement procedures against Poland over judicial independence, while Article 7 proceedings against both Hungary (initiated by the European Parliament in 2018) and Poland (by the Commission in 2017) remained stalled in the Council because they required unanimity to impose sanctions.

The structural tension was clear: member states whose governments were dismantling judicial independence and attacking anti-corruption bodies were simultaneously receiving tens of billions of euros in EU structural funds. The Commission's 2019 "Rule of Law Review Cycle" showed that Hungary's Opacity Index (tracking corruption perception) had worsened every year since 2014, while Poland's judicial reforms had been repeatedly condemned by the Court of Justice. Yet neither state faced any financial consequence under the existing budget rules, which treated rule of law compliance as a political rather than fiscal matter.

The coalition that passed the regulation was a fragile bargain between the European Parliament, the Commission, and the majority of member states, held together by the scale of the 2020 budget negotiations. The European Parliament made the conditionality regulation its central demand for approving the €1.8 trillion 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework and NextGenerationEU recovery fund. In December 2020, Parliament's lead negotiator José Manuel Fernandes (EPP) warned that without a binding mechanism, "we will not vote for the budget." The Commission under Ursula von der Leyen, who had made "protecting the rule of law" a flagship priority, backed the Parliament's position.

Germany, holding the rotating Council presidency in late 2020, brokered the compromise that unlocked the deal. German Chancellor Angela Merkel, who had previously been hesitant about linking funds to rule of law, shifted position after the Court of Justice's June 2020 ruling (Case C-64/18) that EU law allows conditionality measures as long as they are proportionate and linked to budget protection. Germany faced its own pressure: seven member states — including the Netherlands, Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Austria, Luxembourg, and Belgium — had formed a "frugal bloc" that insisted on stricter financial oversight and rule of law enforcement as the price of approving the massive recovery fund.

Poland and Hungary voted against the regulation in the Council (with their opposition recorded in the minutes), but the qualified majority vote of 24 member states in favour easily exceeded the 55% of member states (15) and 65% of EU population thresholds. The regulation was adopted on 16 December 2020, alongside the Multiannual Financial Framework regulation, and entered into force on 1 January 2021.

Timing was decisive: the COVID-19 pandemic had created an unprecedented fiscal emergency requiring unanimous backing for the EU's sovereign borrowing and the €750 billion NextGenerationEU fund. Hungary and Poland initially threatened to veto the entire EU budget unless they received assurances that the conditionality regulation would not be used against them. In a late-night negotiation at the European Council in July 2020, Prime Ministers Viktor Orbán and Mateusz Morawiecki extracted a Declaration stating that the Commission would produce guidelines before applying the regulation — a delay mechanism. However, the European Parliament refused to accept this as binding, and the Commission's formal guidelines were never published after legal experts concluded they would constrain the regulation's application.

What changed was the European Court of Justice's February 2022 ruling (Cases C-156/21 and C-157/21) that dismissed Hungary and Poland's legal challenges to the regulation. The Court held that the regulation's link to the budget was valid and that the EU had competence to protect the sound financial management of its funds, regardless of whether the rule-of-law breaches were "systemic" or linked to individual programmes. This ruling removed the last legal obstacle and triggered the Commission's first formal notification under the regulation in April 2022 against Hungary.

## Timeline

- **2017** — European Commission triggers Article 7(1) TEU against Poland over judicial reforms
- **2018** — European Parliament triggers Article 7(1) TEU against Hungary; both proceedings stall in Council
- **May 2018** — Commission proposes the Rule of Law Conditionality Regulation as part of the 2021-2027 MFF legislative package
- **October 2019** — European Parliament adopts its position, strengthening the mechanism to cover broader rule of law concerns
- **July 2020** — European Council agreement on MFF and NextGenerationEU includes a watered-down declaration on conditionality; Hungary and Poland threaten veto
- **December 2020** — Regulation adopted by qualified majority in Council (24 in favour, Hungary and Poland opposed); European Parliament approves; enters into force 1 January 2021
- **March 2021** — Hungary and Poland file legal challenges at the European Court of Justice (Cases C-156/21 and C-157/21)
- **February 2022** — Court of Justice dismisses both challenges, upholding the regulation's legality
- **April 2022** — Commission sends first formal notification under the regulation to Hungary, initiating a two-month dialogue period
- **September 2022** — Commission concludes that Hungary has not adequately addressed concerns over public procurement, conflict of interest, and judicial independence; proposes freezing €13.3 billion in cohesion funds
- **December 2022** — Council adopts decision cutting €6.3 billion of Hungary's 2021-2027 cohesion fund commitments (approximately 55% of the total conditional amount), after Hungary passed a "compromise" law on judicial independence and anti-corruption measures
- **2023** — Hungary's released funds conditionally unfrozen in phases as it adopts additional measures, including setting up an Independent Anti-Corruption Authority and publishing public procurement data

## Impact and consequences

The regulation has been applied exclusively against Hungary, making it the only EU member state to face budget conditionality measures. The €6.3 billion freeze imposed in December 2022 was the largest financial penalty ever imposed by the EU on a member state. According to the Commission's 2023 Rule of Law Reports, Hungary complied with 17 of the 27 "super milestones" required for fund access, but outstanding issues — including weaknesses in prosecutorial independence and transparency of political parties' finances — prevented full recovery. As of late 2024, approximately €20 billion of Hungary's total EU allocations remained frozen under multiple instruments including the conditionality regulation.

The regulation's impact on Poland was indirect but significant. While the Commission did not apply the regulation to Poland, its existence created new leverage. In 2023, the Commission linked approval of Poland's €60 billion Recovery and Resilience Plan to the country adopting judicial reforms, including replacing the controversial disciplinary chamber with a new Chamber of Professional Responsibility. The Polish government under Mateusz Morawiecki resisted, and the funds remained frozen until after the October 2023 elections, when Donald Tusk's pro-European coalition took power. The new government rapidly passed legislation to restore judicial independence, and in February 2024 the Commission approved €6.3 billion in payments.

Industry and legal experts have noted the regulation's narrow focus. It applies only to budget-linked breaches, not to rule of law violations generally. The Commission's guidelines make clear that isolated incidents or individual court rulings do not trigger the mechanism; it requires "systemic" or "generalised" deficiencies. This design was deliberate: according to the Commission's 2018 explanatory memorandum, the goal was to avoid duplicating Article 7 and to create a financial tool with different triggers and consequences.

The regulation has faced criticism from both sides. Defenders of the mechanism, including European Parliament rapporteurs Petri Sarvamaa (EPP) and Eider Gardiazabal Rubial (S&D), have argued that the Commission has been too cautious in applying it, waiting nearly two years after the Court's ruling before taking action. Critics, particularly from Hungary, have claimed the mechanism politicises EU funding. In March 2024, the Court of Justice dismissed Hungary's follow-up challenge (Case C-123/23) seeking annulment of the Council's decision to freeze funds.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU cut funding to countries that ignore its courts?

Yes, but only under specific conditions. The Rule of Law Conditionality Regulation allows the EU to suspend payments from EU budget funds to member states where rule of law breaches are found to threaten the sound financial management of EU money. This requires the Commission to establish a "sufficiently direct link" between the breach and the budget impact — for example, if a country's judiciary is not independent enough to review public procurement decisions or if anti-corruption bodies are weakened. The mechanism has been used only against Hungary, with €6.3 billion in cohesion funds frozen in December 2022. A similar threat against Poland was resolved through the Recovery and Resilience Facility, where the Commission linked fund access to judicial reforms.

### Why did the EU wait so long before cutting off funds to Hungary and Poland?

The EU did not have a legal tool capable of doing so until 2021. Before the Rule of Law Conditionality Regulation, the EU's primary enforcement mechanism was Article 7 TEU, which requires unanimity to impose sanctions — meaning the targeted member state's allies (also facing rule of law criticisms) could block action. Article 7 proceedings against Hungary and Poland, launched in 2018 and 2017 respectively, remain stalled in the Council. The conditionality regulation created a new route using qualified majority voting, but it was immediately challenged legally by Hungary and Poland, delaying its first use until April 2022. The Commission also moved cautiously to avoid legal defeat: it waited for the European Court of Justice to dismiss both governments' challenges in February 2022 before issuing its first formal notification.

### Is the EU's rule-of-law funding rule the same as withholding money for political reasons?

No. The regulation explicitly ties funding suspensions to specific, verifiable rule of law conditions that affect EU budget management. The Commission must identify concrete breaches (such as the weakening of anti-corruption prosecutions or the politicisation of public procurement oversight) and demonstrate how they endanger EU funds. The €6.3 billion freeze against Hungary was justified by 17 specific findings, including that Hungary's public procurement system showed a single winner in over 90% of EU-funded tenders between 2018-2021, creating a high risk of conflict of interest. The Council's decision to freeze only 55% of Hungary's conditional allocation reflected the requirement that measures be proportionate. Political disagreements unrelated to budget management — such as disagreements over migration policy or media freedom — cannot trigger the mechanism.
