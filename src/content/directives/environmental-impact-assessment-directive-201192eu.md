## What is it?

The Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) Directive requires that public and private projects likely to have significant environmental effects — such as motorways, airports, large industrial installations, waste treatment plants, and hydropower dams — undergo a structured environmental assessment before receiving development consent. The directive sets out a procedural framework: the developer must submit an environmental impact statement; the competent authority consults environmental authorities, affected public, and neighbouring member states; and the final decision, including any mitigation measures, must be made public.

The 2011 version codified and replaced earlier EIA directives (1985/337/EEC and its three amendments) into a single consolidated text. It is not a new directive so much as a recast — the core obligations date from 1985. The recast simplified the legal framework and clarified screening procedures, but the most substantive changes came in the 2014 amendment (2014/52/EU), which updated the annexes, added mandatory climate change and human health assessments, and strengthened monitoring requirements.

## Why was it introduced?

The original 1985 EIA Directive (85/337/EEC) was Europe's response to a growing recognition — driven by environmental disasters and legal challenges in the 1970s and early 1980s — that member states' planning systems were chronically ignoring environmental consequences of large projects. The immediate trigger was not a single catastrophe but a series of high-profile conflicts: the construction of the Austrian Hainburg power plant on the Danube in 1984, which sparked massive protests and a police crackdown; the French Civaux nuclear reactor controversy; and German opposition to the Wackersdorf nuclear reprocessing plant in Bavaria. These clashes exposed a structural gap: no EU-wide mechanism required governments to consider alternatives or inform affected citizens before granting permits.

The Commission's first proposal, in 1980 under President Gaston Thorn and Environment Commissioner Karl-Heinz Narjes, drew on the US National Environmental Policy Act of 1969, which had introduced environmental impact statements. But opposition was fierce. The European Parliament's environment committee, led by German MEP Ursula Schleicher, pushed for mandatory assessments, while the Council — dominated by national planning ministries — resisted binding rules, arguing it infringed on member state sovereignty over land use. The breakthrough came in 1984, when the European Court of Justice's ruling in *Commission v. Germany* (Case 222/83) confirmed that the EU had competence to legislate on environmental impact assessment under what was then Article 235 of the EEC Treaty. That legal unblocking, combined with public outrage over the Hainburg protests, gave the Commission the leverage to push the directive through Council in June 1985, with a qualified majority — only Denmark, the Netherlands, and Germany voted in favour, while France, Italy, and the UK abstained.

The 2011 recast was a mostly technical consolidation — the Commission, under President José Manuel Barroso and Environment Commissioner Janez Potočnik, proposed a codification in 2008 to simplify the legal text after four amendments had made the directive difficult to read. The recast was adopted without major controversy, but it laid the groundwork for the 2014 reform, which responded to widespread criticism that the 1985 framework was failing: a 2009 Commission evaluation found that screening practices varied wildly across member states, with some countries exempting entire categories of projects from assessment (e.g., Finland's national forestry law exempted peat extraction on hundreds of hectares). The 2014 amendment was driven by a coalition of environmental NGOs (BirdLife Europe, Friends of the Earth), the European Parliament's Greens/EFA group, and the Commission's legal services, which warned that the directive was vulnerable to legal challenge under the Aarhus Convention on public participation.

## Timeline

- **1969** — US National Environmental Policy Act introduces environmental impact statements, inspiring European discourse.
- **1980** — European Commission, under President Gaston Thorn, proposes first EIA directive.
- **1984** — ECJ ruling in *Commission v. Germany* (Case 222/83) confirms EU competence for EIA legislation.
- **1985** — Council adopts Directive 85/337/EEC on the assessment of the effects of certain public and private projects on the environment.
- **1997** — Amendment 97/11/EC brings the directive into line with the UNECE Espoo Convention on transboundary environmental impact.
- **2003** — Amendment 2003/35/EC aligns the directive with the Aarhus Convention on public participation.
- **2009** — Amendment 2009/31/EC adds carbon capture and storage projects to the scope.
- **2011** — Recast Directive 2011/92/EU consolidates the text; enters force on 17 February 2012.
- **2014** — Amendment 2014/52/EU introduces mandatory climate change and human health assessments, updates annexes, and strengthens quality control.
- **2017** — Member states required to transpose the 2014 amendment by 16 May 2017.
- **2020** — European Commission opens infringement proceedings against Cyprus, Greece, and Slovakia for incomplete transposition of the 2014 amendment.

## Impact and consequences

The EIA Directive has profoundly reshaped project planning across Europe. According to a 2022 Commission report on the directive's application, over 80% of member states now report that EIA procedures have led to project modifications — most commonly the rerouting of transport infrastructure to avoid protected habitats, the addition of noise barriers, and the redesign of industrial discharge systems. The directive has also been a key tool for the European Court of Justice, which has issued over 40 rulings on EIA matters; the most consequential was the 2004 "Dublin" case (C-127/02), where the Court ruled that screening decisions must be made public and challengeable in court, overturning practices in Ireland and the UK where screening was an internal administrative act.

Industry groups, particularly the construction and energy sectors, have consistently criticised the directive for adding 12-18 months to project timelines. The European Construction Industry Federation (FIEC) estimated in a 2018 position paper that EIA procedures add 5-15% to total project costs for large infrastructure, though the Commission's 2019 evaluation concluded that early identification of environmental risks reduces cost overruns from legal challenges. Countries with the longest average EIA processes — Germany and the Netherlands, where public consultations regularly run 2-3 years — now face pressure from the European Commission to streamline procedures, while Bulgaria and Romania have been accused of allowing "fake impact assessments" that merely rubber-stamp government projects; the Commission opened infringement proceedings against Bulgaria in 2022 for failing to assess a ski resort in the Pirin National Park.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive (2011/92/EU) apply to private projects and not just public ones?

The original 1985 directive was restricted to public projects — those initiated by state authorities — because member states argued that private developers should not face the same administrative burdens. But in the 1990s, the Commission noted that private projects (chemical plants, shopping centres, wind farms) were causing major environmental damage without assessment. Amendment 97/11/EC extended the scope to all projects listed in Annex I (mandatory assessment) and Annex II (screening by member states), regardless of ownership, after the ECJ confirmed in Case C-72/95 (*Kraaijeveld*) that private projects could not be arbitrarily exempted.

### Why did the 2014 amendment to the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive (2011/92/EU) add climate change to the assessment criteria?

Before 2014, the directive required assessment of "effects on climate" only indirectly through air quality or greenhouse gas emissions. The Commission's 2012 impact assessment on the reform noted that 85% of EIA reports examined ignored climate impacts entirely, even for projects like coal-fired power plants or deforestation. The amendment was pushed by MEPs Bas Eickhout (Greens/EFA) and the Parliament's rapporteur for the dossier, after the Fukushima nuclear disaster in 2011 demonstrated that climate risks (sea-level rise, extreme weather) could trigger cascading failures in infrastructure. The final text required assessment of "the impact of the project on climate" (e.g., carbon emissions and climate resilience) as a mandatory category.

### How does the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive (2011/92/EU) interact with the Aarhus Convention on public participation?

The Aarhus Convention, which the EU ratified in 2005, guarantees the public's right to participate in environmental decision-making. The EIA Directive implements Aarhus by requiring that the "public concerned" — including environmental NGOs — have access to the environmental impact statement and a reasonable opportunity to comment before the decision is made. The 2003 amendment (2003/35/EC) was specifically aimed at bringing the directive into compliance with Aarhus, following a non-compliance finding by the Aarhus Compliance Committee in 2002. The European Court of Justice's 2011 ruling in *Brussels Airport* (C-165/09) confirmed that this participation right extends to non-residents whose environment is affected by transboundary projects.
