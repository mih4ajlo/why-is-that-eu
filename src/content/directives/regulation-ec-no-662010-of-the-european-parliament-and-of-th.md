## What is it?

The EU Ecolabel Regulation created the "EU Flower" — a voluntary, multi-criteria environmental label that manufacturers and retailers can apply to products and services meeting rigorous ecological standards across their entire lifecycle, from raw material extraction to disposal. Unlike single-issue labels (e.g. "recyclable" or "organic"), the Flower evaluates multiple environmental impacts: energy consumption, water use, waste generation, toxicity, and biodiversity effects, among others. The scheme is administered by the European Commission, Member State competent bodies, and the EU Ecolabelling Board (EUEB), which sets criteria for specific product groups.

The label is voluntary — companies choose to apply for it — but once awarded, it is valid across all EU member states, Norway, Liechtenstein, and Iceland. The regulation replaced a patchwork of earlier Council Decisions and aimed to harmonise the scheme, make it more credible, and ensure that green claims could be verified. As of 2023, over 80,000 products across 24 product groups carried the EU Flower, according to the Commission’s 2023 evaluation report.

## Why was it introduced?

The EU Ecolabel was born from a crisis of credibility. In the early 1990s, European consumers were flooding into supermarkets convinced that “green” products meant something — until a 1991 Dutch consumer survey, published by the Dutch Ministry of Housing, found that 70% of environmental claims on packaging were either vague, misleading, or outright false. The British Advertising Standards Authority logged similar complaints: in 1992, it ruled against Procter & Gamble for claiming its “Ariel Ultra” was “friendlier to the environment” without substantiation, and against Shell for ads implying its petrol was “cleaner” than competitors’. These were not isolated incidents. A 1993 study by the OECD’s Environment Directorate documented at least 12 national labelling schemes across Europe (Blue Angel in Germany, NF Environnement in France, Nordic Swan in Scandinavia) with wildly different criteria, creating confusion for both consumers and cross-border traders.

The structural tension was clear: the single market required free movement of goods, but each country’s label created a de facto barrier. A German washing machine with the Blue Angel could not easily market its green credentials in France unless it also paid for NF Environnement certification — a costly duplication. Meanwhile, companies making genuinely greener products saw their advantages eroded by competitors slapping unsubstantiated “eco” claims on packaging. The Commission’s 1991 proposal for a voluntary EU-wide scheme, drafted under Environment Commissioner Carlo Ripa di Meana, argued that without harmonisation, the internal market would fragment into “green protectionism.” Industry associations like UNICE (now BusinessEurope) and the European Chemical Industry Council (CEFIC) opposed mandatory rules, warning that binding criteria would stifle innovation. The alternative — the voluntary EU Ecolabel — was a compromise that satisfied neither environmentalists nor industry entirely but broke the deadlock.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. The European Parliament’s Environment Committee, chaired by Italian Green MEP Alexander Langer, pushed for a mandatory scheme with strict lifecycle assessments. The Council, led by a blocking minority of Germany, France, and the UK, insisted on voluntary participation and national control over criteria-setting — fearing that Brussels could override established national labels. The final Regulation (EEC) No 880/92, adopted in March 1992, was a weak compromise: voluntary, with criteria set by national competent bodies, and no enforcement mechanism against false claims. The Flower label launched in 1993, but uptake was abysmal — by 1998, only 86 products had been awarded the label across the entire EU, according to the Commission’s 1999 review.

Timing unblocked the reform. The turning point came with a series of scandals in the late 1990s and early 2000s. In 1998, the Swedish Consumer Agency found that 80% of environmental claims on products sold in Sweden were unverifiable. In 2000, a UK Advertising Standards Authority investigation into “biodegradable” plastic bags revealed that many of the bags labelled as such did not actually biodegrade in landfill conditions. The European Court of Justice’s 2003 ruling in Case C-322/01 — *Deutscher Apothekerverband v 800 Flowers* — clarified that national bans on misleading green claims were permissible under EU law, but the patchwork of enforcement remained.

The decisive moment came after the 2004 enlargement, when the EU grew from 15 to 25 member states, each with its own labelling culture. In 2005, Commission President José Manuel Barroso announced a “Better Regulation” agenda, and Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy and Environment Commissioner Stavros Dimas launched a review of the Ecolabel scheme. A 2006 pan-European consumer survey, commissioned by the EU and published by the European Opinion Research Group, found that 74% of EU citizens wanted a single, trusted environmental label — but only 3% could identify the EU Flower brand. Industry opposition softened: by 2007, both the European Retail Round Table (which included Carrefour, Tesco, and Metro) and the European Association of Chemical Distributors (FECC) formally supported a stronger, harmonised scheme — provided it remained voluntary.

The 2010 Regulation replaced the 1992 system with centralised criteria-setting by the Commission, faster update cycles (every 3–5 years), and a mechanism to verify claims. Crucially, it also introduced a “green claims” enforcement framework: Member States were required to set up verification bodies to check that labelled products actually met the criteria. The regulation passed the European Parliament with 527 votes in favour, 52 against, and 32 abstentions in November 2009. The main opposition came from MEPs from Poland and the Baltic states, who argued that lifecycle assessment criteria would disadvantage their less industrialised economies.

## Timeline

- **1992** — Regulation (EEC) No 880/92 establishes the voluntary EU Ecolabel scheme.
- **1993** — The EU Flower logo is launched; by 1998, only 86 products carry the label.
- **1998-2000** — National consumer agencies in Sweden and the UK expose widespread misleading green claims.
- **2003** — ECJ ruling in *Deutscher Apothekerverband* confirms Member States can ban misleading environmental claims.
- **2005** — Commission reviews the Ecolabel as part of Barroso’s Better Regulation agenda.
- **2006** — Survey finds only 3% of EU citizens recognise the Flower label; 74% want a single trusted ecolabel.
- **2008** — Commission proposes Regulation 66/2010 to overhaul the scheme.
- **2009** — Regulation adopted: 527 votes in favour, 52 against (EP), supported by the Council.
- **2010** — Regulation (EC) No 66/2010 enters into force (20 February 2010).
- **2017** — Commission evaluation report finds the Flower label is on 50,000+ products but remains unknown to most consumers.
- **2020** — The European Green Deal commits to strengthening the Ecolabel as a tool for circular economy.
- **2023** — Commission evaluation shows 80,000+ products carry the Flower; the scheme is updated for 24 product groups.

## Impact and consequences

The 2010 Regulation significantly expanded the EU Flower’s reach: from roughly 2,000 products in 2005 to 80,000+ by 2023, according to the Commission’s 2023 evaluation. However, its impact on green marketing claims is harder to measure. The regulation created a uniform benchmark — a company that wins the Flower has a verifiable, lifecycle-based credential that competitors cannot easily copy — but it remains optional. National labels (Blue Angel, Nordic Swan) continue to thrive; Germany’s Blue Angel alone covers 120,000+ products, outpacing the EU Flower.

Industry response was mixed. Large retailers like IKEA and multinationals like Henkel and Procter & Gamble have invested in the Flower label for specific product lines (detergents, paper, paint), using it to fend off greenwashing accusations. Smaller companies often find the application process too costly and bureaucratic. A 2016 Commission survey found that 62% of SMEs considered the cost of certification a barrier. The regulation has not eliminated greenwashing — the 2024 Commission proposal for a “Green Claims Directive” acknowledges that 53% of environmental claims in the EU are still vague or misleading.

Member State compliance has been uneven. Northern European countries (Sweden, Denmark, Germany) have robust verification bodies and actively promote the label; Southern and Eastern member states have weaker enforcement. In 2022, the Commission launched infringement procedures against six member states (including Poland, Bulgaria, and Romania) for failing to designate competent bodies or report on verification activities.

## Key questions answered

### What is the EU flower label on products?

The EU Flower is a voluntary environmental label given to products that meet strict, independently verified ecological criteria across their entire lifecycle — from raw materials to disposal. It covers everything from detergents and cleaning products to paper, furniture, and tourist accommodation services. The label is meant to help shoppers quickly identify genuinely greener options without needing to parse complex eco-claims.

### Can a company just write "eco-friendly" on a product in the EU?

Technically yes, but they risk enforcement actions. The EU has rules against misleading commercial practices (Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC), and national authorities can order companies to withdraw or modify green claims that cannot be proven. The EU Flower label provides a verified, standardised alternative. However, many companies continue to make unsubstantiated claims; the proposed 2024 Green Claims Directive would make it mandatory to verify such claims before publishing them.

### Why is the EU flower label so little known compared to national labels?

The EU Flower was launched in 1993 with minimal marketing and was initially complicated for companies to apply for, leading to very low uptake. National labels like Germany’s Blue Angel (1978) and the Nordic Swan (1989) had decades of consumer trust and simpler application processes. A 2006 EU survey found only 3% of citizens recognised the Flower; by 2023, recognition had improved but remained low compared to national equivalents. The Commission has allocated €14 million for promotion campaigns between 2021-2027 under the Green Deal.

### Does the EU flower label apply to food?

No. The EU Flower does not apply to food, drinks, or pharmaceutical products. Food environmental claims are governed by separate rules: organic farming is covered by Regulation (EU) 2018/848, and other claims fall under the Food Information to Consumers Regulation (1169/2011). The EU Flower focuses on non-food products and services (cleaning products, electronics, textiles, tourism accommodation, etc.).
