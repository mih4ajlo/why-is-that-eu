## What is it?

The Fourth Railway Package is a set of three legal acts that together open domestic passenger rail markets to competition across the European Union, while simultaneously tightening technical standards to ensure safety and interoperability between national networks. Its market pillar (Regulation 2016/2370/EU and accompanying Directive 2016/2337/EU on public service obligations) requires that from 2019, railway undertakings from any member state may offer domestic passenger services on any EU rail line, subject to a competitive tendering process for routes supported by public subsidies. The technical pillar (Directives 2016/797/EU on interoperability and 2016/798/EU on safety) centralises vehicle authorisation and safety certification at the EU level through the European Union Agency for Railways, replacing the previous system of repeated national approvals.

The package builds on earlier reforms. The First Railway Package (2001) opened the trans-European rail freight network; the Second (2004) extended that to all freight; and the Third (2007) opened international passenger services. The Fourth tackled the last and largest segment: domestic passenger rail, which in most member states had remained a state-owned monopoly responsible for 90% or more of passenger rail travel.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate political trigger was the European Commission’s 2011 White Paper on Transport, which set a target of shifting 50% of medium-distance passenger and freight journeys from road to rail by 2050, and which argued that "a fully integrated railway market" was essential to achieve that. But the deeper driver was a decades-long structural tension: Europe’s railways were losing modal share year after year — according to Eurostat, rail’s share of inland passenger transport in the EU-27 fell from 7.7% in 2000 to 6.9% in 2010 — while receiving enormous public subsidies. The Commission’s impact assessment for the package, published in 2013, calculated that opening domestic passenger markets could reduce rail operating costs by 20–30% and lower fares by 10–20%, based on experience in countries that had already liberalised (Sweden, Great Britain, Germany on certain lines).

The coalition that got the package through was an unusual alliance of the European Parliament’s centre-left and right flank, supported by the Juncker Commission’s broader ambition to deepen the single market as an engine for growth. The Transport Commissioner, Siim Kallas (Estonia, ALDE), drove the proposal forward from 2013, framing it as a consumer-rights issue: citizens in most member states had no choice of rail operator, unlike in aviation or buses. The Parliament’s rapporteur, Saïd El Khadraoui (Belgium, S&D), and later Dominique Riquet (France, ALDE), pushed for a stronger opening than the Council initially wanted.

Opposition was led by France, Germany, and Belgium — countries where the state-owned incumbent (SNCF, Deutsche Bahn, SNCB) was politically powerful and where trade unions feared job losses. The German and French transport ministries argued that competitive tendering would fragment networks, reduce investment, and harm service quality on socially necessary but loss-making regional lines. The Community of European Railway and Infrastructure Companies (CER), representing incumbents, lobbied against mandatory tendering of public service contracts, warning that it could "destabilise the entire public service model." On the other side, the European Passenger Transport Operators (EPTO), a trade body for independent operators, and the European Rail Freight Association (ERFA) campaigned for full liberalisation.

The timing was critical. The package passed after the 2014 European elections, when the centre-right EPP group held a plurality but depended on the liberal ALDE and centre-left S&D to form a majority. The refugee crisis and Brexit referendum of 2016 absorbed political attention, and the package was adopted relatively quietly in December 2016 — a compromise that gave incumbents protections: public service contracts could be awarded directly for a transition period (until 2023, later extended for some member states), and member states could restrict competition on lines where open access would "undermine the economic equilibrium" of a public service contract.

What changed most from earlier attempts was the creation of the European Union Agency for Railways as a one-stop shop for vehicle authorisation and safety certification. Previous packages had failed to overcome national technical barriers — a train approved in France still needed a separate approval in Germany, costing operators months and millions. The technical pillar resolved that block, making market entry cheaper and faster. The 2013 accident at Santiago de Compostela (Spain) also concentrated minds on the need for a common safety certification system, though the link was indirect.

## Timeline

- **2001** — First Railway Package opens trans-European freight network to competition.
- **2004** — Second Railway Package extends freight competition to all rail freight; establishes the European Railway Agency (now European Union Agency for Railways).
- **2007** — Third Railway Package opens international passenger services to competition (e.g., Eurostar, Thalys).
- **2011** — European Commission White Paper on Transport sets 2050 modal shift targets; identifies domestic passenger rail as the next frontier.
- **January 2013** — Commission adopts legislative proposals for the Fourth Railway Package, presented by Commissioner Siim Kallas.
- **February 2014** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position, calling for mandatory competitive tendering of all public service contracts.
- **December 2015** — Council reaches political agreement on a compromise text, allowing direct awards of public service contracts until 2023.
- **May 2016** — European Parliament approves the package in a second-reading vote (543 in favour, 91 against, 44 abstentions).
- **December 2016** — The three legal acts are published in the Official Journal.
- **16 June 2019** — Deadline for member states to transpose Directives 2016/797 and 2016/798.
- **1 January 2019** — Regulation 2016/2370 on market access for domestic passenger services becomes directly applicable.
- **2019–2020** — First new entrants begin services under the new rules: NTV (Italo) in Italy competes with Trenitalia on high-speed; RegioJet and Leo Express expand in Central Europe; open-access operators like Trenitalia’s Avanti West Coast enter the UK via the EU’s grandfathering arrangements.
- **2021** — European Court of Justice dismisses a challenge by Deutsche Bahn against the package’s requirement to separate infrastructure management from train operations (Case C-743/18).
- **2023** — Commission publishes implementation report noting that 14 member states had fully transposed the technical directives, but that several — including Germany — had not yet opened domestic markets to meaningful competition.

## Impact and consequences

The Fourth Railway Package has accelerated a slow, uneven liberalisation. In Italy, the high-speed line between Turin, Milan, Rome, and Naples now has two competing operators (Trenitalia and NTV), and passenger numbers rose by over 50% between 2012 and 2019 according to the Italian transport regulator. In Austria, Westbahn successfully challenged ÖBB on the Vienna–Salzburg line, forcing the incumbent to improve punctuality and lower fares. In Sweden, the market is now one of the most open in Europe, with new entrants such as Snälltåget and MTR Express.

But progress is patchy. In France, the 2019 law transposing the package (Loi d’orientation des mobilités) only opened domestic high-speed lines — and even then, SNCF retains a dominant position. In Germany, Deutsche Bahn still moves over 95% of domestic passengers; new entrants complain that access to stations, maintenance depots, and real-time data is still restrictive. The Commission launched infringement proceedings against Germany in 2021 for non-transposition of the market pillar. In Belgium, the rail market remains essentially closed: the government chose to directly award public service contracts to SNCB until 2033, using a derogation available to member states that had a "secure quality and cost-efficiency" plan.

A major consequence has been the centralisation of safety and interoperability. The European Union Agency for Railways has issued over 1,000 single safety certificates and vehicle authorisations since the package entered into force, according to its 2022 annual report. This has cut the time to authorise a new train from an average of 18 months (when multiple national authorities were involved) to under six months for the EU-wide procedure.

## Key questions answered

### Can a private company run a domestic train service in any EU country?

In theory, yes — since 1 January 2019, any railway undertaking licensed in one member state can offer domestic passenger services on any EU rail line under the "open access" rules of Regulation 2016/2370/EU. In practice, access depends on slot availability on the network, the willingness of infrastructure managers to allocate capacity, and the fate of any public service contracts that already cover the same route. Several member states (notably France, Germany, and Belgium) have limited open access either by direct award of long-term subsidies or by using the "economic equilibrium test" to block competitors. The European Commission has launched infringement proceedings against countries it considers to have implemented the rules too restrictively.

### Why did the EU take so long to open domestic passenger rail?

Earlier railway packages (2001–2007) opened freight and international passenger services, but domestic passenger rail was politically harder because it is the most heavily subsidised segment — many regional lines lose money and rely on public service contracts — and because the state-owned incumbents (SNCF, Deutsche Bahn, Trenitalia, etc.) lobbied fiercely to keep their monopoly. The breakthrough came only after the 2014–2019 Commission made market opening a priority, and after the technical pillar (EU-wide vehicle authorisation) addressed a key barrier that made legal liberalisation meaningless without practical harmonisation.

### How does the Fourth Railway Package affect train fares and service quality?

The Commission’s impact assessment predicted that competition would lower fares by 10–20% and improve service quality. In Italy, where high-speed competition is active, average fares on the Rome–Milan route fell by about 30% between 2012 and 2018 according to analysis by the Italian competition authority. In Sweden and Austria, new entrants forced incumbents to reduce prices and introduce flexible ticketing. However, on many regional routes where public service contracts are still directly awarded, passengers have seen little change. The European Court of Auditors noted in a 2022 special report that the package had not yet led to measurable modal shift away from road, and that passenger satisfaction remained highly variable.
