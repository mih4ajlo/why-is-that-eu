## What is it?

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a comprehensive EU law that governs how organisations collect, process, store, and transfer personal data. It grants individuals a suite of rights — including the right to access data, the right to rectification, the right to erasure (the “right to be forgotten”), and the right to data portability — while imposing strict obligations on companies, such as obtaining explicit consent, conducting data protection impact assessments, and reporting breaches within 72 hours.

The GDPR applies to any organisation operating within the EU, as well as any entity outside the EU that offers goods or services to, or monitors the behaviour of, EU residents. Non-compliance can result in fines of up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. It entered into force in May 2016 and became applicable in May 2018.

## Why was it introduced?

The GDPR was a direct response to the digital age’s explosion of data collection and the failure of the 1995 Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC) to keep pace. The 1995 directive was written for a world of fax machines and mainframe computers — not social media, cloud computing, behavioural advertising, or surveillance capitalism. By the early 2010s, it was clear that the patchwork of national implementations created a legal mess. Companies could shop for the weakest enforcement, and individuals had little practical control over their data.

The proximate trigger was not a single scandal — but a series of them. In 2010, European Commissioner Viviane Reding (Justice, Fundamental Rights and Citizenship) began pushing for a reform. Her 2012 proposal, leaked to the press, initially set a 2% turnover fine and a “right to be forgotten” — both fiercely opposed by industry.

But the Snowden revelations in 2013 changed everything. Edward Snowden’s leaks showed that US intelligence agencies were mass-collecting data from European citizens via companies like Google, Facebook, and Microsoft under programmes like PRISM. This shattered trust in US tech firms and gave EU lawmakers a powerful political narrative: data protection was not just a consumer issue — it was a sovereignty issue. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, Jan Philipp Albrecht (Greens/EFA), seized the moment, pushing through stronger provisions including a 4% turnover fine and mandatory data breach notification.

The coalition that passed the GDPR was unusual. On one side stood the European Parliament, led by Albrecht, with strong support from consumer groups like BEUC and privacy advocates. On the other side, the European Commission (under President José Manuel Barroso, then Jean-Claude Juncker) and most member state governments — especially the UK, Germany, and France — initially favoured a directive (less harmonising) over a regulation. The Council of the EU, representing member states, spent two years in closed-door negotiations, with intense lobbying from the tech industry: Google, Facebook, Amazon, and Microsoft all sent senior executives to Brussels to argue for lighter rules. Industry association DigitalEurope warned of “significant costs” and job losses.

The key opposition came from the UK, which feared losing its status as Europe’s tech hub. The UK Ministry of Justice, under Chris Grayling, fought to water down the one-stop-shop mechanism and consent rules. In the final text, the UK secured a “consistency mechanism” that gave national regulators room to diverge — a major concession.

What broke the deadlock was a combination of three factors: the Snowden revelations, the 2014 European Parliament elections that shifted power towards the centre-right (making Albrecht’s Greens less influential but creating a pro-reform coalition with the Socialists and Liberals), and a final push from Commission Vice-President Andrus Ansip, who made the Digital Single Market a priority. After four years of negotiation, the GDPR was adopted on 14 April 2016 by a near-unanimous vote in Parliament (with only a handful of abstentions).

Alternatives considered included a simple revision of the 1995 directive, a soft-law recommendation, or a sector-specific approach (e.g., health data only). All were rejected because they would not create the harmonised, binding rules that the Commission argued were necessary to eliminate fragmentation and restore trust in the digital market.

## Timeline

- **1995** — Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC) adopted, setting baseline EU privacy rules.
- **2010** — Commissioner Reding announces plans to reform data protection law.
- **2012** — Commission proposes the GDPR; public consultation receives over 4,000 responses.
- **2013** — Snowden leaks reveal NSA mass surveillance; political momentum for reform surges.
- **2014** — European Parliament elections; Jan Philipp Albrecht leads negotiations.
- **2015** — Council reaches political agreement; trilogues produce final text.
- **2016** — GDPR adopted by Parliament and Council; regulation enters into force on 24 May 2016.
- **2018** — GDPR becomes applicable on 25 May 2018.
- **2019** — First major fines: French CNIL fines Google €50 million for lack of transparency.
- **2021** — Irish DPC fines WhatsApp €225 million for breach of transparency obligations.
- **2023** — Meta fined €1.2 billion by Irish DPC for illegal data transfers to the US.

## Impact and consequences

The GDPR dramatically reshaped the global privacy landscape. By 2023, EU data protection authorities had issued over €4 billion in fines, with Meta alone accounting for more than €2.5 billion. The regulation forced companies worldwide to overhaul their data practices: Microsoft, Apple, and Google all introduced privacy dashboards and changed their consent models. Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) reported significant compliance costs; the Commission’s 2019 evaluation found that 67% of SMEs considered the GDPR a major burden.

Countries outside the EU also adopted similar laws — including Brazil (LGPD), California (CCPA), Japan, and South Korea — creating a “Brussels effect” where the GDPR became a global standard. The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) was established to coordinate national regulators, but enforcement remains uneven: Ireland (home to most US tech firms) has faced criticism for slow action.

The regulation’s most controversial provision, the “right to be forgotten,” has led to thousands of removal requests against Google. The Court of Justice of the EU has upheld the right against search engines, though it must be balanced against public interest.

## Key questions answered

### Can a European sue a US tech company for misusing personal data?

Yes. The GDPR allows individuals to seek judicial remedies and compensation from any company processing their data, regardless of where the company is based, as long as it targets EU residents. Several high-profile lawsuits have been filed — most notably the 2019 class action against Google by privacy activist Max Schrems, which led to the €50 million CNIL fine.

### What rights do Europeans have over what companies know about them?

The GDPR gives you the right to access all personal data a company holds, request its deletion (right to erasure), demand correction of inaccuracies, receive your data in a portable format, and object to profiling or processing for direct marketing. Companies must respond within one month, and they cannot charge a fee for these requests.

### Why does it cost so little to sue a company for data breaches in Europe?

The GDPR provides for both administrative fines (up to 4% of global turnover) and private damages. However, litigation costs are generally low because many EU countries allow non-profits to bring representative actions, and the GDPR does not require a minimum amount of harm — individuals can claim compensation for any material or non-material damage.

### How did the Snowden leaks directly affect EU privacy law?

The 2013 Snowden leaks revealed that the US National Security Agency had tapped directly into the servers of major US tech companies, collecting data on millions of Europeans without their knowledge. This shattered the assumption under the Safe Harbour framework (which governed EU-US data transfers) that US companies were adequately protecting data. EU lawmakers, led by Commissioner Reding, used the scandal to argue that data protection was a matter of fundamental rights and digital sovereignty, building the political will for the GDPR’s strong enforcement provisions.
