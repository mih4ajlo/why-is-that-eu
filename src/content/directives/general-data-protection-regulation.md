## What is it?

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is an EU regulation that sets out rules for processing the personal data of people in the European Union. It applies to any organisation — inside or outside the EU — that handles such data, from search engines and hospitals to small online shops and local clubs. It came into force on 25 May 2018, replacing the older Data Protection Directive of 1995.

At its core, the GDPR establishes a set of rights for individuals (access, correction, erasure, portability, objection) and a set of duties for "controllers" and "processors" of data (lawful basis for processing, transparency, security, breach notification, accountability). Because it is a regulation rather than a directive, it applies directly in every member state without needing national transposition, creating a single legal regime across the bloc.

The headline feature is enforcement: national data protection authorities can issue fines up to €20 million or 4% of a company's worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher. This transformed data protection from a compliance footnote into a strategic business risk.

## Why was it introduced?

The story begins with the 1995 Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC), which was drafted in a pre-web world. By the late 2000s, it was visibly broken. Facebook, Google, Amazon, and a global advertising-technology industry had built business models on personal data that the 1995 rules barely contemplated. Worse, because it was a *directive*, each of the 28 member states had implemented it differently, creating 28 overlapping regimes. A company doing business across Europe had to navigate inconsistent definitions, varying enforcement, and "forum shopping" where firms based themselves in lenient jurisdictions (notably Ireland and Luxembourg).

Three political currents converged to force action. First, the **Lisbon Treaty (2009)** elevated data protection to a fundamental right under Article 8 of the EU Charter, giving the Commission a clear treaty basis to legislate. Second, the **Court of Justice** issued landmark rulings — most famously *Google Spain* (2014), which established the "right to be forgotten," and later *Schrems I* (2015), which struck down the EU-US Safe Harbor agreement after Edward Snowden's 2013 revelations exposed mass NSA surveillance of European citizens' data held by US tech firms. Snowden, in particular, electrified the political debate and made data sovereignty a mainstream concern.

Third, **Commissioner Viviane Reding**, who took over the Justice portfolio in 2010, championed reform aggressively, framing it as a competitiveness issue: a single digital market needed a single privacy law. The Commission published its proposal in January 2012. What followed was one of the most heavily lobbied legislative processes in EU history — over 4,000 amendments were tabled in the European Parliament, with intense pressure from US tech firms, advertising associations, and US diplomats on one side, and consumer groups, journalists (notably Max Schrems and the noyb organisation), and several national regulators on the other. Negotiations dragged through trilogue until political agreement was reached in December 2015 and formal adoption in April 2016, with a two-year grace period before the May 2018 application date.

The GDPR was thus not a sudden imposition but the product of two decades of technological change, a constitutional upgrade to fundamental rights status, a series of court decisions exposing the old regime's weaknesses, and a geopolitical moment in which Europe wanted to reassert authority over its citizens' data against US surveillance and US platform dominance.

## Timeline

- **1995** — Data Protection Directive 95/46/EC adopted; basis of EU privacy law for two decades.
- **2000** — EU Charter of Fundamental Rights proclaimed; Article 8 recognises data protection.
- **2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters force, making the Charter legally binding.
- **2010** — Commissioner Viviane Reding announces plans for comprehensive reform.
- **2012 (January)** — European Commission publishes the GDPR proposal.
- **2013 (June)** — Edward Snowden's revelations begin, intensifying European political demand for stronger data rules.
- **2014** — *Google Spain* ruling establishes the right to erasure under existing law.
- **2015 (October)** — *Schrems I* judgment invalidates the EU-US Safe Harbor.
- **2015 (December)** — Trilogue political agreement on the GDPR.
- **2016 (April)** — Regulation 2016/679 formally adopted.
- **2018 (25 May)** — GDPR becomes applicable; flood of consent emails worldwide.
- **2019** — First major fines: €50m against Google by France's CNIL.
- **2021** — €746m fine against Amazon by Luxembourg's CNPD; €225m against WhatsApp.
- **2023** — €1.2 billion fine against Meta for unlawful EU-US data transfers, the largest GDPR penalty to date.

## Impact and consequences

The GDPR's effect was immediate and global. Because it applies extraterritorially to anyone processing EU residents' data, US, Asian, and other non-EU companies overhauled their privacy practices rather than block European users. Many countries — Brazil (LGPD), Japan, South Korea, India, and several US states (notably California's CCPA) — modelled their own laws on the GDPR, a phenomenon dubbed the "Brussels effect."

For businesses, compliance was costly: a class of Data Protection Officers emerged, privacy-impact assessments became routine, and contracts had to be rewritten to allocate controller/processor liability. Small businesses complained about disproportionate burdens; many US news sites simply blocked European visitors rather than comply. Consent banners proliferated — often in dark-pattern forms that regulators and the courts later began policing.

Enforcement was initially slow and uneven, hampered by the "one-stop-shop" mechanism that funnelled cases against US giants through Ireland's under-resourced Data Protection Commission. Frustration with this bottleneck has driven recent reform proposals. Yet over time the fines escalated dramatically, and GDPR has reshaped corporate behaviour: privacy-by-design, breach notification within 72 hours, and explicit lawful bases for processing are now standard global practice.

The regulation has also become a tool in the EU's broader digital sovereignty agenda, complementing later laws like the Digital Services Act, Digital Markets Act, and AI Act.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU replace the 1995 Directive instead of amending it?

Two reasons. The 1995 framework was structurally outdated — written before cloud computing, smartphones, social media, and behavioural advertising. And as a directive it had produced 28 inconsistent national laws; only a regulation could deliver a single market in data with uniform definitions and enforcement.

### Why are the fines so high (up to 4% of global turnover)?

Because previous penalties — typically capped at a few hundred thousand euros — were trivial for global tech firms and routinely treated as a cost of doing business. The drafters explicitly modelled the GDPR's penalty structure on EU competition law, where percentage-of-turnover fines have proven to be the only credible deterrent against multinationals.

### Why does GDPR apply to companies outside the EU?

The 1995 Directive applied only to entities established in the EU, allowing US firms to argue that their European users were served from California and thus outside its scope. Article 3 of the GDPR explicitly extends jurisdiction to any organisation offering goods or services to, or monitoring the behaviour of, people in the EU — closing this loophole and asserting that the relevant connection is the data subject's location, not the controller's.

### Why did it take two years to apply after adoption?

The May 2018 application date gave companies, regulators, and member states (which still had to set up authorities, adapt national laws on derogations, and train DPOs) a transition period. In practice, much of industry waited until early 2018 to begin serious preparation.
