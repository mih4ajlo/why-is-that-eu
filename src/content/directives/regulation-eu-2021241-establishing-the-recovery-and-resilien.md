## What is it?

The Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) is the financial instrument at the heart of NextGenerationEU, the European Union's response to the economic collapse triggered by Covid-19. It offers member states up to €723 billion in 2018 prices — split between roughly €338 billion in grants and €385 billion in loans — to fund national Recovery and Resilience Plans covering the period 2021–2026.

Unlike traditional EU spending, RRF disbursements are not made against invoices but against the completion of specific reform and investment milestones agreed between each capital and the Commission. At least 37% of each national plan must go to climate objectives and at least 20% to digital transformation, locking the pandemic response into the EU's broader green and digital agenda.

To pay for it, the Commission borrows on capital markets in its own name — a constitutional first. The debt is backed by a temporary increase in the EU's "own resources" ceiling, ratified by every national parliament, and is to be repaid by 2058 from the EU budget and from new own resources still being negotiated.

## Why was it introduced?

On 18 March 2020, Italian doctors were rationing ventilators in Bergamo and Christine Lagarde told an investor call that the European Central Bank was "not here to close spreads" — a remark that sent Italian bond yields spiking and forced her into a humiliating clarification within hours. That moment crystallised the fear in Rome, Madrid and Paris that the eurozone was about to repeat 2010–2012: a symmetric health shock turning into an asymmetric debt crisis, with southern economies sliding toward insolvency while Germany and the Netherlands could borrow at negative rates.

The political breakthrough came from Berlin. For a decade, Angela Merkel had refused any form of mutualised debt — eurobonds, "coronabonds", anything that pooled liabilities. On 18 May 2020, alongside Emmanuel Macron, she reversed that position and proposed a €500 billion recovery fund financed by joint EU borrowing and distributed as grants, not loans. German constitutional lawyers were stunned; Bundesbank veterans called it a Rubicon. But Merkel's calculation was that a depression in Italy would destroy the single market that German exporters depended on, and that the alternative — watching Giorgia Meloni's predecessors win on an anti-EU platform — was worse than the moral hazard.

The opposition came from the "Frugal Four": the Netherlands, Austria, Denmark and Sweden, joined informally by Finland. Mark Rutte demanded that any transfers be tied to structural reforms, that grants be cut and loans expanded, and that any member state should be able to pull an "emergency brake" if it suspected another was not delivering. The European Council of 17–21 July 2020 ran to four nights and 90 hours — the longest summit since Nice in 2000. Charles Michel brokered the compromise: the headline figure was cut from the Commission's proposed €500 billion in grants to €390 billion (later €338 billion after recalculation), rebates for the frugals on their EU budget contributions were increased, and a rule-of-law conditionality mechanism was added in parallel (Regulation 2020/2092), which Hungary and Poland would later challenge unsuccessfully before the Court of Justice.

The Parliament, led by rapporteurs Eider Gardiazabal Rubial (S&D), Siegfried Mureșan (EPP) and Dragoș Pîslaru (Renew), pushed back on the Council's text — extracting the 37% climate spending floor, the 20% digital floor, and stronger reporting obligations. The regulation was adopted on 12 February 2021 and entered into force on 19 February 2021. The German Federal Constitutional Court briefly suspended ratification of the Own Resources Decision in March 2021 after a complaint by AfD-linked plaintiffs, but cleared it in April, allowing the Commission to issue its first NextGenerationEU bonds in June 2021.

## Timeline

- **March 2020** — Covid-19 overwhelms Italian hospitals; Lagarde's "not here to close spreads" comment sends Italian yields soaring.
- **April 2020** — Spain proposes a recovery fund of up to €1.5 trillion financed by perpetual EU debt; nine member states call for "coronabonds".
- **18 May 2020** — Merkel–Macron proposal for a €500 billion grants-based fund financed by joint borrowing.
- **27 May 2020** — Commission, under Ursula von der Leyen and Paolo Gentiloni, proposes the €750 billion NextGenerationEU package.
- **21 July 2020** — European Council agrees the package after a 90-hour summit; grants reduced, rebates increased, rule-of-law conditionality added.
- **12 February 2021** — RRF Regulation adopted by Parliament and Council.
- **April–May 2021** — All 27 national parliaments ratify the Own Resources Decision, unlocking borrowing.
- **June 2021** — Commission issues its first 10-year NextGenerationEU bond, attracting record demand.
- **July–December 2021** — Commission approves the first national plans; pre-financing of 13% of allocations begins.
- **February 2023** — REPowerEU chapter added to the RRF in response to the Russian invasion of Ukraine, channelling additional funds to energy independence.
- **2026** — Final deadline for milestone completion and disbursement.
- **2058** — Final repayment of NextGenerationEU debt.

## Impact and consequences

The RRF transformed how the EU finances itself. According to the Commission, NextGenerationEU made the EU one of the largest supranational issuers in euro-denominated debt markets, with bonds repeatedly oversubscribed by factors of ten or more in the early issuances. Italy and Spain are the largest beneficiaries — Italy's plan alone is worth around €194.4 billion in grants and loans, the biggest single national envelope.

Implementation has been uneven. The Commission's own annual reports and the European Court of Auditors have flagged delays in milestone completion, absorption problems in several countries, and difficulty in tracing whether spending is genuinely additional or simply substitutes for what national budgets would have financed anyway. Poland and Hungary saw their funds frozen over rule-of-law concerns: Poland's plan was only unblocked after the Tusk government took office in late 2023, while a substantial share of Hungary's allocation remains withheld pending judicial independence reforms.

Politically, the precedent is the most consequential outcome. Mario Draghi's 2024 report on European competitiveness explicitly cited NextGenerationEU as the template for future joint financing of defence, energy and industrial policy. The "Hamiltonian moment" framing — invoked by Olaf Scholz when he was still German finance minister — remains contested: the Commission and southern member states treat the facility as a proof of concept for permanent fiscal capacity, while Berlin's coalition partners and The Hague continue to insist it was a strictly one-off response to an exceptional crisis.

## Key questions answered

### Did the EU actually issue common debt for the pandemic recovery?

Yes. For the first time at this scale, the European Commission borrows on capital markets in its own name, backed by the EU budget and a temporary increase in member states' guarantees. The proceeds — up to €750 billion in 2018 prices, around €806 billion in current prices — fund both the Recovery and Resilience Facility and several smaller programmes. Repayment runs until 2058.

### Why did Germany agree to joint borrowing after refusing for a decade?

Angela Merkel's reversal in May 2020 was driven by fear that an asymmetric pandemic shock would push Italy and Spain into a second sovereign debt crisis and ultimately fracture the single market on which German exporters depend. The political rise of eurosceptic parties in southern Europe also weighed heavily. Berlin insisted the move was a one-off tied to the pandemic, not a permanent fiscal union.

### What happens if a country doesn't deliver the reforms it promised?

Disbursements are tied to specific milestones and targets. If the Commission judges that a member state has not satisfactorily met them, it can suspend or reduce a payment, and any member state can trigger the so-called "emergency brake" to refer the matter to the European Council. Poland's funds were frozen for years over judicial independence; most of Hungary's allocation remains blocked.

### Who pays back the money?

The debt is serviced from the EU budget — meaning ultimately from member state contributions — and, the Commission hopes, from new "own resources" such as revenues from the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, a share of ETS auctioning, and a planned levy linked to corporate profits. Negotiations on these new own resources are ongoing and politically contentious; if they fail, national contributions will have to rise.
