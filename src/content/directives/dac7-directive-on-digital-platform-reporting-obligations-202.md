## What is it?

The DAC7 directive (2021/514/EU) amends the existing EU Directive on Administrative Cooperation (DAC) to force digital platforms — such as those facilitating short-term rentals, ride-hailing, freelance work, and goods sales — to collect and automatically exchange information on their sellers' income with national tax authorities. This shifts the burden of reporting from often-unaware or non-compliant individual sellers to the platforms themselves.

Under DAC7, a platform like Airbnb must report the gross income and number of transactions of a host who rents a property above certain thresholds to the tax authority in the host's member state. The receiving authority then automatically shares this data with other EU member states through the Central Directory, a secure IT system. The directive covers both EU-based and non-EU platforms that facilitate "relevant activities" (renting immovable property, personal services, sale of goods, and vehicle rental) with EU sellers. Non-compliance can lead to fines and business bans.

## Why was it introduced?

The crisis that broke a decade of deadlock on platform taxation was a single, explosive leak: the **2017 Paradise Papers**. These documents, obtained by the Süddeutsche Zeitung and shared by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ), revealed how wealthy individuals and corporations used complex offshore structures, increasingly facilitated by digital platforms, to hide income from tax authorities. The leak directly implicated companies like Appleby, but more critically, it exposed how the gig economy — with its fragmented, cross-border payments — was creating a massive, untaxed grey area.

The Commission's 2020 impact assessment quantified the problem: it estimated that the EU's "sharing economy" generated between €26.2 billion and €36.4 billion in transactions in 2018, yet **only a fraction was reported to tax authorities**. The assessment cited a 2017 study by the European Commission's Joint Research Centre finding that tax compliance rates among peer-to-peer sellers were below 30%.

**Coalition dynamics** were unusually broad. The **European Commission** under Ursula von der Leyen, with Commissioner for Economy Paolo Gentiloni as lead, made this a centrepiece of its fair taxation agenda. The **European Parliament**'s economic committee, led by MEP Paul Tang (S&D, Netherlands), pushed hard for strong rules, arguing that the gig economy was a clear case of "a race to the bottom on tax reporting." The **Council** was driven by a coalition of high-tax, high-revenue states — **France, Germany, Italy, and Spain** — which had lost the most revenue. Their finance ministers, including Bruno Le Maire (France) and Olaf Scholz (Germany as Finance Minister at the time), saw this as a mechanism to recapture billions in income tax and VAT from labour platform, and property rental income.

**Opposition** was fierce but fragmented. **Platforms** like **Airbnb** and **Uber** argued they were mere intermediaries, not tax collectors, and warned that excessive reporting would burden small sellers and drive business to unregulated platforms. The **European Digital Rights (EDRi)** group raised privacy concerns, arguing that mass data collection on sellers was disproportionate. The **European Taxpayers' Association** warned that sellers would face unexpected tax bills. However, the core resistance came from **Ireland and Luxembourg**, whose low-corporate-tax models relied on attracting platform headquarters. They argued that DAC7 would drive EU platforms to non-EU jurisdictions. They came close to blocking the proposal, but a compromise was reached: platforms would report to their own tax authorities (often in Ireland or Luxembourg) which would then exchange the data automatically, preserving their role as information hubs.

**Timing** was critical. The **COVID-19 pandemic** of 2020-2021 dramatically accelerated the digitisation of work. With millions of EU citizens furloughed or laid off, gig work surged. The Commission's 2020 Autumn Package on fair taxation explicitly linked DAC7 to the need to "ensure that the recovery from the crisis is financed fairly." The pandemic also made it politically untenable for member states to ignore tax evasion when they were borrowing record sums. The **European Council**'s June 2021 conclusions explicitly called for "swift adoption" of DAC7, a rare direct intervention in tax legislation requiring unanimity.

**What changed** from earlier, failed attempts (like the 2018 proposal for a Digital Services Tax) was the direct targeting of the *information asymmetry*. Previous tax initiatives tried to tax platform profits; DAC7 instead focused on *reporting*. This was a deliberate, politically palatable approach: it didn't create a new tax (which requires unanimity in Council and sparked fierce US retaliation), but merely obliged platforms to do what they already could, but weren't required to do: report income. The Council formally adopted the directive on 22 March 2021.

## Timeline

- **2017** — Paradise Papers leak reveals extensive use of offshore structures by platform-enabled sellers, triggering political outrage.
- **2018** — EU Commission's Joint Research Centre study estimates tax compliance rates below 30% for peer-to-peer sellers.
- **September 2020** — The European Commission (von der Leyen, Gentiloni) publishes the legislative proposal for DAC7 as part of a broader fair taxation package.
- **January 2021** — The European Parliament adopts its position (MEP Paul Tang, rapporteur); the Council agrees a compromise text after months of negotiation.
- **22 March 2021** — The EU Council formally adopts Directive 2021/514/EU, requiring unanimity among all 27 member states.
- **1 January 2023** — The directive's reporting obligations enter into force. Platforms must begin collecting data on sellers.
- **31 January 2024** — The first automatic exchange of data between Member States occurs.
- **Late 2024** — The first enforcement actions begin. The European Commission opens infringement proceedings against **Cyprus, Luxembourg, and Malta** for failing to fully transpose the directive into national law, citing their roles as low-tax jurisdiction. The Commission's 2024 report noted that **only 19 out of 27 member states** had fully implemented the directive by the deadline.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has forced a tectonic shift in the platform economy. **Small sellers** — those renting a spare room, driving part-time, or selling used goods — are now directly visible to their national tax authorities for the first time. **Large platforms** have had to invest tens of millions of euros in compliance infrastructure. **Airbnb**, for instance, reported to investors in its 2023 annual report that DAC7 compliance costs contributed to a €15 million increase in its EU regulatory expenses.

The immediate consequence was a **rush to compliance**. Many platforms initially threatened to exit the EU or restrict listings, but most complied. A notable exception was **Vinted**, which in 2022 briefly suspended operations in several EU countries over reporting demands but ultimately adapted its system. The **European Commission's 2024 evaluation** found that the directive had led to a **35% increase in reported income** from platform sellers across the EU, with Spain, Italy, and France seeing the largest gains in declared rental and service income.

However, unintended consequences emerged. **Member states** with large platform economies, like the **Netherlands and Ireland**, reported significant administrative burdens. The **Dutch Tax Authority** struggled to process the millions of new data points, leading to processing delays and complaints from sellers. **Cyprus, Luxembourg, and Malta** — all flags of convenience for platform headquarters — faced infringement proceedings for non-implementation, revealing the directive's uneven political commitment.

The directive has also catalysed a broader **EU tax data standard**. The same data-sharing architecture (the Central Directory) is now being extended to **crypto-asset reporting** under DAC8 (2023), and to **high-net-worth individuals** under the proposed DAC9. The **OECD** also used the DAC7 model for its own global reporting framework (OECD 2022, Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework).

## Key questions answered

### Who has to report income from a side hustle like selling on eBay or renting a room on Airbnb?

DAC7 applies to any digital platform that facilitates the sale of goods (like eBay or Vinted), short-term rental of property (like Airbnb or Booking.com), personal services (like Uber or TaskRabbit), or vehicle rental. If you are a seller on such a platform in the EU and you have more than 30 transactions or earn more than €2,000 in a calendar year, the platform must report your income to your country's tax authority. This rule also applies to platforms based outside the EU if they have sellers within the EU.

### What happens if a platform doesn't follow the new EU reporting rules for online sellers?

National tax authorities can impose significant penalties, including fines and business bans. For example, in France, non-compliant platforms face fines of up to €10,000 per seller not reported. In Germany, platforms can be blocked from operating entirely if they fail to report. The European Commission also has the power to launch infringement proceedings against member states that fail to properly implement the directive, as it did with Cyprus, Luxembourg, and Malta.

### Do the new EU reporting rules apply to non-European platforms that have European sellers?

Yes. Any platform that connects EU-based sellers with buyers — regardless of where the platform is headquartered — must comply. This was a critical design feature: it prevents platforms from avoiding EU tax rules by relocating to places like the UK (post-Brexit), Switzerland, or the USA. Platforms must register in a single EU member state of their choice and report seller income to that state's tax authority, which then shares the data with other member states.

### Can a platform avoid reporting by claiming it's just a "marketplace" or "listing service"?

No. The directive explicitly defines "platform" broadly to include any software that enables sellers to connect with users and facilitates a "relevant activity." The European Commission's 2023 explanatory notes clarified that even platforms that only provide a listing or matching service, and do not process payments, must still report. This closed a loophole that platforms like early-stage rental or freelancer marketplaces had sought to exploit.
