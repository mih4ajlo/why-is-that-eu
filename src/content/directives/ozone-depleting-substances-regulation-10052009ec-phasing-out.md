## What is it?

Regulation (EC) No 1005/2009, adopted on 16 September 2009, governs the production, import, export, placing on the market, use, and emission of substances that deplete the stratospheric ozone layer. It covers controlled substances listed in its annexes, including chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), halons, carbon tetrachloride, methyl chloroform, hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs), and methyl bromide. The regulation prohibits all but strictly limited essential uses, sets phase-out deadlines, requires licensing for imports and exports, and imposes reporting obligations on undertakings.

The regulation is the direct legal expression of the Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer within EU law, but it goes beyond the Protocol in several areas — for example, by banning exports of ODS to non‑Parties earlier than required, and by setting stricter deadlines for phasing out HCFCs. It also introduces measures to prevent illegal trade, including a licensing system and penalties for infractions.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for European action was the discovery of the Antarctic ozone hole by British scientists in 1985, which turned a theoretical concern into a visible global crisis. That year, the Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer was adopted, followed by the Montreal Protocol in 1987. The European Economic Community (EEC) was an early signatory, but internal implementation fractured among member states, creating trade distortions and loopholes for imports from outside the Protocol.

By the early 1990s, scientific evidence — notably from the UN Environment Programme’s 1991 Scientific Assessment of Ozone Depletion — showed that the Protocol’s initial freeze and reduction targets were insufficient to prevent further damage. The chemical industry, led by DuPont, initially resisted mandatory phase-outs, arguing that alternatives were not yet viable. However, after the Montreal Protocol’s 1990 London Amendment tightened schedules and created a Multilateral Fund to help developing countries, DuPont reversed its position and announced it would phase out CFC production by 1995, citing technological breakthroughs in hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) and other substitutes.

The EU’s first dedicated ODS regulation, Council Regulation (EEC) No 3093/94, was adopted in 1994 under Environment Commissioner Ioannis Paleokrassas. It set phase-out dates ahead of the Protocol’s and contained stricter export bans. But by the mid‑2000s, the regulation needed updating: new substances (like HCFCs) had been added to the Protocol through the 1997 Montreal Amendment, the illegal trade in CFCs and halons was booming (the Commission’s 2007 impact assessment cited seizures of several tens of tonnes annually), and the legal framework was scattered across several Council regulations.

The 2009 recast, proposed by Environment Commissioner Stavros Dimas in 2008, consolidated existing laws and introduced tighter controls on imports from non‑Parties, broader reporting requirements, and explicit provisions for essential-use exceptions. The European Parliament’s Environment Committee, led by rapporteur and Green MEP Avril Doyle, pushed for an earlier phase-out of HCFCs than the Commission’s original proposal. Industry associations such as the European Fluorocarbon Technical Committee (EFCTC) argued that the proposed deadlines were too ambitious and would disrupt medical inhaler production, but the Parliament held firm. The regulation was adopted by the Council on 16 September 2009 and entered into force on 1 January 2010.

## Timeline

- **1985** — Discovery of the Antarctic ozone hole by British Antarctic Survey scientists; adoption of the Vienna Convention.
- **1987** — Montreal Protocol signed, committing parties to freeze and reduce CFC production.
- **1990** — London Amendment to the Montreal Protocol accelerates phase-out and establishes a Multilateral Fund.
- **1992** — Copenhagen Amendment adds HCFCs, methyl bromide and other substances.
- **1994** — First EU ODS Regulation (EEC No 3093/94) adopted under Environment Commissioner Ioannis Paleokrassas.
- **2000** — Regulation (EC) No 2037/2000 updated controls, including an earlier HCFC phase-out.
- **2005** — Commission review identifies gaps in enforcement and illegal trade.
- **2008** — Commission proposal for a recast regulation (COM/2008/505) tabled by Commissioner Stavros Dimas.
- **2009** — European Parliament and Council adopt Regulation (EC) No 1005/2009; entered into force 1 January 2010.
- **2012** — Regulation amended by Commission Regulation (EU) No 526/2012 to update essential-use exemptions for methyl bromide.
- **2014** — First comprehensive implementation report by the Commission shows substantial reductions in ODS consumption and emissions.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation accelerated the phase-out of ODS in the EU ahead of Montreal Protocol deadlines. According to the European Environment Agency’s 2019 annual ODS report, EU consumption of controlled substances had fallen by over 99.9% from baseline levels by 2018. The ozone layer over the Northern Hemisphere has been slowly recovering, with the UN’s 2018 Scientific Assessment noting that the Antarctic ozone hole is expected to close by the 2060s.

Economically, the regulation forced the refrigeration, air‑conditioning, foam and aerosol industries to shift to alternatives — initially HFCs, which later came under separate F‑gas regulation (Regulation (EU) No 517/2014) due to their high global‑warming potential. The Commission’s 2011 report on enforcement documented at least 50 confirmed cases of illegal trade in ODS between 2006 and 2010, with fines ranging from €10,000 to over €1 million per case. Some of the largest seizures occurred at EU external borders, notably in the Port of Rotterdam, where customs intercepted shipments of R‑12 (a CFC) disguised as other refrigerants.

Developing countries, which were granted longer transition periods under the Montreal Protocol’s Multilateral Fund, initially struggled to meet phase-out schedules, but EU technical assistance and funding (the Commission contributed around €20 million to the Fund between 2009 and 2015) helped build capacity.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU allow any use of CFCs at all today?
Yes, but only under strictly controlled essential‑use exemptions, primarily for laboratory and analytical uses, and for in‑situ uses in existing fire‑protection systems that contain halons. Medical metered‑dose inhalers containing CFCs were phased out by the EU in 2011 after alternatives became available. Any use requires an annual authorisation from the European Commission.

### Why did the EU adopt stricter rules than the Montreal Protocol required?
The EU positioned itself as a global environmental leader, and its internal market needed uniform rules to prevent a race‑to‑the‑bottom in emission standards and illegal imports. Earlier EU regulations had already set tighter deadlines, and the 2009 regulation codified that approach. The European Parliament also pushed for greater ambition, especially during the 2008‑2009 recast debate, arguing that the EU could not credibly call for global action while allowing loopholes at home.

### What happened to companies that illegally traded ozone‑depleting substances?
The regulation requires member states to impose "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" penalties. National authorities have imposed fines, seized shipments, and in some cases brought criminal charges. The Commission’s 2011 enforcement report noted that penalties varied widely — from a few thousand euros in one member state to over €1 million in another — leading to calls for harmonised sanction levels in subsequent revisions.
