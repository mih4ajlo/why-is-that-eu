## What is it?

The Public Country-by-Country Reporting (pCbCR) Directive (2021/2101/EU) amends the Accounting Directive (2013/34/EU) to force large multinational enterprises (MNEs) with consolidated group revenue exceeding €750 million to publicly report income tax information for each EU member state and a list of non-cooperative tax jurisdictions. The data must be published in a common template and filed with a business register.

The directive covers revenue, profit or loss before tax, income tax accrued and paid, accumulated earnings, number of employees, and the nature of activities. It applies to MNEs headquartered in the EU or non-EU MNEs with EU-subsidiaries or branches exceeding the revenue threshold. The first reports are due for financial years starting on or after 22 June 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive emerged from a decade of political struggle triggered by the LuxLeaks scandal in November 2014, when the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ) revealed over 300 tax rulings granted by Luxembourg to multinational giants like Amazon, McDonald’s, and Pepsi, allowing them to shift profits to near-zero tax rates. The leaked documents showed how PwC, under the direction of Luxembourg tax officials, engineered tax structures that saved companies billions. The European Commission subsequently opened state aid investigations against Luxembourg, Ireland, the Netherlands, and Belgium, ordering Apple to repay €13 billion in back taxes to Ireland in 2016.

The core structural tension was clear: under existing tax rules, MNEs could report profits in low-tax countries while reporting losses in high-tax ones, making it impossible for citizens and investors to assess where actual economic activity occurred. Private country-by-country reporting had already been mandated for tax authorities under the OECD’s Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) Action 13 in 2015, implemented via EU Directive 2016/881/EU, but that data remained confidential.

Commissioner Pierre Moscovici (2014-2019) championed public disclosure as a transparency tool, proposing the first draft in April 2016. He argued that sunlight was the best disinfectant for corporate tax practices. The proposal initially required full publication of data for all non-EU countries as well.

The political coalition that ultimately passed the directive was fragile and took five years to assemble. The European Parliament’s centre-left S&D group, Greens, and Liberals pushed hard, with MEPs like Evelyn Regner (S&D, Austria) and Sven Giegold (Greens, Germany) leading the charge. The centre-right EPP was split: some members, notably from Germany, supported transparency, while others allied with business lobby groups.

The European business lobby generated intense opposition. BusinessEurope argued that public reporting would reveal commercially sensitive information, while the American Chamber of Commerce to the EU warned of competitive disadvantages for EU companies. Key opponent countries included Ireland, Luxembourg, Malta, Cyprus, and the Netherlands — the very jurisdictions most exposed by the proposals.

The deadlock was broken by two factors: the 2019 European Parliament elections, which saw increased Green and left-wing representation, and the Pandora Papers and OpenLux investigations in 2021, which further exposed Luxembourg’s role as Europe’s tax haven capital. In June 2021, the German government under Angela Merkel shifted its position, reportedly after Finance Minister Olaf Scholz came under pressure from coalition partners SPD and Greens. Germany’s support was critical.

The final compromise, reached in trilogue negotiations in June 2021, included a safeguard clause allowing companies to temporarily withhold data if they could prove it would cause “significant commercial harm” — a loophole critics say weakens the directive. The Parliament voted 534-106 in favour in November 2021, with the Council adopting it on 28 September 2021.

## Impact and consequences

The directive is estimated to affect approximately 1,500-2,000 MNEs with EU operations, according to the European Commission’s 2016 impact assessment. This includes both EU-headquartered companies (e.g., Shell, Volkswagen, LVMH) and non-EU giants (e.g., Apple, Google, Amazon).

Industry response has been mixed. Some companies, like Unilever and Vodafone, already voluntarily publish country-by-country data and welcomed the rules as levelling the playing field. Others, particularly US tech firms, lobbied aggressively against the directive, warning of reputational damage if their tax structures were exposed.

The most significant consequence may be behavioural: tax advisors report that many MNEs have already begun restructuring their tax arrangements in anticipation of public disclosure, reducing the gap between where profits are declared and where economic activity occurs.

Implementation has been uneven. By the July 2023 transposition deadline, several member states — including Ireland, Luxembourg, and the Netherlands — had not fully transposed the directive, according to the Commission’s 2023 Single Market Scoreboard. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against three member states in November 2023.

The safeguard clause allowing non-disclosure on commercial grounds has been criticised by transparency NGOs like Oxfam, which warned in a 2022 report that it could be widely abused. The Commission has promised to review the clause’s operation by 2027.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take so long to pass?

The directive faced five years of deadlock in the Council of the EU because a blocking minority of tax-haven member states — Ireland, Luxembourg, Malta, Cyprus, and the Netherlands — opposed any public disclosure. These countries feared exposing their low-tax regimes would drive away multinational clients. Germany’s shift under Chancellor Merkel and Finance Minister Scholz in 2021 broke the deadlock by providing the necessary qualified majority.

### Why is the €750 million threshold used?

The threshold mirrors the OECD’s BEPS Action 13 standard for private country-by-country reporting, adopted by the EU in 2016. It targets the roughly 1,500-2,000 largest MNEs that are most likely to engage in aggressive tax planning. The Commission’s impact assessment determined that lowering the threshold would disproportionately burden smaller companies without significant transparency benefits.

### How does the safeguard clause affect the directive’s effectiveness?

The safeguard clause allows companies to withhold data if they can demonstrate “significant commercial harm” — a broad exemption opposed by transparency advocates. The text requires firms to explain why they are withholding data, and the data must be published later. Critics like Oxfam argue the clause creates a major loophole, as proving commercial harm is subjective and companies have strong incentives to invoke it. The Commission must review the clause’s operation by 2027.
