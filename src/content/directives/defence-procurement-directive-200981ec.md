## What is it?

The Defence Procurement Directive (2009/81/EC) establishes a specific legal framework for the procurement of arms, munitions, and war material by EU member states. It carves out defence contracts from the general EU public procurement directives (2004/18/EC at the time of adoption), allowing for more flexible procedures to protect national security secrets while still encouraging cross-border competition.

The directive applies only to contracts "for military purposes" and those involving "sensitive" information. It permits negotiated procedures without prior publication (Art. 13) and allows member states to exclude contracts entirely under Article 346 TFEU if essential security interests are at stake. It was adopted alongside the Intra-Community Transfers Directive (2009/43/EC) as a package to liberalise the European defence equipment market.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was a direct response to the European Commission's 2004 "Green Paper on Defence Procurement" and a series of European Court of Justice rulings that exposed a legal no-man's-land. The trigger was the 1999 *Commission v. Spain* (C-414/97) case, where the Court ruled that Spain could not use Article 296 (now 346) TFEU to exempt a contract for military ground vehicles from procurement rules unless it could demonstrate a genuine security necessity. This judgment shattered the assumption that all defence contracts were automatically exempt.

The structural tension: Member states had been invoking the Article 346 security exemption as a blanket cover for 50-80% of defence contracts, according to a 2005 Commission study. This created a fragmented market where national champions (e.g., BAE Systems in the UK, Thales in France, Rheinmetall in Germany) enjoyed de facto monopolies, driving up costs by an estimated 20-30% compared to civilian procurement, as reported in a 2007 RAND Europe study. The coalition pushing for change included the Commission under Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy (2004-2010), supported by smaller member states like Sweden, the Netherlands, and Poland, which lacked large domestic defence industries and wanted access to cross-border competition. The European Defence Agency (EDA), created in 2004, also pushed for harmonisation.

Opposition came primarily from France and Germany, whose large defence contractors benefited from national preference. The French government, under President Jacques Chirac, argued that the directive would compromise sovereignty and national security. The European defence industry association ASD (Aerospace, Security and Defence Industry) warned in 2007 that the directive could "increase administrative burdens without achieving genuine market opening."

The timing was driven by the 2008 financial crisis, which exposed the unsustainability of national procurement silos. Defence budgets across the EU were shrinking by an average of 1.5% annually in real terms from 2001 to 2006, according to the EDA's 2007 Defence Data report. The Commission under José Manuel Barroso framed the directive as a way to "do more with less," arguing that cross-border procurement could save 10-15% on defence spending. The final vote in the European Parliament on 14 January 2009 was 528 in favour, 45 against, with 16 abstentions — a comfortable majority, but the delay from 2004 to 2009 reflected the intense national security concerns.

What changed: The key unblocking event was the 2007 Lisbon Treaty, which explicitly recognised the specificities of defence procurement in Protocol 27 on the Internal Market and Competition, providing a legal basis to carve out defence from general single-market rules. This gave the Commission the confidence to propose the directive in December 2007, after years of informal negotiations.

## Timeline

- **1999** — ECJ ruling in *Commission v. Spain* (C-414/97) finds that the Article 296 security exemption is not automatic
- **2004** — Commission Green Paper on Defence Procurement calls for a specific defence procurement regime
- **2005** — Commission study finds 50-80% of defence contracts exempted under Article 296
- **2007** — Lisbon Treaty signed, including Protocol 27 on defence procurement specificities
- **2007, December** — Commission proposes the Defence Procurement Directive (COM(2007) 766 final)
- **2009, January** — European Parliament adopts directive 528-45-16
- **2009, July** — Council of the EU formally adopts the directive
- **2011, August** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law
- **2013** — Commission launches first infringement proceedings against 12 member states for late transposition
- **2017** — Commission's evaluation report finds limited cross-border competition, with 80% of contracts still awarded nationally
- **2020** — Commission updates guidance on the directive, noting "persistent fragmentation" of the defence market

## Impact and consequences

The directive's impact has been modest. According to the Commission's own 2017 evaluation report, only 20% of defence contracts valued over the EU threshold are awarded cross-border, compared to the pre-directive estimate of 15%. The European Court of Auditors' 2019 report found that most member states continue to invoke the Article 346 exemption for the vast majority of contracts — an estimated 80-90% of defence spending remains outside the directive's scope.

Industry response was pragmatic. Large contractors like BAE Systems and Thales adapted by forming consortia across borders (e.g., the Eurofighter Typhoon consortium), but the directive did little to break up national monopolies. Small and medium-sized enterprises reported in a 2014 European Defence Agency survey that the directive added bureaucratic compliance costs without opening significant new market access.

Countries that struggled most with transposition included Cyprus, Malta, and Greece, which lacked dedicated defence procurement legislation. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against 12 member states in 2013 for failing to transpose on time. No fines have been imposed specifically under this directive, but the Commission's 2020 defence package threatened infringement actions for incorrect application.

## Key questions answered

### Why don't EU rules for buying military equipment apply to all defence contracts?

Article 346 TFEU allows member states to exempt any contract if it "would prejudice the essential interests of its security." Because the directive itself cannot override primary EU treaty law, member states retain the discretion to invoke this exemption for contracts involving state secrets, operational needs, or sensitive technology. The directive only applies when a member state chooses not to invoke Article 346.

### Why did smaller EU countries support common rules for buying military gear?

Smaller member states like Sweden, the Netherlands, and the Baltic states lacked large domestic defence industries and were forced to buy from foreign suppliers anyway. For them, the directive offered a chance to reduce costs by mandating competitive procedures, rather than paying inflated prices to national-only bidders. The EDA's 2007 data showed that these countries spent 30-40% more on equivalent equipment compared to larger states with domestic production.

### Why have so few defence contracts been awarded to companies from other EU countries?

Three factors: First, the Article 346 exemption allows member states to bypass the directive easily. Second, defence contracts are structured around long-term partnerships with national champions that include offset agreements (e.g., requiring a portion of the contract value to be reinvested in the buyer's country), which the directive does not ban. Third, the European Defence Fund, established in 2021, arguably competes with the directive by funding collaborative projects on an intergovernmental basis rather than through competitive procurement.
