## What is it?

Directive (EU) 2024/1799, known politically as the "Right to Repair Directive" or R2R, obliges manufacturers to repair certain categories of consumer products on request — even after the two-year legal guarantee has expired — for as long as repair is required under EU product-specific rules. The first products covered include washing machines, dishwashers, refrigerators, vacuum cleaners, smartphones, tablets, displays, welding equipment and certain servers, with the list expandable through the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation.

The directive also extends the seller's liability period whenever a product is repaired under guarantee (adding twelve months), bans manufacturers from using contractual clauses, hardware or software techniques to block independent repairers and refurbishers from using second-hand or 3D-printed spare parts, and requires the Commission and Member States to set up a European online repair platform where consumers can find repairers, refurbishers and sellers of refurbished goods in their area. A standardised "European Repair Information Form" must give consumers an upfront, comparable quote.

It is a directive — not a regulation — so Member States have until 31 July 2026 to transpose it into national law.

## Why was it introduced?

The political trigger was Apple. In 2017 the Italian and French competition authorities fined Apple €10 million and €25 million respectively for deliberately slowing down older iPhones through software updates without telling users — the "batterygate" scandal that crystallised public anger about planned obsolescence across Europe. France had already become the first country in the world to criminalise planned obsolescence in its 2015 energy transition law, and in 2021 added a mandatory "repairability index" for electronics. The Commission could see a patchwork of national rules emerging and chose to pre-empt fragmentation of the single market.

The structural problem was that the EU's existing Sale of Goods Directive (2019/771) gave consumers the right to choose between repair and replacement during the two-year legal guarantee — but in practice manufacturers almost always pushed replacement because it was cheaper for them and customers preferred a new device to a wait. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment accompanying the proposal estimated that premature replacement of viable goods generated millions of tonnes of waste and significant greenhouse-gas emissions every year, with most discarded products still technically repairable. Eurobarometer surveys repeatedly showed that a large majority of Europeans wanted to repair rather than replace but cited cost, inconvenience and lack of available parts as the main obstacles.

Coalition dynamics were unusually broad. Environmental NGOs (the Right to Repair Europe coalition, ECOS, EEB), the Greens and S&D pushed hardest; the centre-right EPP came on board because small independent repair shops — the SME constituency — stood to gain at the expense of large manufacturers; consumer organisations such as BEUC provided technical ammunition. Opposition came primarily from DigitalEurope and Orgalim, representing electronics and engineering manufacturers, who warned about intellectual-property risks, safety liability from third-party parts, and cybersecurity in connected devices. Apple, Samsung and the appliance industry lobbied to keep the scope narrow and to preserve "parts pairing" — the practice of cryptographically binding components to a specific device so non-original parts trigger warnings or disable features.

Timing mattered. Commissioner Didier Reynders (Justice) tabled the proposal in March 2023 as part of the Von der Leyen Commission's Green Deal package, alongside the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation and the Green Claims Directive. The energy crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine had made "buy less, repair more" politically resonant, and the file moved quickly: political agreement between Parliament and Council was reached in February 2024, just months before the June 2024 European elections — a deadline that disciplined both co-legislators.

What changed compared with earlier failed attempts: previous Commission efforts in the 2010s had been confined to ecodesign rules requiring manufacturers to make spare parts available to "professional repairers" only. The Court of Justice's product-liability jurisprudence and France's experiment with the repairability index demonstrated that broader consumer-facing obligations were workable, and the 2020 Circular Economy Action Plan committed the Commission politically to delivering a horizontal right to repair.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's real bite depends on the product-specific ecodesign rules adopted under Regulation (EU) 2024/1781, which determine which goods carry a repair obligation and for how long. Smartphones and tablets are already covered by an ecodesign regulation in force since June 2025 requiring seven years of spare-parts availability and operating-system security updates.

Manufacturers face several concrete changes. Parts pairing — Apple's and John Deere's signature practice — is constrained: firms cannot use software locks to refuse the use of compatible or refurbished spare parts unless they can justify it on legitimate grounds such as safety. Independent repair shops gain a legal right of access to spare parts, tools and repair information on fair, reasonable and non-discriminatory terms. Online marketplaces and platforms must list repairers via the new European repair platform, which national authorities are now building.

The consumer law extension is significant: when a product sold with a two-year guarantee is repaired (rather than replaced) under that guarantee, the seller's liability is extended by one additional year — a deliberate incentive to push traders toward repair. Industry associations including DigitalEurope warned during negotiations that this would increase compliance costs and complicate warranty management across the single market; consumer groups such as BEUC argued it remained too narrow because it does not cover the full lifetime of the product.

Member States are now drafting transposition legislation. France, Belgium and Austria — which already run national repair-bonus schemes subsidising repairs of household electronics — are expected to integrate them with the new EU framework.

## Key questions answered

### Can I force a company to repair my phone in Europe?

Once national laws transposing the directive apply (from mid-2026), yes — for products covered by EU repairability rules, including smartphones, tablets, washing machines and several other categories. The manufacturer must repair on request at a reasonable price, even after the legal guarantee has expired, and cannot refuse on the grounds that the parts are no longer made if EU ecodesign rules required them to keep parts available.

### Is planned obsolescence illegal in the EU?

The EU has not (yet) created a single criminal offence of planned obsolescence the way France did in 2015, but the combined effect of the Right to Repair Directive, the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation and the 2024 directive on "empowering consumers for the green transition" makes most of its standard tactics unlawful: software updates that degrade performance without consent, parts pairing that blocks legitimate repairs, and marketing claims about durability that cannot be substantiated.

### Why can't I use cheap third-party parts to fix my iPhone or tractor?

Until now, manufacturers like Apple and John Deere used "parts pairing" — cryptographic checks that warned or disabled functions when non-original parts were installed — and tightly controlled service manuals. The directive restricts these practices: manufacturers can no longer contractually or technically prevent independent repairers from using compatible, second-hand or 3D-printed parts unless they can prove a specific safety or functional justification.

### Does this mean products will become more expensive in Europe?

Manufacturers, particularly in consumer electronics and home appliances, argued during the legislative process that designing for repair, stocking spare parts for seven to ten years and providing repair documentation will raise costs. The Commission's impact assessment estimated net consumer savings over product lifetimes because repair is cheaper than replacement, but acknowledged some upfront price effects. The real test will come as ecodesign rules expand the list of covered products through the late 2020s.
