## What is it?

The Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Directive (2001/23/EC) is a codified EU law that protects employees when a business, or part of a business, is transferred to a new owner. It requires that employment contracts, terms, and conditions automatically transfer to the new employer, and that employees are protected from dismissal solely because of the transfer.

The directive covers both voluntary transfers (e.g., sale of a company) and statutory transfers (e.g., when a contract to provide services is awarded to a new contractor). It applies to any transfer of an "economic entity that retains its identity," meaning a structured group of resources organised to pursue an economic activity, whether for profit or not. Key obligations include informing and consulting representatives of affected employees before the transfer, and ensuring that the transfer itself is not a valid reason for dismissal.

## Why was it introduced?

The TUPE Directive was born from a structural tension in the single market: as cross-border business transfers and outsourcing increased, workers faced sudden loss of jobs and rights with no legal continuity. The original 1977 directive (77/187/EEC) was the EU's first response, but its vague wording led to widespread legal uncertainty.

The 2001 codification was triggered by two specific crises in the 1990s. First, the Commission's own 1992 report on implementation of the 1977 directive found "significant discrepancies" between Member States, with some countries (like the UK) interpreting the directive so narrowly that it barely applied to outsourcing or service contract changes. Second, the European Court of Justice (ECJ) ruling in *Francovich and Others v Italian Republic* (1991) had just established that Member States could be liable for failing to implement EU directives properly — putting pressure on governments to clarify and enforce worker protections.

The political coalition that pushed for codification was led by the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) and employer organisations like BUSINESSEUROPE (then UNICE). The ETUC wanted stronger worker consultation and broader scope; employers wanted predictability and harmonised rules to avoid "forum shopping" where companies would transfer to jurisdictions with weaker protections. The European Parliament, under its Social Affairs Committee led by MEPs like Stephen Hughes (UK, PES), repeatedly called for clearer rules.

The timing was driven by the 1997 Amsterdam Treaty, which added employment protection to the EU's core objectives, and the 1999 resignation of the Santer Commission, which created a political appetite for concrete social policy achievements under the new Prodi Commission. Social Affairs Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (1999-2004) made the codification a priority, arguing that "workers must not be the losers in the restructuring of European business."

The 98/50/EC amending directive (1998) had already begun tightening the rules, and the 2001 codification consolidated these changes into a single, clearer text. The UK, which had a particularly litigious history with TUPE (the 1981 UK Transfer of Undertakings Regulations, or TUPE, were fiercely contested by employers), opposed stronger consultation requirements but was outvoted in the Council under qualified majority voting.

## Timeline

- **1977** — Original Transfer of Undertakings Directive (77/187/EEC) adopted, requiring employee rights to transfer with business ownership
- **1991** — ECJ ruling in *Francovich* establishes Member State liability for inadequate implementation of EU directives
- **1992** — Commission report finds "significant discrepancies" in how Member States apply the 1977 directive
- **1997** — Amsterdam Treaty adds employment protection to EU objectives; European Parliament calls for revision
- **1998** — Amending Directive 98/50/EC adopted, widening scope to include service contract changes and administrative transfers
- **2001** — Codification as 2001/23/EC adopted by Council on 12 March 2001; entered into force 11 April 2001
- **2002** — Deadline for Member States to transpose into national law; UK, Ireland, and Luxembourg noted as late transposers
- **2005** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against several Member States for incorrect transposition
- **2013** — Commission report on implementation finds broad compliance but notes continuing disputes over definition of "economic entity"
- **2018** — ECJ ruling in *Colino v SGAE* clarifies that the directive applies to judicial administration proceedings, extending protection to insolvency transfers

## Impact and consequences

The 2001 directive created a single, harmonised framework for over 500 million workers across the EU, but its impact varied dramatically by Member State. The Commission's 2013 implementation report found that the directive led to a significant reduction in "fire and rehire" practices during business transfers, though precise data on the number of workers protected annually is not centrally compiled.

The UK, which had introduced its own TUPE Regulations in 1981 (based on the 1977 directive), experienced the most litigation. The 2001 directive forced the UK to amend its regulations in 2006, extending coverage to "service provision changes" — a change the Confederation of British Industry (CBI) estimated would affect 10-15% of outsourcing contracts. According to a 2014 study by the UK's Department for Business, Innovation & Skills, the UK TUPE regulations (post-2006) applied to approximately 1 million workers per year in the UK alone.

The directive's most contested aspect remains the definition of "economic entity that retains its identity." The Commission's 2013 report recorded over 50 ECJ rulings on this single issue, with employers in Germany and France frequently challenging whether service contract changes (e.g., cleaning or catering) constituted a "transfer." The Federation of European Employers (FedEE) argued that the directive discouraged outsourcing and restructuring, while the ETUC maintained it provided essential stability.

Enforcement actions were limited: the Commission launched formal infringement proceedings against only three Member States (Italy, Greece, and Ireland) between 2001 and 2013, concerning inadequate consultation requirements. No fines were imposed, as the cases were resolved through negotiation.

## Key questions answered

### Why does TUPE still matter if the UK left the EU?

The UK's TUPE regulations remain based on the 2001 directive, and the UK retained them after Brexit under the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018. However, the UK government has consulted on reducing TUPE obligations for businesses, and EU case law — including ECJ rulings — no longer binds UK courts, creating potential divergence.

### Why doesn't the directive protect against all dismissals during a transfer?

The directive allows dismissals for "economic, technical or organisational reasons" (ETO reasons) that require changes in the workforce. This exception, heavily lobbied for by employer groups like BUSINESSEUROPE, permits redundancy if the new employer can prove the dismissal is unrelated to the transfer itself. The Commission's 2013 report found that this exception was the most litigated provision, with employee representatives arguing it was too easily abused.

### Why didn't the directive harmonise insolvency protections?

The 2001 directive explicitly allows Member States to decide whether employee rights transfer when a business is transferred from an insolvent entity. This was a political compromise: Germany and France wanted to protect struggling companies from inheriting liabilities, while the UK and Sweden pushed for full protection. The European Parliament's 1997 resolution had called for uniform insolvency rules, but the Council dropped the proposal under pressure from the German Bundesrat.
