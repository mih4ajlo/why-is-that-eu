## What is it?

The Foreign Subsidies Regulation (FSR), which entered into force in July 2023 with full application from October 2023, gives the European Commission new powers to investigate and address distortions caused by financial contributions from non-EU governments to companies active in the EU. It covers three types of transactions: large mergers and acquisitions (where the target or turnover thresholds are met), large public procurement bids (above €250 million), and other market situations on the Commission's own initiative.

Companies must notify the Commission of such transactions if they have received foreign financial contributions exceeding €50 million from a single non-EU country in the three years prior. The Commission can then conduct an in-depth investigation and, if it finds distortion, impose remedies—such as divestitures, repayment of the subsidy, or prohibition of the transaction. The FSR fills a gap: while the EU has long monitored subsidies from its own member states (through state aid rules) and can impose anti-dumping duties on goods, it had no equivalent tool to address subsidies from third countries that distort competition in the internal market, particularly in services, public procurement, and M&A.

## Why was it introduced?

The FSR was born from a decade of rising tensions over Chinese state-owned enterprises buying strategic European assets—and from the EU's inability to respond under existing rules. The trigger was a specific incident: in 2016, Chinese state-owned ChemChina acquired Swiss agrochemical giant Syngenta for $43 billion, the largest foreign acquisition by a Chinese company at the time. Under EU merger control, the Commission could review the deal for competition concerns but had no power to examine whether the €20 billion in Chinese state loans that financed the acquisition constituted an unfair subsidy. The same pattern repeated: in 2017, Chinese state-owned COSCO took a 67% stake in the Piraeus Port Authority in Greece; in 2018, Geely's acquisition of a 9.7% stake in Daimler raised concerns about Chinese state funding of automotive technology.

The structural tension was clear: the EU's state aid regime (Articles 107-109 TFEU) applied only to subsidies from member states, not from third countries. Meanwhile, the EU's trade defence instruments (anti-dumping, anti-subsidy) covered goods but not services, investments, or public procurement. This created an asymmetry: a Chinese state-owned enterprise could use subsidised loans to buy a European port or power grid, and the Commission had no legal basis to intervene—even if the subsidy would have been illegal if granted by a French or German government.

The coalition that pushed for the legislation was unusually broad. The European Parliament's centre-right EPP group, led by German MEP Christian Ehler, had long called for a "level playing field" instrument. France pushed hardest among member states, with President Emmanuel Macron warning in 2018 that China was "buying up Europe." Germany was initially more cautious, concerned about retaliation against its auto exports. But two events shifted Berlin's position: in 2019, the Commission's impact assessment identified that "foreign subsidies had led to market distortions in at least 130 cases" in sectors including railways, renewable energy, and digital infrastructure, and the Chinese state's Belt and Road investments in EU member states—particularly the Piraeus port deal—strained the German consensus. The centrist German industry association BDI changed its position in 2020, calling for new instruments, as Daimler and Siemens grew worried about Chinese competitors in their home market.

The European Commission's proposal, published on 5 May 2021 under Executive Vice-President Margrethe Vestager, explicitly referenced the Piraeus case and the need to "close the regulatory gap." The timing was crucial: three months earlier, in February 2021, the Commission had blocked the merger of Chinese state-owned railway equipment suppliers CRRC and Bombardier Transportation—but only because of competition concerns, not subsidies. That case highlighted the limits of existing tools.

The legislative process moved quickly by EU standards. The European Parliament adopted its position on 15 February 2022 with 590 votes in favour, 80 against, and 30 abstentions—a large majority reflecting cross-party consensus. The Council of the EU adopted the regulation on 28 November 2022, after a final compromise that reduced the notification threshold from the Commission's original proposal of €500 million for procurement bids to €250 million, as favoured by smaller member states worried about administrative burden. The regulation was published in the Official Journal on 23 December 2022, with application from 12 July 2023.

- **2016** — ChemChina's $43 billion acquisition of Syngenta, financed with Chinese state loans, raises concerns in EU capitals about lack of tools to review foreign subsidies

- **2019** — The European Commission publishes its "White Paper on levelling the playing field with regard to foreign subsidies" (17 June), proposing options for a new instrument.
- **5 May 2021** — The Commission adopts the legislative proposal for the Foreign Subsidies Regulation, proposed by Executive Vice-President Margrethe Vestager.
- **15 February 2022** — European Parliament adopts its position with 590-80-30.

- **23 December 2022** — Regulation published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **12 July 2023** — Regulation enters into force; provisions on notification of large concentrations and procurement become applicable from 12 October 2023.
- **October 2023–present** — First notifications filed; Commission opens initial ex-officio investigations into market sectors including public procurement of railway equipment and Chinese solar panel manufacturers.

## Impact and consequences

The FSR's most immediate impact has been on transaction planning. Law firms and investment banks routinely advise clients that any deal involving a Chinese state-owned enterprise or Middle Eastern sovereign wealth fund may trigger notification. According to the Commission's own 2024 enforcement report, after the first year of application, the Commission had received over 100 notifications for mergers and over 20 for public procurement. It opened formal investigations in 8 cases, all involving Chinese or Middle Eastern entities.

The first significant enforcement action came in March 2024, when the Commission opened an investigation into a Chinese state-owned rolling stock manufacturer, CRRC, concerning a public procurement bid in Bulgaria for electric trains worth €610 million. The Commission suspected that CRRC had benefited from subsidies including state guarantees, export credits, and direct grants worth over €100 million. In response, CRRC withdrew its bid—an outcome the Commission described as showing the regulation's deterrent effect.

Companies affected extend beyond China. The FSR also applies to subsidies from the United States (particularly under the Inflation Reduction Act's green subsidies), from the Gulf states, and from Russia. In July 2024, the Commission opened an investigation into subsidies received by a Spanish subsidiary of the Abu Dhabi-based sovereign wealth fund Mubadala. Some European companies—particularly in Germany—have complained that the FSR adds bureaucratic costs to legitimate investments, estimating that compliance costs for a medium-sized firm can exceed €200,000 per transaction.

The FSR has also created tension with trading partners. Beijing has warned that the regulation could be used to discriminate against Chinese companies, and in December 2023, China filed a formal complaint at the WTO, arguing the FSR violates WTO rules on most-favoured-nation treatment. The case remains pending.

## Key questions answered

### Why didn't existing EU rules on government handouts to companies already cover subsidies from non-EU countries?

EU state aid rules (Articles 107-109 TFEU) apply only to subsidies granted by EU member states. They have no jurisdiction over financial contributions from non-EU governments. The EU's trade defence instruments (anti-subsidy and anti-dumping regulations) cover only goods traded across borders—not services, investments, or public procurement. The FSR was designed specifically to fill this gap for all economic activities within the internal market.

### Why do companies have to report foreign subsidies worth €50 million or €250 million to the EU?

The €50 million threshold for foreign financial contributions from a single non-EU country over three years was set after the Commission's impact assessment found that below this level, distortions were rarely material. The €250 million threshold for public procurement bids was a political compromise: the Commission initially proposed €500 million, but smaller member states argued this would miss too many cases, while industry groups wanted a higher threshold to reduce administrative burden. The final figure was chosen to capture the largest, most strategically sensitive bids while not overloading the Commission with notifications for routine contracts.

### How do the EU's new rules on foreign subsidies fit with global trade agreements?

The FSR's compatibility with WTO law is contested. China filed a WTO dispute in December 2023, arguing that the regulation violates the Most-Favoured-Nation principle and the General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS) by singling out companies from specific countries. The EU defends the FSR as a unilateral internal market regulation, not a trade instrument, and points to precedent: the US has similar rules under the US International Emergency Economic Powers Act. The WTO case is still pending as of mid-2025.

### What happens if a company ignores the EU's rules on foreign subsidies?

Companies that fail to notify notifiable transactions can face fines of up to 10% of their global annual turnover. The Commission can also reverse completed transactions. In practice, the deterrent effect has been strong: after the CRRC Bulgaria case, several Chinese companies voluntarily disclosed financial contributions that they had previously not reported. The Commission's investigation powers include dawn raids and requests for internal documents covering up to 10 years of transactions.
