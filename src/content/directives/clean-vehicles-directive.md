## What is it?

The Clean Vehicles Directive (CVD) mandates that public procurement of road transport vehicles — including cars, vans, trucks, and buses — must factor in their lifetime energy consumption and environmental externalities (like CO2, NOx, and particulate matter emissions) alongside purchase price. Originally adopted in 2009 as Directive 2009/33/EC, it was significantly revised in 2019 by Directive 2019/1161/EU to set binding minimum procurement targets for clean vehicles.

Public authorities spend between 14% and 20% of EU GDP on procurement, as the European Commission noted in its 2017 impact assessment. The CVD taps into this purchasing power to create early demand for clean technologies, helping manufacturers scale production and reduce costs — a classic "demand-pull" policy. It covers vehicle purchases, leases, rent-to-buy arrangements, and public service transport contracts (like bus concessions).

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's seeds were planted by a specific event: the European Commission's 2005 launch of a "Green Paper on Public Procurement" (COM(2005) 643), which argued that public buyers' purchasing power could drive markets for environmentally friendlier goods. But the real urgency came from two converging pressures: the EU's struggle to meet its Kyoto Protocol commitments under the 1997 UN Framework Convention on Climate Change, and escalating air quality crises in cities like Milan, Stuttgart, and London — where diesel bus fleets were a major NOx source. In 2006, Eurostat data showed transport was responsible for 24% of EU greenhouse gas emissions, with road transport accounting for 78% of that share.

The Commission's Directorate-General for Mobility and Transport (DG MOVE) proposed the first draft in 2007 under Commissioner Jacques Barrot (Energy and Transport). The primary targets were city authorities and regional transport agencies, which wielded enormous procurement budgets: the German city of Berlin, for example, spent roughly €1 billion annually on public transport procurement in the mid-2000s. The original 2009 version was timid — it required only that contracting authorities "take into account" energy and environmental impacts when buying vehicles, without binding quotas. This was weaker than the Commission had wanted, following heavy lobbying from automotive manufacturers' associations (ACEA) and bus operators (UITP). ACEA warned that binding targets would disrupt production cycles and increase vehicle costs by 15-20%, according to the Commission's 2007 impact assessment.

The coalition that pushed the directive through was unusual. For once, the European Parliament's environment (ENVI) and transport (TRAN) committees united with northern member states (Sweden, Netherlands, Denmark) that already used green procurement criteria, against a blocking minority of southern and central European states (Italy, Poland, Czechia) concerned about costs to regional transport authorities. The final vote in the Council was close: 18 member states in favour, 6 against (including Germany, which later abstained), and 4 abstentions. Germany's opposition stemmed from Volkswagen and Daimler's fear that early adoption of electric buses would undermine their diesel bus sales — a prescient concern, given the 2015 "dieselgate" scandal.

What changed the dynamic between 2009 and the 2019 revision was concrete market evidence. By 2017, the Commission's ex-post evaluation found the original directive had "limited to no impact" — fewer than 15% of member states (Sweden, Austria, Netherlands) had transposed it with meaningful operational targets. The rest treated it as "best-effort" guidance. Meanwhile, China's 2015 "Made in China 2025" plan set aggressive electric bus procurement targets, and Chinese manufacturers (BYD, Yutong) began exporting zero-emission buses to Europe — taking market share from European incumbents. The 2015 Paris Agreement added further political pressure.

The 2019 revision succeeded because of a shifted coalition. France, under President Macron, dropped prior opposition to binding targets in exchange for a long phase-in period. The Netherlands championed the revision through the Dutch presidency of the EU in 2016. The EPP (conservative) group in the Parliament, traditionally pro-industry, backed it after lobbying from Scania and Volvo, which had already developed hybrid-electric truck platforms. The final deal (2019/1161/EU) set binding minimum procurement targets: 17.5% of new public buses and 38.5% of new public trucks (light-duty) must be "clean" by 2025, rising to 30% for buses and 50% for trucks by 2030.

## Timeline

- **2005** — European Commission publishes Green Paper "Public Procurement for a Better Environment", arguing public purchasing can drive clean vehicle markets.
- **2007** — Commission proposes first directive draft under Commissioner Jacques Barrot.
- **2008** — European Parliament's ENVI and TRAN committees vote to strengthen proposals with binding targets; Council waters them down.
- **2009** — Directive 2009/33/EC adopted; enters into force requiring only that energy and environmental impacts be "taken into account".
- **2013** — Commission's ex-post evaluation finds the directive has "limited to no impact"; only Sweden, Austria, and Netherlands have active implementation programmes.
- **2015** — Volkswagen emissions scandal (dieselgate) destroys trust in diesel technology; Chinese electric bus makers begin European exports.
- **2017** — Commission proposal for revised directive (COM(2017) 653 final), with binding minimum procurement targets for 2025 and 2030.
- **2018** — Parliament and Council negotiations; France shifts position under Macron; Scania and Volvo lobby EPP group.
- **2019** — Revised directive (2019/1161/EU) adopted; binding targets set: 17.5% of public buses by 2025, 38.5% of light-duty trucks.
- **2020** — Member states must transpose revised directive into national law by 2 August 2020.
- **2025** — First binding procurement targets become applicable.
- **2030** — Second set of targets (30% buses, 50% trucks) apply.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's first iteration (2009-2019) had minimal market impact: a 2017 Commission evaluation found that only 8% of new public buses purchased in the EU during that period were electric or hybrid. Post-2019, the effects are more visible. By 2022, according to the European Alternative Fuels Observatory, electric bus registrations reached 4,150 units in the EU (about 7% of new public buses), up from 1,200 in 2018. Germany, a former opponent, had 1,000 electric buses by 2023 — though this was still far short of the 2025 target.

The most dramatic consequence has been market disruption. BYD, China's largest electric bus manufacturer, opened assembly plants in Hungary (2017) and France (2018) specifically to serve European public procurement tenders. European bus makers (Mercedes-Benz, MAN, Volvo, Scania) accelerated electric bus development: by 2023, all major European OEMs offered zero-emission bus models. The European Commission's 2022 report on transposition found that 24 of 27 member states had transposed the revised directive correctly — but three (Poland, Czechia, Portugal) relied on exemptions for rural and long-distance routes.

Industry complained of implementation costs, particularly for smaller cities. The German Association of Towns and Municipalities estimated in 2023 that meeting the 2025 bus targets would require €1.5 billion in charging infrastructure investments for German cities alone. The Commission's 2023 implementation review acknowledged that "challenges around grid capacity and charging infrastructure" remain binding constraints, particularly in southern and eastern member states.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the 2009 original fail to change behaviour?

The 2009 directive was drafted too weakly: it asked contracting authorities only to "take into account" lifetime costs, without setting procurement quotas or sanctions for non-compliance. Most member states treated it as non-binding guidance, and procurement officials continued selecting the cheapest upfront purchase price — as the 2017 Commission evaluation showed, only three states actively implemented it. Industry lobbying from ACEA and German manufacturers specifically watered down the Council's version.

### Why did the 2019 revision succeed where earlier efforts failed?

The dieselgate scandal shattered trust in diesel technology's environmental credentials, making continued opposition politically toxic. Chinese electric bus exports to Europe created competitive pressure on European manufacturers (Volvo, Scania) who then lobbied *for* binding targets to secure guaranteed demand. France, under President Macron, dropped prior opposition in exchange for a long phase-in period. The EPA studies on air quality health impacts further shifted the political calculus.

### How does this interact with the Public Procurement Directive (2014/24/EU)?

The Clean Vehicles Directive operates as a *lex specialis* within the broader EU public procurement framework. While Directive 2014/24/EU allows "green" criteria, the CVD mandates them for road transport vehicles specifically. Member states must include lifetime cost calculations (including energy and emissions) in tender evaluations, overriding Directive 2014/24's default "lowest price" rule. The European Court of Justice in Case C-513/99 (2003) had already established that public authorities could include environmental criteria, but the CVD made it compulsory.
