## What is it?

The Late Payments Directive (2011/7/EU) establishes maximum payment periods and interest charges for commercial transactions to prevent one party — typically a larger company or public authority — from deliberately delaying payment to another, usually a smaller enterprise. It replaced an earlier 2000 directive that had proven toothless.

Under the directive, payment must be completed within 60 calendar days for business-to-business transactions unless the parties explicitly agree otherwise and it is not grossly unfair. For public authorities, the maximum is 30 calendar days, with no room for contractual extension beyond 60 days. If a debtor pays late, the creditor is automatically entitled to interest at a rate of at least eight percentage points above the European Central Bank's reference rate, plus a fixed compensation of €40 for recovery costs.

The directive aims to tackle a structural problem in the EU single market: large buyers exploiting their market power to use small suppliers as free lines of credit. When a retailer or manufacturer systematically pays invoices at 120 or 180 days, the supplier must finance the gap with bank loans or overdrafts — costs that the Commission estimated were killing thousands of SMEs annually through forced insolvencies.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a single scandal but a decade of mounting evidence that the original 2000 Late Payments Directive had failed. By 2008, the European Commission's own impact assessment found that one in four bankruptcies in the EU was directly caused by late payment — about 450,000 businesses per year losing €600 million in additional interest costs. The problem was worst in southern Europe: in Italy, average payment times exceeded 100 days; in Spain, public authorities were routinely paying 18 months late.

But the political breakthrough came from a coalition of unlikely allies. Small business associations — notably UEAPME (the European Association of Craft, Small and Medium-sized Enterprises) — had been lobbying Brussels since the early 2000s, arguing that the 2000 directive's weakness was its lack of enforcement mechanisms. The 2000 version had set only aspirational 30-day targets and let member states interpret "grossly unfair" payment terms however they chose. A 2004 Commission report showed it had changed little: payment times had actually worsened in Greece, Portugal, and Italy.

The coalition expanded in 2008 when the financial crisis hit. Suddenly, bank lending to SMEs dried up across Europe. Cash flow became a matter of survival. The European Parliament's Internal Market Committee, chaired by Malcolm Harbour (ECR, UK), began pushing for mandatory maximum terms. Harbour's 2009 draft report proposed slashing the public sector limit to 30 days and introducing automatic interest penalties without requiring the creditor to send a reminder — a key reform demanded by SMEs who feared retaliation if they chased payments.

The opposition was fierce. Large retailers and construction companies — represented by Eurocommerce and the European Construction Industry Federation — argued that 60-day limits would disrupt supply chain financing models. Germany's retail sector warned of "catastrophic" consequences. In the Council, the German and Austrian governments initially resisted strict public sector limits, arguing that local governments needed flexibility. The UK government, under Labour, was also cautious, citing the need for "commercial freedom."

The breakthrough came in 2010. The new Barroso II Commission, with Michel Barnier as Internal Market Commissioner, made late payments a flagship of the Small Business Act. Barnier — a former French minister who had seen the problem up close — personally championed the 30-day public sector limit. In the European Parliament, the rapporteurs were Barbara Weiler (S&D, Germany) and Tadeusz Zwiefka (EPP, Poland), who forged a compromise: public authorities got a hard 30-day limit; businesses could agree to 60 days if it was "not grossly unfair" to the creditor.

The Council adopted the directive in February 2011 by qualified majority, with Italy and Greece — two of the worst offenders — voting against, and Luxembourg abstaining. Italy's government argued that the 30-day public sector limit was impossible given its regional administrations' chronic underfunding. The Commission pushed back, citing a 2010 study showing that Italian public bodies were paying suppliers an average of 190 days late — costing SMEs €7 billion annually in interest and financing charges.

## Timeline

- **2000** — First Late Payments Directive (2000/35/EC) adopted, setting voluntary 30-day targets but with weak enforcement.
- **2004** — Commission report finds the 2000 directive had minimal impact; payment times had worsened in several member states.
- **2008** — Financial crisis hits; SME access to credit collapses. Commission impact assessment finds late payment causes 25% of EU bankruptcies.
- **October 2009** — European Parliament's Internal Market Committee publishes Harbour report proposing strict 30-day limits for public authorities.
- **March 2010** — Commission proposes new directive with 30-day public sector limit and 60-day B2B default, plus automatic interest.
- **February 2011** — Council adopts 2011/7/EU by qualified majority; Italy, Greece vote against; Luxembourg abstains.
- **March 2013** — Member states must transpose the directive into national law. Deadline is missed by several countries.
- **2014** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against Italy, Spain, and Portugal for failing to implement 30-day public sector limits.
- **2017** — Commission report finds improvement but persistent problems: average payment times in Spain still at 85 days for public contracts.
- **2023** — Commission opens a review of the directive, considering whether to introduce a hard 30-day limit for all B2B transactions.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's immediate effect was limited: the Commission's 2016 REFIT evaluation found that while public sector payment times had fallen in countries like Spain (from 190 days to 85 days), overall business-to-business late payment remained endemic. In Italy, average payment times barely budged from 100 days. The problem was enforcement — SMEs were still afraid to claim the automatic interest and €40 compensation for fear of losing future contracts.

The biggest change was in the public sector. Spain's central government introduced an electronic invoicing system to comply, and by 2018 claimed to have cut average payment times to 30 days — though regional and local authorities lagged. Italy created a special state fund in 2013 to pay off €40 billion in accumulated arrears to suppliers, but implementation was staggered and incomplete. The European Court of Justice's 2016 ruling in *Grupo Itevelesa* (C-35/15) clarified that national courts must apply the directive's 30-day limit even if the contract says otherwise.

Industry response was mixed. Large retailers like Carrefour and Metro AG adapted by shortening their standard payment terms to 60 days for most suppliers, but pushed back with longer terms for strategic partners. Construction firms in Germany and Austria continued using 90-day payment chains, arguing the directive's "grossly unfair" exception was vague. The Commission's 2020 evaluation found that 29% of European invoices were still paid late in 2019, with SMEs in Greece, Italy, and Romania hardest hit.

The COVID-19 pandemic saw a temporary reversal: the Commission allowed member states to derogate from the 30-day rule for public health spending, but urged them to maintain discipline. By 2023, the Commission was reviewing whether to introduce a mandatory 30-day cap for all B2B transactions, a proposal backed by SME Europe (the EPP's SME association) but opposed by BusinessEurope.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the 2011 directive replace the 2000 version?

The 2000 directive failed because it set only aspirational targets and let member states define "grossly unfair" payment terms however they chose. A 2004 Commission evaluation found it had no measurable impact; payment times actually worsened in several countries. The 2011 directive introduced hard limits, automatic interest, and fixed compensation — but enforcement remains the weak link.

### Why are public authorities treated differently from businesses?

The Commission's 2008 impact assessment found that public authorities were the worst late payers, with some regional governments in Spain and Italy taking 18 months to pay. They also have little commercial incentive to pay on time — unlike businesses, they do not risk losing future contracts. The 30-day limit was considered stricter because public authorities "set the example" for the economy.

### Why is the directive still controversial?

BusinessEurope and large retail associations argue that 60-day limits are too rigid for global supply chains, where payment terms of 90-120 days are common in sectors like automotive and electronics. SME groups counter that these long terms are exactly the kind of market power abuse the directive was meant to curb. The Commission's 2023 review is examining whether to extend hard 30-day limits to all B2B transactions, a move that would face fierce opposition.

### What happens if a member state ignores the directive?

The Commission launches infringement proceedings. In 2014, it took Italy, Spain, and Portugal to court for failing to transpose the 30-day public sector limit. The European Court of Justice ruled against Italy in 2017 (C-262/15), and the Commission has continued monitoring. However, fining member states is rare — the Commission prefers "structured dialogue" and EU funds conditional on reform.
