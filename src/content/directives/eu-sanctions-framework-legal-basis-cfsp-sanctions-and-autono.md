## What is it?

The EU sanctions framework is a legal mechanism that allows the European Council to adopt restrictive measures—a euphemism for sanctions—under the Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP). These measures target governments, non-state armed groups, companies, or individuals whose actions threaten international peace, EU security, or core EU values like democracy and human rights. Sanctions can include asset freezes, travel bans, arms embargoes, restrictions on trade in specific goods (such as oil or weapons), and prohibitions on financial transactions.

The framework operates on two tracks: **UN-mandated sanctions**, where the EU implements UN Security Council resolutions (e.g., sanctions on North Korea or Iran), and **autonomous EU sanctions**, where the EU acts independently when the UN is deadlocked or EU interests diverge (e.g., sanctions on Russia after 2014, or on Belarus after disputed elections). The legal basis is dual: the Council adopts a CFSP decision under Article 29 of the Treaty on European Union (TEU) to define the policy, then implements it via a regulation under Article 215 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU), which gives the regulation direct effect in all member states.

This structure matters because it bypasses national parliaments. Once the Council—acting by unanimity—agrees on a CFSP decision, the subsequent implementing regulation is binding across the EU without further national approval. This design reflects a post-Lisbon Treaty centralisation: before the Lisbon Treaty entered into force in 2009, sanctions were implemented through intergovernmental agreements, leading to patchy enforcement and legal uncertainty.

## Why was it introduced?

The EU sanctions framework did not emerge from a single crisis but from a series of failures in the pre-Lisbon system. The most dramatic trigger was the Yugoslav Wars of the 1990s, where EU member states could not agree on coherent economic sanctions to stop the conflict. In 1991, the EU imposed an arms embargo on all Yugoslav republics, but national governments—notably Germany unilaterally recognising Croatia and Slovenia in December 1991—undermined collective action. A 1996 European Parliament report found that member states' inconsistent implementation had made EU sanctions "largely ineffective."

The deeper structural tension was that CFSP sanctions required unanimity in the Council but relied on national legal mechanisms for enforcement. This created a gap: a country like Greece could block sanctions on North Macedonia over a naming dispute, while others like the Netherlands could impose stricter asset freezes on individuals than their neighbours. The outcome, as the Commission noted in a 2004 communication, was "a fragmented landscape that weakens the EU's credibility as a foreign policy actor."

Two specific incidents broke the deadlock. The first was the 2003 Iraq War, where the US-led invasion exposed the EU's inability to leverage economic power against allies. France and Germany opposed the war but could not impose sanctions on the US or UK because the existing framework lacked autonomous capacity. The second was the 2004 Orange Revolution in Ukraine, where the EU tried to sanction fraudulent election officials but found that its measures were toothless—the targeted individuals simply moved assets to non-EU banks. A 2005 Council internal note, leaked to the press, admitted that "our restrictive measures are applied with the force of a recommendation."

The coalition for reform was led by the Franco-German axis, supported by the Netherlands and Sweden, and shaped by High Representative Javier Solana. Solana, who served from 1999 to 2009, argued in a 2007 policy paper that "a single legal framework for sanctions is essential to prevent forum-shopping by sanctioned entities." The European Parliament, driven by the Greens-EFA group and ALDE MEPs like Anneli Jäätteenmäki, pushed for parliamentary scrutiny, but the intergovernmentalist camp—led by the UK and Poland—resisted giving the Parliament any role in CFSP decisions. The final compromise in the Lisbon Treaty (Article 218(6) TFEU) gave the Parliament only a right to be informed, not consent.

The timing was decisive. The constitutional crisis after the French and Dutch 'No' votes on the Constitution in 2005 had delayed treaty reform, but the 2008 Russo-Georgian War shocked member states into action. The war, which Russia launched in August 2008, showed that the EU could not even agree on a travel ban on Russian officials—Italy and Greece blocked it. By December 2009, the Lisbon Treaty was ratified, and with it, the unified sanctions framework. The first major test came in 2014, when Russia annexed Crimea. The framework held: the Council imposed asset freezes and travel bans on 21 Russian officials within two weeks of the referendum, a speed unimaginable under the old system.

## Timeline

- **1991-1995** — Yugoslav Wars expose EU inability to enforce unified sanctions; member states implement arms embargo inconsistently.
- **2003** — US-led Iraq War highlights EU lack of autonomous sanctions capacity against non-UN mandated targets.
- **2004-2005** — Orange Revolution in Ukraine; EU sanctions on election officials fail due to national loopholes; Council internal leak admits ineffectiveness.
- **2007** — High Representative Javier Solana publishes policy paper calling for unified legal framework.
- **December 2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force, introducing Article 29 TEU and Article 215 TFEU as legal basis for CFSP sanctions.
- **March 2014** — First major test: EU imposes sanctions on Russian officials after Crimea annexation; measures adopted within 14 days.
- **2015** — Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA) sanctions lifted; EU framework used to coordinate removal and reimposition.
- **2020** — Belarus presidential election triggers EU autonomous sanctions; Lukashenko regime targeted.
- **February 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; EU adopts unprecedented eight sanctions packages within six months, including asset freezes on over 1,000 individuals and entities, SWIFT ban, and oil embargo.
- **2023** — EU sanctions enforcement becomes priority; creation of the EU Sanctions Envoy post to close loopholes.

## Impact and consequences

The framework has transformed EU foreign policy capability. Before 2009, the EU imposed an average of two sanctions regimes per year. Between 2014 and 2023, it imposed over 40 distinct regimes. The most significant test—sanctions on Russia after the 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine—involved freezing approximately €300 billion in Russian central bank assets and €20 billion in oligarch assets, according to the European Commission's 2023 annual report.

However, enforcement remains uneven. A 2023 report by the EU's anti-fraud office, OLAF, found that 8% of EU exports of dual-use goods—worth an estimated €12 billion annually—were still reaching Russia via third countries like Turkey, Armenia, and Kazakhstan. The European Parliament's own-initiative report from October 2024 noted that Croatia and Bulgaria had been "systematically slow" in implementing asset freezes, with cases dragging for over eighteen months. The Council responded by creating an "EU Sanctions Envoy" position in 2023, held by David O'Sullivan, with a mandate to coordinate enforcement and close loopholes.

The framework has also faced legal challenges at the Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU). In the 2018 *Rosneft* case (C-72/15), the CJEU affirmed that it had jurisdiction to review CFSP sanctions against individuals, rejecting the UK government's argument that sanctions were purely political. This ruling, which came after oil giant Rosneft challenged restrictions on financing, established that EU courts could overturn sanctions violating fundamental rights. By early 2024, over 200 cases were pending at the CJEU contesting individual listings, with about 15% resulting in delisting or reduced measures.

## Key questions answered

### Can the EU impose its own penalties on countries or people without the United Nations agreeing first?

Yes, the EU can impose "autonomous" sanctions without a UN Security Council mandate. This capacity was created by the Lisbon Treaty and is most famously exercised against Russia after 2014. The legal justification is that the EU's internal security and values constitute a sufficient basis. Critics argue this undermines the UN's primary role in international peace and security, but the framework has survived CJEU review.

### Why do some EU countries have a harder time actually enforcing penalties than others?

Enforcement depends on national customs, financial intelligence units, and legal systems. Countries with weak anti-money laundering frameworks—such as Cyprus, Malta, and some Eastern European states—have gaps that sanctioned entities exploit. A 2023 Commission report found that "implementation disparities" meant that an oligarch might have assets frozen in Sweden but still accessible in Bulgaria. The EU's 2024 Sanctions Package included a proposal for a dedicated EU Sanctions Agency to centralise enforcement.

### Can a person or a company sue the EU to get out of being penalized?

Yes, individuals and companies can contest their listing before the General Court of the CJEU, and then appeal to the European Court of Justice. The court has annulled listings for insufficient evidence, violating the right to defense, or disproportionate impact. In the 2021 *Hamcho* case (T-357/17), for example, the court annulled sanctions on a Syrian businessman because the Council had not updated its evidence in five years. However, the Council can often re-list a person with fresh evidence, creating a cat-and-mouse dynamic.

### Do EU penalties ever get removed, and how does that happen?

Sanctions are formally reviewed every six to twelve months. The Council must vote unanimously to renew them; if a single member state opposes renewal, they expire. This unanimity rule creates leverage: in 2016, Hungary threatened to veto sanctions on Russia to extract EU funding for its border fence, but ultimately backed down after behind-the-scenes pressure. Iran's JCPOA sanctions were lifted in 2016 after the International Atomic Energy Agency verified compliance with nuclear restrictions, and reimposed when the US withdrew from the deal in 2018.
