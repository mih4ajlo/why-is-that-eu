## What is it?

The Renewable Energy Directive III (RED III, 2023/2413/EU) is the third major revision of the EU's 2009 renewable energy framework. It sets a legally binding target for the EU to obtain **42.5%** of its final energy consumption from renewable sources by 2030, with a further indicative ambition to reach 45%. This is nearly double the previous 32% target set in RED II (2018).

The directive's most operationally significant change is a radical simplification of permitting rules. Member States must now designate "renewables acceleration areas" (go-to areas) where permitting for wind and solar projects is capped at **12 months** (extensions to 18 months for offshore wind). Outside these zones, permits must be issued within **24 months**. All renewable projects are presumed to be of "overriding public interest", sharply limiting legal challenges that can halt construction. National authorities must also prioritise grid connection for projects in acceleration areas and pre-approve the grid capacity needed for the national renewable energy plan.

## Why was it introduced?

The most specific and dramatic trigger was Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on **24 February 2022**. Within days, the European Commission — then led by Ursula von der Leyen (President) and Kadri Simson (Energy Commissioner) — began drafting the REPowerEU plan. The price of TTF gas futures, the European benchmark, jumped from roughly €80/MWh in January 2022 to over €300/MWh by August 2022, pushing European industry and households into crisis. Europe imported approximately 155 billion cubic metres of Russian gas in 2021; by late 2022, this had collapsed to about 60 bcm.

But the permitting crisis was already well-documented before the war. The Commission's **2020 impact assessment** for the original 2030 Climate Target Plan had identified permitting delays as the single biggest bottleneck for renewable deployment. A 2019 report by WindEurope (the industry association) found that onshore wind farms in Germany were taking an average of **5 to 7 years** from application to commissioning; in France, a similar period. Solar projects in Italy could take over **3 years**. The primary cause was not technical — it was legal: under RED II, local courts could suspend projects on procedural grounds, and environmental impact assessments for small projects were often as lengthy as for large ones.

The coalition that drove RED III through was a classic "climate-security" alliance. The **Renew Europe** group in the European Parliament (centrists), the **Greens/EFA**, and the **S&D** (Socialists & Democrats) formed the majority for the higher 42.5% target. The European People's Party (EPP) was split: its German and French MEPs, many from rural districts, opposed the sweeping permitting simplifications — fearing loss of local control and weaker nature protection. The far-right **Identity and Democracy** group voted overwhelmingly against the entire directive, calling it "ideological" in plenary debate.

The timing was critical: the REPowerEU plan, published in May 2022, had introduced an emergency **Council Regulation (2022/2577)** that temporarily allowed accelerated permitting by overriding national planning laws — but this expired in December 2023. RED III made those emergency rules permanent and expanded them, winning over Member States like **Poland** and **Hungary** — which had blocked higher targets for years — by framing the directive as an energy security measure, not just a climate one.

What finally unblocked years of deadlock on higher targets was the **European court of auditors' special report 18/2023**, published in June 2023, which found that the EU was on track to miss its original 32% target by a wide margin: under then-current policies, renewables would reach only 30-31% by 2030. The report also highlighted that 14 Member States had failed to transpose RED II on time. This gave the Parliament's rapporteur, **Markus Pieper (EPP, Germany)**, the leverage to push through the 42.5% target, along with the crucial provision that the Commission would assess the feasibility of 45% within two years.

## Timeline

- **2009** — First Renewable Energy Directive sets a 20% target for 2020
- **2018** — RED II raises the 2030 target to 32% and begins Member State national energy plans
- **2020** — Commission's 2030 Climate Target Plan impact assessment flags permitting as the top bottleneck
- **24 Feb 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; EU gas prices spike to €300+/MWh
- **18 May 2022** — REPowerEU plan published by Commission, introducing emergency permitting rules
- **30 Dec 2022** — Council Regulation (2022/2577) on temporary emergency permitting enters into force
- **16 Mar 2023** — Commission proposes RED III, with a 42.5% target and permanent permitting simplifications
- **12 Sep 2023** — European Parliament adopts RED III with 470 votes in favour, 120 against, 40 abstentions
- **9 Oct 2023** — Council of the EU (Energy ministers) formally adopts the directive by qualified majority
- **20 Nov 2023** — RED III published in the Official Journal of the EU
- **1 Jan 2024** — Member states must designate go-to areas: within 27 months (by **1 Jul 2025**), all Member States must have transposed the main permitting rules into national law
- **1 Jan 2025** — Deadline for more detailed national transposition of the go-to area zoning rules (some provisions by 1 July 2025)

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence has been a surge in renewable project submissions in go-to-area countries. WindEurope reported in early 2024 that Germany had designated its first "wind acceleration areas" covering 1.4% of its land area, with project pipelines increasing by roughly 30% compared to the same period in 2023 under the old rules. In Spain, the government passed Royal Decree-Law 6/2023 in December 2023 transposing elements of RED III early, cutting average solar farm permitting from 18 to 8 months.

But implementation across the 27 Member States is deeply uneven. As of mid-2024, **only 9 countries** had notified the Commission of their go-to area designations. **Austria**, **Belgium**, and **Ireland** are among those that had not, with Ireland's Department of the Environment citing the need for new mapping data on designated natural habitats (necessary for exceptions from the environmental impact assessment requirement). The Commission opened infringement proceedings in July 2024 against **Malta** and **Slovakia** for failing to transpose any part of RED III by the deadline.

For industry, the most contested element is the "overriding public interest" presumption. Environmental NGOs like **ClientEarth** and the **BirdLife International** coalition have argued that this provision, if applied without proper land-use mapping, could undermine the EU's Biodiversity Strategy for 2030 — which requires 30% of EU land and sea area to be protected. ClientEarth filed a legal challenge in the General Court of the European Union in January 2024, arguing that the presumption conflicts with the Birds and Habitats Directives (the core of EU nature law). The case remains pending.

The financial impact is significant: the Commission's 2023 impact assessment estimated that cutting average permitting from 4 years to 1.5 years would reduce project costs by **€8-12/MWh** for onshore wind and **€4-6/MWh** for solar PV, making renewables directly cheaper than gas even without carbon pricing, in most European regions by 2025.

## Key questions answered

### Why does it take so long to build wind farms in Europe?

Before RED III, a typical onshore wind project in Germany or France faced 5-7 years of permitting: local planning reviews, environmental impact assessments (often taking 18 months), grid connection studies, and multiple court appeals by local groups. RED III cuts this to 12 months in designated "acceleration areas" and caps all outside permits at 24 months, while making renewable projects "overriding public interest" — sharply limiting judicial delays.

### Does the EU now control where countries can put wind turbines?

No. Each Member State designates its own "go-to areas" — the directive sets the procedure and time limits, not the map. Countries must assess where renewable potential is high (wind speeds, solar irradiation) and environmental constraints are low (outside Natura 2000 sites, bird migration corridors), then declare these as acceleration zones. They can also decline to designate any areas, meaning the 24-month default cap applies everywhere.

### What happens if a country misses the RED III transposition deadline?

The Commission can open infringement proceedings — a legal process that can ultimately lead to the European Court of Justice imposing fines. As of mid-2024, nine Member States had not submitted national transposition notifications. The first step is a formal notice letter, which the Commission sent to Malta and Slovakia in July 2024. Daily penalty payments, if imposed, can reach tens or hundreds of thousands of euros — the Commission typically uses a formula based on the country's GDP and the seriousness of the infringement.
