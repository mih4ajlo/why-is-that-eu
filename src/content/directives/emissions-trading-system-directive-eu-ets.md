## What is it?

The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is a cap-and-trade system that puts a price on carbon. The EU sets a declining overall cap on emissions from covered sectors — power generation, heavy industry (steel, cement, refineries), and intra-EU aviation. Each year, the cap drops, forcing covered entities to buy enough allowances (each representing one ton of CO2) from an EU-wide auction or secondary market, or to reduce emissions themselves.

Allowance trading began in January 2005, making it the world's first international carbon market. As of 2024, the EU ETS covers about 40% of total EU greenhouse gas emissions, according to the European Environment Agency. A reform known as "Fit for 55" expanded the system to maritime shipping in 2024 and will include road transport, buildings, and small industry via a separate "ETS2" from 2027.

## Why was it introduced?

The EU ETS was born from a political train wreck: the 1997 Kyoto Protocol, where the EU committed to an 8% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from 1990 levels by 2012. The problem was that the EU had no mechanism to enforce that.

Before Kyoto, member states had wildly different climate policies. Germany, under Chancellor Helmut Kohl and Environment Minister Angela Merkel, had introduced generous feed-in tariffs for renewables. The UK, under Tony Blair, was planning a voluntary emissions trading scheme. France had a nuclear-heavy grid with low carbon intensity. But there was no common EU-wide framework.

The crisis came in 1999 when the European Commission's own analysis showed that the existing patchwork of national measures — voluntary agreements, eco-taxes, subsidies — would fail to meet the Kyoto target by a wide margin. According to a 2001 Commission staff working paper, without a binding EU-wide instrument, emissions were projected to rise 5% between 2000 and 2010, not fall.

The coalition that pushed through the ETS was improbable. The European Parliament's Environment Committee, chaired by Dutch MEP Alexander de Roo (Greens/EFA), had been pushing for a carbon tax since the early 1990s. But carbon taxes faced unanimous opposition from industry — specifically from heavy emitters like Lafarge (cement), ThyssenKrupp (steel), and BASF (chemicals) — and from EU member states with energy-intensive economies, notably Poland, Spain, and Italy. The 1992 attempt at a carbon- energy tax had died when a united industry front forced a requirement for unanimous Council approval.

The breakthrough came from a peculiar source: former UK Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher's deregulatory legacy. UK officials at DG Environment, led by Commissioner Margot Wallström (Sweden, 1999–2004), studied the US acid rain cap-and-trade program of the 1990s, which had cut SO2 emissions by 50% at much lower cost than command-and-control regulation. They realized a trading system could be sold to industry as "market-based" — i.e., not a tax. This allowed neoliberal-leaning governments (UK under Blair) to join with environmentalists (Green MEPs) and center-right governments (Germany under Schröder) who feared carbon taxes would hurt competitiveness.

The political timing was decisive. The 2001 US withdrawal from the Kyoto Protocol under President George W. Bush created a crisis of legitimacy for the EU's climate leadership. If Europe did not act quickly and visibly, it would lose all credibility. The European Council in Göteborg in June 2001 explicitly linked the ETS to Kyoto compliance. That October, the Commission published its first formal proposal for a directive.

The opposition was fierce. The European Confederation of Iron and Steel Industries (Eurofer) warned in a 2002 letter to Commissioner Wallström that the scheme would drive steel production out of Europe, destroying 150,000 jobs. The electricity industry lobby, Eurelectric, demanded generous free allowances. Poland and Hungary, with coal-dependent economies, threatened to veto. The compromise: most allowances would be given away for free in the first phases (2005–2007 and 2008–2012), with Member States controlling their own allocation plans. This "National Allocation Plan" system was a disaster waiting to happen — it allowed countries to massively overallocate allowances, which is exactly what happened.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Kyoto Protocol signed; EU commits to 8% reduction by 2012 relative to 1990 levels
- **1999** — Commission analysis shows existing measures insufficient to meet Kyoto target
- **2001** — US withdrawal from Kyoto Protocol; EU Council in Göteborg demands concrete action
- **2001 October** — European Commission publishes first formal ETS proposal
- **2002** — Intense lobbying by industry (Eurofer, Eurelectric, CEFIC) and national governments; free allowances compromise reached
- **2003 October** — Directive 2003/87/EC adopted by European Parliament (379-171 vote) and Council
- **2005 Jan 1** — EU ETS Phase I begins; first day of active trading
- **2006 May** — Verified emissions data published; market crashes when it becomes clear allowances are massively overallocated — price drops from €30 to zero by 2007
- **2008–2012** — Phase II; allowance cap tightened 6.5% below 2005 levels; first inclusion of aviation (scheduled for 2012, later delayed)
- **2013–2020** — Phase III: centralized cap set by EU, auctioning replaces free allocation, cap declines 1.74% annually
- **2016** — Market Stability Reserve (MSR) created to tackle surplus allowances; started operating 2019
- **2017–2018** — "Fit for 55" discussions begin; Commission proposes accelerating cap reduction to 4.2% annually from 2028
- **2021** — Carbon price breaks €50 for first time; by mid-2023 it reaches €100
- **2023 April** — ETS Directive revision (2023/959/EU) adopted, expands to maritime shipping, sets new cap reduction trajectory of 62% of 2005 levels by 2030
- **2024 Jan 1** — Maritime shipping included in ETS
- **2025** — Free allowances for industry begin phase-out (by 2034)
- **2027** — ETS2 for road transport and buildings scheduled to begin (delayed to 2028 if energy prices are exceptionally high)

## Impact and consequences

The result was a roller coaster. In Phase I (2005–2007), overallocation was so severe that the EU carbon price collapsed from €30 to zero within months of verified emissions data being published in May 2006. The European Court of Auditors would later call Phase I "a learning phase that achieved no real emissions reductions." According to the European Environment Agency's 2008 report, emissions in Phase I were actually *higher* than the cap.

The crisis broke the coalitions. Commissioner Wallström's successor, Stavros Dimas (Greece, 2004–2010), pushed for a centralized system with EU-controlled caps. Industry that had benefited from free allowances — notably the German steel giant ThyssenKrupp — argued that the system was too volatile. But the financial crisis of 2008–2009 deepened the problem: industrial output collapsed, so emissions fell, but allowance supply didn't, creating a massive surplus of over 2 billion allowances by 2013.

The turning point came in 2016 with the Market Stability Reserve (MSR). From 2019 onwards, the MSR absorbed surplus allowances when it exceeded a certain threshold, finally putting a floor under prices. By 2021, carbon prices were above €50 and rising, making coal power uncompetitive with gas. The Commission's 2021 Impact Assessment found the ETS had reduced emissions from covered sectors by about 43% compared to 2005 levels — well ahead of the original 21% target.

A concrete consequence: the carbon price spike of 2021–2023 forced several coal-fired power plants in Germany and Poland to close early. The European energy price crisis of 2022 saw governments intervene to cap electricity prices, partly because the ETS was making natural gas power expensive. The Polish government, long opposed to the ETS, began buying allowances on the market for its state-owned energy company PGE, costing billions of zloty.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the price of pollution permits crash in the first trial period of Europe's carbon market?

Because national governments were allowed to allocate their own allowances, and nearly every country overallocated to protect its industries. In Poland, the government issued 28% more allowances than actual emissions. In Germany, the steel lobby succeeded in getting allowances for planned new plants that were never built. The 2006 price crash was entirely predictable — it had happened in the US SO2 program in the 1990s after early over-allocation.

### Why did Europe decide to make airlines buy pollution permits for their flights?

To close a gaping loophole: airlines were entirely exempt from any carbon pricing while the rest of the economy was being regulated. The 2012 inclusion of aviation followed a 2008 Commission proposal, driven by the mounting evidence that air travel emissions were growing 5% annually while road transport declined. Non-European airlines, especially from the US, China, and India, protested, threatening World Trade Organization challenges. The EU ultimately scaled back aviation coverage to intra-EU flights only after a 2011 legal threat from the US.

### Why is Europe's carbon market now seen as working well, even though it failed badly at the start?

Because the switch from free allowances to auctioning, combined with the Market Stability Reserve, finally created scarcity. The 2005–2012 system was essentially a giant subsidy to polluters — they got free permits worth billions of euros. By 2021, auctioning had raised over €57 billion for member states, according to the Commission. The market price signal drove real investment decisions: RWE, Germany's largest CO2 emitter, closed 2.8 GW of lignite capacity in 2022 citing carbon costs. The ETS now works, but only after 15 years of painful reform.

### Why did Europe choose a cap-and-trade system for pollution instead of just taxing carbon directly?

A carbon tax required unanimous approval from all 27 member states, while a directive like the ETS only required qualified majority voting. A single country could veto a tax; Poland vetoed the 1992 carbon tax proposal, and has blocked carbon tax discussion ever since. The cap-and-trade approach was a second-best solution that could pass without unanimity. The irony: the ETS now effectively functions as a carbon tax, but with a variable price set by market conditions rather than a fixed legislative number.
