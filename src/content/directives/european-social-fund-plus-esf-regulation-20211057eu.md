## What is it?

The European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) Regulation (2021/1057/EU) is the EU’s main financial instrument for investing in human capital, merging the previous European Social Fund (ESF), the Youth Employment Initiative (YEI), the Fund for European Aid to the Most Deprived (FEAD), and the EU Programme for Employment and Social Innovation (EaSI). It is part of the 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) and operates under shared management between the European Commission and EU member states.

The ESF+ has a total budget of €99.3 billion in current prices for 2021-2027, according to the Commission’s July 2021 factsheet. Its objectives include promoting employment, supporting education and lifelong learning, fostering social inclusion, and improving health access. A key innovation is the mandatory ring-fencing of at least 25% of member states’ ESF+ resources for social inclusion, with at least 3% of national allocations dedicated to addressing food deprivation and basic material assistance for the most deprived.

## Why was it introduced?

The ESF+ was born from a structural failure in the previous funding architecture: the YEI had been added as an emergency patch after the eurozone crisis, FEAD operated separately from mainstream social policy funds, and EaSI functioned at EU-level without national ties. The Commission’s 2018 impact assessment (SWD/2018/289) found that this fragmentation created "overlaps, administrative burden, and a lack of strategic coherence." Member states often struggled to coordinate YEI-funded youth guarantees with ESF-backed skills programs. The merger aimed to force alignment between active labour market policies and social inclusion measures.

The political coalition for the merger was built around three power centers. The European Parliament’s EMPL committee, led by rapporteur David Casa (EPP, Malta), pushed for stronger social inclusion earmarks. The European Anti-Poverty Network (EAPN) and FEAD stakeholder groups lobbied for at least 5% of ESF+ for food aid, which became 3%. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) demanded binding spending floors for active labour market policies. Germany, France, and Nordic states with strong social models supported tightening coordination but resisted adding health components from the EU4Health programme—a compromise that placed health under ESF+ and excluded it from cohesion policy rules.

Timing was decisive. The regulation was proposed in May 2018, but negotiations were deliberately paced to conclude in 2021, after the COVID-19 pandemic had exposed deep social inequalities. The pandemic’s economic shock, which saw EU unemployment spike from 6.6% in July 2020 to 8.5% in July 2021 according to Eurostat figures, made social investment politically imperative. The Commission under President Ursula von der Leyen explicitly linked the ESF+ to the "European Pillar of Social Rights Action Plan," launched in March 2021, which set EU-level targets for employment (at least 78% employment rate by 2030) and poverty reduction (aiming to lift 15 million people out of poverty). The Council and Parliament reached a provisional agreement on June 26, 2020, just as EU member states were finalising their €750 billion NextGenerationEU recovery plans.

Opposition came from wealthier net contributor states. The Netherlands, Austria, and Sweden argued that increased social spending should remain member state responsibility, not EU competence. They attempted to cap the ESF+ budget during the 2019 MFF negotiations, but failed after the pandemic shifted political sentiment. The European Court of Auditors had also warned in a 2019 report that "the ESF success rate on long-term outcomes is unclear" (Special Report 27/2019 on Skills Training), which critics used to argue against increasing funding. However, the pandemic’s urgency overpowered this scepticism.

What changed the calculus was the May 2020 Commission proposal for a revised MFF that included a dedicated €19.2 billion "REACT-EU" programme for restoring labour markets. This gave negotiators the necessary flexibility: REACT-EU would be channeled through ESF+ rules, providing immediate crisis response while the ESF+ itself was negotiated for the longer term. The merger of YEI, FEAD, and EaSI into one fund also simplified co-financing rules, which member states like Italy and Spain had demanded during the technical discussions.

## Timeline

- **May 2018** — Commission proposes the ESF+ Regulation as part of the 2021-2027 MFF package, with a budget of €101.2 billion (in 2018 prices).
- **January 2019** — European Parliament’s EMPL committee publishes its draft report, demanding stronger social inclusion earmarks.
- **June 26, 2020** — Council and Parliament reach provisional political agreement on the ESF+ Regulation, including the 25% social inclusion ring-fence.
- **December 17, 2020** — European Parliament adopts the regulation by 593 votes in favour, 67 against, and 26 abstentions.
- **June 28, 2021** — Regulation (EU) 2021/1057 is published in the Official Journal of the European Union.
- **July 1, 2021** — The ESF+ enters into force and applies retroactively from January 1, 2021.
- **2022-2023** — Member states submit their ESF+ programmes to the Commission for approval; most are adopted by mid-2023.
- **2024** — First ESF+-funded projects begin reporting under the new monitoring system.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence was administrative simplification for member states. According to the Commission’s 2021 impact assessment, merging four funds reduced the total number of financing instruments from 13 to 5 within the cohesion policy family, cutting reporting requirements by an estimated 20% according to civil service estimates in Poland and Italy. However, the 25% social inclusion ring-fence forced some countries to restructure their spending priorities.

Ireland had to increase its social inclusion allocation from 18% under the 2014-2020 ESF to 28% under ESF+ to meet the requirement, according to the Irish Department of Public Expenditure’s 2022 programme document. Poland redirected €1.2 billion from infrastructure projects to social inclusion, a shift that drew criticism from some local authorities.

The 3% food aid earmark for the most deprived, while small in percentage terms, represented a major policy shift. Under FEAD, food aid accounted for about 60% of total spending in some member states like Portugal. The ESF+ requirement that at least 3% go to food and material assistance meant these programmes were preserved, but capped, which prevented others from expanding them. Food banks in Spain reported in 2023 that the new rules created "uncertainty" about their long-term funding, as the ESF+’s integration into broader social policy programmes made year-to-year allocations harder to predict.

The ESF+ also introduced a mandatory requirement for member states to spend at least 12.5% of their ESF+ resources on youth employment in regions where the youth unemployment rate exceeded the EU average in 2019. This provision directly targeted the 12.9% youth unemployment rate recorded by Eurostat in late 2023 across the EU-27, with Greece, Spain, and Italy still above 20%.

By 2024, the Commission reported that 16 member states had used the ESF+ to fund health workforce training, a welcome but indirect outcome following the COVID-19 crisis. However, the European Parliament’s Committee on Budgetary Control noted in a September 2024 working document that "absorption rates remain slow in several member states, particularly for the social inclusion strand," suggesting implementation challenges persist.

## Key questions answered

### Why merge YEI, FEAD, and EaSI into one fund under the ESF+ Regulation (2021/1057/EU)?

The merger addressed administrative fragmentation that the Commission’s 2018 impact assessment identified as "costly and confusing." Member states running separate programmes for youth employment (YEI), food aid (FEAD), and mainstream labour market support (ESF) often failed to coordinate them, leading to gaps—for example, young people completing YEI-funded training but lacking FEAD-backed social support. The merger forced strategic alignment by requiring all programmes to be part of a single national ESF+ plan.

### Why reserve 3% of ESF+ for food and material aid under the ESF+ Regulation (2021/1057/EU)?

The European Anti-Poverty Network (EAPN) and FEAD practitioners had lobbied for at least 5%, arguing that 4.8 million EU citizens used FEAD food parcels in 2018 according to the Commission’s support system evaluation. The compromise of 3% in the final regulation protected existing FEAD recipients in member states like Portugal and Romania, while not over-committing resources from wealthier states that feared funding would be diverted from active labour market policies.

### Why does the ESF+ include health spending under the ESF+ Regulation (2021/1057/EU)?

The addition was a late compromise during pandemic negotiations. Before COVID-19, health spending under the 2014-2020 MFF was small and fragmented. The Commission’s June 2020 revised MFF proposal merged health into ESF+ while creating a separate, smaller EU4Health programme. The trade-off helped secure parliamentary support from MEPs who wanted larger health buffers after the pandemic, while keeping the main fund focused on social and employment objectives.

### Why did the ESF+ Regulation (2021/1057/EU) take four years from proposal to final adoption?

The delay reflected the broader MFF negotiation, including the pandemic revision. The Commission proposed ESF+ in May 2018, but the MFF negotiations were stalling over net contribution disputes. The pandemic created both the urgency and the budgetary space—by July 2020, the Council had agreed on a €1.8 trillion MFF-plus-NextGenerationEU package. Without the pandemic, the ESF+ might have been adopted in 2020 under the original MFF, but the crisis-linked bump in EU funding actually allowed for a slightly larger fund than initially planned.
