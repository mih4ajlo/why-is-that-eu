## What is it?

The Schengen Information System (SIS) is the European Union’s largest shared law enforcement database. It allows police, border guards, customs, visa authorities, and judicial officers in 30 European countries (all Schengen states plus Ireland) to exchange real‑time alerts on wanted persons, missing people, stolen property, and individuals refused entry.

Regulation 2018/1861/EU is the main legislative revision that upgraded SIS from its second generation (SIS II, operational since 2013) to a more powerful, secure, and legally rigorous system. It took effect on 28 December 2022 for most operational provisions, after a four‑year transition allowed Member States to adapt their national systems.

The regulation introduces new categories of alerts — notably for unknown wanted persons, children at risk, and return decisions against illegally staying third‑country nationals. It also makes biometric data (fingerprints and facial images) compulsory for many alerts, closes data quality loopholes, and strengthens data protection rules by giving individuals a right of access to their own data through national authorities.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2018 reform was driven by two jolts: the 2015 migration crisis and the 2015–2016 terrorist attacks in Paris and Brussels. Before those events, SIS II was seen as a relatively quiet back‑office system used by border guards. After the attacks, it became a political priority to close gaps.

The immediate trigger was the European Council’s demand in February 2016 that the Commission “accelerate work on interoperability of EU information systems for security and border management.” That demand came three months after the Paris attacks of 13 November 2015, in which 130 people died and it emerged that several of the perpetrators had crossed EU internal borders without being flagged in SIS. A subsequent analysis by Europol found that at least two of the Paris attackers had been the subject of alerts in other EU databases but the information was not cross‑referenced.

The Commission, under Home Affairs Commissioner Dimitris Avramopoulos and Security Union Commissioner Julian King, tabled a legislative package in December 2016. The structural tension was that SIS II, designed in the mid‑2000s, had no provisions for storing fingerprints or facial images for most alert types, could not issue alerts for unknown persons (e.g., suspects whose identity is unknown but whose fingerprints were found at a crime scene), and did not cover return decisions — meaning a third‑country national ordered to leave one Schengen state could be re‑admitted by another without warning.

The coalition that pushed the reform was led by France, Belgium, and Germany, supported by Frontex and Europol. They argued that the security deficit was unacceptable. The main opposition came from the European Parliament’s civil liberties committee (LIBE), where some MEPs — notably the Greens and the Left — warned that making biometrics mandatory for all alerts without a clear proportionality test would erode privacy rights and risk creating a de facto identity database for all travellers. The rapporteur for the file, Carlos Coelho (EPP, Portugal), negotiated a compromise: biometrics would be compulsory for alerts on wanted persons for arrest and on missing children, but optional for other categories. The final vote in the Parliament on 8 November 2018 passed with 382 in favour, 113 against, and 29 abstentions — a clear majority but still the most contested part of the security package.

The decisive factor that broke earlier deadlock was the 2015–2016 attack wave. Earlier attempts to upgrade SIS, such as the 2011 Commission proposals for interoperability, had stalled due to member‑state reluctance to share sensitive law‑enforcement data and Parliament’s privacy concerns. The attacks shifted the political calculation: governments could no longer afford to be seen as blocking security improvements. The 2018 regulation was the first major piece of the EU’s “Security Union” agenda to be adopted.

## Timeline

- **1990** — Initial SIS established by the Schengen Convention, operating on a centralised database model.
- **2006** — Decision to develop SIS II, with increased capacity and new functions.
- **9 April 2013** — SIS II goes live, replacing the original system.
- **13 November 2015** — Paris attacks; gaps in SIS data sharing exposed.
- **22 March 2016** — Brussels attacks; political pressure intensifies.
- **February 2016** — European Council calls for quick interoperability of information systems.
- **21 December 2016** — European Commission publishes legislative package to revise SIS (three regulations: 2018/1860, 1861, 1862).
- **November 2017** — EU Parliament committee begins examination; rapporteur Carlos Coelho.
- **May 2018** — Trilogue negotiations conclude.
- **8 November 2018** — European Parliament adopts the three regulations.
- **28 November 2018** — Council adopts the regulations.
- **28 December 2018** — Regulations enter into force (20 days after publication in the Official Journal).
- **28 December 2022** — Main operational provisions apply; all Member States must comply.
- **2023** — First evaluation reports from the Commission, noting data quality and access challenges.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation fundamentally expanded the scope of SIS. By mid‑2023, the system contained over 85 million alerts (according to eu‑LISA, the agency that manages SIS). The new “return decision” alert, which allows Schengen states to exchange information on third‑country nationals who have been ordered to leave, was used more than 1 million times in its first year — demonstrating a sharp increase in intra‑Schengen cooperation on migration enforcement.

Member States had to invest in new biometric processing kits at border crossings and police stations. The estimated cost for all Schengen states was around €250 million, according to the Commission’s 2016 impact assessment. The largest spenders were Germany, France, and Italy, which had the highest volumes of alerts.

Data protection authorities, such as the European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS), raised concerns about the mandatory use of fingerprints for all wanted persons alerts, arguing that it could lead to disproportionate data collection. The EDPS gave a formal opinion in 2017 recommending a stricter proportionality test. The compromise reached kept the obligation but gave individuals the right to request rectification or erasure of their data from SIS through their national authority — a right more visible than before but still difficult to enforce in practice.

Industry vendors, especially IT security and biometrics firms (e.g., Thales, Idemia, Sopra Steria), gained contracts for system upgrades. Frontex, the EU border agency, saw its operational capacity increase because it could now directly query SIS during joint operations.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU share police data across borders?

The Schengen Information System (SIS) allows police, border guards, and customs officers in 30 European countries to enter and view real‑time alerts on wanted persons, missing people, stolen property, and individuals banned from entering the bloc. When an officer checks a person against SIS — for example, during a roadside stop — the system returns a match within seconds if an alert exists. Before 2022, the system stored mainly text‑based alerts; the 2018 reform made it compulsory to include fingerprints and facial images for most wanted persons, making matches more reliable.

### What information is stored in Europe’s largest law enforcement database?

SIS stores alerts under several categories: persons wanted for extradition, missing persons, persons sought to assist with a judicial procedure (e.g., witnesses), persons who should be refused entry to the Schengen area, and — new since 2022 — unknown wanted persons (whose identity is unknown but whose fingerprints are known) and children at risk. For each alert, the database holds identification data (name, nationality, biometrics), a reason for the alert, and instructions for action (arrest/refuse entry/contact authority). Vehicles, firearms, passports, and industrial equipment can also be flagged as stolen. The system does *not* store criminal records or case details beyond what is needed for the alert.

### Why was the system updated after years of use?

The update was a direct response to security failures during the 2015–2016 terrorist attacks and the migration crisis. Two Paris attackers had been subject to alerts in other EU databases, but those alerts were not cross‑referenced with SIS because of technical silos. The 2018 reform introduced mandatory biometrics to reduce reliance on name‑based searches (which can miss aliases or false identities), added a new alert for return decisions to prevent irregular migrants from re‑entering undetected, and enabled the system to issue alerts for unknown persons — addressing the gap that had allowed attackers to travel without document checks. The reform also strengthened data quality rules and gave individuals a statutory right to access and correct their data.

### Does a regular person have any rights regarding data in this database?

Yes. The regulation gives every individual the right to access their personal data stored in SIS, to have it corrected if inaccurate, and to request its deletion if it was entered unlawfully. To exercise these rights, a person must contact the national SIS authority in the country where they believe an alert exists (or through any Schengen state’s data protection authority). The request is processed within a time limit set by national law, typically a few weeks. In practice, the number of access requests has been low — the Commission’s 2023 evaluation noted fewer than 500 across the whole Schengen area in two years — so the remedy remains little used.
