## What is it?

The Schengen Borders Code (SBC) is the foundational legal instrument governing the movement of persons across borders within the Schengen Area — the zone of 27 European countries that have abolished passport and identity checks at their shared internal borders. First adopted in 2006 and recast in 2016, the SBC sets harmonised rules for both external border controls (entry conditions, visa requirements, and systematic checks against databases) and internal border policies (when and how temporary border checks can be reintroduced).

The Code is not a standalone treaty but an EU regulation that codifies and updates the 1985 Schengen Agreement and the 1990 Schengen Convention, integrating them into the EU’s legal framework via the Treaty of Amsterdam. It defines who may enter the Schengen Area, what documents they need, and under what exceptional circumstances a Member State may suspend free movement — such as a terrorist threat, a public health emergency, or a sudden mass influx of people. Its central tension is the trade-off between the right to free movement and the perceived need for security controls.

## Why was it introduced?

The Schengen Borders Code was introduced as a direct response to a political and operational crisis: the partial collapse of the Schengen system during the 2015–2016 migration wave. The trigger was a court ruling and a series of unilateral border closures that exposed the legal vacuum in the original rules.

On 13 September 2015, Germany surprised its EU partners by reintroducing identity checks on its border with Austria, citing an "exceptional influx of migrants" under Article 23 of the 2006 SBC. Within days, Austria, Slovenia, Sweden, Denmark, and France followed suit. By December 2015, six Schengen states had suspended free movement — the most widespread internal border controls since the zone’s creation in 1995. The then-European Commissioner for Migration, Home Affairs and Citizenship, Dimitris Avramopoulos, warned that the Schengen system was "on the verge of collapse."

What had failed was the original 2006 Code’s rules on temporary reintroduction of border controls. Article 23 allowed a Member State to reintroduce checks for up to 30 days in case of a "serious threat to public policy or internal security" — but the text did not clearly define a "mass influx of irregular migrants" as a valid threat. Germany’s move exploited this ambiguity. The European Commission’s 2016 impact assessment (SWD(2016) 335) found that the 2006 rules lacked clarity on duration, grounds, and notification procedures, and that the "prolonged and coordinated use" of internal border checks by multiple states was undermining the very principle of Schengen.

The political coalition for reform was built on an unlikely alliance: border states (Germany, Austria, France) wanted clearer rules to legalise their closures; free-movement advocates (the Commission, the European Parliament, the Benelux countries) wanted stricter conditions and time limits to prevent permanent re-borderisation; and security hawks (led by the European People’s Party, the largest group in the European Parliament) demanded tighter external border screening as a quid pro quo. The European Parliament’s rapporteur on the recast, Dutch MEP Sophia in 't Veld (ALDE), fought for a two-year maximum for internal checks, but the final text settled on six months, extendable for up to two years in "exceptional circumstances."

The timing was shaped by the 2015–2016 migration crisis, but also by two landmark EU court rulings. The Court of Justice of the European Union had in 2015 (Case C-650/13, Delvigne) reaffirmed the fundamental right to free movement, but the European Court of Human Rights in 2016 (Khlifia and Others v. Italy) had validated collective expulsions at external borders, creating legal friction. These rulings, combined with the November 2015 Paris terrorist attacks (which killed 130 people and led France to declare a state of emergency and close its borders), broke the political deadlock. The Commission proposed the recast on 27 September 2016, and it was adopted by the European Parliament on 8 March 2016 (373 votes in favour, 182 against, 100 abstentions) and by the Council on 14 March 2016.

What changed from the 2006 version was significant: the recast introduced a clear list of grounds for internal border controls (terrorist threat, public health emergency, "exceptional circumstances" including migration crises), established maximum durations (six months, extendable to two years only with Commission consent), and required systematic checks of all persons crossing external borders against the Schengen Information System and Interpol databases. It also mandated risk assessments by Frontex, the European Border and Coast Guard Agency, before any prolonged closure.

## Timeline

- **14 June 1985** — The Schengen Agreement is signed by five countries (Belgium, France, Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands) on the riverboat *Princesse Marie-Astrid* on the Moselle River, creating a vision of passport-free travel.
- **19 June 1990** — The Schengen Convention sets detailed implementation rules, including common visa policy and police cooperation, and enters into force in 1995.
- **1 May 1999** — The Treaty of Amsterdam integrates the Schengen acquis into the EU legal framework, making Schengen rules part of EU law via a protocol.
- **15 March 2006** — The first Schengen Borders Code (Regulation 562/2006/EC) is adopted, codifying earlier rules under the EU’s legal order.
- **September–December 2015** — Six Schengen states unilaterally reintroduce internal border controls; the Commission warns of "systemic collapse."
- **13 November 2015** — Paris terrorist attacks kill 130, leading France to declare a state of emergency and close borders for three months.
- **27 September 2016** — The European Commission proposes a recast of the Schengen Borders Code (COM/2016/0499 final), citing "clarification and strengthening" of rules on internal controls.
- **8 March 2016** — The European Parliament adopts the recast in a plenary vote (373 in favour, 182 against, 100 abstentions).
- **14 March 2016** — The Council of the EU adopts the recast.
- **9 March 2016** — Regulation 2016/399 enters into force, replacing the 2006 Code.
- **12 April 2016** — The Commission issues guidelines (C(2016) 2343) clarifying the conditions for temporary border controls.
- **13 November 2017** — The Commission launches the Schengen "governance package" including mandatory systematic checks of EU citizens at external borders (Regulation 2017/458).
- **March 2020** — During the COVID-19 pandemic, all Schengen states reintroduce internal border controls; the Commission issues additional guidance on health-related closures.
- **December 2022** — The Commission reports that 14 Schengen states have internal border checks in place, the highest number since the 2006 Code.

## Impact and consequences

The 2016 recast of the Schengen Borders Code had several measurable consequences. First, it slowed the uncoordinated wave of border closures. According to the Commission’s 2021 Schengen Barometer, the number of individual notifications for temporary internal controls fell from 136 in 2015 to 48 in 2017, though it rose again to 112 in 2020 due to COVID-19. The two-year maximum for "exceptional" controls was invoked only once — by Germany in 2018, for controls on the Austrian border lasting 24 months.

Second, the Code triggered a major infrastructure project: the Entry/Exit System (EES), an automated IT system for recording non-EU citizens crossing external borders. The EES was mandated by the 2016 recast but repeatedly delayed; as of early 2025, it remains not fully operational. The Commission’s 2023 Schengen progress report estimated the total cost of implementing the new checks at €470 million across Member States.

Third, the Code hardened external border controls. The requirement for systematic checks of all travellers — including EU citizens — against databases like the Schengen Information System was particularly controversial. The European Data Protection Supervisor warned in 2016 that this "mass surveillance" risked violating proportionality. In practice, according to Eurostat’s 2023 data, the number of refusals of entry at external borders rose from 215,000 in 2016 to 342,000 in 2019, though this was also driven by increased migration flows.

Fourth, the Code changed the politics of Schengen. The new rules gave the Commission a stronger role — it must approve any extension beyond six months, and it can issue opinions on the proportionality of closures. This centralisation has been contentious: Austria and Germany have resisted Commission oversight, while the European Parliament’s LIBE Committee has criticised the Commission for being too permissive. A 2023 study by the European Policy Centre found that the Commission had never rejected a Member State’s request to extend internal border checks, undermining its own rules.

The biggest unintended consequence was the normalisation of "temporary" controls. By December 2022, 14 Schengen states had internal checks in place — the highest number since the Code’s 2006 entry into force. A 2022 report by the European Court of Auditors found that the average duration of controls was 14 months, far exceeding the Code’s original six-month limit, and that Member States had "exploited ambiguities" in the "exceptional circumstances" clause.

## Key questions answered

### Why is the Schengen Borders Code still controversial?

The Code’s central tension — free movement versus security — remains unresolved. Every crisis, from the 2015 migration wave to the 2020 pandemic to the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war, has triggered mass internal border closures that the Code’s rules cannot prevent. A 2023 Commission impact assessment estimated that permanent internal border controls would cost the EU economy €47 billion per year in lost trade and tourism. Yet national governments continue to cite security and migration fears. The Code’s "temporary" exception has become the rule, and the Commission has lacked the political will to enforce its own deadlines.

### Why did the 2016 recast require systematic database checks on EU citizens?

This was a direct response to the Paris attacks: several of the terrorists were EU citizens who had travelled freely within Schengen. The Commission’s 2015 Security Agenda explicitly linked border controls to the fight against "foreign terrorist fighters." The European Parliament’s LIBE Committee initially opposed systematic checks, arguing they violated the proportionality principle under the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights. The compromise was to limit systematic checks to external borders only, and to exempt EU citizens from fingerprinting under the EES.

### Why can Member States still close their internal borders despite the Code?

The Code cannot force a sovereign state to keep its borders open when it feels threatened. Article 23 allows checks for "a serious threat to public policy or internal security" — a deliberately broad phrase that leaves room for political judgement. The European Court of Justice has consistently upheld Member States’ discretion on what constitutes a "serious threat," most recently in Case C-329/21 (2022), where it ruled that a "sudden and large influx of migrants" could qualify. The Code’s protections are political, not judicial: the Commission can only issue non-binding opinions, and no Member State has ever been fined for violating the rules.

### What is the relationship between the Schengen Borders Code and the Dublin Regulation?

They form the two pillars of EU asylum and border policy. The Dublin Regulation (currently 604/2013) determines which Member State is responsible for processing an asylum claim — usually the country of first entry. The Schengen Borders Code controls who may enter at all. The conflict between them is structural: the Dublin system concentrates asylum seekers at southern external borders, creating overwhelming pressure on Greece, Italy, and Malta. Under the Code, these countries are legally obliged to deny entry to irregular migrants — but Dublin rules forbid them from simply pushing migrants back. The result has been periodic collapses of both systems: in 2015–2016, Greece’s inability to manage its external border triggered internal closures across the entire Schengen Area.
