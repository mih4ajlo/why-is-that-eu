## What is it?

The Credit Rating Agencies Regulation (CRAR) created a binding, EU‑wide registration and supervision regime for credit rating agencies (CRAs) – primarily Moody’s, Standard & Poor’s, and Fitch – whose ratings on sovereign debt, corporate bonds, and structured financial products had been shown to be deeply flawed during the 2007–2008 financial crisis. Before the regulation, CRAs were largely self‑regulated and operated under national law, with no common requirement to disclose their rating methodologies, manage conflicts of interest, or submit to direct public oversight. The regulation mandated that any CRA whose ratings are used in the EU must register with the Committee of European Securities Regulators (CESR, succeeded by the European Securities and Markets Authority ESMA in 2011), comply with a code of conduct, separate rating and advisory activities, and publish annual transparency reports. It also set rules on the independence of rating analysts and prohibited agencies from providing consulting or advisory services to the entities they rate.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was the catastrophic failure of the three dominant CRAs – Moody’s, S&P, and Fitch – to anticipate the collapse of the US subprime mortgage market and the broader financial system that followed. In July 2007, BNP Paribas froze three investment funds because it could not value their holdings, revealing how wildly off‑market the AAA ratings assigned to mortgage‑backed securities had been. By September 2008 the bankruptcy of Lehman Brothers had shown that these same agencies had given investment‑grade ratings to institutions that were, in reality, weeks from insolvency. Internal documents later released by the US Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations described analysts at Moody’s joking that they would “rate a deal that is structured by cows” if they were paid. The financial damage across Europe was immense: banks in Germany, Ireland, and the UK had bought trillions of euros of structured debt based on those ratings.

The political response in the EU was shaped by two factors. First, the United States had already passed the Credit Rating Agency Reform Act in 2006, but it had proved toothless – the US Securities and Exchange Commission’s voluntary registration scheme had not prevented the 2008 failures. Second, the European Commission, under Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy, initially resisted direct regulation, arguing that the industry’s own International Organization of Securities Commissions code was sufficient. That stance collapsed in October 2008, when even the code’s defenders conceded that market discipline had failed. The French presidency of the Council pushed the file forward, supported by the European Parliament’s rapporteur Jean‑Paul Gauzès (EPP, France), who argued that “self‑regulation has had its chance.” The proposal was published on 13 November 2008 – just two months after Lehman – and was adopted by the Parliament and Council in under one year, an unusually fast pace for a financial regulation. The main opposition came from the agencies themselves, which warned of increased costs and a loss of global competitiveness, and from some Member States (notably the United Kingdom) that feared driving business to unregulated jurisdictions such as Hong Kong or Dubai. The compromise that secured UK support was the creation of a “passport” system: a registration in one Member State would be valid across all 27, a feature later centralised under ESMA.

The key innovation was the requirement for agencies to separate their rating business from advisory services, a direct response to the conflicts revealed during the crisis. The Commission’s impact assessment noted that the three largest agencies together rated more than 90% of the EU market, and that the same firms often provided paid advice on how to structure the very securities they were asked to rate. The regulation also forced agencies to disclose the models, assumptions, and historical default rates underlying each rating – transparency that had been fiercely resisted by the industry on grounds of intellectual property. Without a crisis of this magnitude, such a disclosure requirement would almost certainly have been blocked.

## Timeline

- **July 2007** – BNP Paribas freezes funds; the subprime crisis becomes visible in Europe.
- **15 September 2008** – Lehman Brothers files for bankruptcy; the systemic failure of CRA ratings is undeniable.
- **13 November 2008** – European Commission publishes proposal for a regulation on credit rating agencies.
- **23 April 2009** – European Parliament adopts the text (first reading) with 571 votes in favour, 33 against, and 34 abstentions.
- **16 September 2009** – Regulation (EC) No 1060/2009 is signed and published in the Official Journal.
- **7 December 2009** – Regulation enters into application; all CRAs operating in the EU must apply for registration.
- **1 July 2011** – Regulation (EU) No 513/2011 transfers supervisory authority from national authorities to ESMA.
- **January 2013** – First enforcement: ESMA fines the Danish CRA Axesor Monitoring & Rating Ltd. €19,000 for failing to disclose conflicts (later reduction on appeal).
- **May 2013** – Regulation (EU) No 462/2013 (CRA III) introduces further rules on sovereign debt ratings and civil liability of CRAs.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed the operating environment for CRAs, but the market remains highly concentrated. By 2023 ESMA’s register listed around 40 registered agencies, yet Moody’s, S&P, and Fitch still accounted for over 80% of rating business in the EU, according to ESMA’s 2022 Annual Report. The transparency requirements have improved access to methodologies, but critics argue that the agencies’ track record on sovereign debt – particularly during the 2010–2012 Eurozone crisis – showed that old problems persisted. The regulation’s most tangible effect has been administrative: agencies must now submit annual reports, maintain a dedicated compliance function, and publish on a central ESMA website the rating histories and default rates for each asset class.

Enforcement has been limited but not non‑existent. Beyond the 2013 Axesor fine, ESMA imposed a €6 million penalty on Standard & Poor’s in 2018 for failing to maintain adequate internal controls over the timeliness of its sovereign rating actions. No major agency has been barred from the EU market, and no rating has been publicly reversed by a regulator. The European Securities and Markets Authority has acknowledged that the regulation’s deterrent effect is weakened by the “too big to regulate” problem: the three dominant agencies generate most of their revenue outside the EU, reducing the leverage of any single regulator.

One indirect consequence was the creation of a parallel regime for “rating equivalent” third countries. The Commission allowed agencies based in the US, Japan, and other jurisdictions to use their home‑country rules if the European Commission deemed them “equivalent” – a pragmatic move to avoid disrupting cross‑border capital flows, but one that critics said undercut the regulation’s ambition. By 2023, more than 20 third‑country jurisdictions had received equivalence decisions.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU step in to regulate credit rating agencies after the financial crisis?

When the US housing market collapsed in 2007–2008, the three biggest agencies – Moody’s, S&P, and Fitch – had placed AAA ratings on complex mortgage‑backed securities that turned out to be nearly worthless. European banks that bought those securities suffered massive losses, and the EU decided that self‑regulation had failed. The goal was to force agencies to separate rating from advisory work, disclose their methods, and face public oversight – none of which existed before the crisis.

### How do the EU rules prevent credit rating agencies from giving favourable ratings to big clients?

The regulation prohibits agencies from providing consulting or advisory services to the companies they rate, ending the practice where an agency would be paid both to advise a bank on how to structure a bond and then to rate that same bond. It also requires analysts to be rotated off a client after four years, bans employees from owning shares in entities they rate, and forces agencies to publish the assumptions and historical accuracy of every rating they issue.

### Are credit rating agencies still too powerful in Europe?

Despite the regulation, Moody’s, S&P, and Fitch still rate about 80% of EU debt and securities, and the fines imposed by ESMA have been too small to change their business models. The structural problem that prompted the 2009 regulation – a concentrated market with deep conflicts – remains largely intact. However, the rules have made it harder for agencies to hide bad methodology, and a handful of smaller European agencies (such as Euler Hermes Rating in Germany or Scope Ratings in Germany) have gained market share in niche areas.

### What happens if a credit rating agency breaks the EU rules?

ESMA can impose fines of up to 20% of the agency’s annual turnover, publicly censure the firm, or temporarily ban it from issuing new ratings. In practice, ESMA has preferred to settle cases through administrative measures. The most notable enforcement action was a €6 million fine on S&P in 2018 for internal control failures, which was later reduced on appeal. No agency has yet lost its registration, partly because ESMA relies on the agencies’ data for its own market monitoring, creating a mutual dependency.
