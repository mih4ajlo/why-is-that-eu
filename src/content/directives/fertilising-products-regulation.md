## What is it?

The Fertilising Products Regulation (FPR) replaces the 2003 Fertilisers Regulation (2003/2003/EC), which had only covered inorganic mineral fertilisers. The FPR extends EU-harmonised rules to organic fertilisers, soil improvers, growing media, and biostimulants — including those derived from waste and recycled materials. Manufacturers that meet the FPR's requirements can affix the CE mark and sell their products across the entire single market without national-level re-approval.

The regulation sets binding limits on contaminants — most notably cadmium, which was capped at 60 mg/kg of phosphorus pentoxide (P₂O₅) — and establishes criteria for converting waste into safe fertilising products under the "End-of-Waste" framework of the Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC). It also introduces a new category for "biostimulants," products that stimulate plant growth independently of nutrient content, which had not been regulated at EU level before.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for the FPR was a confluence of structural tensions and political pressure that had built up over more than a decade. The core problem was that the 2003 Fertilisers Regulation only covered conventional mineral fertilisers — primarily those based on nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. This left organic and recycled fertilisers, such as those produced from manure, sewage sludge, or food waste, subject to 27 different national approval regimes. A company wanting to sell a compost-based fertiliser across the EU could face separate testing, registration, and labelling requirements in each member state, making cross-border trade nearly impossible.

The structural tension became acute after the 2015 Circular Economy Package, presented by First Vice-President Frans Timmermans as a flagship initiative of the Juncker Commission. The package explicitly called for removing barriers to the use of organic and waste-based fertilisers. The Commission's 2016 impact assessment estimated that the fragmented market cost EU businesses €40–135 million annually in lost trade, and that failing to address cadmium contamination in mineral fertilisers would cost up to €2 billion per year in soil remediation and reduced crop yields by 2030.

Coalition dynamics were critical. A powerful bloc emerged around northern European countries — particularly Sweden, Finland, and Austria — that had long pushed for strict cadmium limits. These countries had naturally low-cadmium phosphate deposits or had invested in de-cadmiation technology. They found allies in the European Parliament's Environment Committee (ENVI), where MEPs led by French centre-right rapporteur Angélique Delahaye argued that the EU's own Scientific Committee on Health and Environmental Risks (SCHER) had warned in 2014 that cadmium in fertilisers posed an "unacceptable risk" to soil and food safety.

Opposition came from the European phosphate industry, represented by the European Fertiliser Manufacturers Association (now Fertilizers Europe), and from southern member states — particularly Spain, Portugal, and Greece — whose phosphate rock deposits from Morocco and Tunisia contained naturally high cadmium levels. Spanish MEP Pilar Ayuso of the European People's Party warned that a 60 mg/kg limit would force the closure of phosphate processing plants in Huelva and Cartagena, putting 1,500 jobs at risk. The industry's alternative proposal was a longer transitional period with a higher initial limit of 80 mg/kg.

The timing was decisive. The Juncker Commission had made the circular economy its signature economic policy, and the 2015 Paris Agreement on climate change added environmental urgency. But what broke the deadlock was a specific event: the 2017 "Cadmium in Chocolate" consumer scandal in Germany, where tests by the NGO Foodwatch found high cadmium levels in cocoa powder from West Africa. Although not directly about fertilisers, it shifted public and political attention to cadmium's food chain impacts. In the European Parliament's July 2018 plenary vote, MEPs passed the 60 mg/kg limit by 488 votes to 168, with 48 abstentions — a decisive majority that surprised industry.

What changed from earlier failed efforts? The 2006 Thematic Strategy on Soil Protection had tried to introduce a cadmium limit, but was blocked by a blocking minority of member states led by Spain. The difference in 2018 was the circular economy framework: the FPR bundled cadmium limits with market access benefits for recycled fertilisers, creating a package that gave northern states their health protection while giving the recycling industry — which had grown into a vocal lobby through organisations like the European Compost Network — a path to single-market harmonisation. The final compromise, negotiated in the trilogue in December 2018, allowed a four-year transitional period before the 60 mg/kg limit took full effect, softening the blow for southern producers.

## Timeline

- **2003** — Regulation 2003/2003/EC adopted, covering only inorganic mineral fertilisers
- **2006** — Commission's Thematic Strategy on Soil Protection proposes cadmium limits, blocked by Council blocking minority
- **2015** — Juncker Commission's Circular Economy Package published, explicitly calls for revising fertiliser rules
- **March 2016** — Commission publishes impact assessment for new Fertilising Products Regulation
- **October 2016** — Commission tables legislative proposal (COM(2016) 157 final)
- **July 2018** — European Parliament plenary adopts its position, including 60 mg/kg cadmium limit, by 488 to 168
- **December 2018** — Trilogue agreement reached with four-year transitional period for cadmium limits
- **May 2019** — Regulation 2019/1009 published in the Official Journal
- **July 2019** — Regulation enters into force
- **July 2022** — Full application date; CE marking required for all fertilising products sold under the regulation

## Impact and consequences

The FPR's most immediate impact was to open the EU single market to organic and waste-based fertilisers. By 2023, the European Compost Network reported that over 600 composting and anaerobic digestion facilities had applied for CE marking. The first CE-marked organic fertiliser — a digestate product from a Dutch biogas plant — was approved in November 2022.

For mineral fertilisers, the cadmium limit forced change. Spanish producer Fertiberia announced in 2020 a €100 million investment in a des-cadmiation plant in Huelva to meet the 60 mg/kg standard, while other European producers shifted sourcing to lower-cadmium phosphate rock from Russia and Kazakhstan. The European Commission's 2022 implementation report noted that by July 2022, approximately 85% of mineral fertilisers on the EU market already met the new limit, up from 60% in 2018.

The biostimulants category created a new market segment. According to a 2023 report by the European Biostimulants Industry Council (EBIC), the EU biostimulant market grew by 12% annually in the first two years after the regulation's application, reaching an estimated €1.2 billion by 2024. Manufacturers of seaweed extracts, humic acids, and microbial plant enhancers gained harmonised access to all 27 member states for the first time.

However, implementation proved challenging for smaller operators. The requirement to demonstrate compliance with the EU's "End-of-Waste" criteria under the FPR's Component Material Categories (CMC) required significant paperwork. A 2023 survey by the European Environment Bureau found that 40% of small composting facilities in France and Italy had not yet applied for CE marking, citing the regulatory burden. The Commission proposed a revised version of the regulation's delegated acts in 2024 to simplify requirements for small-scale producers.

Member states varied in their transposition. Austria adopted the cadmium limit into national law immediately in 2019, while Greece and Portugal did not finalise transposition until mid-2023, after receiving formal notices from the Commission in November 2022. No infringement fines were issued as of 2024, but the Commission opened five pre-litigation (EU Pilot) cases against member states for delayed transposition.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU regulate fertilisers under the Fertilising Products Regulation when they are already covered by national laws?

The 2003 regulation only covered conventional mineral fertilisers, creating a two-tier market: mineral fertiliser makers could use a single CE mark across the EU, while organic and recycled fertiliser manufacturers had to navigate 27 different national approval systems. This contradicted the core principle of the single market and blocked development of a circular economy for nutrients, where recovered phosphorus and nitrogen from waste streams could replace imported mineral fertilisers. The FPR solved this by extending the CE-marking system to all fertilising products, provided they meet EU-wide safety and quality standards.

### Why did the cadmium limit in the Fertilising Products Regulation take 15 years to adopt despite known health risks?

The 2006 Soil Thematic Strategy failed because southern member states with high-cadmium phosphate deposits — particularly Spain — assembled a blocking minority in the Council. These countries argued that the scientific evidence was insufficient and that limits would destroy their phosphate processing industry. By 2015, two things had changed: the circular economy agenda gave the Commission a new policy framework to bundle cadmium limits with market-opening benefits for recycled fertilisers, and the 2017 "Cadmium in Chocolate" scandal increased consumer and political pressure. The FPR's structure — offering single-market access to organic fertiliser producers in exchange for strict cadmium limits — created a coalition that the earlier, standalone cadmium-proposal had lacked.

### Why are biostimulants treated differently from fertilisers under the Fertilising Products Regulation?

Biostimulants — products that enhance plant growth through mechanisms other than nutrient supply, such as improving nutrient uptake or tolerance to abiotic stress — were previously unregulated at EU level and fell into a legal grey area between fertiliser, plant protection, and plant health legislation. The FPR created a distinct category for them, recognising that they are not fertilisers (they do not provide nutrients) but serve an agricultural function. This classification allowed the EU to set specific safety and efficacy requirements without subjecting biostimulants to the more stringent plant protection product rules under Regulation 1107/2009, which would have been disproportionate for products that work through natural processes like microbial activity or humic acid stimulation.
