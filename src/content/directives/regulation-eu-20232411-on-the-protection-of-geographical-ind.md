## What is it?

This regulation, adopted in November 2023, creates an EU-wide system for protecting geographical indications (GIs) for craft and industrial products—everything from handmade lace and carved stone to knives and porcelain. Previously, only agricultural products, wines, and spirits could qualify for EU-wide GI protection (like Parma ham or Champagne). The new law extends the same logic to non-food items: if a product's quality, reputation, or authenticity is essentially linked to its geographical origin, its producers can now register it under a single EU framework and defend it against imitations in all member states.

The system uses a two-tier application process: producers first file with their national intellectual property office. If that office approves, the application goes to the EU Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO) for EU-level registration and enforcement. The regulation entered into force on 1 December 2023, with full application from 1 December 2025, giving national authorities and EUIPO time to set up the administrative machinery.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a crisis but a persistent legal asymmetry. For three decades, EU law had protected only agricultural GIs—a gap that had real economic consequences. Take Murano glass: Venetian glassmakers could register "Murano" as a GI for vases but not for glass jewellery or art installations, because those counted as "industrial" products under EU classification. The result was rampant counterfeiting from China and Eastern Europe, with fake "Murano" glass selling at a fraction of the price while legitimate Venetian producers had to rely on France's system of *indications géographiques*, which only one EU member state had. In 2020, the European Commission estimated that counterfeit non-agricultural products cost EU producers up to €2.5 billion annually in lost sales, according to its impact assessment.

The structural tension was clear: the EU single market removed trade barriers but left a patchwork of national GI systems for non-food goods. France had *indications géographiques* for Limoges porcelain and Laguiole knives; Italy had *decreti* for Murano glass and Carrara marble; the UK had the Scottish Tartan Register. But these protections stopped at national borders. A Czech producer could legally sell "Carrara-style" marble countertops across the EU, because Italian law could not enforce a GI in Prague. This fragmented system also violated the principle of uniform enforcement that underpins the single market.

The coalition that drove the regulation was unusual. It brought together southern European countries with strong craft traditions (Italy, France, Spain, Portugal) with niche producers from northern Europe (Finnish knife-makers, Swedish crystal-blowers). The European Parliament's internal market committee, led by rapporteur Marion Walsmann (EPP, Germany), framed it as a competitiveness issue: "European craftsmanship must not mean European imitation," she told the Parliament in February 2023. The Parliament voted 603-14 in favour on 14 September 2023—one of the largest majorities for any intellectual property file in recent years.

Opposition came from two directions. Consumer goods manufacturers worried about supply chain complexity—if a GI product contained a non-GI component (say, a Murano glass lamp with a Chinese metal base), where was the line drawn? The final regulation solved this by applying GI rules only to the "product itself, not its components," according to the Commission's 2022 legislative proposal. More significant resistance came from EUIPO itself, which initially argued that a new EU body would duplicate its existing functions. The compromise required only a 10-year phase-in: for the first decade, national offices would do most of the work, with EUIPO gradually taking over full responsibility by 2035.

Timing was crucial. The regulation succeeded under the von der Leyen Commission because of two converging factors. First, the 2019 European Parliament elections brought in a strong contingent of regionalist MEPs—from Catalonia, Bavaria, Flanders—who saw GIs as a way to protect local industry from global competition. Second, the COVID-19 pandemic had boosted consumer interest in "authentic" and "local" products: a 2021 Eurobarometer survey found that 53% of EU citizens were willing to pay more for products with a guaranteed geographical origin. The Commission's own 2020 Green Paper on Intellectual Property had already argued that extending GIs would "support the recovery of local artisanal economies" after the pandemic's economic shock.

What broke the previous deadlock was the 2021 Court of Justice ruling in *C-509/19* (a case about "Carrara marble" where Italian producers tried to sue a German importer). The Court held that EU law did not recognise GIs for non-agricultural products unless individual member states had bilateral agreements—which created a legal quicksand where enforcement depended on diplomatic treaties rather than pan-EU law. This ruling, coming just as the Commission was drafting its intellectual property strategy, made legislative action politically urgent.

## Timeline

- **1992** — First EU GI regulation (Council Regulation 2081/92) covers only agricultural products and foodstuffs; craft products excluded
- **2005** — European Parliament adopts resolution urging Commission to extend GIs to non-agricultural products
- **2011** — Commission's Green Paper on intellectual property proposes a study on extending GIs; industry groups like the European Creative Crafts Institute (ECC) begin lobbying
- **2013** — Commission publishes a feasibility study by PricewaterhouseCoopers showing that 35% of non-food GI applicants would use an EU system if created
- **2019** — Commission's "EU Quality Schemes" evaluation concludes the existing system "creates competitive disadvantages for craft producers"
- **2021** — CJEU ruling in *C-509/19* (Carrara marble) demonstrates legal gap in cross-border GI enforcement
- **13 April 2022** — Commission proposes the GI extension regulation, citing €2.5 billion in estimated counterfeiting losses
- **30 June 2023** — Council adopts its position (unanimously)
- **14 September 2023** — European Parliament votes 603-14 in favour
- **27 November 2023** — Regulation published in Official Journal
- **1 December 2023** — Entry into force
- **1 December 2025** — Full application; first applications can be filed with national offices
- **1 December 2033** — EUIPO takes over full examination and enforcement responsibilities

## Impact and consequences

The regulation creates a new category of EU intellectual property rights, estimated by the Commission to affect between 1,500 and 3,000 existing products that currently rely on national GIs or trademark law. The most immediate beneficiaries are likely to be Italy, France, Spain, and Portugal—countries that already have mature national GI systems for non-food products. Italy alone identified 240 potential registrations in its 2022 national plan, including Carrara marble, Murano glass, and Gubbio ceramics.

For producers like the Scottish Tartan Authority, the regulation changes the legal landscape. Previously, tartan patterns could only be registered under the UK's national register—which had no effect in EU courts. Now, Scottish weavers can file an EU GI application through the UK Intellectual Property Office under the post-Brexit framework, preventing non-EU producers from claiming "Scotch tartan" authenticity. Similarly, Finnish knife-makers from Fiskars can now prevent Chinese or Italian competitors from labelling their products as "Fiskars-style" without meeting the criteria.

The main enforcement challenge will be policing non-EU imports. The regulation requires member states to designate competent authorities to conduct market surveillance—something the Commission noted in its 2022 proposal that "several member states currently lack for any GI system." First experiences from the agricultural GI system suggest that online marketplaces are the primary vector for counterfeits; the Commission has already signalled it will push for platform liability provisions in the Digital Services Act to require takedowns of fake "EU GI" claims.

Economically, the Commission's 2020 impact assessment predicted that the regulation could increase the value of protected products by 5-15% within five years, through price premiums and reduced competition from imitations. However, the same assessment warned that small producers face significant administrative costs—estimated at €2,000-€10,000 per application—which could be prohibitive for micro-enterprises.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take so long for EU law to protect things like Murano glass and Limoges porcelain?

The 1992 GI regulation intentionally excluded non-agricultural products because EU agriculture policy dominated intellectual property debates at the time. For 30 years, wine and cheese producers had the strongest lobbying power—their products were worth €54 billion annually by 2020, according to the Commission—while craft industries were fragmented and lacked a powerful Brussels voice. Only after the 2021 Carrara marble court ruling exposed the legal gap, and after COVID-19 boosted demand for "authentic local" products, did the political will finally unite.

### Can a company from outside Europe register an EU protected name for its handmade products?

No. Only products that originate within the EU (or approved trade partners) can qualify for EU GI status. However, non-EU countries can apply for their own GIs to be recognised in the EU under the new system—a provision designed to facilitate trade agreements. The first non-EU filings are expected from Switzerland (for Solothurn watchcases) and Turkey (for Iznik tiles), after bilateral negotiations.

### What happens if a product uses a protected regional name but only some of its parts come from that area?

The regulation allows "processing steps" to occur outside the GI region if the final product's essential characteristics come from the named location. For example, a Carrara marble lamp could be assembled in Germany if the raw marble is quarried and carved in Italy. This flexibility was the key concession that won over German and Dutch consumer goods manufacturers who feared supply chain disruption. But full manufacturing in the GI zone remains the default requirement for most products.

### Does this new EU protection for regional crafts create extra costs for small workshops?

Yes, potentially significant ones. The application fee to EUIPO is expected to be €500-€2500, plus translation costs and legal fees for preparing the product specification. Annual maintenance and enforcement costs add more. The Commission estimates total compliance costs at €5,000-€20,000 per GI registration—a sum that micro-enterprises with one or two artisans may struggle to afford. The regulation tries to soften this by allowing national offices to subsidise applications and by creating simplified procedures for "traditional knowledge" products with long-established reputations.
