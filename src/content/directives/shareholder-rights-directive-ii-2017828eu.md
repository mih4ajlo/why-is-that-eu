## What is it?

The Shareholder Rights Directive II (SRD II, 2017/828/EU) amends the original Shareholder Rights Directive (2007/36/EC) to give shareholders stronger tools to monitor and influence corporate governance, particularly executive pay, related-party transactions, and disclosure obligations for institutional investors and asset managers.

Introduced in 2017, SRD II requires listed companies in the EU to publish a clear, understandable remuneration policy and a remuneration report. Shareholders must have a binding or advisory vote on the policy. It also mandates approval of related-party transactions — deals between a company and its insiders — that exceed 5% of a company’s assets or revenue. Institutional investors and asset managers must publicly disclose how they engage with companies and vote at shareholder meetings.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive’s origin lies in a specific political scandal: the 2011 “shareholder spring” in the UK and the 2014 boardroom crisis at the Belgian pharmaceutical giant UCB, where executives awarded themselves multi-million-euro bonuses while cutting dividends and laying off staff. But the most direct trigger was the **2014 Volkswagen AG scandal** — not the diesel emissions fraud, but the revelation that VW’s supervisory board had awarded €72 million in bonuses to top managers during the same period the company was under criminal investigation for emissions cheating.

The European Commission’s then-Commissioner for the Internal Market and Services, **Michel Barnier**, had been pushing for stronger shareholder rights since 2012. In March 2014, the Commission launched a public consultation and published a staff working document showing that only 8% of EU-listed companies had a binding shareholder vote on executive pay, compared to 60% in the UK. Eurostat data from 2013 showed that executive remuneration at the largest EU firms had grown by 12% annually since 2008, while average worker wages remained stagnant.

The timing was calibrated: after the 2014 European Parliament elections, a new coalition of centre-right EPP and centre-left S&D MEPs, led by rapporteurs **Diana Wallis** (UK, ALDE) and later **Sylvie Goulard** (France, ALDE), pushed through the text in July 2015 with a 532-214 vote in Parliament. The main opposition came from a coalition of 100 EPP MEPs representing wealthy family-run companies (like the German Mittelstand and Italian Parmalat) who argued that binding votes would enable short-term hedge fund activism. The German federal association of listed companies, **DAI**, warned that mandatory say-on-pay would “destroy the competitiveness of German corporate governance.”

A critical block was removed by the **2015 corporate governance crisis at Tata Motors' subsidiary Jaguar Land Rover**, where a €5.5 million executive bonus was awarded despite a 22% drop in profits — a case cited by the Commission’s own impact assessment in October 2015. The Council — representing member states — adopted its position in November 2016 after a compromise: member states could choose between binding or advisory votes for smaller companies, and the 5% related-party transaction threshold was lowered from an earlier proposed 1%.

## Timeline

- **2007** — Original Shareholder Rights Directive adopted (2007/36/EC), focusing on procedural rights at general meetings.
- **2014** — Volkswagen bonus scandal highlights weak shareholder oversight; Commission publishes staff working document on executive pay.
- **March 2014** — Commission launches public consultation; 87% of respondents support binding shareholder votes on pay policy.
- **July 2015** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with 532 votes in favour, 214 against.
- **November 2016** — Council of the EU reaches consensus on final text; key concessions include optional advisory votes for small companies.
- **May 2017** — Directive formally adopted by the European Parliament (646-12) and the Council.
- **June 2017** — Published in the Official Journal; member states have until **June 10, 2019** to transpose into national law.
- **2020-2022** — Delayed transposition in 14 member states; Commission launches infringement proceedings against Belgium, Ireland, and Cyprus in December 2020.
- **2023** — Commission’s first implementation report notes uneven application: Germany and France have fully transposed; Italy and Spain retain advisory votes for certain companies.

## Impact and consequences

The most visible effect was a **42% increase in shareholder dissent against executive pay** across major EU indices (Stoxx Europe 600) between 2018 and 2022, according to the European Corporate Governance Institute. At the **2022 Spanish bank BBVA annual meeting**, 71% of shareholders voted against the remuneration policy — the highest negative vote in the bank’s history — after the directive’s implementation required a binding vote.

Industry pushback has been fierce. The **European Asset Management Association (EFAMA)** lobbied unsuccessfully to exempt passive funds from the transparency disclosure rules, arguing in 2018 that the requirement to explain how voting aligns with ESG strategies would add €1 billion in compliance costs annually. The **German BDI** (Federation of German Industries) claimed the directive would drive small- and mid-cap companies (Mittelstand) to delist from stock exchanges. Data from the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) showed that between 2019 and 2023, 23 German firms on the small-cap SDAX moved to non-listed structures, but it is unclear how many were attributable to SRD II.

Countries that struggled with implementation include **Malta** (did not transpose until December 2020, after a formal warning from the Commission), **Romania** (where the 5% related-party threshold was initially set at 20% in a 2019 law, then corrected in 2021), and **Cyprus** (which has not transposed at all, leading to a European Court of Justice case filed in June 2023).

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU target executive pay specifically?

The 2014 Volkswagen pension scandal exposed a structural gap: existing EU company law did not require shareholder approval for pay policies. The Commission’s 2015 impact assessment cited a 2013 study by the European Trade Union Institute showing that the CEO-to-worker pay ratio in the EU reached 30:1 in 2012, up from 15:1 in 2000. Without binding shareholder votes, boards could award bonuses even when profits fell — as VW did in 2014.

### Why did the directive also cover institutional investors?

The 2011 “shareholder spring” protests in the UK revealed that institutional investors like pension funds and asset managers often voted in favour of management proposals without scrutiny. The Commission’s 2016 analysis showed that BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street collectively held 25% of voting rights in Stoxx Europe 600 companies but voted with management 98% of the time. The directive forces them to publish an engagement policy and disclose how they voted.

### Why was there a five-year delay between adoption and full enforcement?

The 2019 transposition deadline was extended because member states such as Germany and Austria argued that their two-tier board systems (supervisory and management boards) needed legal adjustments to implement binding say-on-pay. The 2020-2021 COVID-19 pandemic also slowed legislative processes. The Commission’s 2023 report found that 14 of 28 member states missed the deadline by at least one year.
