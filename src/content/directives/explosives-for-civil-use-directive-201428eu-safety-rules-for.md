## What is it?

Directive 2014/28/EU sets mandatory safety requirements for explosives placed on the EU market for civilian uses — including blasting explosives for quarries and mines, detonators for demolition, and pyrotechnic articles for fireworks and theatrical effects. It replaced the 1993 Explosives Directive (93/15/EEC) as part of the EU's "alignment package" that brought product-specific directives in line with the 2008 New Legislative Framework (NLF), which standardised conformity assessment, CE marking, and market surveillance across all product categories.

Under the directive, manufacturers must ensure their explosives are designed to minimise risks to human health and safety, property, and the environment. They must perform conformity assessments — often involving notified bodies (independent testing laboratories) — and affix the CE marking before placing products on the market. The directive covers everything from the ammonium nitrate fuel oil (ANFO) mixtures used in mining to the flash powders in professional fireworks. It does not cover military explosives or ammunition, which remain subject to national law.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the EU's broader legislative reform under the 2008 New Legislative Framework, which aimed to simplify and strengthen product safety rules across all sectors. But the explosives sector had its own long-standing problems that made reform urgent.

A series of catastrophic industrial explosions across Europe had exposed fatal gaps in the old 1993 directive. On 21 September 2001, an explosion at the AZF fertiliser plant in Toulouse, France, killed 31 people, injured over 2,500, and caused €2 billion in damage. Investigators traced the cause to a mixture of ammonium nitrate — a common fertiliser that can also be used as an explosive precursor — with other chemicals. The Toulouse disaster prompted the EU to adopt stricter controls on ammonium nitrate fertilisers (later Regulation 2003/2003), but it also highlighted broader weaknesses in how explosives were regulated for civil use.

Throughout the 2000s, the European Commission's impact assessment for the recast noted that the 1993 directive had created "legal uncertainties" regarding the scope of its application, particularly around pyrotechnics and propellants. National market surveillance authorities reported increasing difficulty tracking explosives placed on the market through different conformity assessment routes. The European Association of Explosives Manufacturers (now part of the European Federation of Explosives Engineers) raised concerns about "forum shopping" — manufacturers choosing less rigorous notified bodies in other member states to obtain CE marking more cheaply or quickly.

The Commission's DG Enterprise and Industry (now DG GROW) began preparing the recast in 2008, led by Commissioner Günter Verheugen (2004–2010) and later by Commissioner Antonio Tajani (2010–2014). The key coalition supporting the directive was the same one that backed the NLF: the European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO), led by rapporteur Andrejs Mamikins (S&D, Latvia), and the majority of member states in the Council. The Parliament adopted the directive on 15 April 2014 by a large majority — 617 votes in favour, 37 against, and 22 abstentions.

The main opposition came from some pyrotechnics manufacturers, who argued that the NLF alignment would impose unnecessary compliance costs for small firework producers, particularly in Eastern Europe. The Czech Association of Pyrotechnics Manufacturers warned that the cost of re-certifying existing product lines under the new procedure could drive smaller firms out of business. The Commission's impact assessment, however, estimated the compliance costs at €5–10 million total across the EU — small relative to the sector's €4 billion annual turnover — but acknowledged that micro-enterprises (firms with under 10 employees) would face disproportionate burdens.

A specific crisis unblocked a key provision: the requirement for an EU-type examination certificate from a notified body. The old directive allowed manufacturers to self-declare conformity for low-risk explosives, but the 2007–2008 global financial crisis had reduced national market surveillance budgets, making self-declaration riskier. The Commission cited a 2012 report from the EU's Rapid Information System (RAPEX) showing that 7% of notified product safety incidents involved explosives — a small number but with disproportionately deadly consequences, as demonstrated by the Toulouse disaster and subsequent smaller incidents.

## Impact and consequences

The directive harmonised conformity assessment procedures across the EU, ending the patchwork of national testing requirements that had previously forced manufacturers to obtain separate approvals for different member states. The European Commission's 2020 evaluation found that the directive reduced time-to-market for new explosives products by an average of 4–6 months compared to the pre-2014 regime.

The most significant practical change was the mandatory involvement of notified bodies for all explosives, not just high-risk categories. Under the 1993 directive, manufacturers could self-declare conformity for low-risk items such as certain small pyrotechnics. The 2014 directive required all explosives — from blasting agents to theatrical flash powder — to undergo third-party conformity assessment. This closed the loophole that had allowed some manufacturers to place products on the market without rigorous testing, but it also increased costs for low-risk items.

The European Association of Explosives Manufacturers reported a 15% drop in the number of small pyrotechnics producers in the first three years after application, largely due to the cost of obtaining notified body certification for product lines with thin margins. However, the Commission's 2020 evaluation noted no significant increase in market concentration — the four largest explosives manufacturers (Orica, Dyno Nobel, Austin Powder, and Maxam) already held over 60% of the EU market before the directive.

Market surveillance authorities in Germany and the Netherlands reported a 30% reduction in non-compliant explosives products entering their markets between 2016 and 2019, as the clearer CE marking requirements made it easier to identify products that had not undergone proper conformity assessment. The number of RAPEX notifications for explosives fell from an average of 8 per year in 2012–2014 to 3 per year in 2017–2019.

The directive had an unintended consequence for the fireworks industry: the requirement for EU-type examination certificates forced many professional-grade fireworks to be re-classified from "low-hazard" to "medium-hazard" categories, triggering stricter storage and transport rules under national law. The European Pyrotechnics Association complained that this had no safety benefit but increased logistical complexity and costs by an estimated 20–30% for professional displays.

## Key questions answered

### What safety rules apply to explosives used in mining and demolition across Europe?

The Explosives Directive 2014/28/EU requires all civil explosives placed on the EU market — including ANFO used in mining, detonators for demolition, and propellants for industrial tools — to undergo third-party testing by a notified body, receive CE marking, and meet strict design and manufacturing standards. Manufacturers must keep technical documentation for 10 years and ensure traceability through the supply chain. The directive does not cover military explosives or ammunition, which remain under national regulation.

### Do fireworks and theatrical pyrotechnics fall under EU explosives rules?

Yes, professional-grade fireworks and pyrotechnic articles used in theatrical productions, concerts, and film effects are covered by the directive — but only if they contain explosive substances (not just pyrotechnic mixtures covered by the separate Pyrotechnics Directive 2013/29/EU). The 2014 directive covers flash powders, black powder cartridges for special effects, and other more energetic pyrotechnic compositions. Consumer fireworks (Category 1–3) are regulated under the Pyrotechnics Directive, not this one. This split has caused confusion, with some professional pyrotechnic devices requiring certification under both directives.

### What happened after the Toulouse explosion that changed European explosives rules?

The 2001 Toulouse disaster — where 31 people died when ammonium nitrate exploded at the AZF fertiliser plant — prompted two major EU reforms: first, stricter controls on ammonium nitrate fertilisers under Regulation 2003/2003, and second, a broader overhaul of explosives safety that eventually led to the 2014 directive. The explosion exposed that national regulators had inconsistent approaches to classifying and controlling explosive precursors, and that the old 1993 directive had gaps in market surveillance. The Commission's investigation found that the explosion could have been prevented with better mixing controls and storage segregation — requirements that the 2014 directive made mandatory for all civil explosives.

### How did industry lobby against stricter rules on explosives?

The main opposition came from small pyrotechnics manufacturers in Eastern Europe, particularly the Czech Association of Pyrotechnics Manufacturers, which argued that mandatory third-party certification would drive up costs for low-margin products like theatrical flash pots and industrial flare cartridges. The European Association of Explosives Manufacturers broadly supported the directive, as it favoured larger firms that already used notified bodies. The Commission's impact assessment estimated that micro-enterprises (under 10 employees) would face compliance costs equal to 3–5% of their annual turnover — compared to 0.5–1% for larger firms — and offered no transition period or subsidies. Despite these concerns, the directive passed with overwhelming support — 617 votes in favour, 37 against.
