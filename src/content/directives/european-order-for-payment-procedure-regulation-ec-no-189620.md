## What is it?

The European Order for Payment Procedure (Regulation 1896/2006) is a civil‑procedure tool that lets a creditor recover an uncontested money claim from a debtor in another EU member state without having to go through a full trial. The creditor files a standardised application – often online – in their own language, and if the defendant fails to object within a fixed deadline (usually 30 days), the court issues an order that is directly enforceable in all other member states without exequatur (a separate recognition proceeding).

It covers most cross‑border civil and commercial debts above a low monetary threshold (no upper limit, but purely domestic cases are excluded). The procedure is entirely written: no hearing takes place unless the debtor lodges a statement of opposition. If opposition is filed, the case automatically shifts to ordinary litigation in the member state of the defendant – but the creditor has already secured a risk‑free, low‑cost first step. The regulation has been in force since 2008 and is part of the EU’s broader “mutual recognition” programme in civil justice.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was the direct answer to a structural failure of the internal market: cross‑border debt recovery was prohibitively expensive and slow, discouraging especially SMEs from trading across borders. By 2000, the European Commission estimated that about 1% of the EU’s GDP was tied up in unpaid cross‑border debts, yet the cost of enforcing a simple claim in another member state could exceed the claim itself. National procedural laws differed wildly – some required translation of every document, others insisted on personal service by bailiffs, and many demanded costly lawyer representation from the outset.

The political breakthrough came in 1999 with the Tampere European Council, which set mutual recognition of judicial decisions as the cornerstone of EU justice policy. The Commission under Justice Commissioner Antonio Vitorino (1999–2004) began preparing a Green Paper on a European order for payment, published in 2002. Business groups, led by Eurochambres and the European Small Business Alliance, lobbied heavily for a simple, standardised tool – they argued that non‑tariff barriers in justice were as harmful as customs checks. The 2004 competition report of the European Parliament, drafted by MEP Diana Wallis (ALDE, UK), explicitly called for a fast‑track procedure for uncontested claims.

The timing was also ripe: in 2003 the EU had just adopted the Brussels I Regulation (44/2001), which streamlined jurisdiction and enforcement of judgments, but did not solve the problem of how to *get* a judgment cheaply. The order‑for‑payment regulation was intended to fill that gap. A rival approach – full harmonisation of national summary procedures – was rejected as too intrusive. The final proposal, tabled by Commissioner Franco Frattini in 2004, was approved by the Council in December 2006 after two years of debate, with only a few member states (notably Germany, which already had a well‑functioning national order for payment) voicing scepticism about added value. The Parliament voted 532 in favour, 77 against, with 15 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Tampere European Council sets mutual recognition of judicial decisions as the EU’s core civil‑justice goal.
- **2002** — Commission publishes Green Paper on a European order for payment, seeking stakeholder views.
- **2004** — Commission (under Justice Commissioner Franco Frattini) formally proposes the regulation (COM(2004) 173 final).
- **2006, 12 December** — Regulation (EC) No 1896/2006 adopted by the European Parliament and the Council.
- **2008, 12 December** — Regulation enters into force and applies in all member states except Denmark (opt‑out).
- **2015** — Commission publishes first evaluation report on the regulation, noting growing usage but also uneven national implementation.
- **2017** — European e‑Justice Portal launches a fully online application form for the European order for payment, easing cross‑border filing.
- **2021** — Commission’s second evaluation confirms the procedure is used in thousands of cases annually, but uptake remains low in some member states due to lack of awareness and residual national barriers.

## Impact and consequences

The procedure has been modestly successful. By 2019, the Commission’s evaluation found that approximately 12,000 orders for payment were issued annually across the EU – a figure that, while not trivial, is far smaller than the millions of national summary orders in countries like Germany and Austria. The main beneficiaries have been SMEs and creditors with small to medium claims (under €5,000) who would have otherwise abandoned recovery. For example, a Belgian telecom company can now recover unpaid bills from a Spanish customer in about four weeks, at a fraction of the cost of a full lawsuit.

However, the procedure’s impact has been hampered by several factors. First, many national courts were slow to adopt electronic filing and still require paper submissions, undermining the regulation’s promise of speed. Second, debtors in some member states (notably Italy and Greece) have successfully resisted enforcement by using local procedural tricks – a problem the regulation does not address. Third, the Commission itself acknowledged in its 2017 report that only 15% of potential users were aware of the tool. The regulation’s reliance on the debtor’s passive non‑response (rather than active judgment) also raises due‑process concerns: in a few cases, debtors claimed they never received the form because service was defective, and the Court of Justice of the EU (in Case C‑324/12, *Novontech‑Zala*, 2014) was forced to clarify that national judges must check service validity.

## Key questions answered

### Can a company in Germany sue a customer in France without going to court?  

Yes, if the debt is uncontested (the customer does not dispute it). The German company completes a standardised form (available online in all EU languages) and submits it to a German court. That court sends the form to the French customer, who has 30 days to object. If the customer does nothing, the German court issues an enforceable order – all without any hearing. If the customer objects, the case becomes a normal lawsuit in France.

### How long does it take to get an enforceable European order for payment?  

If the debtor does not object, the order is typically issued within three to four weeks from filing. This is dramatically faster than the average 6–18 months for a cross‑border civil lawsuit. The debtor can still contest the order later in exceptional circumstances (e.g., defective service), but in practice the order is enforceable immediately in all EU member states (except Denmark).

### Does the procedure work for small claims under €2,000?  

Absolutely – and the regulation was partly designed for exactly those cases. The cost of filing is low (typically between €15 and €50 in court fees) and legal representation is not mandatory. For very small debts, many creditors used to simply give up; now they can recover the sum with minimal administrative burden. The optional EU Small Claims Procedure (Regulation 861/2007), which covers claims up to €5,000, is an alternative but requires a hearing if the debtor contests.

### What happens if the debtor claims they never received the notification?  

This is a known weakness. The regulation requires the court to verify that the debtor was properly served, but national practices vary. If the debtor later proves that service was defective (e.g., the form was left at a wrong address), they can ask the court to set aside the order. The Court of Justice has ruled that national judges must actively check service documents before issuing the order – a safeguard that, in practice, some courts overlook.
