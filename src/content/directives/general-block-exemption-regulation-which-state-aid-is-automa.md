## What is it?

The General Block Exemption Regulation (GBER) is a cornerstone of EU State aid simplification. It allows Member States to grant certain types of public subsidies to companies without first seeking the European Commission’s approval. Instead of a multi‑month notification procedure, the aid is considered “automatically compatible” with EU law if it meets the conditions laid down in the regulation — thresholds on aid amounts, eligible costs, maximum intensities, and sectoral exclusions.

Adopted as Commission Regulation (EU) No 651/2014 on 17 June 2014, the GBER consolidates and expands earlier, more fragmented block exemption regulations that existed for SMEs, employment, training, and regional aid. It now covers 12 broad categories: regional investment aid, SME investment and employment aid, aid for female entrepreneurship, environmental protection, research & development, innovation, energy infrastructure, broadband networks, culture and heritage conservation, natural disaster compensation, and more. Each category has its own specific thresholds and conditions — for instance, regional investment aid may not exceed €15 million per investment project for an “assisted area” under the regional aid map.

The regulation is part of the Commission’s State Aid Modernisation (SAM) package, which aimed to shift enforcement focus away from thousands of low‑risk notifications and towards large, potentially distortive cases. It is complemented by the de minimis regulation (1407/2013), which exempts very small amounts of aid (up to €200,000 per undertaking over three fiscal years) from the notification requirement altogether.

## Why was it introduced?

Before the GBER, the Commission’s approach to State aid was administratively heavy. Under Article 108(3) of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, Member States must notify any plan to grant aid to the Commission and wait for approval before implementation. By the early 2010s, the Commission was receiving around 500–800 new notifications each year, the majority of which involved relatively small amounts of aid that posed little risk to competition in the single market. Businesses and governments complained that the process caused delays of six months or more for routine measures such as local SME grants or small regional investment schemes.

The financial crisis of 2008–2009 amplified these frustrations. In 2009–2010, Member States launched large‑scale rescue and restructuring packages for banks and struggling industries, but many also wanted to accelerate support for small businesses and clean energy projects. The existing block exemption regulations — first introduced for SMEs in 1992, and later for employment, training, and regional aid — were piecemeal and outdated. For example, the 2008 General Block Exemption Regulation (GBER I) had combined only four categories; many important aid types still required individual notification.

The political driver for reform came from Competition Commissioner Joaquín Almunia, who took office in February 2010. In May 2012, he launched the State Aid Modernisation (SAM) initiative, a comprehensive overhaul built on three pillars: (1) fostering growth in the internal market by allowing well‑targeted aid, (2) concentrating enforcement on cases with the greatest distortive effect, and (3) streamlining rules and procedures. Almunia explicitly stated that “the Commission should not spend its limited resources on routine cases that do not significantly affect competition”. The GBER was the regulatory core of the second and third pillars.

A coalition of Member States, particularly those with devolved regional subsidy powers (Germany, Austria, Spain) and newer Member States seeking rapid catch‑up (Poland, Romania), supported a broad exemption. Industry associations such as BusinessEurope and DigitalEurope lobbied for higher thresholds and faster procedures. Opposition came primarily from competition law practitioners and some economists who worried that exempting aid from scrutiny could lead to subsidy races within the single market. However, the Commission argued that the detailed conditions — maximum intensities, eligible costs, transparency requirements — would act as sufficient guardrails, and that ex‑post evaluation would catch abuses.

The timing was also ripe because the Commission had just completed a major revision of the Regional Aid Guidelines (2014‑2020) and the Environmental and Energy Aid Guidelines. The GBER could reference these updated frameworks, ensuring consistency. The regulation was adopted by the Commission on 17 June 2014, after a public consultation that drew around 80 contributions from governments, companies, and NGOs, and entered into force on 1 July 2014.

What changed? Previously, Member States had to notify around 90% of all new State aid measures. The Commission estimated in its 2012 impact assessment that the GBER would increase the share of exempted measures to roughly 15% of total State aid expenditure (in value), but over 80% of the measures by number. In practice, the GBER allowed Member States to design and implement aid schemes within weeks rather than months, while the Commission focus shifted to a smaller number of high‑risk notifications.

## Timeline

- **1992** — First block exemption regulation for SMEs (limited, sector‑specific).
- **2001–2008** — Piecemeal block exemptions for employment, training, and regional aid adopted; each with separate regulation.
- **2010** — Joaquín Almunia becomes Competition Commissioner; begins internal review of State aid procedures.
- **May 2012** — Commission launches State Aid Modernisation (SAM) communication; announces intention to consolidate and expand block exemptions.
- **2012–2013** — Public consultation on the draft GBER; 80 submissions received.
- **17 June 2014** — Commission adopts Regulation (EU) No 651/2014 (GBER).
- **1 July 2014** — Regulation enters into force; applies immediately to all new aid measures.
- **2017** — Commission publishes first evaluation report; notes increased use of GBER but also highlights need for clearer conditionality on some categories (e.g., broadband aid).
- **2021** — Amending Regulation (EU) 2021/1237 aligns GBER with revised Regional Aid Guidelines and extends it to 31 December 2023.
- **2023** — Following further evaluations, Commission proposes extended GBER for 2024–2026 as part of the post‑pandemic State aid temporary framework consolidation.

## Impact and consequences

The GBER has significantly reduced the administrative burden for both Member States and the Commission. According to the Commission’s 2020 evaluation report, the number of State aid notifications fell by roughly 40% between 2014 and 2019, while the volume of aid granted under the GBER grew to about €55 billion per year by 2020, covering over 80% of all new aid measures (about 1,000 schemes annually). The largest categories used are regional investment aid, SME aid, and environmental protection aid.

The impact is uneven. Wealthier Member States with long experience in designing State aid (Germany, France, the UK before Brexit) were quick to take advantage of the GBER, while some newer Member States struggled to set up the necessary monitoring and transparency systems. The regulation also led to a shift in Commission enforcement: instead of reviewing thousands of notifications, the Commission now conducts targeted ex‑post evaluations and sector inquiries (e.g., into broadband aid in 2015–2017, and into renewable energy subsidies in 2018).

For businesses, the GBER has meant faster access to public funding. A 2019 study by the European Court of Auditors, however, noted that the transparency requirements (publication of aid on a national website) remain poorly enforced in several countries, limiting scrutiny.

## Key questions answered

### Do European companies need government permission for every subsidy they receive?

No, not for small or medium‑sized subsidies. Under the General Block Exemption Regulation, Member States can grant aid for specific purposes — such as SME investments, regional development, or environmental research — without prior approval from the European Commission, provided the aid stays below certain thresholds (e.g., €15 million per regional investment project) and respects maximum aid intensities. The regulation also covers very small subsidies under €200,000 per company over three years via the de minimis regulation, which is even simpler.

### What types of government aid are allowed automatically in the EU?

The GBER lists 12 categories that are automatically compatible with EU law, including: aid for small and medium‑sized enterprises (SMEs), regional investment aid in disadvantaged areas, aid for environmental protection (e.g., renewable energy, energy‑efficiency upgrades), research, development and innovation projects, training, employment of disadvantaged workers, broadband infrastructure, culture and heritage conservation, and natural disaster compensation. Each category has specific conditions on eligible costs and maximum percentages of public support; failing to meet them means the aid must be notified individually.

### How did the EU simplify State aid rules after the financial crisis?

The State Aid Modernisation initiative, launched by Competition Commissioner Joaquín Almunia in 2012, streamlined rules to reduce bureaucratic delays for low‑risk aid. The key change was the adoption of the General Block Exemption Regulation in 2014, which consolidated previous scattered exemptions and added new categories. It allowed Member States to implement hundreds of small‑ and medium‑scale aid schemes without Commission notification, cutting approval times from months to weeks. The Commission instead focused on monitoring and evaluating large subsidies and cross‑border distortive cases.

### Can a local government give a grant to a small bakery without telling Brussels?

Yes, as long as the grant qualifies under one of the GBER categories (most likely SME investment or employment aid) and stays within the limits. For example, if a municipality gives €50,000 to a bakery with 10 employees to buy new ovens, it would usually be exempt under the SME aid provisions, provided the bakery’s total aid from all sources does not exceed the relevant ceiling. The local government must still publish the aid on a national transparency website and keep records, but it does not need to wait for the European Commission to say yes.
