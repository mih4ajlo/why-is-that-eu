## What is it?

The EU Taxonomy Regulation (2020/852/EU) defines a common classification system — a "taxonomy" — for determining whether an economic activity is environmentally sustainable. It sets six environmental objectives: climate change mitigation, climate change adaptation, sustainable use of water and marine resources, transition to a circular economy, pollution prevention and control, and protection of biodiversity and ecosystems. An activity qualifies as sustainable if it contributes substantially to at least one objective, does no significant harm to the others, complies with minimum social safeguards, and meets technical screening criteria set by the European Commission.

The regulation applies to financial market participants, large companies, and issuers of financial products who must disclose how and to what extent their activities align with the taxonomy. It does not mandate green investment but creates a common language and transparency framework, making it harder for companies to claim environmental benefits without evidence. The first delegated acts covering climate objectives took effect in 2021, with additional environmental objectives phased in later.

## Why was it introduced?

The introduction of the EU Taxonomy Regulation was driven by a convergence of market failures, regulatory gaps, and political pressure following the 2015 Paris Agreement. The most immediate trigger was the European Commission's 2018 Action Plan on Sustainable Finance, a direct response to the financial sector's growing demand for clarity on what counted as "green" amid an explosion of unverified environmental claims.

The underlying structural tension was profound. By the mid-2010s, private investment labelled as sustainable had surged — according to the Global Sustainable Investment Alliance, global sustainable investment assets reached $30.7 trillion in 2018 — but there was no common definition across EU member states. France had its own "greenfin" label for funds investing in energy transition, while Luxembourg and the UK operated different systems. This fragmentation created a clear market failure: investors could not compare products across borders, and companies could exploit ambiguity. The phenomenon was dubbed "greenwashing" — BP, for instance, rebranded as "Beyond Petroleum" in 2000 while continuing to invest heavily in fossil fuels, and in 2019 the Norwegian Consumer Authority threatened to sue Volkswagen for misleading green advertising on its electric vehicles.

The coalition that pushed the Taxonomy through was unusual and broad. The European Commission — under President Ursula von der Leyen, who had made the European Green Deal her flagship initiative in December 2019 — found allies among institutional investors led by BlackRock and the Norwegian Sovereign Wealth Fund, both of which publicly called for standardised definitions. The European Parliament's Green group, particularly MEP and rapporteur Sirpa Pietikäinen, provided legislative muscle. Nordic countries and the Netherlands were early advocates, viewing it as a way to cement their leadership in clean technology. Opposition came from parts of the financial industry — the European Banking Federation initially warned that a rigid taxonomy could reduce capital flows to transitional activities — and from central European states led by Poland and Hungary, who feared it would disadvantage their coal-dependent economies.

The timing was decisive. The European Green Deal, published in December 2019, set the political target of making Europe the first climate-neutral continent by 2050. But the Deal itself was a political declaration; the Taxonomy provided the regulatory backbone to channel private capital — estimated by the Commission at €260 billion annually in additional investment needed — toward that goal. The COVID-19 pandemic, which hit Europe in early 2020, accelerated this logic as the recovery package NextGenerationEU was explicitly tied to green spending targets.

What changed previous deadlocks? Earlier attempts at sustainable finance classification — such as the 2014 Non-Financial Reporting Directive (2014/95/EU) — had stopped at disclosure requirements without defining the content. The deadlock broke because the financial industry, facing reputational risk from scandals and growing pressure from activist investors, moved from opposition to demand. In 2019, the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) highlighted that 42% of sustainability-related claims by asset managers were "vague or misleading," providing concrete evidence of market failure that the Commission used in its impact assessment.

Keen to avoid accusations of favouring one technology over another, the Commission established a Technical Expert Group on Sustainable Finance in 2018, composed of 35 members from civil society, academia, finance, and industry. Their June 2019 report provided the technical blueprint that became the regulation. Notably, the most contentious remaining issue — whether gas and nuclear should be included as transitional activities — was deferred to delegated acts, a politically expedient decision that avoided derailing the main regulation. The European Parliament's final vote on June 18, 2020, passed with 199 votes in favour, 63 against, and 22 abstentions in the ENVI committee, with the full Parliament later endorsing it by 541 to 45 votes.

## Impact and consequences

The EU Taxonomy Regulation has reshaped sustainable finance markets across Europe. By 2023, over 50% of EU-listed companies were required to disclose taxonomy-eligibility metrics. The European Banking Authority reported in 2022 that more than 60% of banks had begun integrating taxonomy criteria into lending decisions. However, compliance costs have been significant: a 2021 Commission study estimated these at €1.5 million to €5 million per large company in the first year.

The most controversial consequence has been the inclusion of natural gas and nuclear energy under strict conditions in a 2022 delegated act. This split the coalition that passed the regulation: Austria and Luxembourg threatened legal action against the Commission, while Germany led the opposite camp arguing that gas was essential for the transition away from coal. The European Parliament initially blocked the act but ultimately failed to overturn it, passing it by default in July 2022 after failing to muster the 353 votes needed for rejection.

Industry response has been mixed. The European Investment Bank began using the taxonomy in 2022 to define green bond eligibility. But the banking sector has struggled with implementation: a 2023 European Banking Authority report found that 70% of banks identified data gaps and methodological divergences between member states as their main operational challenge.

The regulation has also triggered fierce debates about what qualifies as a "transition activity." French nuclear giant EDF successfully pushed for nuclear inclusion, arguing it was a low-carbon baseload power source, while environmental NGOs including WWF and ClientEarth argued this undermined the taxonomy's credibility. The European Commission's own scientific advisors — the Joint Research Centre — recommended excluding nuclear in their technical assessment, but the political decision overrode this advice.

## Key questions answered

### Is all investment labelled "green" in the EU actually sustainable?

Not automatically. The Taxonomy Regulation does not mandate that products labelled "sustainable" must meet its criteria — it only requires disclosure of whether and how they align. Financial products can still be marketed as "green" using the regulator's own definitions. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) has repeatedly warned that firms may exaggerate alignment, and in 2023 it began targeted supervisory actions on asset managers' taxonomy disclosures.

### Why does EU law allow gas and nuclear energy to be classified as sustainable?

The 2022 delegated act on climate objectives included gas and nuclear under strict conditions — for example, gas plants must close by 2035 and emit less than 100g CO2 per kWh. This was a political compromise driven by France (nuclear) and central European countries (gas). The Commission's rationale was that excluding both would hinder certain member states' energy transition, even though many environmental groups fought fiercely against the decision.

### Does the Taxonomy apply to both companies and financial institutions?

Yes, but with different requirements. Large companies (over 500 employees) must disclose the proportion of their turnover, capital expenditure, and operating expenditure aligned with the taxonomy. Financial institutions — banks, insurers, pension funds — must disclose the taxonomy-alignment of their investment portfolios. Small and medium-sized enterprises are largely exempt unless they issue green bonds.

### Can a company be sued for mismatching its green claims with the Taxonomy?

Potentially, under the 2023 Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). The CSRD requires audit-level assurance on taxonomy disclosures. Misleading taxonomy reporting could trigger enforcement by national securities regulators or private lawsuits under EU consumer law, as seen in the 2022 "ClientEarth v. Shell" case where NGOs successfully argued that Shell's renewable energy claims were insufficiently substantiated.
