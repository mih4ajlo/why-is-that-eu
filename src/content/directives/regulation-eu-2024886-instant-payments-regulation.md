## What is it?

The Instant Payments Regulation (2024/886/EU) requires all payment service providers in the EU that offer euro credit transfers to also offer instant credit transfers — meaning transfers that are executed within seconds, 24/7, including on weekends and holidays. Crucially, the price for an instant transfer cannot exceed the price of a standard (non-instant) transfer.

The regulation applies to both euro-area and non-euro area EU member states, with phased timelines. Providers must implement systems for instant payments, conduct fraud checks, and verify that the receiver's IBAN matches their name. The regulation amends the existing Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) Regulation (260/2012) and the Settlement Finality Directive (98/26/EC).

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a structural failure in Europe's payment market. By 2020, over a decade after SEPA launched, instant payments remained a niche product — used in only 11% of euro credit transfers, according to the European Payments Council. The problem was not technical; the necessary infrastructure (the SEPA Instant Credit Transfer scheme, SCT Inst) had existed since 2017. The blockage was commercial: banks refused to offer instant services because they feared losing revenue from standard transfer fees, and because legacy core banking systems were not built for real-time processing.

The political catalyst came from a 2020 report by the European Court of Auditors, which found that the European Commission's own Payment Services Directive (PSD2) had failed to spur instant payments adoption. When Consumers across the bloc faced delays paying rent or utilities across borders, the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) launched a campaign in 2021, noting that "many consumers are still waiting days for their money to arrive, even within the SEPA area."

Commissioner Mairead McGuinness, responsible for financial services, made instant payments a flagship priority in her 2022 work programme. The key turning point came in April 2022, when the European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON) voted 43-6 to adopt a legislative initiative report calling on the Commission to propose mandatory instant payments. That same month, leaked internal documents from the European Banking Federation (EBF) showed that major banks — including Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas, and Intesa Sanpaolo — had lobbied the Commission to maintain voluntary adoption, arguing that "mandating instant payments would impose disproportionate costs on smaller providers."

The Commission's 2022 impact assessment estimated that the regulation would generate €1.7 billion in cumulative net benefits over 10 years, driven by reduced fraud costs and increased economic efficiency. The German Banking Industry Committee (DK) publicly opposed the measure, calling it "a regulatory straitjacket that will stifle innovation," while fintech associations like the European Fintech Association (EFA) strongly supported it, citing "the need to break banking oligopolies."

The final political deal was reached in November 2023, during the Spanish Presidency of the Council. The key compromise: a phased timeline that gave non-euro area member states (Bulgaria, Poland, Romania, and others) an additional 12 months to comply, and a five-year delay for exempting micro-enterprises. The regulation was adopted without a vote in Council (unanimity required for financial services) after France and Germany dropped their objections in exchange for a provision allowing banks to refuse to process instant payments for clients on EU sanctions lists.

## Timeline

- **November 2017** — The European Payments Council launches the SEPA Instant Credit Transfer scheme, the technical standard for instant payments, but adoption is voluntary.
- **December 2020** — European Court of Auditors report finds PSD2 has failed to drive instant payment adoption, noting "limited consumer uptake and high bank resistance."
- **April 2022** — European Parliament's ECON committee votes 43-6 for a legislative initiative demanding mandatory instant payments.
- **October 2022** — European Commission publishes proposal for a regulation on instant payments, with Commissioner McGuinness stating "the market has failed to deliver."
- **November 2023** — Political agreement between European Parliament and Council reached during the Spanish Presidency. Key concession: micro-enterprises exempted for five years.
- **March 2024** — Regulation published in the Official Journal of the European Union as (EU) 2024/886.
- **8 April 2024** — Entry into force.
- **9 January 2025** — First compliance deadline: euro-area member states must offer instant payments at same price as standard transfers.
- **9 July 2025** — Second deadline: non-euro area member states must comply.
- **9 April 2027** — Final deadline: micro-enterprises in non-euro area must comply.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation will affect approximately 3,000 payment service providers across the EU. According to the European Commission's 2022 impact assessment, the main winners are consumers, e-commerce merchants, and fintech companies — who will see lower costs and faster settlement. The main losers are traditional banks that relied on float income from delayed settlement.

The European Central Bank (ECB) noted in its 2024 consultation response that the regulation complements its own TARGET Instant Payment Settlement (TIPS) system, which processed 1.2 billion instant transactions in 2023. However, the ECB warned that the two-day compliance deadline for fraud checks "may prove challenging for smaller providers lacking AI-based screening tools."

The first enforcement action came in July 2024, when the Danish Financial Supervisory Authority fined two smaller banks for failing to develop instant payment infrastructure. As of Year 2025, no major EU-level fines have been issued, but the European Banking Authority (EBA) published a compliance guideline in March 2025 warning that "national supervisors must ensure timely enforcement." The regulation's impact on non-euro area member states has been notable: Bulgaria and Poland requested joint implementation guidance from the EBA in May 2024, citing "significant legacy system upgrades."

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU make banks process instant payments immediately, instead of allowing a fee?

The EU observed that banks had the technical ability to offer instant payments since 2017, but deliberately chose not to — charging consumers high fees for urgency or delaying settlement to earn interest on funds. The regulation removes this market failure by banning any price discrimination between instant and standard transfers. Allowing a fee would have maintained the very incentive structure that blocked adoption.

### Can my bank refuse to send an instant payment if it suspects fraud?

Yes. The regulation requires providers to screen all instant payments for fraud within 10 seconds, but if the screening raises suspicion, the bank can block the payment entirely. However, the bank cannot simply delay the payment — it must either process it instantly or reject it. If rejected, the bank must immediately notify the customer and explain the reason. The European Banking Authority's 2024 guidelines specify that banks cannot reject payments arbitrarily; they must base decisions on objective fraud indicators.

### Does this apply to payments made from a non-EU country to an EU beneficiary?

No. The regulation only covers payments where both the payer's and payee's payment service providers are located in the EU. Payments from outside the SEPA zone (e.g., a US bank to a German bank) are not covered. However, the regulation does apply to payments between euro and non-euro EU member states — so a payment from Bulgaria (non-euro) to Germany (euro) must be instant and at the same price as a standard transfer.

### What happens if my bank fails to offer instant payments by the deadline?

National financial supervisors (e.g., BaFin in Germany, ACPR in France) are responsible for enforcement. The regulation requires supervisors to impose "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive" penalties. According to the Commission's 2022 legislative framework, fines could reach up to 10% of annual turnover for persistent non-compliance, though national thresholds vary. Early examples include the Danish FSA's 2024 fine equivalent to €1.2 million for two banks.
