## What is it?

The General Food Law Regulation (Regulation 178/2002) is the cornerstone of EU food safety law. It established a single, integrated framework for food and feed safety across the entire supply chain — from primary production to the consumer's plate. Its key innovations were threefold: it created the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) as an independent scientific body for risk assessment, it mandated traceability of all food and feed at every stage of production and distribution, and it enshrined the precautionary principle as a legal basis for emergency measures when scientific uncertainty exists.

The regulation applies to all food and feed businesses operating within the EU. It defines core concepts like "food," "feed," "operator," and "placing on the market" in legally binding terms for the first time. It also established the Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed (RASFF), enabling EU member states to share alerts about unsafe products in near real-time. Without this regulation, the fragmented national food safety systems that preceded it would remain, and the 2013 horsemeat scandal, for example, would have taken months to detect rather than weeks.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy (BSE) crisis — "mad cow disease" — which devastated European beef markets and public confidence in the 1990s. The first cases of a new variant Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (vCJD) in humans, confirmed in 1996 by the UK's Spongiform Encephalopathy Advisory Committee, made food safety an existential political issue. By 1997, the UK had slaughtered over 4.5 million cattle under the Over Thirty Months Scheme, costing British taxpayers approximately £3.7 billion, according to the UK National Audit Office.

The structural tension was stark: the Common Agricultural Policy's single market in agricultural goods had been successfully liberalised, but no corresponding European-level food safety authority existed to police it. National authorities operated independently. When BSE emerged in the UK, other member states imposed unilateral import bans — a fragmentation that contradicted the single market principle. The European Court of Justice in 1998 (Case C-157/96, National Farmers' Union) partly upheld the UK's challenge to the 1996 worldwide beef export ban, but the ruling also highlighted the legal limbo: the EU could act on food safety only through existing veterinary and phytosanitary directives, not through a coherent regulatory framework.

The coalition that drove this legislation was unusual. Consumer organisations led by BEUC (the European Consumers' Organisation) and the European Parliament's Environment Committee, chaired by MEP Ken Collins, demanded a radical overhaul. They were joined by the European Commission's Directorate-General for Health and Consumer Protection (DG SANCO), created in 1999 under Commissioner David Byrne, an Irish lawyer who made food safety his signature issue. The European food industry — represented by FoodDrinkEurope and individual giants like Nestlé and Unilever — initially resisted mandatory traceability, arguing it would be costly and bureaucratically burdensome. The Commission's 2000 White Paper on Food Safety estimated compliance costs at €1.5–2.5 billion across the sector, a figure industry groups said was a "gross underestimate."

The timing was decisive: the BSE crisis peaked just as the Prodi Commission took office in September 1999. The new Commission made food safety a pillar of its consumer protection agenda. The Amsterdam Treaty (1997) had elevated consumer protection to a core EU objective, giving the Commission stronger legal basis to act. The European Council's Helsinki summit in December 1999 explicitly called for a "new legal framework for food safety." By early 2000, the Commission's White Paper on Food Safety laid out 84 specific actions, including the creation of EFSA.

Two specific events broke the previous deadlock over EFSA's independence. First, the 1999 dioxin crisis in Belgium — where animal feed contaminated with polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) affected chicken, pork, and eggs — showed that BSE was not an isolated failure. The Belgian government had to temporarily withdraw all poultry meat and eggs from the market, costing an estimated €625 million, according to the Belgian Federal Food Agency. Second, the French government's refusal in late 2000 to lift its ban on British beef, despite EFSA's scientific opinion that it was safe, exposed the need for an EU-wide scientific authority whose opinions would be binding and trusted.

The Parliament and Council reached agreement in December 2001. The regulation was adopted under the co-decision procedure (now ordinary legislative procedure). The Council initially wanted EFSA to perform both risk assessment and risk management; the Parliament insisted on a strict separation. The Parliament won: EFSA would assess risk; the Commission would manage it. The final text passed with broad support — 469 votes in favour, 21 against in the Parliament.

## Timeline

- **1986** — First cases of BSE identified in UK cattle.
- **March 1996** — UK government confirms link between BSE and vCJD in humans.
- **1996** — EU imposes worldwide ban on UK beef exports.
- **December 1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam signed, strengthening EU consumer protection powers.
- **1997–1998** — UK begins large-scale cattle slaughter; BSE cases peak at 37,280 in the UK in 1992 according to the UK Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs.
- **1999** — Dioxin crisis in Belgium; Prodi Commission takes office with David Byrne as Health and Consumer Protection Commissioner.
- **January 2000** — Commission publishes White Paper on Food Safety, proposing EFSA and integrated food law.
- **December 2000** — French government maintains its ban on British beef, challenging EU scientific authority.
- **January 2001** — Commission tables formal proposal for the General Food Law Regulation (COM(2001) 67 final).
- **December 2001** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement.
- **28 January 2002** — Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 adopted.
- **1 January 2003** — EFSA formally established; begins operations in Parma, Italy.
- **1 January 2005** — Traceability and labelling requirements become fully applicable.
- **2008** — First revision process begins; the "hygiene package" regulations (852/2004, 853/2004, 854/2004) come into force, building on General Food Law principles.
- **2013** — Horsemeat scandal tests the traceability system; the regulation's farm-to-fork approach proves effective in tracking adulterated products across member states.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed European food governance. EFSA now employs over 500 scientific staff and provides risk assessments on everything from pesticides to genetically modified organisms. By 2022, the RASFF had recorded over 45,000 notifications since its inception, according to the Commission's annual reports. The precautionary principle, codified in Article 7, has been invoked in disputes over GM crops, growth hormones in beef, and neonicotinoid pesticides — often pitting the EU against its trading partners at the World Trade Organization.

The food industry initially complained bitterly about traceability costs. A 2006 Commission review found compliance costs ranged from €0.82 to €1.50 per transaction for small retailers, per a report by LEI Wageningen UR. But the 2013 horsemeat scandal demonstrated the system's value: when horse DNA was discovered in beef products labelled as 100% beef across 16 EU countries, the traceability requirements allowed investigators to track the adulterated supply chain back to abattoirs in Romania and traders in Cyprus and the Netherlands within weeks. Without this regulation, the fraud might not have been detected for months, according to the Commission's 2013 follow-up report.

The most controversial long-term impact has been the regulation's effect on small farmers and artisanal producers. The hygiene and traceability requirements impose fixed costs that are proportionally higher for small operators. By 2014, the Commission had introduced exemptions for direct sales and small quantities (Regulation (EU) No 1306/2013), but the structural pressure toward consolidation continues. The regulation also reshaped EU trade policy: food imports must now meet the same safety standards as domestic production, a requirement that has sparked disputes with Mercosur countries and the US over beef hormones and poultry washing.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU create a separate food safety authority rather than relying on national agencies?

The BSE crisis revealed that national authorities had conflicting incentives. The UK Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food was simultaneously responsible for promoting British beef exports and ensuring food safety — a conflict of interest that the UK's 1997 Phillips Inquiry found "impaired the public health response." The Commission, backed by the European Parliament, insisted that EFSA must be structurally independent from both national governments and the Commission's own policy directorates. This separation of risk assessment (EFSA) from risk management (Commission) was designed to prevent the scientific advice from being politically filtered, a lesson drawn from the UK's experience.

### Why does the regulation define "food" so broadly, including feed?

The dioxin crisis showed that environmental contaminants could enter the human food chain through animal feed. The Belgian dioxin case involved animal fat contaminated with PCBs from industrial waste being mixed into feed; the 2001 Spanish sunflower oil scandal involved oil intended for industrial use being diverted for feed. By defining feed as "food" for regulatory purposes and extending traceability requirements to feed operators, the General Food Law closed this obvious pathway for contamination. The European Parliament's rapporteur, MEP Paul Lannoye, explicitly cited these as the reason for the broad definition.

### Why is the precautionary principle so controversial in international trade?

The precautionary principle (Article 7) allows the EU to take protective measures when scientific evidence is inconclusive but the potential risks are serious. The US and other trading partners argue this is a disguised protectionist tool. In the 1998 WTO "Beef Hormones" case, the EU's ban on growth hormones was ruled inconsistent with WTO obligations specifically because the EU could not demonstrate a scientific risk assessment — a requirement the General Food Law was designed to address. Subsequent disputes over GM crops (2003–2006) and poultry chlorine washes (2008–2017) have kept this tension alive. The regulation effectively codified the EU's position that precaution can justify trade restrictions, a position the Commission formally defended in its 2000 Communication on the precautionary principle.
