## What is it?

The Rome III Regulation is an EU legal instrument that tells a judge handling a cross-border divorce case *which country’s law* to apply. It does not harmonise divorce law itself — it does not say what the grounds for divorce are — but it prevents couples from “shopping” for the most favourable legal system simply by choosing where to file.

The regulation gives couples the freedom to agree in writing on the law that will govern their divorce, provided it is the law of a country with which they have a genuine connection (e.g. habitual residence, nationality, or last habitual residence). Without an agreement, the judge applies a cascading system: first the law of the couple’s current habitual residence; failing that, the law of their last habitual residence if one spouse still lives there; then the law of their common nationality. Only about 16 EU states are bound by the regulation; the others opted out.

## Why was it introduced?

The driving force behind Rome III was the legal chaos created by free movement. As more EU citizens married, moved, and separated across borders, national courts routinely faced a Kafkaesque choice: a French-German couple divorcing in Belgium could have their case decided under French, German, or Belgian law depending on procedural tricks. The automatic link between the court’s location (the *forum*) and the applicable law meant that whichever spouse got to court first could choose a jurisdiction with faster or more favourable divorce rules — a practice known as "forum shopping".

The European Commission under José Manuel Barroso, with Justice Commissioner Viviane Reding as the lead political figure, tried to advance a full EU-wide regulation in 2006. A Green Paper was followed by a formal proposal in 2008. But it hit a wall. Several member states — notably Sweden, the United Kingdom, and Ireland — opposed any measure that would force foreign divorce law onto their citizens, particularly on moral or religious grounds. The Swedish government warned that its liberal divorce regime could be undermined by couples locked into more restrictive foreign rules. The proposal required unanimity in the Council for family law matters (Article 81 TFEU), and that unanimity was impossible.

The deadlock was broken by an obscure but powerful procedural tool: the Treaty of Lisbon, which came into force in December 2009, explicitly allowed a group of at least nine member states to go ahead without the others through “enhanced cooperation”. In March 2010, nine countries — Belgium, Bulgaria, Germany, Spain, France, Italy, Latvia, Luxembourg, and Malta — wrote to the Council asking for permission to proceed. The European Parliament backed them in June 2010, and the Council authorised enhanced cooperation in July 2010. The regulation itself was adopted in December 2010. Viviane Reding called it a “first step” and “a victory for European citizens,” stressing that the opt-out states could join later.

The coalition that passed it was a mix of secular southern states and central European countries where family law was already relatively standardised. The main opponents — the UK, Sweden, and Ireland — stayed outside. No alternative was seriously considered because the political divisions were too deep for any single-text compromise. The enhanced cooperation route was the only viable path after unanimity had failed.

## Timeline

- **2006** — The European Commission publishes a Green Paper on applicable law and jurisdiction in divorce matters, launching a consultation.
- **2008** — Commissioner Jacques Barrot (then justice commissioner) proposes a full regulation covering all 27 EU members.
- **2009** — Council discussions stall; unanimity impossible. Treaty of Lisbon enters into force, explicitly enabling enhanced cooperation for family law.
- **March 2010** — Nine member states formally request enhanced cooperation on divorce law.
- **June 2010** — European Parliament approves the enhanced cooperation request.
- **July 2010** — Council authorises enhanced cooperation.
- **December 2010** — Rome III Regulation is adopted (Council Decision and Regulation).
- **21 June 2012** — Regulation applies in all participating member states.
- **2012–2018** — Additional states join: Greece, Romania, Portugal, Lithuania, Slovenia, Estonia, Croatia (2018). As of 2025, 16 member states participate.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation reduced legal uncertainty for several hundred thousand cross-border couples in participating states, though the Commission never issued a comprehensive ex-post evaluation quantifying the effect. The most visible impact was a sharp decline in divorce tourism within the participating countries: a couple living in Paris could no longer file in Latvia simply to get a faster divorce under Latvian law, because the regulation applied the same connecting factors regardless of which forum they chose inside the bloc.

However, the patchwork system created its own problems. A multinational couple might have access to Rome III if they live in Germany but not if they live in Sweden. Family law practitioners had to master two regimes simultaneously. The UK, which had been one of the loudest opponents, eventually opted to stay out entirely, and after Brexit the regulation simply never applied there. Ireland and Denmark also remained outside.

Critics — including the European Parliament’s own legal affairs committee in a 2020 working document — noted that the enhanced cooperation had not spurred wider participation and that the fragmentation undermined the original goal of a truly uniform European divorce law. Proponents argued that Rome III at least demonstrated that the mechanism worked, paving the way for later enhanced cooperation in other family law areas such as matrimonial property regimes (Regulation 2016/1103).

## Key questions answered

### What happens to a cross-border couple's divorce in Europe?

If both partners live in a country that participates in the Rome III Regulation and they file for divorce in that country, the judge must use the rules in the regulation to decide which national law applies — not necessarily the law of the court itself. The couple can agree on the law in advance, otherwise a prescribed sequence of connecting factors determines it.

### Can I choose which country's law applies to my divorce?

Yes, but only if both partners agree in writing and the chosen law has a genuine connection to the couple — such as the habitual residence of one partner, the last habitual residence, or a common nationality. You cannot choose the law of a country with no link to your life (e.g. you live in Berlin, are German and French, and choose Italian law). The choice must be made before the divorce proceedings start.

### Why doesn't the EU have a single divorce law for all countries?

Because EU countries are deeply divided on the grounds for divorce — some allow no-fault divorce unilaterally, others require fault or long separation, and some states have strong religious traditions that influence civil law. Unanimity is required for family law in the EU Treaties, and that unanimity is unattainable. The Rome III Regulation side-steps the problem by only harmonising the *choice-of-law* rules, not divorce law itself, and only among a subset of willing member states.
