## What is it?

The Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive (AIFMD) review, adopted as directive 2024/927/EU in April 2024, overhauls the 2011 AIFMD framework for funds that fall outside traditional UCITS (e.g., hedge funds, private equity, real estate funds). The review introduces mandatory liquidity management tools (LMTs) — such as redemption gates, side pockets, and suspension powers — for open-ended funds investing in illiquid assets. It also tightens rules on delegation: when a fund manager in the EU outsources core portfolio management functions to a third party outside the EU, national regulators must now assess whether that arrangement is a "letter-box entity" designed to bypass EU regulations.

The review applies to all EU-based alternative fund managers (AIFMs) managing over €100 million in assets (the existing AIFMD threshold). Key changes include a requirement for funds with "illiquid assets" (e.g., real estate, infrastructure, private credit) to hold a minimum of 30% of their portfolio in liquid assets, according to the European Commission’s 2023 legislative proposal. Member states must transpose the directive into national law by July 2026.

## Why was it introduced?

The AIFMD review was driven by two seismic financial events that exposed critical gaps in the 2011 regime: the sudden collapse of Greensill Capital in March 2021 and the liquidity crisis in UK pension funds triggered by the September 2022 "mini-budget" chaos. Both incidents revealed that alternative funds, often employing leverage and holding illiquid assets, lacked mandated tools to handle redemption runs — and that regulators had weak powers to police delegation arrangements used to shift risk offshore.

Greensill Capital, a UK-based supply chain finance firm backed by Credit Suisse and SoftBank, folded after a credit rating downgrade triggered a run by investors. The firm was structured as a "shadow bank," mixing lending with fund management, and its assets were opaque. European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) chair Steven Maijoor told the European Parliament in April 2021 that Greensill "highlighted vulnerabilities in the regulation of non-bank financial intermediation." The fall left thousands of jobs and €10 billion in investor funds at risk, per a UK Treasury inquiry. The fact that Greensill had no EU home regulator but marketed to EU investors exposed cross-border supervision gaps.

The second catalyst was the September 2022 UK gilt market crash, where liability-driven investment (LDI) funds — a type of alternative fund used by pension schemes — faced margin calls on leveraged derivatives, triggering fire sales of government bonds. The Bank of England was forced to intervene with a £65 billion emergency bond-buying package, warning of "a material risk to UK financial stability." The episode demonstrated that open-ended funds promising daily redemptions while holding illiquid assets (like swaps and long-dated bonds) could transmit liquidity shocks into core sovereign bond markets.

Commissioner Mairead McGuinness, responsible for financial services under the von der Leyen Commission, proposed the review in November 2021 as part of the Capital Markets Union package. In February 2023, ESMA published a report finding that 38% of EU alternative funds had "material exposure" to illiquid assets, yet only 22% of managers had pre-agreed LMTs in their fund rules — a gap the Commission targeted with mandatory tools.

The delegation rules were driven by a different fear: regulatory arbitrage. ESMA’s 2020 peer review of delegation arrangements found that 80% of EU alternative fund managers used third-party delegates for at least some functions, often in jurisdictions like the US or Switzerland. The 2011 AIFMD had allowed delegation as long as the manager retained "effective control," but national authorities lacked enforcement. In 2019, the German regulator BaFin fined a dozen fund managers for "letter-box entities" — managers with no real substance in the EU — netting fines totalling €2 million, according to BaFin’s annual report. The review closes this by requiring that at least two board members or full-time managers be based in the EU, and that ESMA maintains a public register of delegation arrangements.

The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, German MEP Markus Ferber (EPP), pushed for tougher rules after lobbying by European asset managers like Amundi and DWS, who complained that EU managers were undercut by non-EU firms with lighter regulation. Ferber told POLITICO in May 2023: "We don't want a race to the bottom where fund managers can just set up a letterbox in Luxembourg and manage everything from New York."

The final directive passed the Council with a qualified majority vote in February 2024. Opponents included Luxembourg, Ireland, and the Netherlands — jurisdictions hosting large fund hubs that feared losing business to London or New York. Finance Minister Yuriko Backes of Luxembourg warned in a March 2024 Council memo that the delegation rules risked "disproportionate compliance costs for smaller managers." A compromise allowed delegation to continue but required annual reporting and ESMA oversight. The Parliament voted 562 to 49 in favour in March 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The review directly affects an estimated 1,200 alternative fund managers in the EU, managing over €12 trillion in assets, according to ESMA’s 2023 statistical report. The liquidity rules impact the largest segment: open-ended real estate funds, which accounted for 25% of EU alternative fund assets as of 2022 (ESMA data). In Germany, for instance, the open-ended real estate sector (€230 billion AUM) had experienced three fund suspensions between 2020 and 2022 due to redemption runs — the review now mandates that such funds must hold minimum liquid buffers.

The delegation rules hit the largest fund hubs hardest: Luxembourg, which hosts 60% of cross-border AIFMs by assets (€5.7 trillion), and Ireland (€3.2 trillion). ESMA provided in its 2024 opinion a warning that 15% of all EU-based fund managers had "significant delegation" — defined as over 50% of portfolio management outsourced — primarily to the US, Switzerland, and the UK. A 2024 study by the law firm Allen & Overy estimated that smaller managers (under €500 million) would incur compliance costs of €50,000-100,000 per fund for restructuring delegation arrangements.

European industry association Invest Europe criticised the delegation rules as "protectionist" in its January 2024 position paper, warning they could reduce access to global expertise and increase costs for EU pension investors. Conversely, France and Germany — whose fund industries compete with Luxembourg — backed the rules.

Enforcement: The directive does not set financial penalties but requires national regulators to impose "proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions. ESMA will coordinate a peer review of delegation compliance by 2028. Market participants expect operational concentration in London and New York to shift back to EU hubs, with the Irish regulator (Central Bank of Ireland) already staffing up its delegation oversight team.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU rewrite rules for hedge funds and private equity funds after 2022?

The rewrite was triggered by two crises: the 2021 Greensill Capital collapse, which showed that funds lending to illiquid assets could freeze redemptions quickly, and the 2022 UK pension fund liquidity crisis, where funds holding long-dated bonds faced margin calls that threatened broader markets. EU rules had not required fund managers to pre-plan for such runs. The review mandates that open-ended illiquid funds hold minimum liquid buffers (30%) and have legal tools — like redemption gates — ready to use before a crisis, not after.

### Can an EU-based fund manager still outsource portfolio management to a firm outside the EU?

Yes, but with stricter conditions. The 2024 AIFMD review requires that the fund manager retain "substance" in the EU: at least two board members or full-time senior managers based in the EU, and prior notification to the regulator if portfolio management delegation exceeds 50% of assets. Regulators must assess whether the arrangement is a "letter-box entity" designed to escape EU rules. ESMA will maintain a public register of all delegation deals. The goal is to prevent regulatory arbitrage where EU firms move management to New York or London while maintaining a low-tax EU headquarters.

### What happens if an alternative fund in the EU fails to meet new liquidity rules?

The fund faces suspension of redemptions by its regulator if liquidity falls below mandatory levels (e.g., a 30% liquid asset threshold for open-ended illiquid funds). Managers must also have a pre-approved plan ("dealing with liquidity stress") filed with regulators. In practice, a fund that repeatedly breaches liquidity rules could face forced wind-down or revocation of its AIFM license. The directive gives ESMA authority to coordinate cross-border enforcement across member states.

### Why did Luxembourg and Ireland oppose parts of the new rules?

Luxembourg and Ireland are the two largest EU hubs for alternative funds, together hosting over €8 trillion in assets, because of their favourable tax regimes and light-touch regulation. The delegation rules threaten their business model: many funds registered in Luxembourg are actually managed from London or the US. Both governments argued in Council negotiations that the rules would drive fund management jobs outside the EU. They secured a compromise allowing delegation to continue but with more oversight — a less stringent option than the outright ban France and Germany had proposed.
