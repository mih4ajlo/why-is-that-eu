## What is it?

The Financial Conglomerates Directive (FCD) is an EU law requiring that mixed financial groups — those combining banking, insurance, and investment services under common ownership or control — be supervised not just by individual sector regulators but by a single "coordinator" responsible for assessing group-wide risks. It aims to prevent regulatory arbitrage where a conglomerate exploits gaps between banking and insurance rules to hide debt, concentrate risk, or move capital between subsidiaries without adequate oversight.

The directive applies to groups whose combined activities exceed certain thresholds — typically total assets over €6 billion, with cross-sector activities representing at least 10% of total business. It mandates risk concentration reporting, intra-group transaction disclosure, and "solo-plus" capital adequacy tests that consider the entire group's exposure, not just each legal entity in isolation.

## Why was it introduced?

The direct trigger was the 1990s collapse of BCCI (Bank of Credit and Commerce International) and the 1991 failure of the Maxwell Group in the UK, which together exposed a fatal flaw in European financial supervision: no regulator had full sight of a conglomerate's total risk. BCCI had operated through a web of banks, investment houses, and insurance subsidiaries across Luxembourg, the UK, and the Cayman Islands, each supervised by a separate national authority. When fraud surfaced, the Bank of England and Luxembourg's banking commission blamed each other, while the conglomerate's insurance arm in the Caymans had no regulator at all. The Maxwell case similarly showed that a single holding company could transfer assets between its banking, insurance, and pension-fund units without any agency tracking the overall exposure.

The European Commission first attempted to address this in 1993 with a Communication on financial conglomerates, but industry opposition — led by Allianz, Axa, and Deutsche Bank — blocked legislation. These groups argued that imposing combined capital requirements would force them to hold more capital than the sum of sector-specific rules, reducing returns on equity. The German Insurance Association (GDV) and the European Banking Federation (EBF) jointly warned in 1997 that the proposal would "damage the competitiveness of European financial groups against US and Japanese rivals."

The deadlock broke in 1999 with the publication of the Lamfalussy Report, which recommended overhauling EU financial regulation to match the scale of integrated European capital markets. Then-Internal Market Commissioner Frits Bolkestein, a Dutch liberal, took up the cause, framing the issue as a systemic risk problem after the 1998 collapse of Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) in the US demonstrated that even non-bank financial entities could threaten the entire system. The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee pushed for stronger powers, with German MEP and rapporteur Othmar Karas insisting on mandatory capital add-ons for cross-sector groups.

The final compromise — reached after three years of negotiation — created a "supplementary supervision" model rather than full consolidation. This was a concession to the insurance industry, which successfully argued that insurance liabilities are long-term and actuarially predictable, unlike bank deposits subject to runs. The directive passed with 388 votes in favour, 139 against, largely opposed by British Conservatives and German Christian Democrats who wanted lighter oversight for insurance-dominated groups.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Collapse of BCCI (Bank of Credit and Commerce International) reveals cross-sector supervision gap; Bank of England cannot access insurance subsidiary data
- **1993** — European Commission issues first Communication on financial conglomerates; no legislative action due to industry opposition
- **1997** — Parliament calls for binding legislation in a resolution; Commission drafts initial proposal stalled by German and French industry lobbying
- **1999** — Lamfalussy Report recommends integrated EU financial supervision; LTCM crisis provides new systemic risk evidence
- **2001-04-26** — Commission formally proposes the Financial Conglomerates Directive (COM(2001) 106)
- **2002-12-16** — European Parliament adopts directive with 388-139 vote
- **2005-02-11** — Directive entered into force; member states had until 2007-02-11 to transpose into national law
- **2008** — Financial crisis exposes remaining gaps; many conglomerates (e.g., Fortis, Dexia) had complied with FCD but still failed due to liquidity risks not covered by the directive
- **2011** — Commission tables revisions (Omnibus II) to align FCD with new Solvency II and CRD IV rules

## Impact and consequences

The directive immediately affected approximately 50 financial conglomerates identified by the Commission in 2005 as meeting the threshold criteria — groups like Allianz-Dresdner Bank, ING Group, Fortis, and Crédit Agricole. National regulators struggled with implementation: the Bank of Spain and Bundesbank lacked experience with insurance supervision, while Italy's Banca d'Italia had to create new inter-agency protocols with insurance regulator ISVAP.

A 2009 Commission review found that most groups had reduced intra-group transactions by 30-40% in response to disclosure requirements, but that risk concentration remained underreported by 40% of conglomerates. The review also noted that the "coordinator" model created confusion — in cross-border groups like Fortis, Dutch and Belgian regulators disputed who held lead authority, contributing to the failure to prevent Fortis's 2008 collapse.

No fines were directly issued under the FCD, but its gaps became painfully clear when the 2008 crisis hit. Fortis, which held €871 billion in assets across banking and insurance, had met FCD capital requirements but failed because its bank unit could not roll over short-term funding — a liquidity risk the directive did not cover. The crisis led to the post-2010 revision that added liquidity and intra-group exposure limits.

## Key questions answered

### Does Europe have a single rulebook for financial groups that mix banking and insurance?

Yes, the Financial Conglomerates Directive requires these mixed groups to be supervised by a single coordinator who monitors group-wide risks, capital adequacy, and internal transactions. But it supplements, not replaces, individual banking and insurance rules — so a bank owned by an insurer still follows separate banking directives for its lending business.

### Why did European regulators miss the warning signs before the 2008 financial crisis?

The FCD focused on capital adequacy at the group level but ignored liquidity risk — the risk that a short-term funding freeze could collapse a conglomerate even if its capital ratios looked solid. Fortis's 2008 failure showed that a group could pass the FCD's capital tests while its commercial paper market evaporated; the directive was later amended to address this.

### What happens when a conglomerate operates in multiple EU countries under this law?

National regulators must designate a single "coordinator" among themselves — typically the regulator of the country where the parent group is headquartered. This coordinator has the power to request data from all subsidiaries and discuss risks with other regulators, but cannot override other countries' banking or insurance supervisors. The 2008 crisis showed this arrangement was fragile when cross-border groups like Dexia collapsed because Belgian and French regulators disagreed on rescue terms.

### Do smaller financial groups with both banking and insurance activities face the same rules?

Generally no — the directive applies only to groups exceeding a size threshold (total assets over €6 billion with cross-sector business over 10%). Smaller mixed groups are supervised under existing sector-specific rules unless a national regulator decides to apply the directive voluntarily to capture emerging systemic risks.
