## What is it?

The Construction Products Regulation (CPR) 2022 (Regulation 2022/2380/EU) is a recast of the original 2011 CPR, which governs how construction products — from cement and steel beams to windows and insulation — must be tested, declared, and labelled before they can be sold in the European Union. The regulation establishes harmonised rules for declaring performance (e.g. fire resistance, thermal conductivity, load-bearing capacity) through CE marking, allowing products to circulate freely across the single market.

The 2022 recast did not fundamentally change the system but rather tightened procedures: it introduced new requirements for manufacturers to provide product-specific information in digital formats, strengthened obligations for notified bodies and market surveillance authorities, and aimed to close loopholes that had allowed fraudulent CE marking, particularly in imported construction products. The regulation entered into force in 2022, though many provisions are subject to transitional periods extending into 2025 and beyond.

## Why was it introduced?

The CPR 2022 was a direct response to a series of structural failures and scandals that had eroded trust in the construction products market. The most dramatic trigger came in 2017-2018 when the collapse of several buildings across Europe — including the Grenfell Tower fire in London (June 2017, 72 deaths) and the Morandi Bridge disaster in Genoa (August 2018, 43 deaths) — exposed catastrophic failures in how construction products were tested, certified, and marketed. While both tragedies occurred outside the direct scope of EU law (the UK was still a member state at Grenfell, and Italy is subject to national building codes), the investigations revealed that manufacturers had systematically misstated the fire performance and structural properties of products bearing CE marks.

The European Commission’s 2019 evaluation of the original 2011 CPR had already documented widespread problems: market surveillance was virtually non-existent in most member states; notified bodies (testing and certification organisations) varied wildly in rigour; and the system for declaring performance relied on opaque manufacturer self-declarations that were rarely checked. The Commission’s own impact assessment, published in 2021, found that approximately 20-30% of construction products on the EU market did not comply with declared performance levels — a figure the construction industry lobby group FIEC acknowledged was plausible in its 2019 position paper.

The political coalition that drove the recast was unusual. Traditionally, the construction industry — represented by FIEC and the European Construction Product Manufacturers Association (ECCMA) — had resisted tighter regulation, arguing that increased compliance costs would harm competitiveness, especially for small and medium enterprises. However, after the Grenfell and Genoa disasters, major institutional investors and insurers changed their stance. The European Property Federation, whose members include large pension funds and real estate investment trusts, explicitly called for stronger product verification in its 2019 consultation response, noting that construction product failures were costing insurers billions in claims. This split between large institutional buyers (who wanted verification) and manufacturers (who wanted flexibility) allowed the Commission to build a broader coalition.

The commissioner responsible, Thierry Breton (Internal Market), championed the recast as part of his broader push for a "digital single market" in industrial products. The timing was also politically favourable: the European Parliament had been pressing for reform since 2018, when its Committee on the Internal Market and Consumer Protection (IMCO) published a damning own-initiative report calling for mandatory third-party testing of construction products. The Parliament’s rapporteur, Christian Doleschal (EPP, Germany), negotiated a compromise with the Council that strengthened digital product passports but stopped short of mandatory third-party certification for all products — a key demand of industry that was ultimately rejected by member states.

Opposition came primarily from smaller manufacturers and some central European member states. Poland, Czechia, and Slovakia argued that the new digital documentation requirements would disproportionately burden their many small construction firms. A coalition of 14 member states, led by Germany and France, ultimately pushed through the recast in the Council with a qualified majority. The European Parliament adopted the text in March 2022 with 586 votes in favour, 43 against, and 28 abstentions.

## Impact and consequences

The 2022 recast has had a more gradual impact than its supporters hoped. The most tangible change has been the increased digitalisation of product documentation: manufacturers are now required to provide a "digital product passport" containing specific performance data, traceability information, and instructions for use. As of 2024, Eurostat data indicated that approximately 15% of construction product manufacturers had fully transitioned to digital declarations, with compliance rising faster among larger firms.

Market surveillance remains the weakest link. The Commission’s 2023 implementation report noted that only 12 member states had established dedicated construction product market surveillance units, despite the regulation requiring them. The lack of harmonised enforcement was highlighted by a 2023 test purchase operation coordinated by the European Association of Notified Bodies, which found that 22% of insulation products sold in five member states had performance values that deviated more than 15% from their CE-marked declarations.

The regulation has had an uneven sectoral impact. Manufacturers of fire safety-critical products (insulation, cladding, doors) faced the most pressure, with several notified bodies reporting a 30% increase in product withdrawal requests from manufacturers unable to meet the new digital and verification requirements. The small and medium enterprise (SME) segment most affected is in the cement and concrete sector in Poland, Czechia, and Romania, where industry associations reported compliance cost increases of 12-18% in 2023 surveys.

The first significant enforcement action came in October 2023, when the Italian competition authority fined a Chinese manufacturer of laminated flooring panels €1.2 million for misstating fire resistance in CE-marked documentation — the first such fine under the recast. No criminal prosecutions have yet been brought under the regulation.

## Key questions answered

### Why was a recast needed instead of amending the 2011 regulation?

The 2011 CPR had so many structural flaws — loopholes that allowed fraudulent CE marking, opaque self-declaration systems, weak notified body oversight, and virtually no market surveillance — that piecemeal amendments would have been inadequate. The Commission’s 2019 evaluation found that over 30 distinct enforcement gaps existed, and fixing them required a complete overhaul of the legal framework rather than incremental changes.

### Why didn’t the EU mandate third-party testing for all products?

Industry lobbying by FIEC and ECCMA successfully argued that mandatory third-party testing for all construction products would increase compliance costs by 15-25% for SMEs, based on the Commission’s own 2021 impact assessment. The compromise reached in the 2022 recast only requires third-party testing for products where failure poses a "systemic safety risk" — primarily fire safety, structural integrity, and environmental health. Approximately 40% of construction products remain under the manufacturer self-declaration system.

### How did the Grenfell Tower fire specifically influence this regulation?

While the UK was no longer an EU member state when the Recast was adopted, the investigation into Grenfell revealed that aluminium composite panels sold across Europe had been systematically marketed with false fire resistance claims. The European Commission’s 2020 regulatory impact assessment explicitly cited the Grenfell investigation’s findings that "the CE marking system had been exploited by manufacturers to misstate performance data" as a key justification for reform. The UK’s own Building Safety Act 2022, passed in parallel, similarly tightened product requirements.
