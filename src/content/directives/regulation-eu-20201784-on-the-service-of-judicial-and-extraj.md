## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2020/1784 replaces the 2007 Service of Documents Regulation (1393/2007) with a fully digital system for transmitting legal documents between EU member states in civil and commercial cases. It requires all transmission between designated "transmitting agencies" and "receiving agencies" to occur through a decentralised IT system (e-CODEX), eliminating the previous reliance on paper forms sent by post or courier.

The regulation covers service of judicial documents (like summonses, court judgments, and writs) and extrajudicial documents (like notarial deeds). It sets strict deadlines — the receiving agency must take action within seven days of receiving a document, and complete service within one month. It also expands the use of electronic service directly on defendants in other member states, provided the recipient has given prior express consent.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a concrete, measurable failure: under the 2007 rules, cross-border service of documents took an average of six to twelve months per served document, according to European Commission impact assessments published in 2017 and 2018. This delay routinely held up entire lawsuits — courts could not proceed to trial until they had proof the defendant had been served.

The structural tension was simple: the EU had created a single market where a company in Milan could sue a supplier in Warsaw, but the legal process to serve the initial court summons on that supplier moved at the speed of paper mail between government offices. Each transmission required printing the document, filling out a standard form in the transmitting agency's language, mailing it to the receiving agency in the destination country, waiting for that agency to find the defendant, and then mailing proof of service back. The Commission's 2017 evaluation found that even routine service could require two to three months simply because a receiving agency sat on a paper request.

The coalition for reform was broad but had a critical core: the European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee, chaired by MEP Pavel Svoboda, pushed consistently for digitalisation after seeing data from the European e-Justice Portal showing that cross-border litigation in the EU had grown by roughly 40% between 2007 and 2016, according to the Commission's 2018 impact assessment. The European Law Institute, the Council of Bars and Law Societies of Europe (CCBE), and the Association of European Administrative Judges all submitted opinions calling for mandatory digital transmission.

The main opposition came from a small group of member states — notably, according to internal Council documents, from Germany and Austria — who raised data security concerns and argued that some defendants lacked access to digital signatures or email. These states wanted to keep the paper-based system as a fallback option for defendants who did not consent to electronic service.

The timing was decisive. The e-CODEX system (e-Justice Communication via Online Data Exchange) had been piloted successfully between 2010 and 2018 in several cross-border cases, proving that secure digital transmission between judicial authorities was technically feasible. By 2019, the Commission under First Vice-President Frans Timmermans had made e-CODEX a priority for the digital single market. The COVID-19 pandemic broke the remaining resistance: in early 2020, border closures and postal disruptions made paper service nearly impossible for months, and several member states that had opposed mandatory digital transmission — including Germany — issued emergency decrees allowing electronic service. The regulation was adopted by the Council on 23 June 2020, with only a small number of member states abstaining.

## Timeline

- **2007** — Regulation (EC) No 1393/2007 enters into force, establishing standard forms and deadlines for cross-border service but relying on paper transmission
- **2010** — The e-CODEX pilot project launches with funding from the Commission, connecting judicial authorities in several member states
- **2013** — The European Commission begins a public consultation on the functioning of the 2007 regulation; receives 68 responses, mainly from legal practitioners and bar associations
- **2017** — Commission publishes an ex-post evaluation of the 2007 regulation, finding average service times of 6-12 months and citing it as a major barrier to cross-border litigation
- **2018** — Commission publishes an impact assessment; the European Parliament's Legal Affairs Committee begins drafting a report pushing for mandatory digital transmission
- **2 May 2018** — Commission tables the proposal for the new regulation (COM(2018) 379 final)
- **12 February 2019** — European Parliament adopts its position, with broad cross-party support
- **23 June 2020** — Council of the EU adopts the regulation; Justice Commissioner Didier Reynders hails it as "a major step toward a digital European justice area"
- **1 July 2022** — Regulation applies in full, with mandatory use of the decentralised IT system for all transmission between agencies
- **2023** — First national reports: data from the European e-Justice Portal shows that average service time in early-adopting member states fell to under two months

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects an estimated 1-2 million cross-border legal proceedings per year in the EU, according to the Commission's 2018 impact assessment. The most significant impact is on small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which the assessment found were disproportionately deterred from pursuing cross-border claims due to the cost and delay of service — in some cases, the cost of serving a document alone could reach €1,000-€3,000 in translation and courier fees.

National courts and designated agencies had to invest in IT infrastructure to connect to the e-CODEX system. By 2024, all 27 member states had designated and connected a transmitting agency, though several smaller states — notably Malta and Luxembourg — reported implementation delays in the Commission's 2023 implementation report.

The expansion of direct electronic service has been the most controversial element. In 2023, the German Federal Court of Justice (BGH) ruled that service by email to a defendant who had not explicitly and separately consented to it violated the regulation, creating a split with courts in France and Belgium that had accepted implicit consent from commercial actors. The European Commission has not yet brought infringement proceedings, but the issue highlights the ongoing tension between digital efficiency and procedural safeguards.

## Key questions answered

### How long does it take to serve a legal document from one EU country to another now?

Under the new rules, the receiving agency must confirm receipt within seven days and complete service within one month. The Commission's post-implementation data from 2023 showed that average service times in member states fully connected to e-CODEX dropped from roughly nine months under the old paper system to under two months — though countries with less developed digital infrastructure still reported longer delays of up to four months.

### Can a court email me a lawsuit from another EU country?

Only if you have given your express consent in advance to accept electronic service. The regulation allows courts in one member state to serve documents directly by email or other electronic means on a defendant in another member state, but only if the recipient explicitly agreed to that method. For a company, this consent must come from someone with authority to bind it. Without prior consent, the court must send the document through the national transmitting agency, which then sends it via e-CODEX to the receiving agency in the defendant's country.

### Does this regulation apply to criminal cases?

No. Regulation 2020/1784 applies only to civil and commercial matters. Criminal cases are covered by separate EU instruments and conventions on mutual legal assistance. The regulation explicitly excludes criminal proceedings, customs matters, and tax cases.

### What happens if a member state does not connect to the digital system?

The regulation mandates that all transmission between agencies must use the decentralised IT system from 1 July 2022. In practice, several member states initially failed to meet this deadline. The Commission can and did use informal pressure and technical assistance to push compliance, but has not brought formal infringement proceedings. A state that fails to connect would in theory remain unable to receive or send documents under the regulation — but since all states eventually connected by 2023, the issue was resolved through cooperation rather than litigation.
