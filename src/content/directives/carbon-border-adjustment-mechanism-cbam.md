## What is it?

The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism is an EU import levy that puts a carbon price on goods entering the single market, matching the price EU producers pay under the Emissions Trading System (ETS). Importers of cement, iron and steel, aluminium, fertilisers, hydrogen and electricity must declare the embedded emissions in their products and surrender CBAM certificates at the weekly average ETS auction price.

CBAM works in tandem with the phase-out of free ETS allowances for European industry: as those free permits disappear between 2026 and 2034, the CBAM levy ramps up correspondingly. Non-EU producers can deduct any carbon price already paid in their home country, meaning a Chinese steelmaker who paid into the Chinese ETS pays less at the EU border.

A transitional phase began on 1 October 2023, requiring quarterly emissions reporting but no financial payments. The full financial regime applies from 1 January 2026.

## Why was it introduced?

The political trigger was a numerical contradiction at the heart of the European Green Deal. When Frans Timmermans, then Executive Vice-President for the Green Deal, proposed raising the EU's 2030 emissions target from 40% to 55% in September 2020, European steel and cement producers — led by Eurofer and Cembureau — warned that tougher carbon prices would simply push production to Turkey, Russia, China and North Africa. ArcelorMittal had already announced it was considering shifting investment outside Europe, and Lafarge-Holcim had been lobbying Brussels for years about Moroccan cement imports undercutting French plants exempt from carbon costs.

The "carbon leakage" problem was not new — the ETS had compensated heavy industry with billions of euros worth of free allowances since 2005, precisely to prevent this — but those free allowances had become politically toxic. A 2018 European Court of Auditors report found that free allocation was poorly targeted and that companies including the steel sector had earned windfall profits estimated by CE Delft and others in the billions. Climate NGOs including Carbon Market Watch and Sandbag had documented these windfalls for a decade.

Ursula von der Leyen made CBAM a centrepiece of her Commission from day one, listing it in her July 2019 political guidelines. The proposal landed on 14 July 2021 as part of the "Fit for 55" package, drafted under Commissioner Paolo Gentiloni's tax portfolio. The negotiations were brutal: France, championed by Bruno Le Maire, pushed hard; Germany worried about retaliation against its exporters; Poland and Bulgaria feared damage to imports from neighbours. The European Parliament's rapporteur Mohammed Chahim (S&D, Netherlands) successfully pushed to expand the scope to hydrogen and to accelerate the phase-out of free allowances.

The United States, Russia, China, India, Brazil and South Africa all formally objected at the WTO. Russia's deputy PM Andrei Belousov in 2021 estimated CBAM could cost Russian exporters €1.1 billion per year — a moot point after the 2022 invasion of Ukraine ended Russian steel exports to the EU anyway. The trilogue deal was struck on 13 December 2022.

## Timeline

- **2005** — EU ETS launches with free allowances to prevent carbon leakage
- **2018** — European Court of Auditors criticises free ETS allocation as poorly targeted
- **December 2019** — Von der Leyen Commission announces European Green Deal, commits to CBAM
- **March 2021** — European Parliament adopts own-initiative report by Yannick Jadot calling for WTO-compatible CBAM
- **14 July 2021** — Commission publishes CBAM proposal as part of "Fit for 55" package
- **15 March 2022** — Council reaches general approach
- **22 June 2022** — Parliament adopts position after a chaotic first vote two weeks earlier was rejected
- **13 December 2022** — Trilogue political agreement
- **10 May 2023** — Regulation (EU) 2023/956 signed
- **1 October 2023** — Transitional phase begins; first quarterly reports due January 2024
- **1 January 2026** — Financial obligations begin; free ETS allowances start phasing out
- **2026** — Commission to review extending CBAM to downstream goods and indirect emissions
- **2034** — Free ETS allowances fully phased out; CBAM at 100%

## Impact and consequences

In the first reporting quarter (Q4 2023), the Commission and member state authorities received reports from over 10,000 EU importers, though completeness was patchy — many filings used default values rather than actual supplier data, and the Commission delayed enforcement penalties. The German customs authority reported in early 2024 that a majority of submissions had quality issues.

Trading partners reacted sharply. India announced it would challenge CBAM at the WTO and accelerate its own carbon market. Turkey, whose steel and cement exports to the EU exceeded €5 billion annually, launched its own ETS partly to claw back revenue. The UK announced its own CBAM for 2027. The US under Biden flirted with a "Climate Club" alternative but never legislated; the returning Trump administration in 2025 treated CBAM as a trade barrier.

Within the EU, the simplification debate gathered force. In February 2025, the Commission's "Omnibus" package proposed exempting around 90% of small importers — those bringing in less than 50 tonnes per year — covering an estimated 99% of emissions while removing administrative burden from roughly 180,000 small operators. Eurofer and Cembureau welcomed CBAM but kept lobbying for export rebates, arguing CBAM does nothing for European exporters competing in third markets — a question the Commission has promised to address by 2026.

LDCs were largely exempted from concern; the bigger problem is middle-income exporters like Mozambique (aluminium) and Serbia (steel) where CBAM-covered exports represent a large share of trade with the EU.

## Key questions answered

### Why does CBAM only cover six sectors initially?

The Commission picked sectors that were both emissions-intensive and trade-exposed, where carbon leakage risk was highest and emissions were measurable: cement, iron and steel, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity and hydrogen. Including chemicals or refined products would have made the system unmanageably complex at launch given the difficulty of attributing emissions to specific molecules in integrated petrochemical sites. A 2026 review will consider extension downstream.

### Why is CBAM compatible with WTO rules when other border taxes have been struck down?

The legal architecture mirrors the ETS: importers pay the same effective carbon price as EU producers, and any carbon price already paid abroad is deducted. This positions CBAM as a non-discriminatory internal measure under GATT Article III rather than a border tariff, and the General Exceptions in Article XX provide environmental cover. Whether a WTO panel agrees has not yet been tested — India and others have signalled they will file complaints once financial obligations bite in 2026.

### Why did the EU phase out free ETS allowances at the same time?

Free allowances and CBAM are two solutions to the same problem — preventing leakage — and running both simultaneously would be double protection that the WTO would never accept. The 2022 trilogue settled on a parallel ramp: as CBAM rises from 2.5% in 2026 to 100% in 2034, free allocation falls from 97.5% to zero. Eurofer fought to keep free allowances longer; Parliament's Chahim won a faster phase-out.

### Why was electricity included given that it cannot easily be traced to a specific power plant?

Electricity imports from neighbouring grids — particularly Western Balkans coal power flowing into Italy, Greece and Croatia, and Ukrainian and Moldovan electricity — were a specific carbon leakage channel that pre-existed CBAM debates. The Energy Community Treaty had failed to harmonise carbon pricing in the Western Balkans, so CBAM provides the lever instead. Default emissions values are used since point-of-origin tracing is impossible.
