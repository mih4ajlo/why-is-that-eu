## What is it?

The Nature Restoration Regulation is the European Union's first legally binding framework requiring member states to restore degraded ecosystems, not just protect remaining ones. It sets numerical targets for restoring specific habitats — wetlands, forests, grasslands, rivers, marine habitats, and urban green spaces — across at least 20% of the EU's land and sea areas by 2030, and all ecosystems in need of restoration by 2050.

The regulation imposes concrete obligations: by 2030, member states must reverse the decline of pollinators, ensure no net loss of urban green space, remove barriers from 25,000 km of rivers to restore free-flowing water, and improve biodiversity on agricultural land (including rewetting drained peatlands). Each member state must submit a National Restoration Plan to the European Commission detailing how it will meet these targets, with progress reviews every three years. Non-compliance can trigger infringement proceedings.

## Why was it introduced?

In May 2020, the European Environment Agency published its "State of Nature in the EU" report, which found that 81% of protected habitats were in poor condition and only 15% were adequately conserved. The report's author, EEA director Hans Bruyninckx, described the situation as "systemic failure" — decades of EU nature directives (the Birds and Habitats Directives) had slowed decline but had not reversed it. The EU's own 2020 Biodiversity Strategy admitted that "we are not on track to meet the 2020 targets."

The structural tension was clear: the EU had world-class protection rules for Natura 2000 sites (covering 18% of land and 8% of marine areas), but the ecosystems *outside* those sites — agricultural land, forests, urban areas — continued to degrade. The European Commission's 2021 impact assessment for the regulation cited data from the Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES) showing that 75% of the EU's terrestrial environment was "seriously degraded," and the European Environment Agency calculated that the economic cost of losing ecosystem services in the EU was €100-200 billion per year.

The coalition that pushed for binding restoration targets was unusual. Ursula von der Leyen made the European Green Deal her Commission's flagship project in December 2019, and the EU's 2030 Biodiversity Strategy (published May 2020) initially promised only "legally binding restoration targets" by 2021 — but with no specifics. The push for *numerical* targets came from the European Parliament's Environment Committee, chaired by Pascal Canfin (Renew, France), and from a cross-party group of MEPs including César Luena (S&D, Spain) and Jutta Paulus (Greens/EFA, Germany). They argued that targets without numbers were meaningless — the EU's 2011 Biodiversity Strategy had set 20 restoration targets for 2020, and not one had been fully met.

The fiercest opposition came from three quarters. The European People's Party (EPP) — the largest group in Parliament — led the charge, arguing that binding restoration targets would hurt farmers and landowners. In June 2023, EPP leader Manfred Weber wrote to Commission President von der Leyen demanding the regulation be withdrawn entirely, calling it "unrealistic and dangerous for food security." The far-right Identity and Democracy group and European Conservatives and Reformists joined the opposition. More surprisingly, the European Council of Young Farmers (CEJA) and Copa-Cogeca (the main farm lobby) warned that rewetting peatlands would reduce agricultural output by 30% in some regions, though the Commission's impact assessment estimated the total impact on EU agricultural production at 1-2%.

The timing turned on three factors. First, the Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 — the EPP argued that restricting agricultural land threatened food security during a war. Second, the European Court of Auditors published a special report in June 2023 finding that "EU biodiversity policy has failed to prevent the loss of biodiversity," giving ammunition to both sides. Third, a revolt within the EPP itself: 14 centre-right governments in the Council (including Germany's coalition of SPD, Greens, and FDP) supported binding targets, meaning the EPP's opposition in Parliament did not translate to a blocking minority in the Council.

The decisive moment came on July 12, 2023, when the European Parliament adopted its position by a margin of just 336 to 300 (with 13 abstentions) — the narrowest vote on any major environmental law in the 2019-2024 parliamentary term. The EPP had tried to kill the bill entirely, but failed. In the trilogue negotiations that followed, the original target of restoring 30% of degraded habitats by 2030 was watered down to 20%, and provisions on rewetting peatlands for agriculture were weakened — farmers could delay compliance until 2035 under certain conditions. The final regulation was adopted by the Council of the EU on June 17, 2024, with 20 member states in favour — just enough to avoid a blocking minority.

- **August 18, 2024** — Regulation enters into force 20 days after publication in the Official Journal
- **By 2026** — Member states must submit first National Restoration Plans to the European Commission

## Impact and consequences

The regulation immediately faced implementation challenges. Poland, Italy, and Finland voted against the final text in Council and signalled they would resist enforcement. The European Commission's own 2023 assessment found that member states would collectively need to spend €100-150 billion on restoration measures by 2050 — but the new EU Multiannual Financial Framework (2021-2027) allocated only €5 billion for biodiversity. Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius acknowledged in a March 2024 interview that "the money is not there yet."

For industry, the biggest impact falls on agriculture and forestry. The obligation to rewet drained peatlands — which cover 8% of EU agricultural land but produce 25% of agricultural greenhouse gas emissions directly — threatens farming models in the Netherlands, Germany, and Ireland. Dutch farmers' groups reacted angrily after the regulation passed; Agriculture Minister Piet Adema warned that "20% of Dutch dairy production could be at risk." On the other hand, the regulation's urban greening targets (no net loss of tree canopy cover and green space by 2030) were fairly uncontroversial across cities.

The regulation's enforcement mechanism is weaker than initially proposed: the Commission can launch infringement proceedings for non-compliance, but only if a member state fails to submit a National Restoration Plan — not for missing the actual restoration targets. This was a deliberate compromise to overcome opposition in Council. Environmental groups including WWF and ClientEarth criticised this as a "toothless" loophole. The Commission's own legal service warned in a 2022 opinion that without meaningful enforcement, the regulation would "repeat the failure of the 2011 Biodiversity Strategy."

## Key questions answered

### Does every EU country now have to restore its nature?

Yes, but with significant flexibility. Each member state must submit a National Restoration Plan by 2026 showing how it will meet EU-wide targets for specific habitats — wetlands, forests, rivers, urban green space, and pollinators. But the regulation does not set uniform per-country targets for every habitat type; countries can negotiate which ecosystems to prioritise, as long as the EU collectively meets the 20% by 2030 aim. The Commission reviews plans and can request revisions, but cannot unilaterally impose a plan on a refusing member state.

### Can farmers be forced to rewet their land under this law?

Only indirectly, and not immediately. The regulation requires member states to restore drained peatlands (which are often agricultural land), but the most controversial provisions — requiring restoration on 50% of peatland area under agricultural use — were delayed in the final text. Countries must begin restoring 30% of organic soils by 2030, but the agricultural use provision applies only from 2035, and only on sites where restoration is "technically feasible without significantly impacting food production." Some countries (the Netherlands, Ireland, Germany) may still need to impose restrictions on farming drained peatlands, but the law leaves that decision to national governments, not Brussels.

### Why did this law almost fail in the European Parliament?

Because the centre-right European People's Party (EPP) — the largest group — opposed it from the start, arguing it would hurt farmers and landowners. The EPP tried to kill the bill entirely in a July 2023 vote, but failed by just 36 votes (336-300). The pro-restoration coalition consisted of Socialists & Democrats (S&D), the Greens/EFA, Renew Europe, and The Left — plus about 50 defectors from the EPP who broke party lines. Without those defectors — many from German and French delegations — the law would not have passed. The narrow vote forced the Commission to accept weaker targets in trilogue.

### What happens if a country simply ignores its restoration obligations?

The Commission can launch infringement proceedings at the Court of Justice of the EU, but only if the member state fails to *submit* a National Restoration Plan — not for missing the actual restoration targets. This was a deliberate compromise to get the law passed. If the Court finds a country in violation of its obligation to submit a plan, it can impose fines. But if a country submits a weak plan and then fails to meet it, the Commission's legal options are very limited — it must prove the country "manifestly failed" to implement its own plan, which is a much higher legal bar. Environmental lawyers have warned this enforcement gap could make the regulation toothless.
