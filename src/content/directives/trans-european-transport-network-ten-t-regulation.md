## What is it?

The TEN-T Regulation (1315/2013/EU) is the legal backbone of the European Union's effort to build a single, seamless transport network. It creates two layers: a "comprehensive network" (all EU regions connected by 2050) and a "core network" (the most strategic routes and nodes, to be completed by 2030). The regulation sets binding technical standards—such as electrification of railways, minimum speeds for freight trains, and inland waterway compatibility—to ensure that a train, truck, or barge can cross internal borders without stopping for gauge changes, power system swaps, or paperwork.

It is not a funding program—the Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) provides that—but the master plan that determines which projects are eligible for EU money. The regulation also introduced the concept of "core network corridors" (nine, later revised), governed by European Coordinators who have the power to mediate disputes between member states and push projects forward.

## Why was it introduced?

The TEN-T Regulation was a direct response to a failure that the European Commission’s 2011 White Paper on Transport called "the persistence of national fragmentation." Despite decades of internal market legislation, Europe’s transport infrastructure remained a patchwork of national projects, built to national standards. A 2010 report by the European Court of Auditors found that only 12% of cross-border rail projects funded under the previous TEN-T guidelines had been completed on time.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had removed border checks and customs, but the physical infrastructure—tracks, tunnels, bridges, ports—still reflected the logic of 19th-century nation-states. A Polish freight train could not run to Spain because its braking systems were incompatible with German signaling, and the French electrification voltage stopped at the Belgian border. The cost of these "missing links" was estimated by the Commission’s 2011 impact assessment at €100 billion per year in lost economic output.

The coalition for action was broad but fragile. The European Parliament’s Transport Committee (TRAN), led by rapporteur Marian-Jean Marinescu (EPP, Romania), pushed for binding deadlines and a stronger coordinating role for the Commission. The European Rail Freight Association (ERFA) and the International Union of Railways (UIC) lobbied for mandatory technical harmonization, arguing that voluntary agreements had failed—only 6% of rail freight crossed international borders, according to Eurostat’s 2010 data.

Opposition came from two quarters. Member states with already well-developed networks (Germany, France, the Netherlands) resisted binding timelines that might force them to fund upgrades in other countries. In the Council, a blocking minority emerged in 2012 over the proposal to divert Cohesion Fund money to TEN-T projects outside poorer regions. The deadlock was broken by the European Council’s 2012 agreement on the Multiannual Financial Framework (2014-2020), which allocated €26.25 billion to the CEF, making it clear that TEN-T would get tangible funding.

The final text, adopted in December 2013, was a compromise: the 2030 deadline for the core network was maintained, but with escape clauses for member states that could show "economic inviability." The Parliament’s attempt to make rail interoperability mandatory on all core lines was weakened to "recommended" for existing infrastructure, though binding for new builds.

## Timeline

- **1993** — The Maastricht Treaty creates the first legal basis for a trans-European network, listing TEN in the new Title XII of the TEC.
- **1996** — First TEN-T guidelines adopted (Decision 1692/96/EC), identifying 14 priority projects, including the Channel Tunnel high-speed rail link.
- **2001** — The "Van Miert" High-Level Group, led by former Commissioner Karel Van Miert, recommends 30 specific priority projects and proposes a "project coordinator" model.
- **2004** — TEN-T budget doubled to €8 billion (2007-2013), but a Commission evaluation finds that only 7 of 30 priority projects are on schedule.
- **2011** — European Commission White Paper on Transport (presented by Commissioner Siim Kallas, 28 March) calls for binding completion deadlines and a two-layer structure.
- **2012** — Commission President José Manuel Barroso proposes the Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) with €50 billion; the European Council (7-8 February) cuts it to €26.25 billion for transport.
- **26 October 2012** — Commission formally proposes the revised TEN-T Regulation (COM(2011) 650 final).
- **December 2013** — The regulation (1315/2013/EU) is adopted by the Council and Parliament, with 579 MEPs voting in favor, 58 against, and 36 abstaining in the Parliament vote of 7 October.
- **1 January 2014** — Regulation enters into force, replacing the 1996 guidelines.
- **2016** — The first TEN-T "work plan" is published; 300 key projects are identified.
- **2021** — The Commission proposes a revision (July) to align TEN-T with the European Green Deal, adding a third layer (the "fast core network" by 2040) and stricter interoperability targets.
- **2022** — The Commission’s 2022 progress report shows that 83% of the core network's rail lines are electrified, but only 58% meet the 100 km/h freight train standard.
- **2024** — The European Parliament and Council agree on the revised regulation (December), setting a 2030 target for the "fast core" and extending corridors to Ukraine and Moldova.
- **18 July 2024** — The revised regulation (2024/.../EU) enters into force.

## Impact and consequences

The impact has been mixed but measurable. The regulation forced member states to produce binding national investment plans aligned with EU deadlines, which the Court of Auditors’ 2020 report noted had "improved transparency" but also "created unrealistic expectations." By 2022, the Commission reported that €86 billion had been invested in TEN-T projects through the CEF (2014-2022), including the completion of 1,200 km of new high-speed rail lines.

The most significant consequence was political: the regulation gave the Commission and its European Coordinators real leverage. In 2017, Coordinator Matthias Ruete—a former German state secretary—successfully mediated a dispute between Austria and Germany over the Brenner Base Tunnel’s approach lines, threatening to reallocate CEF funds to another corridor. Austria subsequently approved a €2 billion upgrade.

Conversely, the 2030 deadline for the core network was missed for several key links. The Commission’s 2022 evaluation found that only 35% of the core network’s cross-border rail sections would be complete by 2030, with the Baltic Rail Baltica project (connecting Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania to Poland) delayed by at least 4 years. Estonia’s Ministry of Economic Affairs and Communications cited "cost overruns of 30%" in a 2023 report.

The regulation also had unintended consequences for peripheral regions. The focus on nine core corridors meant that the "comprehensive network" in southern Italy, Greece, and the Baltic states received less attention; Eurostat’s 2021 territorial data showed that investment per capita in TEN-T was 40% lower in cohesion regions than in core EU areas.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU rules for building cross-border transport links need updating in 2021?

The original 2013 regulation did not anticipate the European Green Deal (2019) or the war in Ukraine (2022). The 2021 revision, proposed by Commissioner Adina Vălean, added a "fast core network" to be completed by 2040, with mandatory 160 km/h passenger trains, 100 km/h freight trains, and full electrification on all lines. The invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 further accelerated the revision: the European Council’s Versailles Declaration (March 2022) called for "rapidly" connecting Ukraine and Moldova to the TEN-T, and the final agreement in 2024 extended the network to Lviv, Kyiv, and Chișinău, with deadlines pushed to 2050 for those extensions.

### Why are key transport connections still unfinished 30 years after the EU started planning them?

The main reason is that national governments resist funding infrastructure that primarily benefits other countries. A 2020 study by the European University Institute found that the average cost overrun for cross-border TEN-T projects was 24%, compared to 12% for domestic projects, because two countries cannot agree on standards, procurement rules, or construction timelines. For example, the Lyon-Turin high-speed rail link—planned since the 1990s—remains unbuilt as of 2024, with Italy’s Piedmont region opposing the project in a 2023 court case. The regulation’s binding deadlines help, but they cannot override national sovereignty over land use and budgets.

### What do EU-appointed coordinators actually do to help finish major transport projects?

They have no formal enforcement power, but significant political leverage. The regulation gives them the right to convene annual corridor forums, publish progress reports naming and shaming delays, and recommend that the Commission withhold CEF funding for non-complying projects. In 2020, Coordinator Catherine Trautmann (a former French minister) used this power to block €150 million in CEF funding for a Romanian rail upgrade until Romania agreed to install the European Rail Traffic Management System (ERTMS) on 100% of the line, not the 50% it had proposed. Member states cannot remove coordinators, who are appointed by the Commission for a renewable four-year term.

### Why do EU transport rules require trains to run at certain speeds and use specific electric systems?

Because interoperability is the single biggest barrier to a single transport market. The European Court of Auditors’ 2016 special report found that a freight train crossing from Germany to Poland lost an average of 2 hours at the border due to voltage changes, signal incompatibility, and paperwork. The regulation’s Article 39 sets binding minimum speeds (100 km/h for freight on core network lines by 2030) and requires all new core network lines to be electrified with the 25 kV AC system. The European Railway Agency (now part of the European Union Agency for Railways) estimates that full implementation of these standards could cut cross-border rail travel times by 30-40%.
