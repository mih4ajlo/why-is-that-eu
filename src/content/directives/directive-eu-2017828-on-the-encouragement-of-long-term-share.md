## What is it?

The Shareholder Rights Directive II amends the original 2007 directive to tackle what the Commission called the "short-termism" of listed European companies and the passivity of their largest shareholders. It introduces four big building blocks: a "say-on-pay" regime under which shareholders vote on directors' remuneration policy and the annual remuneration report; new rules requiring board approval and public disclosure of material related-party transactions; transparency obligations for institutional investors, asset managers and proxy advisors, who must publish engagement policies and explain how they integrate stewardship into their strategy; and a right for companies to identify their shareholders down the chain of intermediaries, so they can communicate with them directly.

The directive applies to companies with registered offices in an EU member state and whose shares are admitted to trading on an EU-regulated market. It is a minimum harmonisation instrument: member states can — and many did — go further, particularly on whether the say-on-pay vote is binding or merely advisory.

## Why was it introduced?

The political fuse was lit in the wreckage of the financial crisis. When Royal Bank of Scotland, Fortis, Dexia and a string of other European banks collapsed or were nationalised between 2008 and 2011, regulators and parliamentarians asked an obvious question: where were the shareholders? The European Commission's 2010 Green Paper on corporate governance in financial institutions, championed by then Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier, concluded bluntly that institutional shareholders had failed to monitor risk-taking at the banks they owned. A 2011 follow-up Green Paper extended the diagnosis to all listed companies.

Two further scandals turned diagnosis into legislation. The first was a string of executive pay rows that radicalised public opinion — in 2013 Swiss voters approved the Minder Initiative banning golden parachutes by a 68% margin, and the same year EU finance ministers struck a deal capping banker bonuses at 100% of salary (200% with shareholder approval) under CRD IV. Barnier seized the momentum: in April 2014 the Commission tabled the SRD II proposal alongside a recommendation on corporate governance reporting. The second was a wave of related-party transaction abuses in companies with controlling shareholders, particularly in southern and eastern Europe, where minority investors had little recourse against tunnelling by dominant owners — a problem documented in the Commission's 2014 impact assessment accompanying the proposal.

The coalition behind the file was unusual. The European Parliament rapporteur, Italian centre-right MEP Sergio Cofferati, pushed hard on say-on-pay and pay-ratio disclosure (CEO pay versus average employee pay), which appealed to the S&D group and Greens. Centre-right EPP members, backed by BusinessEurope, preferred the advisory model and resisted disclosing the pay ratio at individual-employee granularity. The proxy-advisory provisions — covering firms like ISS and Glass Lewis — were inserted after asset owners complained about the influence of US-based advisors on European AGMs. The institutional-investor transparency rules drew heavily on the UK Stewardship Code, which Barnier's team explicitly used as a template.

Negotiations stretched nearly three years, far longer than expected, largely because the related-party transactions article became a proxy fight between countries with dispersed ownership (UK, Netherlands, Nordics) and those with concentrated ownership (Italy, France, Germany's Mittelstand). The final compromise — material transactions must be publicly announced and approved by the board or general meeting, with member states defining "material" — was watered down from the Commission's original text, but still went further than what existed in most national company laws.

## Timeline

- **2010** — Commission Green Paper on corporate governance in financial institutions (Barnier).
- **2011** — Green Paper extending the debate to all listed companies.
- **April 2014** — Commission proposes SRD II as part of a wider company law package.
- **July 2015** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position, including a binding say-on-pay vote.
- **December 2016** — Trilogue agreement reached under the Slovak Council presidency.
- **March 2017** — Parliament approves the final text; Council adoption follows in April.
- **17 May 2017** — Directive 2017/828 enters into force.
- **10 June 2019** — Transposition deadline for member states.
- **3 September 2020** — Commission Implementing Regulation 2018/1212 on shareholder identification and information transmission becomes applicable.
- **2023** — Commission publishes its report on the application of SRD II, noting uneven transposition and continued weaknesses in proxy-advisor regulation.

## Impact and consequences

Say-on-pay votes became routine across Europe by 2020, and several produced political earthquakes. Pearson, Royal Dutch Shell, BP and Renault all faced significant shareholder revolts on executive pay between 2016 and 2020 — in 2016 the Renault AGM rejected CEO Carlos Ghosn's remuneration package by 54%, a vote the French board controversially overrode, a decision that fed into the broader scandal that ended Ghosn's career two years later. Pay ratios began appearing in annual reports across the continent, and academic studies, including work cited by the Commission's 2023 review, found that European CEO pay growth moderated relative to the US during the post-SRD II period.

Implementation was bumpy. The Commission's 2023 application report flagged that Germany, Italy, Spain and several smaller member states transposed the directive late or incompletely; the Commission opened infringement procedures against multiple states for failing to notify transposition measures by the June 2019 deadline. The shareholder identification chain — designed to let issuers communicate with end-investors held through layers of custodians — proved technically painful, with custodian banks and CSDs investing heavily in messaging systems based on ISO 20022 standards to comply with Implementing Regulation 2018/1212.

The stewardship transparency rules pushed asset managers like BlackRock, Amundi, DWS and Legal & General to publish detailed engagement reports, expanding a practice that had been largely confined to UK signatories of the Stewardship Code. Proxy advisors remained the weakest link: SRD II requires them to disclose their code of conduct on a comply-or-explain basis, which industry critics — including the European Securities and Markets Authority in its 2023 advice to the Commission — consider too light-touch given that two firms still dominate the global market.

## Key questions answered

### Can shareholders in Europe vote on how much CEOs are paid?

Yes — since 2019, shareholders in EU-listed companies vote on both the company's remuneration policy (typically every four years) and on the annual remuneration report. Whether the vote is binding or advisory depends on the country: the Netherlands, Sweden and Germany made the policy vote binding, while others kept it advisory. Even where votes are non-binding, large "no" votes have forced boards to redesign pay packages — most public after Renault's 2016 Ghosn revolt and several FTSE 100 rebellions.

### Why did EU regulators start telling pension funds how to vote their shares?

Because the financial crisis exposed institutional investors as absentee owners. After bank collapses in 2008-2011, the Commission concluded that asset managers and pension funds had failed to scrutinise the companies whose shares they held on behalf of millions of savers. The rules now require them to publish an engagement policy or explain why they don't, and to report annually on how they voted and engaged — modelled on the UK Stewardship Code.

### What stops a controlling shareholder from siphoning value out of a listed company?

The related-party transaction rules require listed companies to publicly announce material deals with insiders — controlling shareholders, directors, their families and affiliated entities — and put them to board or general meeting approval. Member states define the "materiality" threshold, which means protection varies: minority investors in Italian or French companies with dominant families still face weaker safeguards than in jurisdictions like the Netherlands, but the disclosure obligation alone has made tunnelling harder to hide.

### Why are firms like ISS and Glass Lewis controversial in Europe?

These two US-headquartered proxy advisors effectively decide how a large share of global institutional capital votes at European AGMs by issuing recommendations. The 2017 directive requires them to disclose their methodology and any conflicts of interest, but on a comply-or-explain basis — which ESMA and several MEPs consider inadequate given the duopoly's influence over outcomes that can sink board candidates or compensation plans.
