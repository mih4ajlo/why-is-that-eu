## What is it?

Directive 2009/119/EC mandates that all EU member states hold emergency oil stocks (crude oil and/or petroleum products) equal to at least 90 days of average daily net imports. It replaced two earlier directives (68/414/EEC and 2006/67/EC) to close loopholes, improve transparency, and align with International Energy Agency (IEA) commitments. The directive also creates a system for mutual assistance between member states during oil supply disruptions and requires detailed reporting on stock levels.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the 2006 Russia-Ukraine gas crisis, which exposed how easily energy supply disruptions could cascade across Europe. While the crisis involved natural gas, it reignited fears about oil supply security, particularly after the 1973 oil embargo had already prompted the original 1968 directive.

The deeper structural problem was fragmentation. By 2009, EU member states maintained oil reserves under different legal frameworks: 22 were also IEA members with 90-day import coverage obligations, while others had weaker requirements or no mutual-assistance mechanisms. The 1998 and 2006 directives had left loopholes—for example, Member States could count stocks held by private operators that might not be available in a real crisis, and reporting standards varied.

Coalition dynamics saw the European Commission under President José Manuel Barroso push for harmonisation, backed by net-importing states like Germany, Italy, and Spain, which feared supply disruptions. The United Kingdom and several Nordic states, already strong IEA members, supported tighter rules but insisted on maintaining flexibility for national stockholding agencies. The main opposition came from the European petroleum industry, led by FuelsEurope (then CONCAWE), which argued that mandatory state-controlled stocks would raise costs and distort commercial storage markets.

Timing was critical. The 2006 Russia-Ukraine gas dispute—which cut supplies to 18 EU countries for three days in January 2006—had shattered the assumption that Russia was a reliable energy partner. The 2008 financial crisis then created a political opening: governments were already intervening in energy markets, and securing oil reserves seemed prudent as demand plunged and prices collapsed. The Commission’s 2007 impact assessment found that existing stockholding arrangements were "insufficiently robust" to cope with a major disruption, and that the cost of non-action could reach €100 billion.

What changed was the explicit knowledge that Russia had weaponised energy supplies. The 2009 directive introduced compulsory mutual assistance clauses, mandatory electronic reporting, and clearer rules for stock release. It also required member states to designate a single central stockholding entity—a structural shift from the previous patchwork of private and public schemes.

## Timeline

- **1968** — Directive 68/414/EEC first requires Member States to hold 65 days of average daily consumption
- **1998** — Directive 98/93/EC raises requirement to 90 days of net imports for IEA-compliant states
- **2006** — Russia-Ukraine gas crisis disrupts EU supplies; Commission begins review
- **2007** — Commission impact assessment finds existing stock rules "insufficiently robust"
- **2008** — Financial crisis breaks political deadlock; companies warn of cost increases
- **2009** — Directive 2009/119/EC adopted by Council and Parliament
- **December 2012** — Deadline for Member States to transpose directive into national law
- **2014** — Russia annexes Crimea; oil stocks again prove critical for member states like Poland and Lithuania
- **2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; EU activates emergency oil stock release mechanism coordinated with IEA

## Impact and consequences

The directive standardised reporting and made crisis response predictable. By 2014, Eurostat data showed all EU member states met the 90-day requirement, with average stocks at 116 days of net imports. The mutual assistance clause was invoked for the first time in 2011, when several southern states released stocks during the Libya crisis.

Industry response was mixed. Major oil companies like BP and Shell initially opposed mandatory state holdings, arguing commercial stocks were sufficient. However, the 2022 crisis proved the directive's value: when Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, the EU coordinated the release of 240 million barrels of oil stocks within two weeks, stabilising prices.

Countries that struggled included Malta and Cyprus, which had limited storage infrastructure. Both received derogations until 2016 and later built state-owned facilities. The cost of compliance has been estimated at €0.2–0.5 per barrel per year by the Commission, but the avoided costs of even a short supply disruption are orders of magnitude higher.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU require a 90-day oil reserve?

The 90-day figure originates from the IEA's 1974 founding treaty, designed to cover the average duration of a major supply disruption. The EU adopted the same standard to ensure all member states—including non-IEA members—could survive a sudden cut-off, such as during the 1973 oil embargo or the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war.

### Which countries struggled to meet the 90-day requirement?

Malta and Cyprus received temporary derogations because they lacked oil storage facilities and imported nearly all their oil. Both built state-owned tank farms by 2016. Smaller landlocked members like Austria and the Czech Republic relied on shared storage with neighbours to meet the target.

### How does the EU coordinate emergency oil releases with the IEA?

The directive requires member states to report stock levels monthly to the Commission and the IEA. In a crisis, the IEA decides on a collective release, and the EU's mutual assistance clause kicks in automatically—allowing stocks held in one country to be released to another. The 2022 coordinated release was the largest ever, covering 240 million barrels.

### Does the directive apply to all petroleum products?

Yes—the directive covers crude oil, motor gasoline, gas oil, jet fuel, and heavy fuel oil. Member States must hold stocks in a mix of these categories based on their consumption patterns. For example, Germany holds more diesel because its economy relies heavily on trucking.
