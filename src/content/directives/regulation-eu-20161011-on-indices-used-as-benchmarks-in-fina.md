## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2016/1011, known as the Benchmark Regulation (BMR), establishes a comprehensive framework for financial benchmarks—like LIBOR, EURIBOR, or the Swiss Franc LIBOR—used in financial instruments, investment funds, and contracts. It requires administrators of such benchmarks to be authorised by national competent authorities, follow transparent governance rules, and ensure the data used is reliable. The BMR also introduces mandatory rules for critical benchmarks, such as those based on submissions from panel banks, and allows for EU-recognised third-country benchmark administrators.

The regulation applies to a wide range of entities: benchmark administrators, who publish the indices; contributors, who supply input data; and users, who reference benchmarks in their financial products. It classifies benchmarks into three tiers—critical, significant, and non-significant—each with tiered obligations, calibrated to the benchmark impact on financial stability. The BMR was part of a broader post-crisis reform package that also included the Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MiFIR) and the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR).

## Why was it introduced?

The BMR’s origin is the LIBOR scandal that erupted in 2012, when regulators found that traders at major global banks—including Barclays, UBS, and Deutsche Bank—had systematically manipulated the London Interbank Offered Rate (LIBOR) for years. LIBOR, a benchmark for trillions of dollars in mortgages, derivatives, and loans, was set based on unverified submissions from panel banks, who often quoted rates that benefited their trading positions rather than true borrowing costs. In June 2012, the UK Financial Services Authority fined Barclays £59.5 million for its role, and soon after, the US Commodity Futures Trading Commission fined the same bank $200 million. These were just the opening salvos: by 2015, Deutsche Bank paid a record $2.5 billion in fines to US and UK regulators, and UBS $1.5 billion.

The scandal revealed a structural tension: the existing system relied on voluntary, non-transparent self-regulation by the banking industry, leaving benchmarks vulnerable to manipulation. In the EU, the European Commission under then-Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier proposed the BMR in September 2013, arguing that the “integrity of benchmarks is a pre-condition for well-functioning markets.” The coalition for the regulation was broad: the European Parliament, led by rapporteur Sven Giegold (Greens/EFA, Germany), pushed for strict oversight and criminal liability, while EU member states, particularly the UK, resisted heavy-handed rules that could burden the City of London. The timing was crucial—the crisis had peaked in 2012, and the EU needed to act before public and investor trust evaporated completely.

Opposition came primarily from the UK banking sector and the European Central Bank, which argued that the regulation could drive benchmarks out of the EU to jurisdictions with lighter rules. The UK, home to LIBOR’s administrator, the British Bankers’ Association, was a key holdout. In the Parliament, Giegold’s proposal to extend the scope to commodity benchmarks and exchange rates was narrowly defeated after intense lobbying from industry groups like the European Banking Federation. The final text, adopted in June 2016 by a 528–94 vote (with 81 abstentions), was a compromise: it required mandatory contribution to critical benchmarks—preventing banks from simply withdrawing—and introduced a phased transition for existing benchmarks.

What changed that unblocked the deadlock was the sheer scale of the scandal’s fallout. By 2014, more than 20 financial institutions had been implicated, with total fines exceeding $9 billion globally, according to the Financial Stability Board. The collapse of the Swiss Franc floor in January 2015 further exposed the fragility of currency benchmarks. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) was given powers to oversee critical benchmarks jointly with national authorities, while the Commission’s 2014 impact assessment estimated that manipulation of just one major benchmark could cause losses of up to €50 billion across the EU economy.

## Timeline

- **2012** — The LIBOR manipulation scandal breaks, with Barclays fined £59.5 million by the UK FSA and $200 million by the US CFTC in June.
- **2013** — EU Commissioner Michel Barnier proposes the Benchmark Regulation on September 18, following a public consultation.
- **2014** — The European Parliament’s ECON Committee adopts its position, with rapporteur Sven Giegold pushing for criminal sanctions. German regulator BaFin begins investigating Deutsche Bank.
- **2015** — Deutsche Bank pays $2.5 billion in fines to US and UK regulators for LIBOR manipulation. The Swiss National Bank abandons the EUR/CHF floor on January 15, causing chaos in FX benchmarks.
- **2016** — The European Parliament votes 528–94 in favour of the BMR on June 8; the Council adopts it on June 30. The regulation enters into force on June 30, 2016, with application from January 1, 2018.
- **2018** — The BMR becomes fully applicable on January 1, requiring all benchmark administrators in the EU to be authorised. ESMA publishes first list of authorised administrators.
- **2020** — The EU extends the transition period for third-country benchmarks to 2022 due to Brexit disruptions, amid the LIBOR phase-out planned for end-2021.
- **2022** — The EU adopts a BMR review to address challenges from the shift to risk-free rates (e.g., €STR replacing EURIBOR-style benchmarks), harmonising rules further.

## Impact and consequences

The BMR dramatically reshaped the EU’s financial benchmark industry. By 2019, ESMA had authorised 20 benchmark administrators within the EU, forcing legacy unregulated administrators—like the ICE Benchmark Administration (LIBOR’s overseer)—to seek recognition. This led to the phasing out of LIBOR entirely by end-2021, replaced by central-bank-backed risk-free rates (€STR in the EU, SOFR in the US). The regulation imposed mandatory contribution rules for critical benchmarks, such as EURIBOR, preventing banks from abandoning submissions—a key fear after the scandal. The European Banking Authority estimated in 2020 that EURIBOR alone underpinned over €180 trillion in financial contracts, and the BMR ensured its continuity.

The costs of compliance were significant. A 2017 Commission study found that smaller administrators—such as those producing regionally significant property indices—faced compliance costs of up to €500,000 annually, forcing some to exit the market. Countries like Luxembourg and Malta struggled with the burden, as their small financial centres lacked the supervisory capacity ESMA demanded. In response, the BMR was amended in 2020 to reduce requirements for non-significant benchmarks, allowing a lighter regime.

For users, the regulation increased transparency: contracts now specify whether benchmarks are EU-authorised, reducing legal uncertainty. The biggest consequence was unforeseen—the BMR accelerated the global transition away from LIBOR earlier than many banks anticipated, pushing the industry toward more resilient benchmarks.

## Key questions answered

### How was LIBOR manipulated by big banks and why did it take years to uncover?

LIBOR was set daily by a panel of banks submitting estimated borrowing costs, which were unaudited and unverified. Traders colluded to submit artificially low rates to paint a healthier picture of their bank’s creditworthiness—or high rates to profit from derivative positions. The practice went undetected for years because regulators relied on self-reporting and lacked access to trading data. Whistleblowers like UBS’s Tom Hayes eventually exposed the system, leading to his conviction in 2015, but the lax oversight cost the global financial system billions.

### Do EU rules on financial benchmarks also apply to indexes created outside Europe?

Yes, the BMR allows third-country benchmarks to be used in the EU only if their administrator is registered with ESMA or the benchmark is endorsed by an EU-recognised entity. For example, after Brexit, the UK’s Sterling Overnight Index Average (SONIA) had to get a temporary equivalence decision from the Commission in 2021 to avoid disrupting £100 trillion in UK-EU contract linkages. This was a direct response to the LIBOR crisis—ensuring no offshore benchmark could slip through the cracks.

### What happens if the company running a major stock or interest rate index suddenly stops publishing it?

The BMR requires administrators of critical benchmarks to have a formal wind-down plan to avoid abrupt market disruption. If an administrator plans to stop, it must give regulators at least two years’ notice, and ESMA can compel continued contributions. This clause was triggered in 2020 when the ICE Benchmark Administration announced LIBOR’s end—giving markets time to transition to €STR and SOFR, preventing a repeat of the 2015 Swiss Franc shock.

### Why did the EU specifically crack down on currency benchmarks like the Swiss Franc LIBOR?

The scandal extended beyond LIBOR to currency manipulations, with traders colluding to fix the WM/Reuters 4pm fix for foreign exchange rates—a benchmark used in corporate treasuries and pensions. In 2014, the UK FCA fined five banks £1.1 billion for rigging these rates. The BMR was expanded late in negotiations to include FX benchmarks based on input data, as Commissioner Barnier’s 2013 draft only covered interest-rate benchmarks. This was a direct response to the FX rigging crisis.
