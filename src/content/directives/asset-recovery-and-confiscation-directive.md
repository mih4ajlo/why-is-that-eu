## What is it?

The Asset Recovery and Confiscation Directive (2024/1260/EU) is a sweeping EU law that forces member states to find, freeze, manage, and confiscate criminal money and property — even when there is no criminal conviction. It replaces the weaker 2014 Directive and closes loopholes that allowed organised crime to hide assets across borders.

The directive empowers national Asset Recovery Offices (AROs) to trace assets in real-time, freeze them within hours, and manage them so they do not lose value before confiscation. Critically, it introduces **non-conviction based confiscation** (NCC) for suspects who flee, die, or feign illness; for assets transferred to third parties who knew or should have known; and for property whose value is disproportionate to a convict's legitimate income.

## Why was it introduced?

The case of Marco Raduano tells you everything. In February 2023, Italy's most wanted mafia boss — head of the Foggia mafia, sentenced *in absentia* to life in prison — walked out of a maximum-security prison in Sardinia using knotted bedsheets. He was on the run for over a year before being arrested in Corsica. During that time, his assets — real estate, businesses, luxury cars — sat untouched because Italian authorities had no legal basis to confiscate them without his conviction. EU police estimated his organisation controlled drug routes and extortion rackets worth €4 billion annually. The Raduano case was a live demonstration of the 2014 Directive's fatal flaw: if a suspect flees or dies, the assets stay with them.

The European Commission's 2019 evaluation of the 2014 Directive was damning. It found that less than 1% of estimated criminal proceeds in the EU are ever confiscated — €1.2 billion recovered against an estimated €110 billion in criminal profits, according to Europol's 2021 "EU Serious and Organised Crime Threat Assessment." The evaluation specifically cited the Netherlands, where AROs reported that 70% of suspected criminal assets could not be frozen because suspects exploited legal loopholes — transferring ownership to family members, fleeing jurisdiction, or dying before trial.

The political coalition that forced this through was unusual. On one side stood the **Scandinavian and Baltic states** — Sweden, Finland, Denmark, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania — who had already adopted aggressive NCC laws and wanted the rest of the EU to catch up. On the other side were the **Southern member states** (Italy, Spain, Greece, Portugal), battered by mafia infiltration and debt-financed austerity, who saw confiscation as a way to recover public money and disrupt organised crime without raising taxes. The Netherlands, Belgium, and Germany — traditional free-trade advocates — were initially skeptical of encroaching on national criminal procedure, but a series of terrorist attacks and the Panama Papers leak shifted opinion. In October 2022, the European Parliament even blocked a first draft of the directive, demanding stronger provisions on tracing assets held through shell companies and trusts; MEPs on the Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee cited Europol data showing 80% of criminal assets were hidden through legal entities.

The trigger was the **2023 EU Strategic Agenda for Organised Crime**, adopted under the Swedish Presidency. Sweden had just suffered its worst-ever wave of gang violence, with 62 shootings and 12 deaths in Stockholm alone in 2022, largely funded by drug money. Swedish Justice Minister Gunnar Strömmer made asset recovery the centrepiece of his EU agenda. In March 2023, he told the Council: "We cannot defeat organised crime by arresting foot soldiers alone. We must take the money."

The Commission proposal, led by Home Affairs Commissioner Ylva Johansson (Sweden) and Justice Commissioner Didier Reynders (Belgium), was published in May 2022. It survived 18 months of Council negotiations, with France and Ireland pushing for stronger human rights safeguards on NCC, and Malta and Cyprus resisting rules that would force them to freeze assets from Russian oligarchs. The final text, adopted in April 2024, compromised: NCC would apply only where the suspect has "fled, died, or is permanently medically unfit for trial" — explicitly excluding the presumption of guilt for the living. The vote in the European Parliament was 476-87, with only the Identity and Democracy group and some conservative MEPs in opposition.

## Timeline

- **2014** — Directive 2014/42/EU on freezing and confiscation of instrumentalities and proceeds of crime comes into force. Weak NCC provisions, no asset management rules.
- **2019** — European Commission evaluation finds less than 1% of criminal proceeds confiscated. Member states report 27 different interpretations of "fled" and "died."
- **2022** — Commission proposal for a new directive published 25 May, including expanded NCC, asset management obligations, and cross-border real-time asset tracing.
- **2023** — European Parliament blocks initial draft in October, demands stronger rules on shell companies and trusts. Council reaches political agreement in December.
- **2024** — Directive adopted 24 April (476-87 in Parliament). Member states have 24 months to transpose into national law.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate consequence is for **asset management**. Member states must now appoint public bodies — usually AROs or dedicated asset management agencies — to preserve confiscated assets pending final forfeiture. Italy's *Agenzia Nazionale per l'Amministrazione e la Destinazione dei Beni Sequestrati e Confiscati* — which has managed over 20,000 properties since 2010, leasing them to charities and municipalities — is the model. Countries without such agencies (Poland, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria) will need to establish them. The Commission estimates the EU-wide maintenance cost at €800 million annually, offset by income from managed assets (rental income, business operations, auction sales).

The non-conviction confiscation provisions affect **third-party acquirers** — family members, lawyers, front companies. Under the directive, a person who receives assets from a suspected criminal *and* knew or should have known their origins may have those assets confiscated even if they are not charged. This targets the longstanding practice of mafia families transferring property to spouses, children, or trusted accountants. In Italy alone, the 2022 report of the *Direzione Nazionale Antimafia* noted that 65% of seized mafia assets were ultimately returned through appeals by family members claiming independent ownership.

The real-time asset tracing power — requiring AROs to respond to cross-border requests within 8 hours for urgent cases — borrows directly from the European Investigation Order model. For the first time, a Polish ARO can freeze a bank account in Luxembourg within hours. This closes the "banking window" that organised crime used to move money before court orders could be served.

The biggest losers are **lawyers and notaries** in high-crime regions. The directive obliges professional trustees and company formation agents to report suspicious asset transfers. In Greece and Cyprus, the Notary Associations lobbied heavily — unsuccessfully — against these provisions, arguing attorney-client privilege was violated. The directive carves out a narrow exception for "core legal advice" but not for routine asset management services.

## Key questions answered

### Can European police seize the house of a mafia suspect who fled before trial?

Yes, if he fled, died, or is permanently medically unfit to appear in court. The directive permits non-conviction based confiscation (NCC) in those three specific cases. The suspect's absence alone is not enough — you still need a judicial finding, typically a criminal proceeding *in absentia* or a separate civil action, that the property was derived from crime. In practice, this covers the Raduano scenario where a convicted fugitive's assets sit untouched during years on the run.

### What happens to a murder suspect's Ferrari if they die before being convicted?

The car is frozen by the Asset Recovery Office within hours of the death and managed to preserve its value. Under the directive, a competent state body (often the ARO) must "maintain the economic value" of frozen assets — so the Ferrari would be stored, insured, and possibly sold on auction if it cannot be kept safe, with proceeds held until final confiscation. If the suspect died before any conviction, the state still confiscates the vehicle if it can prove it was acquired through crime — this is NCC triggered by death.

### Do EU rules specifically target yachts owned by Russian oligarchs?

Not directly. The directive is about proceeds of *crime*, not political sanctions. However, it makes it easier for member states to freeze assets suspected of being criminal proceeds even when the owner claims diplomatic immunity or resides outside the EU. The 2022 sanctions wave against Russian oligarchs exposed how easily yachts and planes were transferred to shell companies or family trusts before freezing orders could be issued. The directive's real-time tracing (8-hour response) and third-party acquirer rules close those gaps. Cyprus and Malta, where many Russian-owned entities are registered, pushed back during negotiations but ultimately accepted these provisions — the final text includes no exemption for sanctioned persons.

### Why do EU countries struggle to take back more money from criminals?

Because the legal burden has historically been too high. The pre-2024 system required a criminal conviction before confiscation, which is impossible when suspects flee, die, or offload assets to family. The directive shifts the logic from "follow the criminal" to "follow the money" — asset recovery offices now have independent powers to freeze and manage property based on a reasonable suspicion it is criminal, with the final confiscation decision left to a judge. Even so, the Commission acknowledges that the 1% recovery rate will only rise to an estimated 3-4% under the new rules — a massive improvement, but still a drop in the proverbial ocean of organised crime profits.
