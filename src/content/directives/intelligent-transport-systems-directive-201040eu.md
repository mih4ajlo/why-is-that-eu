## What is it?

The Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS) Directive (2010/40/EU) is EU-wide legislation that created a coordinated legal and technical framework for digital traffic management and real-time road data services. It requires EU member states to ensure that traffic information, navigation data, and safety-related warnings — like speed limits, roadworks, and accident alerts — are collected, shared, and displayed using interoperable standards across national borders.

The directive covers four priority areas: (1) traffic and travel information services, (2) traffic management systems, (3) road safety and security applications, and (4) the integration of vehicles with transport infrastructure (so-called vehicle-to-infrastructure, or V2I, communication). It delegates detailed technical specifications to delegated acts and implementing regulations — meaning the core directive sets principles, while follow-up rules define the nitty-gritty of data formats, update frequencies, and access conditions.

## Why was it introduced?

The ITS Directive emerged from a structural tension that became acute in the mid-2000s: Europe’s roads were increasingly digital, but national traffic systems spoke different languages. A truck driver from Poland navigating Germany, France, and Spain encountered three incompatible traffic information platforms, three different data formats for roadworks alerts, and no way to get a single cross-border route optimisation. The European Commission’s 2008 impact assessment found that this fragmentation cost the EU economy an estimated €5–10 billion annually in lost time, fuel waste, and accidents — though the precise figure was disputed by industry.

The coalition that drove the directive was unusual. The European Automobile Manufacturers Association (ACEA) lobbied heavily for standardisation, seeing interoperable data as a precondition for selling connected car features across the Single Market. BMW, Volkswagen, and Daimler were particularly vocal — their internal market was fragmenting as national road authorities licensed proprietary data formats. On the other side, public road operators in Germany (Toll Collect) and France (SANEF) resisted, fearing loss of control over traffic data they considered commercial assets.

The political breakthrough came in 2008, when the European Parliament’s Transport Committee, chaired by Italian MEP Paolo Costa (ALDE), held hearings that revealed the human cost: the Commission cited studies showing that up to 20% of road fatalities in the EU were linked to poor information about road conditions and traffic jams. The timing aligned with Barroso’s first Commission (2004–2009), which had made the Digital Agenda a signature priority. Transport Commissioner Antonio Tajani (Italy, EPP) made the directive his flagship initiative, arguing that Europe was falling behind Japan’s advanced VICS system.

The directive faced stiff opposition from the European Committee for Standardization (CEN), which had spent years developing voluntary ITS standards that member states had largely ignored. Industry groups like ERTICO (the European Road Transport Telematics Implementation Coordination Organisation) warned that a mandatory approach would stifle innovation. But the 2008 financial crisis shifted the calculus: the Commission’s 2009 “European Economic Recovery Plan” explicitly identified ITS deployment as a job-creating infrastructure project.

The final text passed Parliament on 6 July 2010 with 546 votes in favour, 62 against, and 29 abstentions. The strongest opposition came from the European People’s Party group, whose northern European members feared the costs of retrofitting existing infrastructure. The Council of the EU adopted it unanimously on 30 September 2010, with one notable footnote: the Netherlands abstained, arguing that the directive gave the Commission too much power to set technical standards through delegated acts.

## Timeline

- **2006** — The Commission publishes a communication (COM(2006) 373) identifying ITS fragmentation as a barrier to the digital single market in transport
- **2007** — The European Parliament adopts a resolution calling for binding ITS standards; Transport Committee hearings begin
- **2008** — Commission impact assessment estimates €5–10 billion annual economic loss from non-interoperable ITS; financial crisis shifts political priorities
- **October 2008** — Commission Vice-President Günter Verheugen (Germany, SPD) publicly supports the directive as part of economic stimulus
- **16 December 2008** — Commission adopts the proposal COM(2008) 887
- **May 2009** — European Parliament first reading; 546-62 vote in favour
- **30 September 2010** — Council adopts directive unanimously; Netherlands abstains
- **6 July 2010** — European Parliament final adoption; directive published in Official Journal on 7 July 2010
- **7 February 2012** — Member states must transpose directive into national law
- **2013–2015** — First delegated acts: real-time traffic information (2013/885/EU), multimodal journey planners (2015/962/EU)
- **2017** — Commission reports that 12 member states failed to fully transpose the directive on time
- **2021** — Commission evaluates the directive in its “Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy”; identifies gaps in data accessibility
- **2022** — Revision process begins: Commission proposes an update to cover mobility-as-a-service, connected vehicles, and automation

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s most visible effect was the mandatory deployment of EU-wide standards for traffic message channels (RDS-TMC) and DATEX II data exchange formats. By 2022, all 27 member states had implemented real-time traffic information services covering at least 80% of their motorway networks, according to the Commission’s 2022 report.

Industry adaptation was uneven. TomTom and Waze, which had built proprietary traffic services, were forced to open their data to public authorities at regulated prices — a move the companies initially fought but later accepted as the new normal. The European Road Federation (ERF) reported that its members’ investment in ITS-compliant toll collection and traffic management systems totalled €3.2 billion between 2012 and 2020.

The biggest compliance challenges came in Eastern Europe. Poland, Romania, and Bulgaria were among the 12 member states that the Commission took to the Court of Justice of the EU for late transposition. Poland eventually complied in 2014 after a fine of €20,000 per day was threatened. By 2023, the Commission has not proceeded with financial penalties against any member state for substantive breaches, though infringement proceedings against Slovenia and Greece have remained open since 2017.

The directive’s impact on safety is harder to quantify. Eurostat data show a 21% reduction in road fatalities between 2010 and 2019, but the Commission itself attributes only a portion of this to ITS, citing improved vehicle safety standards and drunk-driving laws as larger factors. What is clear: the directive enabled the 2018 eCall mandate (Regulation 2015/758/EU), which requires all new cars to automatically call emergency services after a crash — a system that relies directly on ITS data standards.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU force all countries to use the same traffic data formats?

National road authorities had developed incompatible traffic information systems, creating a patchwork where a driver crossing from Germany to France would lose access to real-time warnings, roadworks alerts, and navigation updates. The Commission estimated this fragmentation cost the EU economy billions annually in wasted time and fuel, and studies cited in Parliament hearings linked poor information to up to 20% of road deaths. The directive mandated common standards — like DATEX II — so that a connected car or navigation app works seamlessly across borders.

### Do I need a special device to benefit from EU traffic data rules?

No. The directive requires that all public traffic information — speed limits, roadworks, accidents, weather warnings — be made available in standardised, machine-readable formats that any navigation app or in-car system can use. If your phone’s maps app shows live traffic alerts or road conditions in another EU country, it’s very likely using data shared under these rules. The EU also mandated that all new cars sold after 2018 must include eCall, an automatic emergency call system that works across borders.

### Which companies fought against these rules and why?

TomTom and Waze initially opposed mandatory data sharing, arguing that forcing them to provide their real-time traffic data to public authorities at regulated prices undermined their commercial models. The German Autobahn operator Toll Collect and French road authority SANEF resisted losing control over what they considered proprietary commercial data. On the other side, BMW, Volkswagen, and Daimler lobbied for standardisation because their connected car features needed consistent data across Europe to be marketable.

### Can a city be fined for not following ITS rules?

Not directly — the directive applies to member states, not individual cities or road operators. But if a country fails to implement the standards nationally, the European Commission can take it to the Court of Justice of the EU and seek financial penalties. Poland faced a threatened fine of €20,000 per day in 2014 before it complied. As of 2023, no member state has been fined for substantive breaches of the ITS Directive, though infringement proceedings against Slovenia and Greece remain open.
