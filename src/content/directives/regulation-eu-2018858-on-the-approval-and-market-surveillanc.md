## What is it?

The Type-Approval Regulation (2018/858/EU) replaced the 2007 framework (Directive 2007/46/EC) that governed how carmakers obtain permission to sell new vehicles in the EU. Under the old system, national authorities certified vehicles and were solely responsible for ensuring compliance with EU safety and environmental rules. The new regulation shifts power to the European Commission and creates a system of peer reviews, mandatory independent testing, and financial penalties.

It applies to all new cars, vans, trucks, buses, and trailers sold in the EU. Carmakers must now submit to emissions and safety tests by third-party technical services, and national approval authorities face audits by the Commission. If a manufacturer is caught cheating, the Commission can impose fines of up to €30,000 per vehicle, and the approval authority that issued the certificate can be suspended.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation is a direct response to the Volkswagen diesel emissions scandal (“Dieselgate”) that erupted in September 2015. Volkswagen had installed software in 11 million diesel vehicles worldwide to cheat laboratory emissions tests by the US Environmental Protection Agency; the European Union had no equivalent enforcement mechanism to detect or punish such fraud. When the scandal broke, it exposed a fundamental structural tension in the EU’s car approval system: national authorities had both the power to certify vehicles and the incentive to protect “their” domestic manufacturers. Germany’s Federal Motor Transport Authority (KBA) had approved VW’s software, and other countries’ authorities had rubber‑stamped similar devices.

The political coalition that drove the reform was unusual. The European Parliament’s Internal Market Committee, led by British Conservative rapporteur Daniel Dalton, initially favoured a light‑touch revision. But the Parliament’s ENVI Committee and consumer groups pushed for tougher independent testing after the scandal. The Emilian‑born Commission Vice‑President Elżbieta Bieńkowska, responsible for internal market and industry, signalled early support for an overhaul. The key blocking force came from Germany, home to VW, Daimler, and BMW, and from Czech and Slovak MEPs defending their own automotive heavyweights. Berlin argued that the existing system had worked for decades and that new EU oversight would slow down innovation.

The tipping point came in January 2016, when the US Department of Justice fined VW $4.3 billion – a sum unimaginable under EU rules where no trans‑national penalty existed. The European Commission, under President Jean-Claude Juncker, proposed the new regulation in April 2016. After bitter negotiations, the Parliament adopted its position in April 2017, and the Council reached a deal in December 2017. The final regulation was published in July 2018 and entered into force on 1 September 2020.

- **September 2015** — The US EPA reveals Volkswagen used defeat devices to cheat NOx emissions tests; the company admits to 11 million affected vehicles worldwide.
- **October 2015** — The European Parliament holds a hearing with VW CEO Matthias Müller; MEPs demand a reform of the type‑approval system.
- **January 2016** — The US Department of Justice fines VW $4.3 billion; the European Commission announces it will propose new legislation within months.
- **April 2016** — The Commission publishes a formal proposal for a regulation replacing Directive 2007/46/EC, including EU‑wide market surveillance, independent audits, and financial penalties.
- **April 2017** — The European Parliament adopts its first‑reading position with a strong mandate for independent testing and a €30,000 per‑vehicle fine.
- **December 2017** — The Council of the EU agrees its general approach, weakened on some points (e.g., national authorities still responsible for enforcement) but keeping the penalty framework.
- **May 2018** — Trilogue negotiations conclude; the final text grants the Commission powers to audit national authorities and to impose fines directly.
- **July 2018** — Regulation (EU) 2018/858 is published in the Official Journal.
- **1 September 2020** — The regulation becomes applicable across the EU, replacing the old directive.
- **2021–2022** — The Commission conducts first peer reviews of six national approval authorities, identifying weaknesses in Spain, Italy, and among others.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation has reshaped how carmakers bring vehicles to market in the EU. National approval authorities must now submit to periodic audits by the Commission; if they fail, the Commission can suspend their right to issue new type‑approvals. Carmakers can be fined up to €30,000 per non‑compliant vehicle – a sharp increase from the previous zero‑penalty regime. In practice, the Commission imposed its first fines in 2022 on several Romanian car‑parts makers, and in 2023 it started an investigation into suspected defeat devices in FIAT and Renault vehicles.

Industry adapted by investing in new test‑rigs and software to avoid the risk of retroactive penalties. The European Automobile Manufacturers’ Association (ACEA) privately warned that costs would rise, but public positions praised the rules as giving “a level playing field”. Several smaller member states, particularly those without domestic carmakers, gained a louder voice in the system: they can now report suspicious vehicles to the Commission rather than rely solely on the approval authority.

The most visible consequence is the shift in testing from “laboratory” to “real‑driving emissions” (RDE) tests, which the new regulation made mandatory for all new types from 2017 and all new cars from 2019. According to a 2021 study by the European Environment Agency, average NOx emissions from diesel cars fell by about 20% between 2017 and 2020, partly due to RDE testing.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU car tests fail to catch Volkswagen’s cheating before the US did?

Under the old Directive 2007/46/EC, national authorities (like Germany’s KBA) tested cars themselves or authorised private laboratories. Carmakers could avoid catching cheat software because the standards were static and predictable. The US Environmental Protection Agency, by contrast, used “random” on‑road tests. When the EPA found VW’s TDI diesel produced 40 times the legal limit during a drive cycle in California, it triggered a criminal investigation. No EU authority performed any equivalent real‑world check before 2015.

### Does the new law make car testing independent from the carmakers?

Partially. Carmakers still arrange their own certification tests with approved “technical services” (testing laboratories). But the regulation now requires that a sample of vehicles from each new type be tested independently by the Commission’s Joint Research Centre or by another member state’s authority. The Commission can also conduct “mystery” tests on cars already on the market. The 2018 regulation created a new EU‑wide market surveillance system under which any member state can flag a suspected non‑compliant vehicle.

### What penalties can a manufacturer face today compared to before 2020?

Before 2020, a manufacturer caught cheating faced only a national recall, with no EU‑level financial sanction. The new regulation empowers the Commission to impose fines of up to €30,000 per non‑compliant vehicle, and to ban the sale of that vehicle type across the EU. In theory, a mass‑cheating scheme affecting 500,000 vehicles could generate a fine of €15 billion. In practice, the first fines were modest (under €1 million) and levied on suppliers, not manufacturers. The system is still untested against a full‑scale scandal.

### Can a national authority still ignore a cheating carmaker if it is a domestic champion?

Yes, but with greater risk. The new regulation subjects national approval authorities to regular peer reviews by the Commission and other member states. If a national authority is found to have failed in its duties, the Commission can demand corrective actions, and if the failure persists, it can suspend that authority’s right to issue new type‑approvals. In 2022, Italy’s Ministry of Transport was audited after the Commission found deficiencies in its oversight of FIAT emissions. The Italian authority avoided suspension but was required to hire additional inspectors.
