## What is it?

The Health Technology Assessment Regulation (HTAR), formally Regulation (EU) 2021/2282, is a landmark piece of EU legislation that for the first time mandates joint clinical assessments (JCAs) for new health technologies — primarily innovative cancer drugs, advanced therapies, and high-risk medical devices — across all EU member states. It creates a permanent framework for EU member states to systematically share evidence on the clinical benefits and risks of new treatments before they are priced and reimbursed nationally.

Under the HTAR, the Commission’s new Coordination Group on Health Technology Assessment, comprising national HTA bodies, will produce a single joint clinical assessment report for each eligible new medicine. This report is binding on member states as the clinical evidence base, though each country retains full sovereignty over pricing, reimbursement, and non-clinical (e.g. economic or ethical) evaluations. The regulation applies from January 2025 for cancer therapies and advanced therapy medicinal products (ATMPs), with a phased rollout to orphan drugs by 2028 and all central marketing authorisation medicines by 2030.

The HTAR is the culmination of two decades of voluntary cooperation under the European Network for Health Technology Assessment (EUnetHTA) Joint Actions, which failed to achieve consistent uptake and duplication across member states, leading to delays in patient access and high regulatory burden for pharmaceutical companies.

## Why was it introduced?

The HTAR’s origins lie in a structural tension that became unsustainable: while the EU had centralised market authorisation for innovative medicines through the European Medicines Agency (EMA), each of the 27 member states then conducted its own separate HTA, requiring companies to submit essentially the same clinical evidence in different formats, different languages, and at different times. By the late 2010s, the Commission’s 2018 impact assessment found that this duplication cost the pharmaceutical industry an estimated €80–160 million annually, with each new cancer drug typically requiring 10 to 15 separate HTA submissions across the bloc.

The political moment that broke the deadlock was the 2016–2019 period, during which three factors converged. First, the **Juncker Commission’s “Europe with a Social Dimension”** priority, under Health Commissioner Vytenis Andriukaitis, explicitly included improving patient access to innovative treatments. Second, a 2017 study by the European Court of Auditors found that EU-level HTA cooperation under EUnetHTA had not delivered measurable reductions in duplicative work, as only about 40% of the Joint Actions’ assessments were used by all member states who needed them. Third, the **2017 scandals over the pricing of the CAR-T therapy Kymriah** (Novartis, launched at $475,000) and **Yescarta** (Gilead/Kite, $373,000) triggered public and political outcry across member states, with health ministers calling for better evidence-sharing to justify high prices.

The coalition that passed the regulation was built by the **Commission’s DG SANTE** (led by Andriukaitis and later Stella Kyriakides under von der Leyen) together with a core group of member states that had already invested heavily in national HTA systems: **France (HAS), Germany (IQWiG/G-BA), the Netherlands (ZIN), and Sweden (TLV)**. These countries saw EU-wide joint assessments as a way to reduce their own redundancy — the Netherlands’ HTA agency reported spending 40% of its budget on assessing drugs that had already been reviewed by two or three other countries. The pharmaceutical industry, represented by **EFPIA**, was cautiously supportive of harmonisation, provided it reduced timelines and burdens, but European generics associations warned that small developers could face disproportionate costs.

Opposition came from **Poland and Hungary**, which feared that mandatory joint assessments would force them to adopt clinical evaluations generated by richer member states with more developed HTA systems, potentially limiting their capacity to negotiate lower prices. Poland’s health ministry argued that national HTA processes had to reflect local disease prevalence and treatment pathways. **Denmark** also expressed concerns about sacrificing national sovereignty in clinical judgment. However, the final text preserved national sovereignty over pricing through Article 20, which explicitly states that JCAs “in no way prejudge” national decisions on pricing, reimbursement, or clinical guidelines. The regulation passed the European Parliament in December 2020 by a large majority (631 to 33) and the Council in March 2021 without formal opposition.

The timing was politically opportune: during the **COVID-19 pandemic in 2020–2021**, the urgent need for European cooperation on vaccine and treatment evaluation — from the EMA’s conditional marketing authorisations to national HTA decisions on remdesivir and tocilizumab — vividly illustrated the costs of fragmentation. The European Council in July 2020 explicitly referenced the need for a stronger HTA framework in its conclusions on EU health sovereignty. The regulation was formally adopted in December 2021, with the first mandatory submissions beginning in January 2025.

## Timeline

- **2004** — The Commission launches the first pilot for EU-level HTA cooperation with the “EUnetHTA” project under the Public Health Programme
- **2011–2015** — EUnetHTA Joint Action 2 conducts trial joint assessments of 30 health technologies, but only 35% of member states consistently use the results
- **2017** — European Court of Auditors report publishes critical review, finding that EU HTA cooperation had “limited added value” and failed to reduce duplication
- **2017** — The Juncker Commission includes HTA harmonisation in its “European Health Union” concept paper
- **January 2018** — Commission publishes legislative proposal COM(2018)51 for a Regulation on HTA
- **2018** — Impact assessment estimates €80–160 million annual savings from harmonisation; stakeholder consultation receives 300+ responses
- **November 2020** — European Parliament (Rapporteur Margrete Auken, Greens/EFA) votes 631-33 to adopt the regulation
- **March 2021** — Council adopts the regulation without amendment
- **December 2021** — Regulation published in Official Journal of the EU (L 458/1)
- **January 2025** — Mandatory application begins for new cancer medicines and Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMPs)
- **January 2028** — Mandatory application expands to orphan medicinal products
- **January 2030** — Full application for all new centrally authorised medicines and high-risk medical devices
- **January 2025** — First batch of joint clinical assessment submissions expected; three companies prepare dossiers

## Impact and consequences

The HTAR reshapes the EU pharmaceutical regulatory landscape by creating a de facto two-tier assessment system: the EMA authorises marketing, and the HTAR joint clinical assessments inform national pricing and reimbursement. For pharmaceutical companies, the most immediate impact is the requirement to submit a single dossier to the Coordination Group rather than 27 separate HTA bodies, but the data format requirements (the new HTA Regulation Implementation Guideline) add a new procedural layer.

Early implementation has been rocky: by early 2025, only **7 of 27 member states** had formally notified the Commission that their national HTA systems were fully ready for the JCA process, according to Commission quarterly monitoring reports. The coordination group held its first formal meeting in December 2024, but industry representatives reported that documentation requirements for the JCA submission remained unclear, with nine separate annexes required — leading to concerns among smaller biotechs that the burden would be prohibitive.

The first test case for the regulation is the joint assessment of **Novartis’s cancer drug** (a CDK4/6 inhibitor) submitted in January 2025 — the evaluation timeline of 90 days is far shorter than the typical 12–18 months national processes had required. If successful, this could set the precedent for faster patient access. If the review misses its deadline or produces a report that key member states (Germany, France) reject, the credibility of the entire system will be undermined.

From an industry perspective, the regulation is a mixed outcome: while it reduces duplication, it also removes the option of “HTA shopping” — firms used to target countries with weaker evidence demands or faster reviews. Smaller biotechs in particular struggled with the regulation’s requirements, with the European Biotech Federation (EuropaBio) warning in a 2024 white paper that small companies “risk being priced out of the EU market entirely” due to JCA compliance costs. The Commission has responded with a €5 million training programme for SMEs, funded under the EU4Health programme, but uptake has been slow.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a regulation instead of continuing voluntary cooperation?

The EUnetHTA voluntary network from 2004 to 2019 produced joint assessments, but uptake was low — only 35–40% of member states used the reports in their national decisions. The Commission’s 2017 evaluation found that duplication persisted because no member state was obliged to accept the output, allowing countries with strong HTA systems (e.g., Germany’s IQWiG) to ignore the European report and run their own processes. A regulation was necessary to create legal obligations that the previous intergovernmental structure could not enforce.

### Why did the HTAR exclude health economic assessments?

The compromise that enabled passage preserved national sovereignty over pricing and reimbursement. Countries like Germany (G-BA), France (NICE-equivalent HAS), and Denmark argued that cost-effectiveness thresholds depend on local GDP, willingness-to-pay, and budget impact — all nationally determined. Including economic evaluation would have required harmonisation of healthcare funding models, which no member state was willing to cede. The result is that joint clinical assessment remains mandatory, while economic evaluation stays under national control, preserving the core tension the regulation was designed to partially resolve.

### Why did the regulation take so long — 20 years from first pilot?

The main obstacle was political fears among member states of losing control over healthcare budgets and treatment decisions. Early proposals (2004–2010) floated a full EU HTA agency with binding authority, which was rejected as a sovereignty violation by 14 member states, particularly those with strong existing HTA systems (Germany, France) and those worried about being overruled (Poland, Hungary). The breakthrough came only when the Commission adopted a “clinical-only” scope and deferred economic evaluation. The final design also gave member states a majority on the Coordination Group, assuaging sovereignty fears.

### What happens if a member state rejects the joint clinical assessment?

The regulation (Article 23) does not compel a member state to accept the JCA conclusions — it only requires that the *report* be used as the clinical evidence base. A member state may still decide that a drug is not cost-effective, or that local clinical practice differs, and refuse reimbursement. However, if a member state rejects or diverges from the JCA, it must provide a formal justification published in a public database. This transparency requirement is designed to expose unjustified national protectionism or duplication.
