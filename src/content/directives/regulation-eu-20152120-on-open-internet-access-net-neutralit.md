## What is it?

The Net Neutrality Regulation prohibits internet service providers (ISPs) from blocking, slowing down, or discriminating against any legal content, application, or service, regardless of its source or destination. It enshrines the principle that all internet traffic must be treated equally, with only narrow exceptions for reasonable traffic management (e.g., to prevent congestion) and for court‑ordered blocking of illegal content.

The regulation also addresses “zero‑rating” – commercial practices where ISPs do not count certain services (like a particular video‑streaming app) against a user’s data cap. While not banned outright, zero‑rating is subject to strict oversight by national regulators, guided by the Body of European Regulators for Electronic Communications (BEREC). In practice, most zero‑rating offers that favour specific providers have been found to violate the open‑internet principle.

## Why was it introduced?

In 2011, mobile operators T‑Mobile and Orange began blocking Voice‑over‑IP services such as Skype in several European countries, arguing that these calls competed with their own voice revenues. The move sparked a public outcry and a campaign by consumer groups (notably the European Consumer Organisation, BEUC) and digital‑rights activists (EDRi). Meanwhile, in Belgium and the Netherlands, ISPs had started zero‑rating their own video‑on‑demand services, raising fears of a “two‑speed internet.”

The European Commission’s Digital Agenda Commissioner, Neelie Kroes, a vocal net‑neutrality supporter, introduced a draft regulation in September 2013. The proposal aimed to protect the open internet while allowing “specialised services” (such as IPTV or emergency communications) that required guaranteed quality. The telecom industry, led by ETNO (European Telecommunications Network Operators’ Association) and individual operators like Deutsche Telekom and Orange, warned that strict neutrality rules would stifle investment in networks and innovation. They argued for more flexibility, especially to manage congestion and offer commercial zero‑rating deals.

In the European Parliament, the centre‑left (S&D) and Greens joined forces with liberal MEPs to push for a strong ban on blocking and throttling. The Parliament’s industry committee, chaired by German conservative Herbert Reul, initially proposed amendments that would have allowed operators to charge content providers for priority access – a move that would have effectively ended net neutrality. A cross‑party revolt forced Reul to withdraw those amendments, and in April 2014 the Parliament adopted a text that banned all discrimination of internet traffic. The Council of the EU, representing member states, adopted a more industry‑friendly position in March 2015, permitting zero‑rating and specialised services as long as they did not “impair” the open internet.

The final regulation, adopted in November 2015 after tense trilogue negotiations, was a compromise. It kept the core ban on blocking/throttling but allowed “reasonable” traffic management and left the door open for zero‑rating – conditional on national regulator assessments. The timing was influenced by the US Federal Communications Commission’s 2015 Open Internet Order, which had adopted the strongest net‑neutrality rules in US history. The EU wanted to show a comparable commitment, though the European approach ended up being more flexible for telecom operators.

## Timeline

- **2009** – The EU Telecoms Package introduces transparency requirements but no explicit net‑neutrality rules.
- **2011** – T‑Mobile (now part of Orange) and other operators block Skype in several countries; BEUC and EDRi launch campaigns for EU legislation.
- **September 2013** – Commissioner Neelie Kroes proposes a Regulation on open internet access (COM(2013) 627).
- **April 2014** – European Parliament votes for a strong net‑neutrality ban, rejecting industry amendments.
- **March 2015** – Council of the EU adopts a more permissive position, allowing zero‑rating and specialised services.
- **25 November 2015** – Regulation (EU) 2015/2120 adopted after trilogue compromise.
- **30 April 2016** – Regulation applies in all member states (except roaming provisions, which applied from 15 June 2017).
- **30 August 2016** – BEREC publishes its first Net Neutrality Guidelines, interpreting the rules and setting criteria for zero‑rating.
- **2019** – BEREC’s implementation report finds that most zero‑rating offers (e.g., Austrian regulator RTR blocking T‑Mobile’s music‑streaming zero‑rating) have been stopped or modified.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation has given national regulators a clear legal basis to enforce net neutrality. In 2019, the Austrian regulator RTR ordered T‑Mobile Austria to end its “Music Streaming” zero‑rating offer after BEREC found it violated the open‑internet principle. In Germany, the Federal Network Agency forced Vodafone to stop throttling video traffic. The European Commission’s 2021 evaluation confirmed that no major violations of the core ban on blocking or throttling have occurred since implementation, but noted ongoing concerns about zero‑rating practices – particularly in the Netherlands and Poland, where some operators still offer “free” social‑media bundles.

Telecom operators have adapted by shifting away from outright blocking toward more transparent traffic‑management policies. Smaller competitors and startups have benefited: the Commission’s 2021 impact assessment cited a 15% increase in new digital‑service launches in the two years after the regulation entered force, though the figure is qualitative and based on anecdotal evidence from industry reports. The regulation also influenced the 2018 US net‑neutrality repeal – EU policymakers pointed to the European model as a stable alternative.

## Key questions answered

### Can an EU internet provider block or slow down specific websites?

No, the regulation prohibits ISPs from blocking, throttling, or discriminating against any lawful content, application, or service. The only exceptions are court‑ordered blocking, measures to prevent cyberattacks, and “reasonable” traffic management (e.g., stopping congestion during peak times, as long as all traffic of the same type is treated equally). ISPs cannot prioritise or degrade specific services based on commercial agreements.

### What is zero‑rating, and is it allowed in the EU?

Zero‑rating is a commercial practice where an ISP does not count data from a specific service (like a music‑streaming app or social‑media platform) against a user’s monthly data cap. Under EU rules, zero‑rating is not banned outright, but it is subject to strict oversight: national regulators must assess whether it “impairs” the open internet. If a zero‑rating offer favours a specific provider and distorts competition, it is likely to be found illegal. Most such offers have been withdrawn or modified following regulator interventions.

### How does the EU’s net neutrality law differ from the US approach?

The US Federal Communications Commission’s 2015 Open Internet Order classified internet services as common carriers under Title II of the Communications Act, giving it strong authority to ban blocking, throttling, and paid prioritisation. The EU regulation is lighter‑touch: it relies on ex‑post enforcement by national regulators, guided by BEREC, and explicitly allows “specialised services” and reasonable traffic management. The US rule was repealed in 2018, leaving the EU as the world’s largest jurisdiction with a legally binding net‑neutrality framework.

### What happens if a telecom operator violates the rules?

National regulatory authorities (e.g., Ofcom in the UK, BNetzA in Germany) are responsible for enforcement. They can impose fines, order the operator to cease the violation, or require changes to commercial offers. The 2019 case of T‑Mobile Austria being forced to end its music‑streaming zero‑rating shows that regulators will act. The Commission can also intervene if a member state fails to enforce the rules. Penalties vary by country; the maximum fine in Germany, for example, is €500,000.
