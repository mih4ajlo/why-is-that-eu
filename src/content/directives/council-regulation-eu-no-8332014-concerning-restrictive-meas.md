## What is it?

Council Regulation 833/2014 is the legal backbone of the EU's economic sanctions against Russia. Unlike Regulation 269/2014, which targets named individuals through asset freezes, 833/2014 imposes *sectoral* restrictions: bans on exports of dual-use goods, advanced technology, and oil-industry equipment; restrictions on Russian banks' access to EU capital markets; arms embargoes; and, from 2022 onwards, a vast catalogue of import bans covering crude oil, refined fuels, coal, gold, steel, cement and luxury goods.

The Regulation has been amended more than a dozen times since 2014. The post-invasion "packages" — numbered sequentially by the Council and Commission — have layered on SWIFT disconnection for designated banks, the freezing of roughly €200 billion in Russian central bank reserves held in the EU (most of them at Belgium's Euroclear), an oil price cap coordinated with the G7, and a sweeping ban on providing accounting, legal, IT, engineering and consultancy services to Russian entities.

It operates alongside the Common Foreign and Security Policy decisions adopted unanimously by the Council under Article 29 TEU, with Regulation 833/2014 itself adopted under Article 215 TFEU by qualified majority — a procedural detail that has mattered when Hungary threatened vetoes.

## Why was it introduced?

On 17 July 2014, a Buk surface-to-air missile fired from separatist-held eastern Ukraine destroyed Malaysia Airlines flight MH17, killing all 298 people on board, 196 of them Dutch citizens. Within days, an EU that had spent months arguing about whether asset freezes on Putin's inner circle were enough switched course. The Dutch government, previously cautious about damaging its trade with Russia, pushed hard for sectoral measures. Council Regulation 833/2014 was adopted on 31 July 2014 — barely two weeks after the disaster.

The structural tension had been building since February 2014, when Russian forces in unmarked uniforms seized Crimea and Moscow staged a referendum the Commission and Council declared illegal. The initial EU response — travel bans and asset freezes on Crimean officials under Regulation 269/2014 — was widely seen, including by the European Parliament, as inadequate. Germany, with its deep industrial ties to Russia and the Nord Stream pipeline, and Italy, with its energy dependence, resisted moving to sectoral sanctions. France was still planning to deliver two Mistral-class warships to the Russian navy. The MH17 atrocity collapsed that coalition: Chancellor Angela Merkel publicly aligned with the harder line pushed by Poland, the Baltics, Sweden and the UK, and President François Hollande eventually cancelled the Mistral contract.

The Regulation then sat in a kind of stasis for eight years. The Minsk II agreement of February 2015 froze the conflict but never produced compliance, and successive Council presidencies — under Donald Tusk, then Charles Michel — rolled the sanctions over every six months by unanimity, despite periodic objections from Hungary, Cyprus and Italy. Russian counter-sanctions, particularly the 2014 ban on EU agricultural imports, hit Polish apple growers and Baltic dairy producers hard, but the political consensus held.

Then on 24 February 2022, Vladimir Putin ordered the full-scale invasion of Ukraine. The transformation of Regulation 833/2014 from a targeted instrument into the most comprehensive sanctions regime ever imposed on a major economy happened in days. Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, working with High Representative Josep Borrell and a Franco-German axis suddenly aligned with the previously hawkish eastern member states, pushed through the first three "packages" within a fortnight. The decisive moment came when Berlin — under new Chancellor Olaf Scholz — abandoned the Nord Stream 2 pipeline and accepted the freezing of Russian central bank reserves, a step previously considered unthinkable in financial diplomacy. By June 2022, the sixth package included a phased embargo on Russian seaborne crude oil; by December, the G7 price cap was in place.

The coalition dynamics shifted again with Hungary's Viktor Orbán, who extracted carve-outs for pipeline oil (preserving Druzhba deliveries to Hungary, Slovakia and Czechia) and repeatedly delayed packages until the last moment. Cyprus negotiated exemptions on services restrictions to protect its professional sector. But unanimity, remarkably, held through more than a dozen successive packages.

## Timeline

- **February–March 2014** — Russia annexes Crimea; EU adopts first individual sanctions (Regulation 269/2014).
- **17 July 2014** — MH17 shot down over Donetsk oblast.
- **31 July 2014** — Council adopts Regulation 833/2014, introducing sectoral sanctions on finance, arms, dual-use goods and oil technology.
- **September 2014** — Sanctions tightened; major Russian state banks barred from EU capital markets.
- **2015–2021** — Sanctions rolled over every six months; scope largely unchanged despite repeated Hungarian objections.
- **24 February 2022** — Russia launches full-scale invasion of Ukraine.
- **February–March 2022** — Packages 1–4: SWIFT disconnection for seven Russian banks, freezing of Russian central bank reserves, ban on transactions with the CBR, exclusion of Russian aircraft from EU airspace.
- **April 2022** — Fifth package: ban on Russian coal imports.
- **June 2022** — Sixth package: phased embargo on Russian seaborne crude oil and refined products.
- **December 2022** — Eighth package implements the G7 oil price cap mechanism.
- **2023–2024** — Successive packages (10th, 11th, 12th, 13th, 14th) tighten anti-circumvention rules and target third-country re-exports.
- **2024** — Council agrees to use windfall profits from frozen Russian sovereign assets at Euroclear to support Ukraine.

## Impact and consequences

The European Central Bank reported that EU–Russia trade collapsed dramatically after 2022, with Russia's share of EU goods imports falling from around 7% before the invasion to roughly 2% by 2023. Russian pipeline gas, which according to Eurostat supplied over 40% of EU gas imports in 2021, fell to a small fraction of that within two years — replaced by LNG (much of it American and Qatari), Norwegian pipeline gas, and demand destruction. Germany shut down its remaining Russian gas dependency at considerable cost; the Commission's REPowerEU plan, presented by Energy Commissioner Kadri Simson in May 2022, was designed explicitly to accelerate that decoupling.

Enforcement has been uneven. The Commission appointed David O'Sullivan as International Special Envoy for the implementation of EU sanctions in late 2022, specifically to address circumvention through third countries — Turkey, the UAE, Kazakhstan, Armenia and Georgia all saw sharp spikes in exports of sanctioned categories to Russia. The 11th and 12th packages introduced an anti-circumvention tool allowing the EU to restrict exports to specific third countries acting as conduits, though it has been used sparingly.

European companies have absorbed substantial losses. According to filings compiled by the Kyiv School of Economics, large EU firms including TotalEnergies, BP (UK), Shell, BASF/Wintershall, Renault and Société Générale wrote down billions of euros in Russian assets in 2022. Around €200 billion in Russian central bank reserves remain immobilised at Euroclear in Brussels; in 2024 the EU agreed to channel the windfall profits — running at several billion euros annually — to Ukraine, while leaving the underlying principal untouched.

The oil price cap, set initially at $60 per barrel, was designed by the G7 and operationalised through EU regulation to keep Russian oil flowing to global markets while limiting Kremlin revenue. The Commission's reviews and IEA data suggest it suppressed Russian export earnings significantly in 2023, though enforcement weakened in 2024 as a "shadow fleet" of tankers using non-Western insurance expanded.

## Key questions answered

### Why can Hungary keep buying Russian oil when the rest of the EU cannot?

When the EU agreed its oil embargo in June 2022, Viktor Orbán threatened to veto the entire sixth sanctions package unless landlocked countries dependent on the Druzhba pipeline — Hungary, Slovakia and Czechia — were exempted. The compromise allowed pipeline crude to keep flowing while banning seaborne imports, which accounted for the large majority of EU purchases. Hungary has since used each subsequent package as leverage, delaying agreement and extracting further carve-outs, including on nuclear cooperation with Rosatom for the Paks II reactor.

### What happened to the €200 billion in frozen Russian central bank money?

Most of it sits at Euroclear, the Belgian securities depository, where it cannot be touched without breaching sovereign immunity principles that the EU's legal services and the ECB have warned against violating. After two years of debate, the Council agreed in 2024 to a compromise: the principal stays frozen, but the windfall profits generated by the immobilised assets — interest and reinvestment income — are transferred to a fund supporting Ukraine. The US and UK have pushed for more aggressive use of the assets themselves; the EU, led by ECB President Christine Lagarde's warnings about euro reserve credibility, has so far resisted.

### Have the sanctions actually hurt Russia's economy?

The evidence is mixed and contested. The Russian economy did not collapse as some 2022 forecasts predicted — military spending and import substitution from China kept GDP growing in 2023 and 2024 according to IMF figures. But the Bank of Finland's BOFIT institute and the European Commission's economic reports document that Russia has lost access to advanced semiconductors, aviation parts and Western oil-services technology, and that its energy revenues fell sharply in 2023 under the oil price cap. The longer-term industrial degradation, particularly in oil and gas production capacity, is what EU policymakers point to when defending the regime.

### Can European companies still do business in Russia?

Many can, but the scope has narrowed dramatically. Energy, finance, dual-use technology, advanced electronics, luxury goods, accounting, legal advisory, IT consultancy and management consulting services to Russian entities are all prohibited. Companies like Raiffeisen Bank International, which still operates in Russia, have come under sustained pressure from the ECB and US Treasury to exit. The 14th package introduced "best efforts" obligations requiring EU parent companies to ensure their non-EU subsidiaries do not undermine sanctions — a significant extraterritorial step.
