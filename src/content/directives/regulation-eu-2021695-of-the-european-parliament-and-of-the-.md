## What is it?

Horizon Europe is the European Union's seventh research and innovation framework programme, covering the period from 2021 to 2027. It succeeds Horizon 2020 (2014-2020) and represents the largest multinational research programme in the world. With a total budget of €95.5 billion (in current prices, including €5.4 billion from the NextGenerationEU recovery instrument), it funds collaborative projects across three pillars: Excellent Science (via the European Research Council and Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions), Global Challenges and European Industrial Competitiveness (clusters covering health, climate, energy, digital, and more), and Innovative Europe (via the European Innovation Council and the European Institute of Innovation and Technology).

The regulation sets out the rules for participation, the strategic planning process, and a new feature: five "EU Missions" — bold, measurable targets such as restoring ocean health by 2030, adapting to climate change for 150 regions, and curing 100 cancers. It also introduces a simplified funding model using a single, fixed reimbursement rate of 100% for most actions, reducing the administrative burden compared to Horizon 2020.

## Why was it introduced?

The push for Horizon Europe began against a backdrop of stark structural tension: Europe’s booming tech sector was failing to translate world-class research into commercial success. The European Commission’s 2018 impact assessment noted that the EU accounted for only 20% of global research and development expenditure but produced 28% of the world's scientific publications. Yet it produced dramatically fewer unicorns than the US or China. As the then-Commissioner for Research, Science and Innovation Carlos Moedas put it, "we are good at science, we are good at innovation, but we are not good at turning science into growth."

The immediate trigger was the Commission's 2017 reflection paper on the future of EU finances, followed by Moedas’s mid-term review of Horizon 2020. That review identified that Horizon 2020, while successful in funding frontier research, had failed to channel enough funding to disruptive, high-risk innovation. European industry associations like BusinessEurope and the European Association of Research and Technology Organisations (EARTO) lobbied for a larger budget — the Commission's initial proposal in June 2018 was €100 billion, a 30% increase over Horizon 2020. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Christian Ehler (EPP, Germany), pushed for even more, eventually settling on €95.5 billion after bitter budget negotiations with the Council in July 2020.

A key political moment was the realization during negotiations that Horizon 2020's budget had been cut from €80 billion to €77 billion, and the 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework was also under severe strain from Brexit and the UK's departure from the programme (though a third-country association agreement was later reached in 2023). The COVID-19 pandemic, which broke out in early 2020 during the final stage of the trilogue, fundamentally reshaped priorities. The European Council in July 2020 agreed to tie Horizon Europe to the NextGenerationEU recovery plan, injecting an additional €5.4 billion and explicitly linking research funding to the green and digital transitions. The final regulation included expanded focus areas: pandemic preparedness, digital health, AI, and climate-neutral technologies.

The coalition that supported the regulation included the Commission's Directorate-General for Research and Innovation (led by Commissioner Mariya Gabriel), the European Parliament’s ITRE Committee, Member States from the "Innovation Union" camp (notably Finland, Sweden, and Germany), and the European University Association (EUA). Opposition came primarily from the European Court of Auditors (which questioned the additionality of the new "missions" and "partnerships" compared to existing instruments) and from budget-sceptic Member States like Austria, the Netherlands, and Denmark, which argued that the €12.5 billion increase over Horizon 2020 was excessive. Those states succeeded in trimming the Commission's original €100 billion proposal to €83.1 billion in the Council's July 2020 deal, before the Parliament and NextGenerationEU pushed it back up.

## Timeline

- **2017** — The Commission publishes a reflection paper on the future of EU finances, sparking debate on the next research framework programme
- **21 March 2018** — Commissioner Moedas presents the Horizon Europe proposal, with a €100 billion budget request
- **July 2018** — The European Parliament's ITRE Committee begins its work, with Christian Ehler as rapporteur
- **April 2019** — The Parliament adopts its first-reading position, supporting the €100 billion figure
- **December 2019** — The von der Leyen Commission takes office; Mariya Gabriel becomes Commissioner for Innovation, Research, Culture, Education and Youth
- **July 2020** — The European Council agrees on a €83.1 billion Horizon Europe budget as part of the broader MFF deal; the Parliament resists
- **11 December 2020** — Political agreement in the trilogue: €95.5 billion, including €5.4 billion from NextGenerationEU
- **28 April 2021** — Regulation formally adopted by the Council and Parliament
- **1 January 2021** — Regulation enters into force retroactively; first calls for proposals open
- **2022** — First missions launched: Cancer, Climate Adaptation, Ocean, Climate-Neutral Cities, Soil Health
- **2023** — The UK signs an association agreement to Horizon Europe, ending a two-year post-Brexit deadlock
- **By 2027** — First interim evaluation reports expected; full end-of-programme evaluation due in 2029

## Impact and consequences

Horizon Europe has been both lauded and criticised. By early 2025, the programme had funded over 15,000 projects, with a success rate around 14% (similar to Horizon 2020). The European Institute of Innovation and Technology (EIT) has expanded to 9 Knowledge and Innovation Communities (KICs), including a new one on cultural and creative industries. The European Innovation Council (EIC), a new addition, awarded its first "Seal of Excellence" certifications to high-quality proposals that could not be funded due to budget limits, helping startups attract alternative co-financing.

The missions have been controversial: a 2024 report from the European Court of Auditors found that their governance was too complex and that the €1.6 billion budget allocated to missions from 2021-2023 was insufficient to meet the "bold, time-bound" targets originally set. For example, the "50 cancer centres" mission had only funded 5 centres by early 2025. Industry remained divided: large R&D spenders like Siemens, SAP, and Airbus continued to benefit significantly, but SMEs often complained that the administrative burden of the grants — despite simplifications — remained too high.

The most significant structural change is the shift away from a "fixed triple-A" reimbursement model (direct costs plus 25% indirect costs) to a flat 100% reimbursement of direct costs plus a 25% flat rate for indirect costs. This simplified the model but made it more expensive for participants (since indirect costs are no longer audited but capped). The programme has also faced questions from the European Parliament about the widening participation gap — applicants from high-performing Member States (Germany, France, Netherlands) account for nearly 40% of all funded projects, while inland and southern Member States (Croatia, Romania, Greece) struggle to participate.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU's main research funding programme create "Missions" instead of sticking with the old project structure?

The missions concept was championed by Commissioner Moedas and modelled on the Apollo programme and the UN Sustainable Development Goals. The idea was to shift from funding research topics to achieving clear, measurable societal outcomes. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment argued that Horizon 2020 had spread funding too thinly across "bottleneck" challenges; missions would focus resources on a few visible, high-stakes targets that could capture public and political attention. The Parliament and Council broadly supported the idea, but the Court of Auditors later criticised their implementation as underfunded and poorly coordinated.

### Why was the budget for EU research funding cut from €100 billion to €95.5 billion?

The €4.5 billion cut came during the July 2020 European Council, where net contributor states (Austria, Denmark, the Netherlands, Sweden) demanded overall MFF reductions. The Commission proposed €100 billion, the Parliament supported it, but the Council initially settled on €83.1 billion. The final figure of €95.5 billion — plus the €5.4 billion from NextGenerationEU — was a compromise brokered by European Council President Charles Michel, allowing the Parliament to claim victory (total €100.9 billion with recovery funds) while the Council saved face by capping core EU budget spending.

### Why did it take the UK so long to rejoin the EU's big research funding programme?

Post-Brexit, the UK became a third country but continued to participate in Horizon 2020 until the end of 2020. The EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement (signed December 2020) included a protocol on Horizon Europe association, but the EU delayed formalisation due to disputes over the Northern Ireland Protocol and the UK's refusal to pay the €2.6 billion contribution the EU demanded for 2021-2027. The deadlock was broken in September 2023 when the EU and UK reached a new financial agreement after the Windsor Framework resolved the post-Brexit trading arrangements.
