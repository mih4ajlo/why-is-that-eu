## What is it?

The NIS2 Directive (Network and Information Security 2) is the European Union’s updated legal framework to improve cybersecurity resilience across the Union. It replaces the original NIS Directive (2016/1148/EU) and sets binding requirements for a broader range of sectors, including energy, transport, banking, health, digital infrastructure, public administration, and food supply. The directive forces companies to adopt risk-management measures—such as encryption, access controls, and incident detection—and to report significant cyber incidents to national authorities within 24 hours.

NIS2 applies to two tiers: "essential entities" (e.g., large energy companies, banks, hospitals) and "important entities" (e.g., smaller digital providers, postal services). Essential entities face stricter oversight, including ex-ante inspections, while important entities are subject to ex-post enforcement. Member states must transpose the directive into national law by 17 October 2024, and penalties for non-compliance can reach up to €10 million or 2% of worldwide annual turnover for essential entities.

## Why was it introduced?

NIS2 was born from a recognition that the original NIS Directive, adopted in 2016, was too weak, fragmented, and slow to adapt to escalating cyber threats. The original directive left too much discretion to member states: it defined sectors vaguely, allowed voluntary self-classification, and set inconsistent incident reporting thresholds. As a result, cyber resilience varied wildly across the EU. In 2020, the European Commission’s review found that only 30% of member states had fully transposed the original NIS rules, and that many critical operators—like hospitals, energy grids, and cloud providers—fell outside its scope.

The catalyst for tougher regulation was a series of high-profile cyber incidents that exposed systemic vulnerabilities. In 2017, the NotPetya ransomware attack disrupted shipping giant Maersk, costing €300 million, and the WannaCry attack crippled the UK’s National Health Service. In 2020, a cyberattack on the Czech hospital system in Brno forced a full shutdown during the COVID-19 pandemic. The EU’s own institutions, including the European Parliament, experienced repeated breaches. These events demonstrated that cyber attacks were no longer just data breaches but could paralyse critical infrastructure.

Industry lobbying was split. Large tech firms and cloud providers (e.g., Microsoft, Google) generally supported harmonised rules to avoid a patchwork of national laws. Energy and telecom operators argued for lighter touch, fearing compliance costs. However, the EU’s political leadership—especially the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen (a former German defence minister who prioritised digital sovereignty)—pushed for a more muscular approach. The 2020 EU Cybersecurity Strategy explicitly called for a "new generation" of rules, and the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, which triggered a surge in state-sponsored cyberattacks on EU infrastructure, added urgency.

Alternatives considered included a regulation (which would be directly binding) or a narrower update to the 2016 directive. But the Commission chose a revised directive (NIS2) to retain flexibility for member states while raising minimum standards. The final text, agreed in 2022, expands sector coverage from 7 to 18 sectors, introduces strict liability for top-level management, and creates a new European Cybersecurity Alert System.

## Timeline

- **July 2016** — Original NIS Directive (2016/1148) adopted, aiming to improve EU cybersecurity baseline.
- **2017** — NotPetya and WannaCry attacks cause global disruptions, highlighting weaknesses in critical infrastructure protection.
- **May 2018** — Entry into force of the GDPR, raising awareness of data protection but not covering operational security.
- **December 2020** — European Commission publishes EU Cybersecurity Strategy, calling for an updated NIS Directive.
- **December 2020** — Commission adopts proposal for NIS2 Directive, expanding scope and introducing stricter obligations.
- **May 2022** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement on the final text.
- **November 2022** — NIS2 Directive published in Official Journal of the EU.
- **17 January 2023** — Directive enters into force.
- **17 October 2024** — Deadline for member states to transpose NIS2 into national law.
- **2025 onward** — Gradual implementation: essential entities face ex-ante inspections; important entities subject to ex-post enforcement.

## Impact and consequences

NIS2 has significantly expanded the perimeter of EU cybersecurity regulation. Companies that previously faced no mandatory cyber obligations—such as postal services, food processors, and public administrations—now must invest in risk management. The directive has forced boards and C-suite leaders to grapple with personal liability for security failures, a shift from earlier voluntary frameworks.

Industry response has been mixed. Large firms in energy, finance, and telecom have invested in compliance teams and security audits, seeing it as a de facto standard. Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and local governments, especially in Eastern and Southern Europe, have struggled with compliance costs, leading to calls for more EU funding and guidance. Some sectors (e.g., cloud providers) have lobbied for mutual recognition of third-country certifications.

In practice, NIS2 has shortened incident reporting times from 72 hours (under original NIS) to 24 hours for initial notification. This has forced companies to improve detection capabilities. The directive also requires member states to set up a single national contact point and cooperate through the EU-CyCLONe network. Non-compliance penalties—up to €10 million or 2% of turnover—have already deterred some companies from underreporting. Lithuania, for example, has fined a telecom operator for failing to classify itself accurately.

## Key questions answered

### Why was NIS2 needed instead of just updating the original NIS Directive?

The original 2016 directive had weak enforcement, insufficient sector coverage (only 7 sectors), and inconsistent incident reporting thresholds across member states. Cyberattacks on hospitals, energy grids, and digital infrastructure surged after 2017, and the EU feared that national fragmentation would undermine the single market and collective resilience. A new directive allowed the EU to expand scope, introduce stricter liability, and mandate faster reporting without the direct binding effect of a regulation.

### Why are top-level managers now personally liable under NIS2?

The EU determined that previous laws gave cybersecurity insufficient boardroom attention. In national consultations, many companies reported that security budgets were cut or delayed because executives saw it as a technical issue, not a business risk. By imposing personal liability for negligence, NIS2 aims to embed cybersecurity into corporate governance—similar to how GDPR made data protection a board-level priority. This was inspired by the US Sarbanes-Oxley Act for financial accountability.

### Why does NIS2 cover food supply and public administration?

The 2020 review found that attacks on food processors and local governments—such as the 2021 ransomware attack on the Irish Health Service Executive—could disrupt essential public services and supply chains. Food supply was added after the COVID-19 pandemic exposed digital dependence in logistics; public administration was included because many member states had not regulated their own government IT. The EU’s goal was to widen the net so no critical operator is left unprotected.

**How does NIS2 differ from the EU Cybersecurity Act (EU 2019/881)?**
The Cybersecurity Act created a voluntary certification framework for products and services (e.g., the EUCC scheme for common criteria). NIS2 is a mandatory, risk-management and incident-reporting directive for organisations. They complement each other: under NIS2, essential entities can use EU-certified products to meet compliance obligations, but certification remains optional. The Cybersecurity Act also established ENISA’s permanent mandate; NIS2 gives ENISA a stronger operational role in incident coordination.
