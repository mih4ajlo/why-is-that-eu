## What is it?

The European Account Preservation Order (EAPO) is a regulation that creates a single, uniform procedure for freezing a debtor’s bank accounts across the European Union (except Denmark and, initially, the United Kingdom). A creditor can apply for the order in the competent court of a single member state — where the debtor lives or where the debt is to be enforced — and then serve that order on any bank in any participating member state. The bank must immediately freeze the amount claimed, without needing a separate court proceeding in its own country.

The EAPO is not a judgment on the merits of the debt; it is a protective measure. The creditor must later obtain a final judgment to actually seize the money. The order can be obtained without notifying the debtor first (ex parte) if the creditor can show an urgent need — for example, a real risk that the debtor will dissipate assets. To prevent abuse, the creditor must provide security (usually a bank guarantee) and can be held liable for damages if the underlying claim fails. The regulation entered into force in 2014 and became fully applicable on 18 January 2017.

## Why was it introduced?

Before 2017, a creditor trying to freeze a bank account in another EU country faced a Kafkaesque maze of procedures. In most member states, a domestic freezing order — known as a *saisie conservatoire* in France, an *ingiunzione* in Italy, or a *Mareva injunction* in common‑law systems — had no automatic effect across the border. The creditor had to first obtain an order in the debtor’s country through a time‑consuming and costly *exequatur* procedure. Delays of six months to a year were common, during which the debtor could easily empty the account.

The problem was most acute for small and medium‑sized enterprises (SMEs). According to the European Commission’s 2011 impact assessment, cross‑border debt recovery costs could amount to 15–30% of the claim value — often enough to make enforcement uneconomical. The Commission estimated that cross‑border debt in the EU totalled around €600 billion annually, with a significant portion never recovered.

The political push came from two directions. First, the European Parliament, led by the Legal Affairs Committee, had long argued that the EU’s “area of freedom, security and justice” was incomplete without a truly cross‑border asset‑freezing tool. Second, business associations — particularly the SME lobby group UEAPME and the European Payments Council — complained that the patchwork of national procedures gave a structural advantage to fraudulent debtors who simply shifted money across borders.

The timing was ripe under the Barroso II Commission, where Vice‑President Viviane Reding (Justice) made the EAPO a key deliverable of the 2010 Stockholm Programme on judicial cooperation. A public consultation in 2009 had shown overwhelming support: 85% of respondents, mostly legal practitioners and business organisations, favoured a uniform EU procedure. Only a handful of member states — notably Germany and the UK — raised concerns about debtor protection, fearing that ex parte freezing could be abused.

The negotiation in the Council was unusually quick for a civil‑justice file. The main compromise was the inclusion of a mandatory security deposit, which satisfied German concerns, and the exclusion of Denmark and the UK (the latter opted out of the regulation under Protocol 21). The regulation was adopted by the European Parliament on 15 May 2014 with 641 votes in favour, 8 against, and 12 abstentions. It was formally approved by the Council on 12 June 2014.

What changed this time? Earlier attempts to harmonise cross‑border seizure of assets had stalled because of the sheer diversity of national procedural laws. But by 2011, the EU already had three successful “European procedures” — the European Enforcement Order, the European Payment Order, and the European Small Claims Procedure — which had created a blueprint for mutual recognition without intermediate *exequatur*. The EAPO was the logical next step: if a judgment could circulate freely, why not a freezing order?

## Timeline

- **2004** — The European Commission issues a Green Paper on improving cross‑border debt recovery, highlighting the absence of a uniform freezing mechanism.
- **2008** — The European Parliament’s Legal Affairs Committee calls for a regulation on “European assets preservation orders” in a resolution.
- **2010** — The Stockholm Programme (December 2009) sets “facilitating the recovery of debts” as a priority; Vice‑President Viviane Reding announces legislative plans.
- **25 July 2011** — The Commission publishes its proposal for a European Account Preservation Order (COM(2011) 445 final), accompanied by an impact assessment.
- **15 May 2014** — The European Parliament adopts the regulation with 641 votes in favour, 8 against, 12 abstentions.
- **12 June 2014** — The Council of the EU formally adopts the regulation.
- **17 July 2014** — The regulation is published in the Official Journal (L 189, pp. 59–90) and enters into force the following day.
- **18 January 2017** — The regulation becomes fully applicable, giving member states 18 months to adapt national procedural rules.
- **2019** — The first European Account Preservation Order database of competent courts and enforcement authorities goes live on the e‑Justice Portal.

## Impact and consequences

The EAPO significantly reduced the time and cost of cross‑border asset freezing. Practitioners report that a standard ex parte application now takes three to five weeks from filing to enforcement, compared with six to twelve months before. The Commission’s own 2016 evaluation of the regulation (before full application) estimated potential savings of €3,000–€9,000 per case for SMEs.

However, uptake has been slower than expected. The European Commission’s 2022 report on the operation of the regulation found that many national courts still lacked clear procedures for issuing preservation orders in English or other widely spoken languages, and that some banks were slow to comply. The report also noted a “low number of applications” in several member states, attributed to insufficient awareness among small businesses and lawyers.

Debtor‑protection measures have worked largely as intended. National courts have rarely imposed damages for wrongful freezing, and the requirement to provide security has deterred frivolous applications. The regulation has also spurred procedural reforms: several member states — including France and Italy — updated their domestic freezing laws to align with the EAPO’s common standards, benefiting domestic debt recovery as a side effect.

## Key questions answered

### Can I freeze someone’s bank account in another EU country without going to court there?

Yes — if the other country is a participating EU member state. You apply once to the competent court in the country where the debtor resides or where the debt is enforceable. That court issues the Preservation Order, and you serve it on any bank in the target country. The bank must freeze the amount immediately, without requiring any local court order. The procedure is uniform across all participating states.

### What protects the person whose account is frozen?

Several safeguards. First, you as the creditor must usually provide a security deposit (often a bank guarantee) to cover potential damages if your claim later fails. Second, the debtor can challenge the order after it is enforced, and if you lose the underlying case, you may have to pay compensation for any harm caused by the freeze. Third, the order cannot exceed the value of the debt claimed, and the debtor’s basic living expenses (e.g., salary, pension) are partially protected under national law.

### Does this regulation replace national freezing orders?

No. The European Account Preservation Order is an *alternative* to domestic freezing orders. Creditors can still use national procedures if they prefer, but those procedures do not automatically cross borders. The EAPO only applies to cross‑border cases — where the bank account is in a different EU country than the creditor, the debtor’s residence, or the court where the application is filed. For purely domestic cases, national rules remain unchanged.

### Why doesn’t this cover all EU countries?

Denmark opted out of the entire area of freedom, security and justice, so the regulation never applied there. The United Kingdom, before Brexit, was also an opt‑out — and post‑Brexit, the regulation has no effect in the UK at all (though the EU‑UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement includes some mutual recognition of freezing orders for certain civil claims). Ireland, which has a flexible opt‑in on justice measures, chose to participate and is bound by the regulation.
