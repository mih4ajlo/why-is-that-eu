## What is it?

The EU Taxonomy Regulation is a classification system that defines which economic activities can be marketed as environmentally sustainable. It sets performance thresholds (technical screening criteria) across six environmental objectives: climate change mitigation and adaptation, sustainable use of water, circular economy, pollution prevention, and biodiversity protection. An activity qualifies as taxonomy-aligned if it contributes substantially to at least one objective, does no significant harm to the other five (the DNSH principle), and meets minimum social safeguards.

The regulation applies to financial market participants offering financial products in the EU, large companies (over 500 employees) already subject to the Non-Financial Reporting Directive, and companies issuing green bonds. They must disclose what share of their turnover, capital expenditure, or operating expenditure is taxonomy-aligned. The regulation entered into force in July 2020, with climate-related criteria applying from January 2022 and the remaining four environmental objectives from January 2023.

## Why was it introduced?

The EU Taxonomy was born from a regulatory vacuum exposed by the 2015-2016 Volkswagen diesel emissions scandal. In September 2015, the US Environmental Protection Agency revealed that Volkswagen had installed defeat devices in 11 million vehicles worldwide to cheat emissions tests. The scandal cost Volkswagen over €30 billion in fines, settlements, and buybacks by 2020, according to company financial statements. It became a symbol of how "green" claims could be entirely unverifiable: VW's "clean diesel" marketing had been certified as compliant by regulators using tests that bore no relation to real-world driving.

The scandal had a direct European policy echo. In December 2015, as the Paris Agreement was being signed, the European Commission's High-Level Expert Group on Sustainable Finance began meeting. Chaired by Christian Thimann, then at AXA, the group included representatives from the European Investment Bank, WWF, and major banks. Their January 2018 final report explicitly warned: "The term 'sustainable' is currently used in so many different ways that it has lost meaning. This creates confusion, greenwashing, and ultimately distrust among investors." The report recommended a unified classification system.

Political momentum accelerated under the Juncker Commission's Capital Markets Union agenda, but the decisive push came from the von der Leyen Commission's 2019 European Green Deal. Ursula von der Leyen made sustainable finance a pillar of her €1 trillion investment plan to make Europe climate-neutral by 2050. The Taxonomy was presented as essential infrastructure: without knowing what "green" meant, the market could not channel capital toward the Green Deal's objectives. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Bas Eickhout (Greens/EFA, Netherlands), pushed for stricter environmental criteria and exclusion of nuclear and gas.

The most contested element was the inclusion of gas and nuclear energy. In December 2021, the Commission published a draft Complementary Delegated Act classifying certain gas and nuclear activities as "transitional" activities, provided they met specific conditions (e.g., gas plants must replace coal plants and meet emissions thresholds). Environmental NGOs like WWF and ClientEarth sued the European Commission in March 2022 over the gas and nuclear classification, arguing it violated the DNSH principle. The European Parliament attempted to veto: a January 2022 resolution to reject the Delegated Act, proposed by MEPs from the Greens, Socialists, and Left, failed with 328 votes against, 278 in favour, and 33 abstentions — a margin closer than most observers expected.

The timing of the final regulation's adoption, in mid-2020, was shaped by the COVID-19 pandemic. The Taxonomy was positioned as a key tool for the NextGenerationEU recovery fund to direct €750 billion (in 2018 prices) toward sustainable investments. At the SameTime, the Commission faced a coalition of member states led by France and supported by Poland, Czechia, and Hungary, arguing that nuclear should be classified as sustainable to meet decarbonisation targets. Germany, Austria, and Luxembourg opposed this, citing waste and safety concerns. The compromise solution, reached after months of delay, placed gas and nuclear in a separate Complementary Delegated Act published in February 2022, with stricter conditions for nuclear (e.g., waste disposal plans, safety upgrades by 2045).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects approximately 50,000 large EU companies required to disclose taxonomy alignment in their non-financial reports, according to the Commission's 2022 impact assessment. Financial market participants managing over €500 billion in assets (based on ESMA's 2023 market monitoring) must disclose the taxonomy alignment of their products. Initial compliance data from the first reporting year (2022) showed extremely low alignment: the EEA's 2023 analysis found that under 5% of large companies' turnover was taxonomy-aligned on average, with real estate and manufacturing leading and agriculture trailing.

The regulation's definitional power has significant economic consequences. In 2023, the European Banking Authority found that 80% of loan portfolios of major EU banks contained some exposure to taxonomy-aligned activities, but banks struggled to assess alignment for SME borrowers lacking reporting capacity. The Platform on Sustainable Finance, established under Article 20 of the regulation, has faced criticism from industry that the technical screening criteria are too complex: the Commission's 2023 evaluation heard from 57% of stakeholders that "usability" remains a major barrier, particularly for smaller companies.

Political backlash has been concentrated around the gas and nuclear classification. Austria's government, led by Chancellor Karl Nehammer, formally announced in October 2022 it would support the legal challenge against the Complementary Delegated Act. In 2023, the European Ombudsman found maladministration in the Commission's handling of stakeholder consultations on the gas and nuclear criteria.

Greenwashing enforcement has begun. In May 2023, the Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) issued its first fines under the Taxonomy and Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), fining asset manager Robeco €0.5 million for misleading sustainability claims, citing mismatches between funds' marketing labels and taxonomy alignment disclosures. By mid-2024, national regulators in France (AMF), Germany (BaFin), and the UK (FCA) had issued formal warnings to over 200 financial firms about non-compliant green labelling.

## Key questions answered

### How does Europe define a "green" investment? 

The EU Taxonomy sets six environmental goals and establishes technical screening criteria for economic activities to qualify as "environmentally sustainable." An activity must contribute substantially to at least one goal, do no significant harm to the other five (including social safeguards), and meet minimum labour and human rights protections. Companies and financial products that meet these criteria can legally claim their shares or investments are "taxonomy-aligned."

### Can fossil fuels like natural gas ever be considered "green" under EU rules? 

Yes, under limited and controversial conditions. The Taxonomy's Complementary Delegated Act classifies certain gas-fired power plants as "transitional" activities if they replace coal plants and meet strict emissions thresholds. Nuclear energy is also included under conditions related to waste management and safety upgrades. Environmental groups have sued the Commission over these inclusions, arguing they violate the "do no significant harm" principle.

### Does the Taxonomy apply to small businesses? 

Not directly. The disclosure obligations apply to large companies (over 500 employees) and financial market participants. However, small and medium-sized enterprises are indirectly affected because banks and investors ask them to provide data on whether their activities are taxonomy-aligned when assessing loan or investment eligibility. The EU is developing simplified reporting standards for SMEs, but as of early 2024, the burden remains a concern.

### Have any companies been penalised for breaking these rules? 

Yes. In May 2023, the Dutch regulator AFM fined asset manager Robeco €0.5 million for misleading sustainability claims that did not match their Taxonomy disclosures. By mid-2024, over 200 financial firms across the EU had received formal warnings from national regulators about non-compliant green marketing. No major criminal cases have been concluded yet, but the European Securities and Markets Authority conducts annual sweeps to identify greenwashing patterns.
