## What is it?

Directive 2009/110/EC defines "electronic money" as a digital store of value that can be used to make payments, typically held on prepaid cards or in online accounts. It establishes a single licence for companies that issue e-money — known as electronic money institutions (EMIs) — allowing them to operate across the EU under the supervision of their home member state.

The directive sets prudential requirements (initial capital, own funds, safeguarding of customer money) that are considerably lighter than those for credit institutions. EMIs are not allowed to take deposits — they simply issue electronic value in exchange for funds, which must be redeemable at par. The framework covers everything from mobile wallets to prepaid transit cards, and sits alongside the Payment Services Directive (PSD), which governs payment initiation and account information services.

## Why was it introduced?

The first attempt to regulate e-money — Directive 2000/46/EC — was a failure. By 2003, the European Commission’s internal review found that fewer than ten electronic money institutions had been licensed across the entire EU. The 2000 directive had imposed an initial capital requirement of €1 million and treated e-money issuance almost like banking, deterring innovators. Prepaid mobile phone credit, travel cards, and early online wallets fell into legal grey areas or were forced to use costly banking licences.

The real political push came after the Payment Services Directive (2007/64/EC) created a new category of payment institutions with lower barriers. Industry players — notably PayPal, which had expanded rapidly across the Atlantic after its 2002 acquisition by eBay — argued that the e-money regime needed similar liberalisation. The London-based Electronic Money Association, representing prepaid card issuers and mobile payment firms, lobbied the European Commission’s Internal Market Directorate-General for a rewrite.

Internal Market Commissioner Charlie McCreevy (2004–2010) made the revision a priority as part of the Commission’s “Better Regulation” agenda. The European Central Bank initially resisted lowering capital requirements, warning that weak regulation could undermine monetary stability. But the Commission’s 2005 impact assessment showed that high barriers were stifling innovation without delivering consumer protection benefits — most e-money was already held in accounts that were effectively deposits, and no major fraud cases had been attributed to weak oversight.

The breakthrough came in 2006 when the Commission launched a formal public consultation. The resulting proposal (October 2008) cut the minimum initial capital from €1 million to €350,000 and introduced a sliding scale for ongoing own funds based on transaction volume. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, Arlene McCarthy (UK, S&D), pushed for stronger consumer safeguards, including mandatory redeemability within 30 days. The final text, adopted in September 2009 by a comfortable majority in the Parliament (543 in favour, 69 against), also allowed EMIs to combine e-money issuance with payment services under a single regime for the first time.

## Timeline

- **2000** — Directive 2000/46/EC on electronic money adopted; very few institutions licensed.
- **2003** — Commission review finds fewer than ten EMIs licensed; identifies high barriers as the cause.
- **2006** — Commission launches public consultation on revising the e-money framework.
- **2008** — Commission proposal for a new directive published (October).
- **2009** — Directive 2009/110/EC adopted by Council and Parliament (September); enters into force 20 days later.
- **2011** — Deadline for transposition into national law (30 April).
- **2012** — Commission begins first implementation checks; several member states, including Greece and Luxembourg, notified late.
- **2018** — Commission evaluation under the REFIT programme finds that the directive has “functioned well” but notes fragmentation in supervisory practices.
- **2020** — Digital Finance Package proposes to merge e-money and payment institution regimes into a single regulatory framework (ongoing).

## Impact and consequences

The directive succeeded in expanding the e-money market. By 2015, the Commission reported that over 150 electronic money institutions were operating across the EU, up from a handful under the previous regime. PayPal obtained its first EU e-money licence from the Luxembourg regulator in 2007 (under transitional arrangements) and later moved to a Belgian licence in 2013 after the new rules were in force.

Consumer benefits included stronger safeguard requirements: EMIs must separate customer funds from their own capital, either through an insurance policy or by depositing them in a segregated account. The European Banking Authority’s 2019 peer review found that most national authorities enforced this rule effectively, though smaller EMIs in Eastern Europe sometimes struggled with compliance.

Industry response was rapid. Prepaid card issuers, mobile wallet providers, and later challenger banks (like Revolut, which obtained a Lithuanian e-money licence in 2018) used the lighter regime to launch products without needing a full banking licence. The directive also enabled “FinTech” passporting: a licence in one member state (typically Lithuania, the UK until Brexit, or Luxembourg) gave access to all 27 markets.

However, the directive was never fully harmonised. National implementation varied: some countries (Germany, France) imposed additional reporting requirements, while others (UK, Netherlands) adopted a more permissive approach. The Commission’s 2018 REFIT evaluation noted that inconsistent interpretation of “safeguarding” rules created barriers to cross-border scaling, prompting the 2020 proposal to merge e-money and payment institution regimes into a single, more uniform framework.

## Key questions answered

### What counts as electronic money under EU law?

Electronic money is a digital store of value that can be used to make payments to third parties. Typical examples are prepaid debit cards, mobile wallets (like Apple Pay or Google Pay when funded from a non-bank account), and online payment accounts like PayPal balances. The directive does not cover bank deposits, which are already regulated separately.

### Why does Europe have separate rules for e-money instead of just using banking licences?

The original 2000 directive aimed to encourage innovation by offering lighter rules than banking, but set the bar too high. The 2009 revision deliberately lowered capital requirements and supervisory obligations so that non-bank companies — especially startups and tech firms — could issue digital money without taking deposits or making loans. This kept the risk profile narrow: EMIs cannot lend out customer funds, so the systemic risk is far lower than for banks.

### How did this directive help companies like PayPal expand across Europe?

PayPal obtained an e-money licence from the Luxembourg regulator under the 2009 rules, which allowed it to accept and hold customer balances throughout the EU without needing a banking licence in each country. The directive’s passporting provision meant that a single authorisation gave access to all member states. By 2013, PayPal had moved its EU headquarters to Belgium and served over 50 million European users, using the lighter capital and reporting rules to keep costs down.

### What happens if an e-money institution goes bankrupt?

Customer funds must be safeguarded separately from the EMI’s own assets — either through an insurance policy, a bank guarantee, or by depositing them in a segregated account with a credit institution. If the EMI fails, those safeguarded funds are returned to customers ahead of other creditors. The European Banking Authority’s 2020 report found that this “safeguarding” rule was generally well observed, though smaller institutions occasionally failed to keep adequate records.
