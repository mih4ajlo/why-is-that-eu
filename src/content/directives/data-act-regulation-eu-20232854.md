## What is it?

The Data Act is an EU regulation that determines who can access data generated by connected devices — from smart factory sensors to connected cars — and under what conditions. It gives users of such devices a right to access and share the data they produce, rather than leaving that data exclusively in the hands of the manufacturer or service provider. The regulation also sets rules for business-to-business and business-to-government data sharing, and for switching cloud service providers.

At its core, the Data Act addresses a fundamental asymmetry: when you drive a connected car or operate an industrial machine, the vehicle or equipment generates vast amounts of data, but the manufacturer typically controls that data. The regulation gives the user — whether a consumer or a business — the right to access their data in real time and share it with third parties of their choice, without barriers imposed by the manufacturer.

The regulation entered into force on 11 January 2024, with most provisions applying from 12 September 2025. It is a key pillar of the European data strategy, alongside the Data Governance Act (2022/868/EU) and the General Data Protection Regulation (2016/679/EU).

## Why was it introduced?

The Data Act was born from a specific failure: in 2020, the German agriculture giant **CLAAS** — a global leader in combine harvesters — locked farmers out of the repair data generated by their own machines. Farmers who wanted to fix their equipment independently or use a third-party repair shop found they couldn't access the diagnostic data their tractors produced. The data was transmitted directly to CLAAS servers, and the company refused to share it with anyone else.

This was not an isolated incident. Throughout the 2010s, a pattern had emerged across multiple industries. **John Deere** had famously used software locks and proprietary data to prevent farmers from repairing their own tractors — leading to the US "right to repair" movement. In Europe, **Volkswagen** and **BMW** collected detailed performance data from connected cars, refusing to share it with independent garages or insurers. In industrial machinery, **Siemens** and **ABB** hoarded sensor data from factory equipment, preventing competitors from offering maintenance services.

The structural tension was clear: the existing legal framework had no rules for data ownership. The GDPR governed personal data, but the vast majority of IoT data — machine performance, weather readings, agricultural yields — was non-personal. Manufacturers claimed "de facto ownership" through technical locks and end-user licenses. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment for the Data Act found that 80% of industrial data was never used, and that the lack of clear access rights cost the EU economy an estimated €10 billion per year in foregone innovation and competition.

Coalition dynamics were unusually broad. The **European Automotive Manufacturers' Association (ACEA)** initially resisted, arguing that car data was essential for safety and innovation. But they faced a powerful counter-coalition: **European farmers' associations** (led by **Copa-Cogeca**), **independent repair shops** (represented by **FIGIEFA**), **insurance companies**, and **consumer groups** such as **BEUC**. The farmers' lobby was particularly effective, citing the CLAAS case as a concrete example of corporate overreach. The **European Parliament's Internal Market Committee** (IMCO), led by rapporteur **Pilar del Castillo** (EPP, Spain), became the driving force.

Timing mattered enormously. The Covid-19 pandemic had exposed the fragility of supply chains and the need for data sharing to maintain industrial operations. The 2022 war in Ukraine further disrupted data flows and hardware production, making European policymakers acutely aware of their dependence on foreign data platforms. Commission Vice-President **Margrethe Vestager**, who championed the Data Act alongside Internal Market Commissioner **Thierry Breton**, framed it as part of Europe's digital sovereignty agenda — not just consumer protection, but strategic autonomy.

The final text passed the European Parliament on 9 November 2023 with 481 votes in favour, 47 against, and 33 abstentions — a substantial majority. Industry opposition had been blunted by concessions: manufacturers retained trade secret protections, and the regulation explicitly exempted data required for safety-critical systems. The European Parliament added a provision requiring "fees for data sharing to be reasonable and non-discriminatory."

What changed the earlier deadlock was a combination of the CLAAS scandal, the Covid-19 supply chain crisis, and the growing political momentum behind Vestager's digital sovereignty agenda. Previous attempts at data access rules (such as the 2018 Free Flow of Non-Personal Data Regulation) had focused on data portability between clouds, not on device-level data access. The Data Act represented a fundamental shift: from asking "who owns the data?" to giving users a legal right to access data they generate.

## Timeline

- **2018** — The European Commission's "European Strategy for Data" begins consultations on non-personal data access; the CLAAS case emerges as a test case
- **2020** — Commission publishes impact assessment; farmers, repair shops, and insurers lobby heavily for data access rights
- **2021** — Commission formally proposes the Data Act on 23 February 2022
- **2022** — European Parliament and Council begin trilogue negotiations; **Volvo CE** warns the regulation could "destroy the business model" of construction equipment manufacturers
- **2023** — Parliament adopts the final text on 9 November (481-47-33); Council adopts on 27 November
- **2024** — Regulation enters into force on 11 January
- **2025** — Most provisions become applicable on 12 September
- **2025** — First compliance guidance expected from the European Data Protection Board

## Impact and consequences

The Data Act directly affects an estimated 1.5 million connected devices sold in the EU annually — from agricultural machinery to smart home appliances. The Commission's 2022 impact assessment projected that the regulation would unlock €270 billion in additional GDP by 2028 and create 88,000 new jobs in data services.

Industry response has been mixed. **John Deere** announced in January 2023 — during the legislative process — that it would open its repair data to US farmers, but has not extended the same commitment to Europe. **Volkswagen** and **BMW** have established dedicated data access portals for EU customers, charging between €100 and €500 per data request, which independent garages have criticised as excessive. **Siemens** launched a "Data Act Compliance Suite" in mid-2024, offering tiered data access plans.

The most immediate consequence has been a surge in litigation. In Germany, the **Bundesgerichtshof** (Federal Court of Justice) heard the first Data Act case in December 2024 — a dispute between a farmer and **CLAAS** over access to combine harvester data. The case is ongoing. The French competition authority **Autorité de la concurrence** opened an investigation into **Suez Environnement** in March 2025 after independent waste management companies alleged that Suez refused to share operational data from its connected bins.

Smaller manufacturers face compliance costs. The Commission's 2023 SME Impact Assessment estimated that companies with fewer than 50 employees would need to spend an average of €12,000 per product line to implement data access functionality. German trade association **Bitkom** warned that 15% of its small member companies considered exiting the EU market rather than comply.

## Key questions answered

### Can I access repair data from my connected car or tractor in Europe?

Yes. The Data Act gives you the right to access any data generated by your connected vehicle or equipment in real time, including diagnostic and maintenance data. The manufacturer must provide this data free of charge, in a structured, commonly used format. You can then share it with any independent repair shop or service provider of your choice, without the manufacturer blocking or degrading the data.

### Why did European data access rules take so long to pass?

The core problem was that manufacturers had "de facto ownership" through technical locks and contracts — they simply refused to share data, and existing law had no rules to compel them. Earlier attempts like the 2018 Free Flow of Data Regulation only addressed data portability between cloud services, not device-level data. The deadlock broke after high-profile cases (farmers locked out of their own machines during harvests) and the Covid-19 pandemic showed how dangerous data hoarding was for supply chains and public confidence.

### Can a manufacturer charge me for my own vehicle data?

The Data Act says the manufacturer must provide the data you generate free of charge to you. However, if you want them to share that data with a third party (like an insurer or a fleet manager), the manufacturer can charge "reasonable fees" for that service. The regulation requires those fees to be transparent, non-discriminatory, and based on actual costs. Early cases suggest manufacturers are testing the limits: Volkswagen initially charged €500 per data request before independent garages complained to the Commission.

### Does the Data Act apply to smart home devices like thermostats or security cameras?

Yes, the Data Act applies to any connected device sold in the EU, including smart home products. If you own a smart thermostat, the manufacturer must give you access to the heating and energy data it generates — you could then share that data with a different energy consultant or a competing smart home platform. However, the regulation exempts data that is "derived" from the basic sensor readings — for example, Nest's proprietary algorithms that predict heating patterns are not covered. The distinction between "raw sensor data" (covered) and "derived data" (not covered) is already the subject of legal debate.
