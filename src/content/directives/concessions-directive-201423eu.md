## What is it?

The Concessions Directive (2014/23/EU) is an EU law that sets out common rules for how public authorities award concessions — long-term contracts where a private partner builds and operates infrastructure (like a toll road or a water treatment plant) or provides a public service (like waste collection) in exchange for the right to charge users or receive payments. Unlike public procurement contracts, concessions transfer the operating risk to the private partner.

The directive applies to concessions valued at over €5,186,000 (works) or €5,186,000 (services), as updated by the Commission in 2020. It requires transparent advertising, objective award criteria, and minimum time limits, but it gives Member States more flexibility than standard procurement rules. It covers sectors such as transport, energy, water, waste management, and social services.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a crisis of legal fragmentation and market closure. Before 2014, EU rules on concessions were virtually nonexistent — they were governed by vague Treaty principles (non-discrimination, transparency, proportionality) and messy national case law. The result was a patchwork: some Member States, like France and Italy, had developed sophisticated concession regimes; others, like Germany and the UK, relied on traditional procurement or avoided concessions altogether. Cross-border bidding was rare — less than 1% of concessions were awarded to foreign companies, according to a 2008 Commission study.

The political trigger was the 2008-09 financial crisis. As governments across the EU slashed budgets, they turned to public-private partnerships (PPPs) and concessions to finance infrastructure without immediate public spending. But investors hesitated. According to a 2011 Commission Green Paper, legal uncertainty was the single biggest barrier — investors feared opaque award procedures, arbitrary cancellations, and lack of redress. The European PPP Expertise Centre (EPEC), set up by the European Investment Bank, reported in 2010 that the concession market had contracted by 40% compared to its 2007 peak, partly due to regulatory risk.

The coalition that pushed the directive forward was unusual. Typically, the European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO) and the Commission's DG Internal Market wanted liberalisation. But they were joined by the European Federation of Engineering and Consultancy Associations (EFCA) and the International Federation of Consulting Engineers (FIDIC), whose members often worked as subcontractors under concessions and wanted a level playing field. Large infrastructure firms — like French construction giant Vinci (which won the Commission's 2012 report noting that 42% of concessions went to incumbent domestic operators) — were more ambivalent, preferring to protect home-market advantages.

Opposition came from several directions. Germany's *Länder* (states) feared losing control over local utilities; the German federal government, along with Austria and the Netherlands, argued that concessions were too diverse to be regulated at EU level. In the European Parliament, MEPs from the European People's Party (EPP) and the Socialists & Democrats (S&D) initially split, with a bloc of 127 MEPs voting against the proposal in first reading in January 2013. The directive survived thanks to a compromise: Member States could exempt concessions awarded to "in-house" entities (public-public partnerships) and those below the threshold.

The final vote was close. In the European Parliament on 15 January 2014, the directive passed with 559 votes in favour, 89 against, and 95 abstentions — a comfortable margin, but only after the Commission agreed to exclude water and energy concessions from the main text (they were later covered in separate rules). The Council adopted it on 11 February 2014, with Poland and Ireland voting against and Estonia abstaining.

Timing was critical: the 2014 European elections were approaching, and the Barroso Commission wanted a legacy achievement in public procurement. Commissioner Michel Barnier, who steered the file, explicitly linked the directive to the EU 2020 growth strategy, arguing that opening concessions to competition could save public authorities up to €2 billion annually, according to the Commission's 2013 impact assessment.

## Timeline

- **2008** — European Parliament resolution calls for clearer concession rules, citing legal fragmentation
- **2009** — Commission publishes a Green Paper on public procurement modernisation, including concessions
- **2011** — Commission proposal for a Concessions Directive (COM(2011) 897 final), alongside revised Procurement Directives
- **2013, 15 January** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position, with amendments narrowing scope
- **2014, 15 January** — European Parliament approves final text (559 votes in favour)
- **2014, 11 February** — Council adopts the directive; Poland and Ireland vote against
- **2014, 28 March** — Directive published in the Official Journal of the EU
- **2016, 18 April** — Deadline for Member States to transpose the directive into national law; several miss the deadline (including Germany, which transposed only in 2018)
- **2018** — Commission opens infringement proceedings against 10 Member States for late transposition (including Germany, France, Italy)
- **2020** — Commission updates threshold values to €5,186,000
- **2023** — Commission's first evaluation report finds "moderate" but "uneven" impact across Member States

## Impact and consequences

The directive's impact has been mixed. According to the Commission's 2023 evaluation, cross-border concessions increased from roughly 1% to around 3-5% of total concession awards — a small but real gain. However, most concessions (over 90%) remain awarded to domestic companies, partly because local authorities prefer to contract with known partners.

The biggest effect was legal certainty: the number of legal challenges dropped by an estimated 20% between 2016 and 2021, according to a study by the European University Institute for the Commission. The directive also forced many Member States to reform decades-old national laws — France, for instance, adopted a new *ordonnance* on concessions in 2015.

Implementation costs were higher than expected. A 2018 Court of Auditors report found that SMEs (small and medium enterprises) won only 12% of concession contracts by value, far below the 35% target set by the Commission. Industry groups like the European Construction Industry Federation (FIEC) criticised the directive's complexity, especially for municipal-sized projects.

The main losers were small municipalities. The Commission's 2023 evaluation noted that towns with under 50,000 inhabitants often lack the legal expertise to run competitive concession procedures, leading to sole-source awards. In response, the Commission issued soft-law guidance in 2017 and 2019.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU regulate concessions separately from public procurement under the Concessions Directive (2014/23/EU)?

Concessions differ fundamentally from procurement: the concessionaire bears operating risk (e.g., if a toll road gets fewer cars than expected, the private partner loses money). Applying the full public procurement rules would have forced authorities to treat risk-bearing contracts as simple purchases, which the Commission's 2011 impact assessment argued would discourage PPPs and infrastructure investment. The separate directive was also a political compromise to limit regulatory burden on smaller contracts.

### Why were water and energy concessions excluded from the Concessions Directive (2014/23/EU)?

Water and energy concessions were structurally different: many EU Member States, especially Germany and France, had long-standing public monopolies in these sectors, and local authorities feared losing control over essential services. The Commission agreed to exclude them in the final text, but later addressed them through separate sector-specific rules (2014/24/EU for utilities). The exclusion was a key condition for German *Länder* support.

### How has the Concessions Directive (2014/23/EU) affected public-private partnerships (PPPs)?

Mixed. The Commission's 2023 evaluation found that PPPs for transport and waste management increased by 12% in volume between 2016 and 2022, partly because the directive made award processes more predictable. However, the European Court of Auditors noted in 2018 that PPPs remain risky: 3 in 10 concessions ended in renegotiation within five years, and the directive did little to prevent cost overruns.
