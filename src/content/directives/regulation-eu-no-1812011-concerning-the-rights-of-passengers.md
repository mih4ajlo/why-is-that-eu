## What is it?

Regulation (EU) No 181/2011 is the first EU-wide rulebook governing passenger rights for bus and coach services. It guarantees that passengers on regular scheduled and occasional services—primarily those operating distances over 250 km—receive basic protections: non-discrimination in ticket pricing regardless of nationality, compensation of 50% of the ticket price for delays over two hours or service cancellations, and mandatory assistance for persons with reduced mobility (PRMs) at terminals and onboard, including travel companions free of charge. It also requires coach operators to provide basic information about passenger rights in a "visible, legible and clear" manner at stations and on company websites.

The regulation applies to all EU Member States, but with significant opt-outs for certain services: domestic services under 250 km can be exempted by Member States for a transitional period of up to four years (with renewals), and occasional services (e.g., charter tours) are partially excluded from cancellation and delay provisions unless they are part of a package holiday. The regulation also allows Member States to temporarily derogate from certain obligations in cases of force majeure, such as extreme weather or natural disasters, but such derogations must be notified to the European Commission.

## Why was it introduced?

The driving force behind Regulation 181/2011 was a structural tension in EU transport policy: air and rail passengers had already received protections under Regulation 261/2004 (air passenger rights, adopted after the 1998 IATA airfare cartel scandals) and Regulation 1371/2007 (rail passenger rights, following the 2006 EU rail liberalization), but bus and coach passengers—a mode used by over 60 million Europeans annually according to the European Commission's 2009 impact assessment—were left with no equivalent rights framework. This created an unfair patchwork: a passenger delayed on a cross-border flight from Brussels to Paris could claim up to €600 in compensation, while the same passenger delayed on a FlixBus or Eurolines coach from Brussels to Paris received nothing.

Coalition dynamics were unusually fractured. The European Parliament, led by the center-left and Greens, pushed for strong protections, including full compensation for delays and mandatory advance notice for cancellations. The Council, dominated by Member States with large coach fleets like Germany, Spain, and Poland, resisted, arguing that bus operators—often small-to-medium-sized firms—could not bear the administrative costs of maintaining claims systems and terminal assistance. The European Coach Federation (now part of the International Road Transport Union, IRU) warned in 2009 that adding compensation obligations would "cripple the affordability of coach travel," a claim echoed by national associations in Spain, where ALSA, the country's largest coach operator, reported that compliance costs could reach €30 million annually for its 2,400 buses alone.

Timing was critical. The Regulation was proposed in 2008 under the Barroso I Commission, with Transport Commissioner Jacques Barrot (France) as lead. The 2008 financial crisis initially slowed negotiations as Member States prioritized economic recovery over regulatory expansion. But the appointment of Siim Kallas (Estonia) as Transport Commissioner in 2010 revived the file, with Kallas framing passenger rights as a "consumer protection issue vital for the EU's social dimension" during Parliamentary hearings. The 2009 European Parliament elections had also strengthened the Socialist and Greens group, who made bus rights a campaign issue. The final text, adopted in February 2011, represented a compromise: the Parliament won compensation for delays, but the Council secured the 250 km threshold—effectively excluding most domestic services, which account for 90% of EU bus journeys according to the Commission's 2020 report on the regulation.

What changed most dramatically was the shift from a sector-specific to a universal-rights approach. Earlier attempts to extend passenger rights across all transport modes (the 2003 "Passenger Rights" Green Paper) had stalled because Member States insisted on mode-by-mode rules. The 2005 Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) guidelines had briefly considered a single omnibus regulation, but this was abandoned after lobbying from the European Federation for Transport and Environment and national transport ministries argued that technical differences between rail, air, and road made a one-size-fits-all solution unworkable. Instead, the Commission adopted a piecemeal strategy, starting with air (2004), then rail (2007), then maritime (2010, Regulation 1177/2010) and finally bus and coach (2011). Each successive regulation became easier to pass as the political principle of minimum passenger protections was already established.

## Timeline

- **2005** — European Commission publishes "Passenger Rights in the European Union" Green Paper, identifying gaps in protections for bus and coach passengers.
- **2008** — Commission proposes Regulation on bus and coach passenger rights (COM/2008/0817), citing the need for consistency with air and rail regimes.
- **2009** — European Parliament transport committee votes 38-12 in favor of including compensation for delays of over 90 minutes; Council rejects this and insists on a 2-hour threshold.
- **2010** — Trilogue negotiations reach compromise: compensation only for delays over 120 minutes; 250 km threshold introduced for Member State exemptions; PRM assistance mandated at all terminals serving over 50 passengers per day.
- **February 16, 2011** — Regulation (EU) No 181/2011 adopted by the European Parliament (399 votes in favor, 78 against) and Council (unanimous approval).
- **March 1, 2013** — Regulation enters into force and applies to all scheduled services throughout the EU.
- **2018** — European Commission publishes first evaluation report (COM/2018/413), finding that only 12 Member States had fully implemented the regulation; five Member States (Austria, Belgium, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, and Poland) had extended the 250 km exemption to domestic services, contrary to the four-year transitional limit.
- **2020** — Commission launches revised "Passenger Rights" legislative package (COM/2020/270), proposing to strengthen enforcement and remove the domestic exemption loophole; proposal remains under negotiation as of 2024.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's impact has been uneven. According to the Commission's 2018 evaluation, the regulation affected approximately 1.5 million coach journeys per year across the EU—mostly long-distance cross-border services operated by major carriers like FlixBus (Germany), ALSA (Spain), Eurolines (multinational), and National Express (UK). For PRMs, the regulation brought tangible improvements: national disability associations in France, Italy, and the Netherlands reported that coach stations had installed accessible ramps and audible announcements, though the European Disability Forum noted in 2019 that "coach companies still regularly deny boarding to wheelchair users, particularly on occasional services."

However, the 250 km threshold created a massive loophole. In 2013, Spain, Italy, and Poland immediately exempted all domestic services from the regulation's compensation and assistance provisions—meaning passengers on a 249 km between Madrid and Valladolid had no rights, while those on a 251 km service to Barcelona did. The Commission's 2020 revision package explicitly aimed to close this exemption, proposing to remove the distance threshold for compensation and extend assistance obligations to all domestic services over 50 km. Industry response has been hostile: the IRU's 2021 position paper argued that extending obligations would "increase ticket prices by up to 15%," citing a study commissioned by FlixBus that claimed compliance costs of €50 million annually across its EU network.

Enforcement has been inconsistent. In 2017, the European Commission opened infringement proceedings against Bulgaria and Romania for failing to designate a national enforcement body. Spain was fined €10 million in 2022 for systematic non-compliance with PRM obligations, according to the Commission's 2023 Passenger Rights Scoreboard. By 2024, Germany had the highest number of individual complaints (1,200 filed annually), while Malta, Cyprus, and Luxembourg reported fewer than 10 each, raising questions about passenger awareness and enforcement capacity.

## Key questions answered

### Why are bus trips under 250 km not covered by EU passenger rights?

The 250 km threshold was a political compromise that allowed Member States with extensive domestic networks (Germany, Spain, Poland) to exempt the vast majority of their bus services, which are regional and short-haul. The European Parliament initially proposed covering all regular services regardless of distance, but Council negotiators successfully argued that rural and small-operator routes could not absorb the administrative costs of maintaining compensation claims systems. The Commission's 2020 revision package proposes removing this exemption entirely, but as of 2024, the proposal remains stalled in Council.

### Why do EU bus passenger rights mostly not apply to charter and tour buses?

Occasional services (e.g., ski holiday charters, organized tours) were exempted after lobbying from the European Tour Operators Association and groups like Thomas Cook and TUI, who argued that pre-planned package holidays already have their own consumer protections under the Package Travel Directive (90/314/EEC, later 2015/2302/EU). The exemption was meant to avoid double-regulation, but it has left a gap: passengers on a one-off charter to a festival have no right to compensation for delays, even though the same delay on a scheduled service would trigger payment. Consumer rights groups like BEUC have called for this exception to be removed.

### Why is help for disabled bus passengers only guaranteed at stations with more than 50 passengers a day?

This threshold was a cost-saving measure, inserted at the request of the European Coach Federation, to avoid obligating small, rural bus stops—often no more than a signpost—to install ramps, tactile paving, or induction loops. The Commission's 2018 evaluation found that the threshold effectively excludes 60-70% of EU bus stops, particularly in Southern and Eastern Europe, from PRM obligations. The European Disability Forum has criticized this as "discrimination by design," arguing that passengers in rural areas are denied the same rights as those in cities.

### What can I do if my bus is delayed and the company won't help?

The passenger must first file a claim with the coach operator, typically using a standard claim form required to be available at the terminal and on the company website. If the operator rejects or ignores the claim, the passenger can escalate to the National Enforcement Body (NEB) designated by their Member State. The Commission maintains a database of NEBs, but as of 2024, only 18 Member States had functioning NEBs with dedicated bus passenger rights procedures, according to the Commission's 2023 Passenger Rights Report. In practice, many passengers simply do not bother—the 2018 Commission evaluation found that fewer than 2% of eligible passengers actually claimed compensation.
