## What is it?

The Industrial Emissions Directive 2024/1785/EU is a recast of the 2010 Industrial Emissions Directive (2010/75/EU), the EU's main instrument for regulating pollution from around 52,000 large industrial installations — power plants, refineries, chemical factories, waste incinerators, and intensive livestock farms. It entered into force in August 2024 and must be transposed by Member States by July 2027.

The recast tightens emission limit values (ELVs) based on Best Available Techniques (BAT) conclusions, expands the directive's scope to cover new sectors (large-scale mining, battery manufacturing, and intensive cattle and pig farms), and mandates full digitisation of permit applications and compliance reporting. It also introduces mandatory environmental management systems for all installations, including energy efficiency and resource-use plans, and strengthens public participation rights by requiring early consultation on permit conditions. Non-compliance penalties must be dissuasive, proportionate, and effective — with a new minimum penalty framework for serious violations.

## Why was it introduced?

The push for a recast began with a 2017 European Court of Auditors report that found the 2010 Directive's implementation was inconsistent across Member States. The auditors noted that nearly half of industrial permits in the EU did not reflect the required BAT conclusions, and compliance checks were rare — only 3% of installations were inspected annually in some Member States. This structural tension — between ambitious EU-level rules and lax national enforcement — made legislative reform inevitable.

The European Commission, under Ursula von der Leyen's Green Deal agenda, proposed the recast in April 2022 as part of the Zero Pollution Action Plan. Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius framed it as essential to meeting the 2030 climate targets and the 2050 zero-pollution ambition. But the real drama played out over the inclusion of intensive livestock farms. Meat industry associations like Copa-Cogeca and national governments from France, Spain, and Poland argued that extending BAT rules to cattle farms (previously only large pig and poultry farms were covered) would cost farmers €200 million annually and drive production outside the EU — a classic carbon leakage argument. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment estimated that 40% of EU ammonia emissions came from livestock, and the European Environment Agency's 2021 data showed agricultural ammonia remained a leading cause of eutrophication. The European Parliament's ENVI Committee, led by Rapporteur Radan Kanev (EPP, Bulgaria), pushed for inclusion, while the centre-right EPP group split, with some MEPs from farming-heavy regions voting against.

The coalition that passed the recast in November 2023 was an unusual alliance: the Socialists & Democrats, Greens/EFA, Renew Europe, and most of the EPP (after the farm provisions were watered down). The Liberal group's Rapporteur Kanev brokered a compromise: cattle farms with over 350 livestock units (roughly 250 cows) would be included, but with a 17-year transition — by 2040 instead of 2030 — and exemptions for farms using organic or extensive systems. The final vote in the European Parliament was 393 in favour, 118 against, with 211 abstentions. The Council's opposition was led by Hungary and Poland, who warned the rules would harm food security, but the qualified majority held.

Timing mattered: the recast succeeded in 2023 because the Green Deal had already passed its flagship laws — the Climate Law (2021), the Fit for 55 package (2021-2023), and the revised Emissions Trading System (2023). Industrial pollution was the next logical frontier. Two specific events broke deadlock: the 2022 launch of the EU's Digital Decade strategy, which made permit digitisation a concrete, non-controversial efficiency gain, and a 2023 European Court of Justice ruling (Case C-375/21) that fined an Austrian cement company €50,000 for failing to update its permit after new BAT conclusions were adopted — showing courts would enforce the old rules, making reform more palatable to industry.

## Timeline

- **2010** — Original Industrial Emissions Directive (2010/75/EU) adopted, consolidating seven earlier directives on industrial pollution.
- **2017** — European Court of Auditors special report finds inconsistent permit compliance across Member States.
- **2020** — European Commission impact assessment estimates 40% of EU ammonia emissions from livestock.
- **2021** — European Green Deal's Zero Pollution Action Plan includes commitment to revise the IED.
- **April 2022** — Commission proposes recast, including livestock farms and digital permits.
- **June 2023** — European Parliament adopts its position (393-118-211) after controversial farm provisions debate.
- **November 2023** — Council of the EU adopts the recast with qualified majority; Hungary and Poland oppose.
- **March 2024** — Directive published in the Official Journal as 2024/1785/EU.
- **August 2024** — Directive enters into force (20 days after publication).
- **July 2027** — Deadline for Member States to transpose into national law.
- **2030** — First BAT conclusions for new sectors (battery, mining) expected.
- **2040** — Deadline for intensive cattle farms to comply with BAT rules.

## Impact and consequences

The recast directly affects an estimated 52,000 industrial installations in the EU, adding around 20,000 new farms and 10,000 mining/battery operations under permit requirements. The Commission's impact assessment projected it would reduce emissions of SO₂, NOx, and particulate matter by 10-15% by 2040 compared to a business-as-usual scenario, with health savings of €3-5 billion annually due to reduced air pollution-related illnesses.

Industry reaction was mixed. The European Chemical Industry Council (Cefic) warned that the new energy efficiency and resource-use plans would add €1-2 billion in compliance costs for chemical plants, potentially undermining competitiveness against US and Chinese rivals. The European Livestock Voice, a coalition of 20 agricultural organisations, said the 2040 deadline for cattle farms was "a victory for common sense" but warned that implementing BAT conclusions by then would require €300-400 million in investments for ventilation, manure storage, and feed management.

Environmental groups like the European Environmental Bureau praised the expanded scope and digitisation elements but criticised the livestock transition period as too long. ClientEarth noted that the minimum penalty framework remained vague, allowing Member States to set fines that may still be too low to deter non-compliance. Early adopters include Germany and the Netherlands, which already had digital permit systems; Poland, Romania, and Bulgaria face the biggest implementation challenges, with the Commission estimating they need €1.5-2 billion in technical assistance to digitise their permitting processes.

## Key questions answered

### Do bigger farms in the EU now face stricter pollution rules than small farms?

Yes. For the first time, the recast sets binding Best Available Technique emission limits for intensive cattle farms with over 350 livestock units (roughly 250 cows), pig farms, and poultry farms. Smaller farms are exempt unless they operate near protected areas and cause significant pollution. Medium-sized farms (150-350 units) can opt in for a simpler permit process.

### Why does EU law still allow some industrial sites to pollute for another decade after new rules take effect?

The recast gives existing installations 4 years to comply with new BAT conclusions, and cattle farms a 17-year transition until 2040. This long timeline was a political compromise necessary to pass the law — farming states required it to avoid abrupt closures. New installations must also comply immediately, but existing ones get phased compliance windows.

### Can citizens now challenge industrial permits more easily in the EU?

Yes. The recast strengthens public participation rights by requiring permit authorities to consult the public early in the process — before the permit is drafted, not just after. Citizens and NGOs can now submit comments on the draft conditions, and authorities must respond in writing. Non-compliance with this early consultation requirement can invalidate the permit in court, as confirmed by the 2023 Court of Justice ruling on the Austrian cement case.

### What happens if a country ignores these new pollution rules?

The recast introduces a minimum penalty framework: for serious violations that cause harm to human health or the environment, Member States must impose fines that are "at least equivalent to the economic benefit" of non-compliance. The Commission can also initiate infringement proceedings and refer cases to the European Court of Justice, which can impose lump sum or penalty payments. In practice, enforcement will depend on national courts — the minimum penalty standard is new and untested, so early cases (expected in Germany, Poland, and France) will determine its teeth.
