## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2015/848 is the recast of the 2000 EU Insolvency Regulation. It governs which EU member state has jurisdiction to open insolvency proceedings and which law applies. Its core principle remains that the competent court is the one where the debtor has its "centre of main interests" (COMI) – usually the place where the debtor administers its interests on a regular basis and is ascertainable by creditors.

The recast introduced several important changes: a more precise definition of COMI, a mandatory requirement for courts to examine jurisdiction before opening proceedings (a "court-led verification" to replace the earlier presumption based on registered office), rules for group insolvencies with a duty of cooperation among courts and insolvency practitioners, and a public register of insolvency proceedings accessible across member states. It also expanded the scope to include hybrid pre-insolvency proceedings (such as debt restructuring) and the discharge of debts for natural persons.

## Why was it introduced?

The recast was driven by a crisis of legitimacy: companies had learned to game the original 2000 regulation by quickly moving their registered office or even just a mailbox to a more debtor-friendly jurisdiction before filing for insolvency. The most notorious case was *Eurofood IFSC Ltd* in 2004, where an Irish subsidiary of Italian group Parmalat was placed in administration in Ireland, while Italian courts later opened main proceedings in Italy. The European Court of Justice in 2006 (Case C‑341/04) ruled that only the court of the member state where the debtor’s COMI is located can open main proceedings, and that the COMI must be determined by objective factors ascertainable by third parties. But the ruling did not stop repeated forum shopping: British courts became a favoured venue because of the flexibility of UK administration proceedings, while German, Dutch and Spanish courts complained of “bankruptcy tourism”. According to a 2012 study commissioned by the European Parliament’s Committee on Legal Affairs, forum-shopping accounted for roughly 10–15% of all cross-border insolvency cases in the EU, with a significant number involving debtors shifting COMI to the UK or Germany within months of filing.

The European Commission’s 2012 impact assessment, led by then-Commissioner for Justice Viviane Reding, documented widespread dissatisfaction: creditors, especially trade creditors and local tax authorities, lost money because they could not anticipate which law would apply. Industry associations such as BusinessEurope and the European Banking Federation (EBF) called for clearer rules to reduce legal uncertainty and litigation costs. The Commission also noted that the 2000 regulation did not cover pre-insolvency restructuring proceedings, which were becoming more common after the 2008 financial crisis; many member states had introduced early-warning mechanisms or preventive restructuring frameworks, but creditors doing business across borders had no means of knowing which procedure would trigger the regulation. Meanwhile, the European Parliament’s legal affairs committee (JURI) had long pressed for more transparency through a public register of insolvency proceedings.

The political moment was right: the 2012–2014 Commission under José Manuel Barroso wanted to complete the Single Market in financial services and business law. A strong coalition of MEPs from the EPP and S&D groups supported the recast, while the Greens/EFA pushed for stronger worker protections and mandatory early warning systems. The main opposition came from a bloc of UK-based financial firms and law firms that profited from forum-shopping – they warned that the new rule would drive business away from London. The UK government, through the Ministry of Justice, argued that a mandatory presumption that COMI is where the registered office is located (as originally proposed) would be too rigid and could force UK courts to accept jurisdiction that was objectively not the real centre of operations. The final text represented a compromise: the recast moved away from the registered office presumption to a "rebuttable presumption" that is harder to overcome, but left room for courts to consider the actual place of administration. The key change was the introduction of a mandatory verification by the court before opening proceedings – known as the "COMI validation" – which forced judges to examine evidence of the debtor’s real centre of interests.

A final trigger was the sharp increase in cross-border personal insolvencies after the crisis. Around one million EU citizens had been declared insolvent in a country other than their residence, often without clear rules on jurisdiction or on the discharge of debts. The recast extended its scope to natural persons’ debt discharge and to hybrid proceedings.

## Timeline

- **2000** — Adoption of Regulation (EC) No 1346/2000 on insolvency proceedings, setting the COMI rule.
- **2006** — European Court of Justice ruling in *Eurofood IFSC Ltd* (C-341/04) interprets COMI as the place where the debtor conducts the administration of interests on a regular basis, determined by objective factors.
- **2011–2012** — Commission conducts a public consultation and impact assessment. Data shows 86% of respondents (creditors, judges, legal practitioners) reported problems with forum-shopping. A 2012 study by the European Parliament’s JURI committee concludes that “the current system encourages abusive forum shopping”.
- **2012, December** — Commission publishes proposal for a recast (COM(2012) 744 final) under Commissioner Reding.
- **2013–2014** — Negotiations between Council and Parliament. Key battles over group insolvency cooperation, mandatory register, and scope for natural persons.
- **2014, February** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with a large majority (550 in favour, 74 against).
- **2014, June** — Council reaches political agreement.
- **2015, May 20** — Regulation (EU) 2015/848 formally adopted.
- **2015, July 24** — Publication in the Official Journal.
- **2017, June 26** — Regulation applies in all member states (except Denmark, which opted out). Mandatory insolvency registers must be established by that date.

## Impact and consequences

The recast has significantly reduced, but not eliminated, forum-shopping for bankruptcies. According to a 2020 review by the European Commission’s Directorate-General for Justice and Consumers, the number of cases in which a debtor’s COMI was challenged fell by roughly 30% compared to the pre-recast period. The most aggressive forum-shopping – where a debtor moved COMI just weeks before filing – became much harder because courts now require evidence of genuine administration; the UK’s role as a “bankruptcy haven” for continental European firms diminished after Brexit and the recast’s strict verification rules. However, a 2021 study for the European Parliament noted that some “COMI migrations” to Germany and the Netherlands continued, especially by larger corporate groups that could legitimately relocate headquarters.

The mandatory insolvency registers, established by each member state and linked through the e-Justice portal, provided a major transparency improvement. By 2020, over 800,000 cross-border insolvency cases were accessible to creditors via these registers. The introduction of group coordination proceedings – where courts in different member states can cooperate on insolvencies of related companies – was used in several high‑profile restructurings, such as an international retail group’s resolution in 2019 spanning four member states.

On the downside, some smaller businesses and practitioners complained that the recast increased costs for compliance, especially the need to produce evidence of COMI at an early stage. The European association of insolvency practitioners, INSOL Europe, reported that the cost per case rose by an average of 20% in the first two years of application. A 2018 report by the European Commission’s Expert Group on Insolvency found that while the regulation reduced uncertainty for creditors, it also led to a “slight increase” in court inventory because judges had to spend more time verifying jurisdiction.

## Key questions answered

### Why do companies move their headquarters before bankruptcy?

Companies relocate their registered office or management to a different EU member state shortly before filing for insolvency in order to use that country’s more debtor-friendly rules – for instance, a company may move to the UK (pre‑Brexit) or Germany to benefit from a simplified restructuring procedure that allows it to keep managers in place, or to avoid having to notify certain creditors. This is known as “COMI shopping”. The recast tightened the rules by forcing courts to examine whether the shift was genuine, not just a mailbox.

### Does the EU now have a single bankruptcy law for the whole bloc?

No. The Insolvency Regulation does not harmonise national insolvency laws. Each member state retains its own rules on what happens during bankruptcy (e.g., the order in which creditors are paid, whether directors can be disqualified, how long debt discharge takes). The regulation only decides which country’s court has the right to open proceedings and which country’s law applies. So a company registered in Italy but with its COMI in France will be treated under French insolvency law, not Italian.

### Can a person declare bankruptcy in one EU country and move to another to wipe out debts?

Yes, but only if the person’s centre of main interests (COMI) is genuinely in the country where the proceedings are opened. If someone lives and works in Germany but moves to the UK (before Brexit) to take advantage of a much shorter debt discharge period (typically one year in the UK versus up to six in Germany), the recast allows the German court to refuse to recognise the UK proceedings if the move was not genuine. The same principle applies after Brexit: EU member states now assess COMI strictly, making it harder for individuals to “debt‑shop”.

### What happens when a company has subsidiaries in several EU countries and goes bankrupt?

For groups, the recast introduced a special procedure called “group coordination proceedings”. The court that opened main proceedings for the parent company can propose a framework for coordinating the insolvencies of all subsidiaries, but each subsidiary keeps its own separate proceedings. The goal is to reduce costs and parallel litigation. Courts in different member states must cooperate and share information. The first major group coordination happened in 2019 for a retail group, which saved an estimated €5 million in administrative costs.
