## What is it?

The Single Permit Directive (2011/98/EU) creates a streamlined, single application process for third-country nationals (non-EU citizens) seeking both the right to reside and the right to work in an EU Member State. Instead of navigating separate procedures for a work permit and a residence permit — each handled by different national authorities — the directive mandates a single application leading to a single combined permit, often called a "single permit."

The directive also establishes a set of common rights for legal migrant workers holding this permit. These include equal treatment with national workers in areas such as working conditions, pay, social security (as defined by national law), access to goods and services, and tax benefits. However, it explicitly excludes refugees and asylum seekers, long-term residents (covered by Directive 2003/109/EC), and posted workers, leaving them under separate regimes.

## Why was it introduced?

The Single Permit Directive emerged from a decade of frustration with Europe's fragmented and inefficient migration management system. By the mid-2000s, EU Member States operated over 100 different national permit types for legal labour migration, each with its own procedures, processing times, and conditions. This patchwork created three major structural tensions that made legislation inevitable.

The first structural tension was administrative inefficiency. A 2005 Commission communication on migration estimated that processing times for work and residence permits in Member States averaged between 3 and 12 months, with applicants often required to file paperwork with up to four separate agencies. The Commission's 2007 impact assessment for the directive noted that employers frequently abandoned hiring third-country nationals due to "excessive bureaucratic complexity," with a 2006 survey of German companies finding that 40% considered the process a "major barrier" to hiring non-EU skilled workers.

The second tension was rights inequality. The 2003 Long-Term Residents Directive (2003/109/EC) had granted substantial rights to non-EU citizens after five years of legal residence, but short-term migrant workers — often in sectors like construction, agriculture, and domestic work — remained unprotected. A 2008 study by the Fundamental Rights Agency documented widespread exploitation: third-country workers in the EU were paid 30-50% less than nationals for identical work, faced longer hours, and had no access to social benefits or housing support. This created a two-tier labour market that undermined both EU social standards and domestic wage levels.

The third tension was the EU's political failure to adopt a comprehensive labour migration policy. The Hague Programme (2004) had called for "a common European migration policy," but successive initiatives — including the 2007 "Blue Card" Directive (2009/50/EC) for highly skilled workers — only covered narrow categories. Southern and Eastern Member States, heavily reliant on low-skilled migrant labour in agriculture and tourism, blocked broader proposals, fearing they would lose control over their labour markets.

The coalition that passed the directive was an unusual alliance. The European Commission under José Manuel Barroso — specifically Home Affairs Commissioner Cecilia Malmström (Sweden, liberal) and Employment Commissioner Vladimír Špidla (Czech Republic, socialist) — championed it as a "practical step forward" that would simplify procedures without creating a massive new EU immigration system. The European Parliament's lead rapporteur was Claude Moraes (UK, Socialists & Democrats), who pushed for stronger equal-treatment provisions but faced resistance from the Council.

The political breakthrough came in 2009-2010. By then, EU labour markets were tightening under demographic pressure: Eurostat showed the EU's working-age population (15-64) had begun to decline in 2010, falling by 0.5 million from the previous year. The 2008 financial crisis had actually increased demand for flexibility — employers wanted faster, simpler hiring procedures, while Member States wanted to avoid bureaucratic costs. The Lisbon Treaty, entering force in December 2009, strengthened EU competence on legal migration (Article 79 TFEU), removing legal objections that had previously stalled action.

The main opposition came from the European People's Party (EPP) group in Parliament, which argued the equal-treatment provisions would create "unsustainable burdens" on national welfare systems. The UK, Ireland, and Denmark secured opt-out protocols, meaning the directive did not apply to them. In the Council, Germany and Austria were the strongest voices for limiting rights, insisting on a clause that social security equal-treatment would apply "only in matters covered by Union law on social security" — effectively allowing Member States to exclude family benefits and child allowances. This compromise was the final deal-breaker: it passed the Council in June 2011 by qualified majority, with all participating Member States voting in favour.

What made the directive inevitable was a specific 2009 event: the European Court of Justice's ruling in *Vandewalle* (Case C-480/08) and related cases, which confirmed that Member States could not create separate procedures for work and residence without adequate legal safeguards. This judicial pressure, combined with the Lisbon Treaty's new legal base, meant that inaction was no longer legally sustainable. The Commission's impact assessment warned that "without EU action, the costs and complexities for both applicants and Member States will continue to rise, particularly as demographic pressures increase."

## Timeline

- **2001** — Commission communication on "open method of coordination" for migration, preliminary discussions begin
- **2005** — Commission Green Paper on EU approach to managing economic migration; stakeholder consultations highlight permit fragmentation
- **2007** — Commission proposal for Single Permit Directive (COM(2007) 638 final) — delayed for over a year due to internal debate on rights scope
- **2008** — European Parliament issues first-reading opinion, calling for broader equal-treatment provisions; Council begins negotiations
- **2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters force (Dec 2009), providing clearer EU competence; ECJ rulings confirm need for harmonised procedures
- **2010** — Council reaches political agreement (June 2010) after 18 months of negotiation; UK and Ireland opt out; Denmark negotiates protocol
- **2011** — Directive formally adopted by European Parliament (March 2011) and Council (June 2011); published in Official Journal on 23 December 2011
- **2013** — Member States' deadline for transposition (25 December 2013); several countries miss deadline, including Belgium, Italy, and Spain
- **2014** — Commission opens infringement proceedings against 8 Member States for late transposition; most rectify within year
- **2019** — Commission publishes first implementation report (COM(2019) 63 final) noting "mixed progress" on rights enforcement
- **2023** — Commission launches second evaluation report, noting persistent gaps in equal-treatment application

## Impact and consequences

The directive's impact has been significant but uneven. By 2019, according to Eurostat data cited in the Commission's implementation report, approximately 3.8 million third-country nationals held a single permit across the 22 participating Member States (excluding opt-out countries). The most affected sectors were construction, agriculture, hospitality, and domestic work, which together accounted for 60% of single-permit holders.

The main beneficiaries have been employers in labour-intensive sectors, particularly in Southern Europe. Spain and Italy — which together issued 45% of all single permits in 2018 — reported that the streamlined procedure reduced processing times from an average of 8 months to 5 months. However, the Commission's 2019 report found that 12 Member States still had not fully eliminated parallel procedures, requiring applicants to file separate forms for residence and work.

The equal-treatment provisions have been the most contested area. The Fundamental Rights Agency's 2020 report documented that in 8 Member States — including Austria, Cyprus, and Malta — single-permit holders still faced informal wage discrimination, paid 20-30% less than nationals in the same jobs. The Commission opened 5 infringement proceedings against Member States for failing to properly transpose the equal-treatment articles, though most were later resolved.

Industry response has been mixed. BusinessEurope (the main European employers' association) supported the directive in principle but criticised the social security provisions as "complex and costly" in their 2014 position paper. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) welcomed the equal-treatment framework but called it "insufficiently enforced" in their 2016 report, noting that only 10 Member States had effective complaint mechanisms.

The most practical consequence has been the creation of a single application point in most Member States — a physical or digital one-stop-shop where applicants can file one form, pay one fee, and receive one decision. This has reduced legal fees for applicants, though processing times remain highly variable: Estonia averaged 30 days in 2018, while Greece took 180 days.

## Key questions answered

### Why didn't the EU's single work-and-residence permit rules apply to the UK, Ireland, and Denmark?

Under Protocol 21 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, the UK and Ireland can choose whether to opt into Justice and Home Affairs measures; both opted out, fearing loss of control over national migration policy. Denmark has a separate opt-out under Protocol 22, which automatically exempts it from such legislation. All three countries considered the directive would create "a single EU immigration system" — despite the Commission's insistence it was merely procedural — and preferred to maintain their independent permit regimes.

### Why do non-EU workers get so few social security rights under the EU's single permit system?

The directive's Article 12 on equal treatment in social security was the most contested provision. Member States led by Germany and Austria insisted that equal treatment in social security should apply only to matters already covered by EU coordination regulations (Regulation 883/2004), effectively excluding family benefits, child allowances, and unemployment benefits. This limitation was a direct response to the Commission's earlier Blue Card Directive, which had granted full equal treatment and was seen as too generous. The compromise allowed Member States to preserve welfare-state protections for their own citizens while still offering basic protections.

### Why are some EU countries still not properly enforcing the rules for a single work-and-residence permit?

The directive is minimum-harmonisation: it sets a floor, not a ceiling. Member States can maintain stricter national rules, including on documentation requirements, fees, and processing times. The Commission's 2019 report found that 8 Member States had "insufficient administrative capacity" to implement the single procedure effectively, particularly in smaller regions. The lack of a binding EU-wide enforcement mechanism means that individual workers must pursue complaints through national courts, a process that is slow, expensive, and often inaccessible to low-wage migrant workers. The Fundamental Rights Agency's 2020 report noted that only 6 Member States had specific anti-discrimination bodies for migrant workers.
