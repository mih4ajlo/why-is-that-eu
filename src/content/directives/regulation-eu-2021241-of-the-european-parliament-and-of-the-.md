## What is it?

The Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) is the centrepiece of NextGenerationEU, the EU's unprecedented €806.9 billion (in current prices) recovery instrument. The RRF itself makes up €723.8 billion — comprising €385.8 billion in loans and €338 billion in grants — disbursed to EU member states between 2021 and 2026. Unlike traditional EU structural funds, which reimburse costs incurred, the RRF pays out based on member states achieving pre-agreed milestones and targets (e.g. passing a specific law, completing 1,000 km of railway track, or training a defined number of workers). Each country submitted a National Recovery and Resilience Plan (NRRP) detailing reforms and investments across six pillars: green transition, digital transformation, smart and sustainable growth, health and social resilience, policies for the next generation, and social and territorial cohesion. The green and digital pillars must account for at least 37% and 20% of each plan's total allocation, respectively. The Commission assesses progress and recommends payments; disbursement requires approval by the Council by qualified majority. As of March 2025, the Commission has disbursed over €260 billion, but several countries — notably Hungary and Poland — have seen funds frozen due to rule-of-law concerns.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the COVID-19 pandemic and its economic devastation. In spring 2020, as lockdowns brought economic activity to a near-halt, the EU faced a crisis of existential proportions. Italy's GDP fell by nearly 9% in 2020; Spain lost over 10%. The European Commission's spring 2020 economic forecast projected EU GDP shrinking by 7.4% that year, the deepest recession since the Great Depression. National fiscal responses were constrained by the Stability and Growth Pact limits, and the European Central Bank's pandemic emergency purchase programme provided monetary relief but could not address structural damage. The worst-hit southern member states — Italy, Spain, Greece, Portugal — had no fiscal space to mount stimulus on their own.

But the structural tension was deeper: the EU's existing fiscal framework was designed for normal times, not pandemics. The Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) 2014-2020 was locked in. Even emergency instruments like the European Investment Bank's pandemic loans or the temporary SURE unemployment scheme were limited in scale. A joint borrowing mechanism — issuing common EU debt — had been taboo since the eurozone crisis. Germany and the Netherlands, supported by Austria, Finland, Sweden, and Denmark (the so-called "Frugal Four"), had consistently opposed any mutualisation of debt, fearing moral hazard and fiscal transfers without reform conditions.

The coalition that broke this deadlock was forged in a moment of existential urgency. On 8 April 2020, nine EU finance ministers from France, Italy, Spain, Belgium, Luxembourg, Ireland, Portugal, Greece, and Slovenia published an open letter in the Financial Times calling for "a common debt instrument issued by an EU institution." On 9 April, Eurogroup agreed to a €540 billion safety net (SURE, EIB loans, ESM credit lines) — but this was insufficient. The real breakthrough came on 18 May 2020, when French President Emmanuel Macron and German Chancellor Angela Merkel announced a joint Franco-German proposal for a €500 billion recovery fund based on EU borrowing. This was a historic reversal for Germany, a country that had refused eurobonds during the 2010-2012 sovereign debt crisis. Several factors drove Merkel's shift: the scale of the collapse threatened the single market and German exporters; Germany's constitutional court ruling in May 2020 (the Weiss/SRB saga) had threatened the ECB's bond-buying, creating uncertainty; and Merkel faced a looming succession battle within her party, making a grand European gesture politically useful. On 27 May 2020, the European Commission, led by President Ursula von der Leyen and Budget Commissioner Johannes Hahn, proposed NextGenerationEU, borrowing €750 billion (in 2018 prices) with the Commission's own resources as collateral. The European Parliament, led by the EPP's Manfred Weber and S&D's Iratxe García, strongly supported the proposal. Opposition came from the Frugal Four (the Netherlands, Austria, Sweden, Denmark) who demanded strict conditionality, repayment guarantees, and a link to the rule of law. The final deal, reached at the marathon 17-21 July 2020 European Council summit, reduced the grant-to-loan ratio, introduced a rebate system for net contributors, and tied disbursement to respect for rule of law — a provision deliberately targeting Poland and Hungary.

## Impact and consequences

The RRF fundamentally reshaped EU fiscal governance. It introduced a performance-based model that required countries to deliver concrete reforms — not just spend money — creating unprecedented leverage for the Commission to push structural reforms. Countries like Italy (€191.5 billion allocation) and Spain (€163 billion) used their plans to streamline public administration, reform justice systems, and accelerate renewable energy permitting. Greece (€30.5 billion) committed to tax administration digitisation and court case backlogs. However, implementation was slow: by end-2023, only 30% of investments and 49% of reforms had been completed across all countries, according to the European Court of Auditors' 2023 special report. The biggest delays came from administrative capacity shortages and complex procurement procedures. The green conditionality (37% minimum) directed massive sums toward energy efficiency, renewables, and sustainable transport — for example, Portugal used RRF funds to support its hydrogen strategy and forest fire prevention. The digital conditionality (20% minimum) boosted 5G rollout, public administration digitisation, and skills training.

The rule-of-law conditionality had the most politically explosive impact. Poland's NRRP (€59.8 billion, including loans) was blocked until March 2024, when the new pro-EU government under Donald Tusk agreed to restore judicial independence conditions. Hungary's plan remains largely frozen as of early 2025, with the Commission withholding payment over concerns about public procurement, conflict of interest, and LGBTQ+ rights constraints. In response, Hungary threatened to block EU aid to Ukraine and sanctions on Russia — a recurring pattern of conditionality-related blackmail.

The RRF also deepened the shift toward joint EU borrowing: the Commission issued over €100 billion in green bonds by end-2023, becoming the world's largest green bond issuer. This created a new EU sovereign asset class, but also raised concerns about debt mutualisation and fiscal transfers. The repayment burden (through new own resources like the carbon border adjustment mechanism and digital levy) remains politically contested.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Germany agree to joint debt issuance for the first time?

Chancellor Merkel's reversal was driven by the collapse of German export markets — 30% of German GDP depends on exports to the EU single market. With Italy, Spain, and France in deep recession, German carmakers and machinery companies faced collapsing demand. The alternative — national-level bailouts — risked splitting the eurozone and triggering a financial crisis. The historic taboos around eurobonds were broken by the pandemic's severity and the temporary, conditional nature of the fund: it was a one-off instrument, not a permanent fiscal union, and was tied to reform commitments.

### Why does the RRF tie money to reforms instead of just spending?

Years of structural funds had shown that simply reimbursing expenditures did not guarantee reform implementation. The Greek bailout programmes (2010-2018) had demonstrated the power of cash-for-reform conditionality — but those were imposed in crisis, not voluntarily. The RRF's novelty is that countries *themselves* propose reforms in their NRRPs, creating ownership. The Commission's capacity to withhold payment if milestones are missed creates a binding commitment device. This model was directly inspired by the experience of the 2013-2019 "Reform Delivery" contracts experimented with under the European Semester.

### Why were Hungary and Poland's funds frozen under the RRF and how did they react?

The July 2020 European Council compromise included a rule-of-law conditionality mechanism (Regulation 2020/2092) that allows the Commission to suspend payments if breaches affect the EU budget's financial interests. Poland and Hungary initially challenged this mechanism before the European Court of Justice, which upheld it in February 2022. Poland's funds were unblocked only after the new Tusk government adopted judicial independence measures in 2024. Hungary's case remains deadlocked: Prime Minister Orbán's government refused to implement anti-corruption reforms, and in response, Orbán vetoed EU sanctions packages on Russia and blocked Ukraine's EU accession negotiations as leverage to unlock the funds. The standoff illustrates the RRF's evolving role as a political enforcement tool.

### Will the RRF be renewed after 2026?

Formally, the RRF expires in August 2026 (loans must be drawn by that date, grants by December 2026). However, the Commission's 2024-2029 strategic agenda increasingly references the need for a permanent "European Competitiveness Fund" modelled on the RRF's performance-based grants. The high-level report by former Italian Prime Minister Enrico Letta ("Much More Than a Market", April 2024) and the Mario Draghi competitiveness report (September 2024) both called for permanent RRF-like instruments for industrial policy and the green transition. A renewal faces fierce opposition from frugal member states, who argue one-off debt mutualisation was an emergency measure, not a precedent. Any extension would require a new unanimity decision from the Council.
