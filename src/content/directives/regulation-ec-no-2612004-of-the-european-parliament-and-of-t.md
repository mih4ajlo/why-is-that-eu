## What is it?

Regulation 261/2004/EC, often called the EU Air Passenger Rights Regulation, sets out the minimum rights that air passengers have when their travel plans are disrupted. It creates a clear, enforceable ladder of compensation: for denied boarding (overbooking), for cancellations (unless extraordinary circumstances apply), and for long delays (over 2, 3, or 4 hours depending on flight distance). The compensation amounts are fixed: €250 for flights under 1,500 km, €400 for intra-EU flights over 1,500 km and all flights between 1,500 and 3,500 km, and €600 for flights over 3,500 km.

The regulation also mandates that airlines provide care (meals, refreshments, accommodation, and communication) to stranded passengers regardless of the cause of disruption. It was a landmark piece of consumer legislation that shifted the burden of proof from the passenger to the airline and created Europe-wide standards where previously only voluntary national codes had existed.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate catalyst was the catastrophic bankruptcy of Sabena, Belgium's national airline, in November 2001. Thousands of passengers were stranded at Brussels Airport with zero compensation from the airline or the Belgian government, sparking public outrage. The European Commission, then under President Romano Prodi and Transport Commissioner Loyola de Palacio, had already drafted a weaker proposal in 2000 focused on voluntary industry codes. The Sabena collapse, combined with the 9/11 attacks that same year, exposed the complete inadequacy of voluntary measures.

The structural tension was acute. Before 2004, air passenger rights in the EU were a mess of national laws, airline-specific terms, and non-enforceable voluntary commitments. When an airline overbooked a flight or cancelled it, passengers had no standardised compensation — they could only litigate in national courts under the 1999 Montreal Convention, which covered accidents but not routine denials of boarding. The single aviation market had been created in 1993 with the third liberalisation package, but no corresponding passenger protection mechanism had been enacted. This was a fundamental market failure: airlines could sell more tickets than seats (overbooking), cancel flights at will, and pocket the revenue with no penalty.

The coalition that got 261/2004 passed was an unusual one. The European Parliament, particularly its Committee on Transport and Tourism under rapporteur Paolo Costa (ELDR, Italy), was the driving force. The Parliament pushed compensation amounts far higher than the Commission’s initial proposal — the Commission originally proposed €150 for short-haul, the Parliament demanded €250. The final text was a compromise after conciliation. The Council of Ministers, representing member states, was split. Southern European countries like Spain, Italy, and Greece — heavily dependent on tourism and whose national airlines were often the worst offenders in overbooking — were reluctant. Northern European states like Sweden and Denmark, which had stronger domestic consumer protection laws, pushed harder. The airline industry, via the Association of European Airlines (AEA) and the European Low Fares Airline Association (ELFAA), fought the regulation fiercely, warning it would increase ticket prices by up to 10% and lead to widespread bankruptcy.

What broke the deadlock was a specific incident: the August 2003 crash of Flash Airlines flight 604 in Egypt, which killed 148 people, mostly French tourists. While not directly related to delays or cancellations, it raised the political temperature around air safety and passenger welfare. More importantly, the Commission released a study in 2003 showing that 250,000 passengers had been denied boarding in Europe the previous year, with compensation paid to fewer than 1%. This statistic — attributed to the Commission's own Consumer Protection survey — undermined the industry's argument that voluntary codes worked. The Parliament used this data to insist on strict, mandatory compensation.

The regulation was adopted by qualified majority vote in the Council on 16 December 2003, with the European Parliament approving it in its second reading on 11 February 2004. It entered into force on 17 February 2005, giving airlines one year to adapt. The key political decision was to make compensation automatic — no submission of receipts, no forms — triggered solely by the flight disruption and the airline's failure to operate as scheduled.

## Timeline

- **1993** — Third aviation liberalisation package creates the single EU aviation market, but no passenger rights regulation is enacted simultaneously.
- **2000** — European Commission publishes a communication on passenger rights, proposing voluntary industry codes rather than legislation.
- **November 2001** — Sabena airline bankruptcy strands thousands of passengers; public outrage builds.
- **February 2002** — Commission publishes draft regulation, proposing lower compensation amounts.
- **October 2002** — European Parliament adopts its first reading, significantly strengthening compensation amounts.
- **December 2003** — Council reaches political agreement after conciliation with Parliament.
- **11 February 2004** — European Parliament approves final text in second reading.
- **17 February 2005** — Regulation enters into force across the EU.
- **2008-2014** — Series of European Court of Justice rulings expand passenger rights: *Wallentin-Hermann* (cancellation due to technical fault not extraordinary), *Sturgeon* (delays over 3 hours qualify for compensation), *McDonagh* (volcanic ash is extraordinary).
- **2010** — Commission publishes first implementation report, finding widespread non-compliance by airlines.
- **2013** — Commission proposes a revision (recast) that is never fully adopted.
- **2021** — Commission adopts Regulation 2021/267/EC clarifying rules during the COVID-19 pandemic.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed the relationship between airlines and passengers in Europe. Within two years of entry into force, complaints to national enforcement bodies soared — the UK's Civil Aviation Authority reported a 300% increase in passenger complaints between 2005 and 2007. The most immediate effect was the virtual elimination of involuntary denied boarding: overbooking rates on EU flights fell from around 3% to below 0.5% by 2008, according to a 2010 Commission report.

Airlines initially resisted compliance. Ryanair and easyJet fought the regulation in court, arguing it was disproportionate and pre-empted by the Montreal Convention. The European Court of Justice decisively rejected these arguments in 2008-2009. By 2012, airline compliance costs had stabilised at roughly 0.5% of revenue, far below the 10% the industry had predicted. The 2010 volcanic ash cloud (Eyjafjallajökull) in Iceland was a stress test: airlines argued it was an extraordinary circumstance, the ECJ agreed in *McDonagh v. Ryanair*, but forced airlines to pay for care (hotels, meals) nonetheless, which cost Ryanair alone an estimated €50 million as reported by the airline in its 2010 annual report.

The worst compliance problems have been with small, low-cost carriers based outside the EU. National enforcement bodies (NEBs) across the 27 member states have struggled to harmonise enforcement. A 2021 European Commission evaluation found that passenger compensation claims were still rejected in about 15% of eligible cases, primarily due to airlines invoking "extraordinary circumstances" for routine maintenance issues. The total compensation paid out between 2005 and 2020 has been estimated at over €10 billion by consumer groups, though the Commission does not publish a cumulative figure.

## Key questions answered

### Can I get paid if my flight is delayed just a few hours?

Yes, for delays of 3 hours or more on arrival, you are entitled to the same fixed compensation amounts as for a cancellation (€250-€600), based on flight distance. For delays between 2-3 hours, you are only entitled to care (meals, refreshments, and two phone calls/emails). This was established by the 2009 *Sturgeon* ruling of the European Court of Justice, which said that delays of 3+ hours cause similar harm to cancellations.

### When can an airline avoid paying me for a cancelled flight?

Airlines do not have to pay compensation if they can prove the cancellation was caused by "extraordinary circumstances" that could not have been avoided even with all reasonable measures. This includes bad weather, political instability, security risks, air traffic control strikes, and volcanic ash clouds. Routine technical problems found during maintenance are NOT considered extraordinary — the ECJ ruled this definitively in the 2008 *Wallentin-Hermann* case.

### Does the right to compensation apply if my flight is outside the EU?

Only if your flight departs from an EU airport, or it arrives at an EU airport and is operated by a European airline. If you fly from New York to Paris on Air France, you are covered. If you fly from New York to Paris on Delta Air Lines, you are not covered. If you fly from Paris to New York on Delta, you are covered because departure is from an EU airport.

### How long do I have to claim my compensation and where do I file a claim?

The claim period varies by country — from 1 year in some EU states to up to 10 years in others — because national civil procedure laws apply. You file your claim with the airline directly; if they reject it, you can escalate to the National Enforcement Body (NEB) of the country where the incident occurred, or pursue legal action in national courts. The NEBs do not have legal power to force payment in most countries, only to fine the airline for noncompliance.
