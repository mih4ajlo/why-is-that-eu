## What is it?

Regulation (EC) 261/2004 creates a floor of mandatory protections for air passengers flying within the European Union, and for flights to or from an EU airport by any carrier. When a flight is cancelled, delayed by three hours or more, or when passengers are denied boarding against their will, the regulation triggers rights to: monetary compensation (€250–€600 depending on distance), reimbursement or re-routing, and "right to care" (meals, accommodation, and communication). The carrier cannot contract out of these obligations; they apply automatically.

Crucially, the regulation contains a "force majeure" exception — carriers are not liable for compensation if they can prove "extraordinary circumstances" beyond their control (e.g., weather, security risks, air traffic control strikes). The line between ordinary operational problems and extraordinary circumstances has been the subject of extensive litigation, culminating in several landmark rulings by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU).

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to a massive, industry-wide failure of voluntary self-regulation and market incentives. In the late 1990s and early 2000s, budget carriers like Ryanair and easyJet were exploding in growth, and legacy airlines were competing by cutting costs. Overbooking was routine and profitable: airlines would sell more tickets than seats, betting that enough passengers wouldn't show up. When they were wrong, passengers were bumped — sometimes with little or no compensation, often with no assistance. Cancellations due to "technical problems" or "crew scheduling" were common, leaving passengers stranded for days at their own expense.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had liberalised the aviation market (through the "Third Package" of 1992–1997), creating fierce competition and lower fares, but had failed to protect consumers against the resulting risk. A 2000 Commission report found that "denied boarding occurs on about 2.5 million passengers per year in the EU" — and many received nothing. The US had its own "Denied Boarding Compensation" rules since 1976 (codified in 14 CFR Part 250), but the EU had none. European airlines argued that market competition would solve the problem; passengers would choose airlines with better treatment. That argument collapsed when low-cost carriers, which had no economic incentive to compensate, dominated growth.

The coalition for the regulation was unusual. The European Parliament's Committee on Transport and Tourism, led by rapporteur Paolo Costa (ELDR, Italy), pushed aggressively for strict compensation rules. Consumer groups — BEUC (the European Consumers' Organisation) — lobbied heavily, citing specific horror stories of passengers stranded at airports without food or shelter for 48 hours. The Commission, under Commissioner for Transport Loyola de Palacio (1999–2004), made passenger rights a flagship consumer-protection issue within the broader Lisbon Agenda. On the other side, the airline industry — represented by the Association of European Airlines (AEA) and the European Low Fares Airline Association (ELFAA) — warned that mandatory compensation could cost €1–3 billion annually, force up ticket prices, and hurt competitiveness. Ryanair CEO Michael O'Leary publicly threatened to pull flights from some EU routes if the rules passed; no such withdrawal materialised after adoption.

Timing was decisive. The regulation was proposed in late 2001, just months after the September 11 attacks. The aviation industry was in crisis — airlines globally lost $7 billion in 2001 (IATA data, cited in Commission staff working papers). Most EU governments were focused on bailing out or subsidising carriers, not constraining them. The Commission initially proposed weaker protections: compensation only for denied boarding, not cancellations or delays. But the Parliament, in a vote in March 2002, amended the proposal to include cancellations and delays, overriding industry resistance. The Council of Ministers, driven by consumer-friendly states like the Netherlands and Sweden, accepted the expansion. The final regulation was adopted in February 2004, entering into force in 2005.

What changed from earlier attempts? A 1991 voluntary code on denied boarding (Council Resolution 91/C 147/01) had been largely ignored by airlines. The 2000 Commission consultation showed that enforcement by national authorities was patchy. The real unblocking event was the 2003 CJEU ruling in *Carpenter v. Secretary of State for Home Department* (C-60/00) — while not about air travel, it established that EU law could directly impose obligations on private companies for consumer protection, setting a legal climate favourable to mandatory rules.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Council Resolution on a voluntary code for denied boarding; widely ignored.
- **2000** — Commission publishes a report estimating 2.5 million denied boardings per year in the EU.
- **11 September 2001** — 9/11 attacks trigger aviation crisis; industry focus shifts to survival.
- **December 2001** — Commission proposes Regulation (COM(2001) 784 final), limited to denied boarding.
- **November 2002** — European Parliament votes to include cancellations and delays, against Commission recommendation.
- **2003** — CJEU ruling (*Carpenter*) bolsters EU consumer-protection competence.
- **11 February 2004** — Regulation (EC) 261/2004 adopted by Parliament and Council.
- **17 February 2005** — Regulation enters into force and becomes applicable.
- **2008–2012** — Wave of CJEU rulings (e.g., *Sturgeon* C-402/07, *Wallentin-Hermann* C-549/07) clarifying "extraordinary circumstances" and compensation for delays.
- **2013** — Commission proposal to revise the regulation (COM(2013) 130 final); stalled in trilogues over airline-union resistance on re-routing rules.
- **2021** — CJEU ruling in *Transportes Aéreos Portugueses* (C-514/20) confirms that "extraordinary circumstances" include sudden illness of a pilot, tightening the exception.
- **2024** — Revision process still ongoing; Council fails to agree on new rules as of mid-2024.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation fundamentally changed European air travel. By 2010, denied boarding had dropped sharply; airlines invested in better booking systems and overbooking algorithms. Consumer claims exploded: by 2019, EU passengers submitted an estimated 1.5 million compensation claims per year (European Consumer Centre Network data). The biggest beneficiaries were not the chronically delayed — one 2018 study by the European Commission's Joint Research Centre found that 95% of compensation payments go to passengers on low-cost or legacy carriers for cancellations and delays — but enforcement remained uneven.

Italy, Spain, and the Netherlands created robust national enforcement bodies (ENAC, AESA, ILT) that fined airlines aggressively. In 2012, Spain fined Ryanair €2.3 million for systematic violations of the right to care during the 2010 Icelandic volcanic ash crisis. Ryanair appealed; the Spanish Supreme Court confirmed the fines in 2016. The UK Civil Aviation Authority, before Brexit, forced British Airways to pay £100 million in compensation for a 2018 IT crash that stranded 75,000 passengers — the largest single enforcement action under the regulation.

Airlines responded by attempting to outsource liability. Many carriers (notably Ryanair) directed passengers to file claims through third-party legal firms or claim aggregators like AirHelp, which grew into a €200-million turnover business. By 2023, the European Consumer Centre estimated that only 40–60% of eligible passengers actually filed claims, often due to complex forms or language barriers.

The "extraordinary circumstances" defence became the central battleground. The CJEU consistently narrowed it: technical faults (even those caused by manufacturing defects) are not extraordinary (*Wallentin-Hermann*, 2008); crew strikes by airline employees are not extraordinary (*Krüsemann* C-195/17, 2018); only events "not inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier" (e.g., air traffic control strikes, security threats, severe weather) qualify. By 2024, airlines had lost approximately 75% of contested claims at CJEU level, according to a Court of Justice press release.

The economic effect has been debated. A 2019 Commission evaluation found no evidence of significant price increases attributable to the regulation; the €250–€600 compensation amounts represent, on a typical €50–€200 ticket, a meaningful but manageable cost. However, some smaller airlines — especially those in Eastern Europe — argued that compliance costs (legal teams, call centres, accommodation contracts) were disproportionately high. The revision proposal from 2013, which would have increased compensation to €600 for all long-haul flights and added "right to connect" for missed connections, has failed to pass, blocked primarily by a coalition of six states (including the UK before Brexit, and later Hungary and Poland) and low-cost carriers.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the regulation cover cancellations and delays, not just denied boarding?

The original Commission proposal in 2001 covered only denied boarding. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Paolo Costa and consumer advocates, successfully argued that cancellations and long delays cause equivalent or greater harm to passengers — a family stranded for 24 hours without food or hotel faces the same distress as one bumped from an overbooked flight. The Parliament's November 2002 vote to extend the scope was adopted by a clear majority over industry objections, establishing the principle that the regulation's purpose is passenger welfare, not correcting airline yield management.

### Why is there no compensation for strikes?

The regulation's Article 5(3) exempts carriers from compensation if they can prove "extraordinary circumstances." The CJEU, in *Krüsemann* (2018) and later cases, drew a distinction: strikes by the carrier's own employees (e.g., pilots, cabin crew) are "inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier" — they are operational risks the airline must manage — so they are not extraordinary and do not exempt the carrier. However, "wildcat" strikes by air traffic controllers, airport staff, or third-party ground handlers may be extraordinary if the carrier could not have prevented them. This means that whether a strike excuses compensation depends on who went on strike and why, creating significant litigation.

### Why has the regulation not been revised despite clear problems?

The Commission proposed a revision in 2013 (COM(2013) 130 final) to: increase compensation for long-haul flights, add rights for missed connections, and clarify the "extraordinary circumstances" definition. The Council of Ministers could not agree. Low-cost carriers — Ryanair, easyJet, Wizz Air — lobbied heavily against expansion, arguing it would raise ticket prices by 5–10%. A coalition of six states (including the UK, until its 2016 Brexit referendum) sided with them, fearing higher costs for their national carriers. Meanwhile, the Parliament demanded stricter rules. The trilogue negotiations collapsed in 2015. By 2024, the file remained stalled, with the Commission focusing on enforcement of existing rules rather than reopening the political battle.
