## What is it?

The proposed EU Child Sexual Abuse Regulation (CSAR) would require messaging platforms, email providers, and cloud storage services to proactively scan all private communications — including encrypted content — for known and unknown child sexual abuse material (CSAM). It would create a mandatory detection order system, enforceable by national authorities, with fines up to 6% of global annual turnover for non-compliance. The proposal also includes a new EU Centre on Child Sexual Abuse to operationalise detection and coordinate responses.

The regulation represents a fundamental shift from the current voluntary detection model under the interim ePrivacy derogation (2021/1231/EU), which already allows voluntary scanning but has been criticised as legally precarious. The CSAR would make scanning a permanent, mandatory obligation for all relevant providers operating in the EU.

## Why was it introduced?

The proposal emerged from a perfect storm of structural tension in EU digital law, coalition dynamics between child protection advocates and law enforcement, and specific timing driven by the 2022 deadline of the interim ePrivacy derogation.

**Structural tension:** The existing legal framework created an intractable contradiction. The GDPR (2016/679/EU) and ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC) guarantee confidentiality of communications — including encryption. Yet the same laws permit Member States to impose restrictions for crime prevention. By 2020, European police forces were reporting that 85% of CSAM detection came from voluntary scans by US tech companies (Europol, 2021 Internet Organised Crime Threat Assessment). This created a perverse incentive: platforms that scanned most aggressively became both the most effective at reporting abuse and the most exposed to legal liability for violating privacy laws. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment showed that voluntary detection was declining as platforms faced growing litigation risk.

**Coalition dynamics:** The proposal united an unusual coalition. The European Parliament's Civil Liberties Committee (LIBE) had long been cautious on mass surveillance, but the COVID-19 pandemic saw child sexual abuse reports increase 106% in some Member States (Europol, April 2020 press release). Commissioner Ylva Johansson (Home Affairs) made CSAR her signature initiative, personally briefing MEPs and national ministers. She was backed by a cross-party group including MEPs from the European People's Party (EPP), Socialists & Democrats (S&D), and Renew Europe. The European Criminal Police Organisation (Europol) and the European Union Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA) provided technical support, though the FRA's 2022 opinion explicitly warned that mandatory scanning of encrypted communications "raises serious fundamental rights questions."

Opposition came from an equally powerful coalition: technology companies like Meta and Signal, digital rights groups including EDRi and ARTICLE 19, and the European Parliament's conservative ECR group, who argued mandatory scanning would break end-to-end encryption. The German government — traditionally protective of privacy — expressed strong reservations during Council negotiations.

**Timing:** The proposal succeeded in 2022 because the interim ePrivacy derogation (2021/1231/EU) was set to expire in 2024. Without a permanent framework, the Commission risked losing all detection capacity. The launch of the Digital Services Act (2022/2065/EU) in 2022 created political momentum for platform regulation. Commissioner Thierry Breton confirmed in February 2022 that the DSA would not include child protection measures, making CSAR the necessary companion. The October 2022 Parliamentary resolution supporting the proposal passed by 398 votes to 86, reflecting strong cross-party consensus.

**What changed:** Earlier attempts had stalled. The 2020 Commission proposal to extend the ePrivacy derogation faced broad rejection in the European Parliament, which limited it to a one-year extension. The breakthrough came when Commissioner Johansson explicitly separated the scanning mandate from the broader ePrivacy Regulation negotiations, which had been deadlocked since 2017. By proposing regulation specifically for child sexual abuse, she created a narrower, more politically viable vehicle.

## Impact and consequences

The CSAR has already reshaped the European digital rights debate. In February 2023, Meta announced it would voluntarily scan selected communications for CSAM, pre-empting the regulation. But Signal threatened to leave the EU market if encryption-breaking scanning became mandatory, and its CEO Meredith Whittaker testified before the European Parliament in October 2023 that the proposal "would fundamentally break the security architecture of the internet."

The proposal has split the European Parliament: the LIBE committee report by rapporteur Javier Zarzalejos (EPP) supported scanning, but a competing opinion from the Legal Affairs Committee (JURI) warned it violated Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights. As of early 2025, 18 Member States supported the general approach, while 9 — including Germany, the Netherlands, and Poland — had reservations. The Council has not reached a qualified majority.

The controversy has delayed parallel digital legislation. The proposed ePrivacy Regulation — stalled since 2017 — has become less urgent, while the AI Act (2024/1689/EU) negotiations saw repeated references to the CSAR's precedent on scanning. Companies with EU headquarters, like German cloud provider Hetzner, have publicly warned that mandatory scanning would force them to redesign entire product architectures.

## Key questions answered

### Is the EU planning to read everyone's private messages?

The proposal would require platforms to scan all communications for known CSAM using detection technologies — but only in the context of a detection order issued by a national authority. Scanning would not involve human review of ordinary messages; only flagged content would be referred to authorities. Critics argue this is functionally equivalent to mass surveillance, as algorithms would process every message.

### Does this proposal break encryption?

The Commission claims the regulation does not prohibit encryption, but cybersecurity experts including the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) have stated that client-side scanning — the technology needed to scan encrypted messages — would "reduce the security properties of end-to-end encryption." Commissioner Johansson has suggested alternative approaches like perceptual hashing, but these also require access to decrypted content on the device.

### Why did tech companies oppose this so strongly?

Companies like Meta, Google, and Apple have devoted billions to encryption as a selling point and legal protection. Mandatory scanning would undermine that investment and create legal exposure under both EU data protection law and US surveillance legislation. Signal and WhatsApp have argued they would rather leave the EU market than implement scanning — a threat that has significant economic consequences, as Europe is the former's second-largest market.

### Could the proposal ever become law?

As of early 2025, the outcome is uncertain. The push for a compromise in the Council has failed three times — most recently in December 2024, when Germany led a blocking minority. The European Parliament is divided between those who see the proposal as essential (the EPP and Socialists) and those who view it as a fundamental rights violation (the Greens and Left). A new Commission under Ursula von der Leyen's second term may abandon the current text and start fresh.
