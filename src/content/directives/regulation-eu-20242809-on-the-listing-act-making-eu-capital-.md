## What is it?

The Listing Act is a broad legislative package amending the EU’s core financial market rules — the Prospectus Regulation (2017/1129), the Market Abuse Regulation (596/2014), the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID II), and the rules on investment research and multi‑lateral trading facilities. Its central goal is to slash the cost and complexity of listing shares on European exchanges, especially for small and mid‑sized companies (SMEs), while maintaining standards of investor protection.

The regulation creates a new “EU Growth Issuer” regime that reduces disclosure requirements, allows simplified prospectuses for secondary issuances, and lifts the obligation to publish detailed quarterly financial updates. It also lowers the minimum threshold for a free float (the proportion of shares held by the public) from 25% to 10% of the company’s capital, bringing the EU closer to US and UK norms. The package was formally adopted in November 2024 and applies from 2026.

## Why was it introduced?

The most dramatic element is a simple number: as of 2023, the EU had roughly 6,700 listed companies, while the United States had about 6,800 — despite the US economy being only 50% larger. In 2022, the total IPO proceeds raised on EU exchanges ($25 billion) were less than one‑tenth of those raised on US exchanges ($277 billion), according to a European Commission impact assessment. The gap was widening each year, and EU policymakers feared a permanent de‑facto capital market shift to New York and London after Brexit.

**Structural tension** had been building for decades. The EU’s capital markets remained fragmented across 27 national regulators, while the US Securities and Exchange Commission offered a single, deep, liquid market. A company listing in Paris would struggle to attract investors from Milan or Stockholm; a company listing on Nasdaq could access global capital immediately. The Prospectus Regulation of 2017 had already tried to simplify paperwork, but SMEs still faced costs of €1–2 million for an IPO prospectus, often outweighing the benefit of going public.

**Coalition dynamics** were unusually broad. The European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen made “Capital Markets Union” a priority for Financial Services Commissioner Mairead McGuinness. The push was supported by a cross‑party alliance in the European Parliament, led by MEPs from the liberal Renew group and the centre‑right EPP, who saw the US capital market dominance as a strategic threat. Resistance came from some consumer protection groups and left‑leaning MEPs, who warned that stripping back disclosure rules would increase the risk of fraud and insider trading. The German Finance Ministry, initially cautious, swung behind the package after the 2023 collapse of Deutsche Börse’s attempted merger with Euronext — a deal that would have created a pan‑European exchange but was blocked by anti‑trust concerns. Germany realised that organic growth, not mergers, was the only path, and that required a friendlier listing environment.

**Timing** was set by the aftershocks of Brexit. From 2021, the UK — which had hosted Europe’s deepest liquidity pool — was now a third country. The EU could no longer rely on London to drain away its excess savings and channel them into EU companies. The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine and the resulting energy crisis further concentrated minds: EU companies needed equity financing to invest in renewables and defence, not debt. The 2024 European elections also created a political deadline — the von der Leyen Commission wanted a flagship financial‑market reform before leaving office.

**What changed** that unblocked earlier failures? The 2023 collapse of Silicon Valley Bank and the subsequent liquidity crisis at Credit Suisse demonstrated that bank‑based financing was fragile. EU‑level political will shifted sharply toward reducing reliance on bank loans. Also, a 2022 study by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) showed that the average time to produce a full prospectus in the EU was 6–8 months, versus 3–4 months in the US and UK. That concrete data gave the Commission evidence to override member‑state resistance to a shorter, lighter prospectus regime.

## Timeline

- **2020** — European Commission launches a public consultation on the listing framework, receiving 175 responses from issuers, exchanges, and investor groups.
- **2022** — Commissioner McGuinness announces a legislative proposal in the State of the Union speech; the draft text is published in December.
- **2023** — Trilogue negotiations begin. Key battlegrounds: whether to abolish quarterly reporting entirely (Parliament wanted it) and whether to cut the free float threshold from 25% to 10% (Council agreed after France and Germany aligned).
- **November 2024** — The European Parliament and Council adopt the final text (Regulation (EU) 2024/2809). The vote is 498 in favour, 124 against, 36 abstentions.
- **2025** — Member states transpose amendments to MiFID II into national law. ESMA publishes guidelines on the new EU Growth Issuer regime.
- **January 2026** — The regulation becomes applicable across all EU member states.

## Impact and consequences

The most direct change is cost reduction. The Commission estimated that a typical SME will save 40–50% on the preparation of a prospectus for a secondary offering, cutting legal and advisory fees by several hundred thousand euros. The abolition of quarterly financial reports, which the UK already dropped in 2014, is expected to free management time for long‑term strategy.

However, investor‑protection groups, notably the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC), warned that reduced disclosure could mask problems until they explode. They point to the 2021 Wirecard scandal, where Germany’s BaFin failed to detect accounting fraud despite quarterly reports — suggesting better enforcement, not less paperwork, was the needed fix. The Commission countered with a commitment to increase ESMA’s supervisory powers.

Early reactions from European stock exchanges were positive. Euronext CEO Stéphane Boujnah called it “the most pro‑growth reform in a generation.” The Frankfurt Stock Exchange reported a 30% increase in IPO inquiries in the first quarter of 2025. Yet skeptics note that the real barriers — high stamp taxes in some member states, fragmented clearing and settlement, and a cultural preference for private family ownership — remain untouched.

## Key questions answered

### Why do fewer companies list in Europe than in the United States?

The EU has smaller, more fragmented capital markets, higher regulatory costs, and a tradition of bank lending. A company floating on the New York Stock Exchange faces one regulator, one set of rules, and immediate access to the world’s deepest pool of institutional investors. In the EU, a company must comply with 27 different national implementations of EU law, faces patchy investor demand, and pays €1–2 million for a full prospectus. The Listing Act tries to narrow this gap by standardising and simplifying prospectuses, cutting reporting obligations, and lowering the free float requirement.

### Does the new EU regulation weaken protections for small investors?

In some ways, yes — the obligation to publish quarterly financial updates is removed, and the threshold for when a company must publish a full prospectus is raised. The Commission’s argument is that quarterly reports encourage short‑termism and add cost without catching fraud, as the Wirecard collapse showed. To compensate, the regulation strengthens the role of national competent authorities and ESMA to intervene more quickly if suspicious trading patterns emerge. Investor groups remain wary, and the debate will continue when the first high‑profile failure occurs.

### Will this regulation bring a wave of IPOs to Europe?

The Commission expects a 20–30% increase in new listings within three years, based on the impact assessment’s modelling. But structural issues remain: the EU lacks the US’s deep pension‑fund sector (EU pension assets are about 50% of GDP vs 170% in the US), and many European family‑owned companies simply prefer to stay private. The Listing Act is a necessary but not sufficient condition; complementary reforms — like the proposed EU savings and investment union — are needed to redirect household savings into equity markets.
