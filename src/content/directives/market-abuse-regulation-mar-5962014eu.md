## What is it?

The Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) is the EU's central legal framework prohibiting insider trading, unlawful disclosure of inside information, and market manipulation in financial instruments traded on EU-regulated markets, multilateral trading facilities (MTFs), and organised trading facilities (OTFs). It replaced the Market Abuse Directive (2003/6/EC, or MAD) and its implementing directives, creating a directly applicable Regulation to ensure uniform enforcement across all member states.

MAR applies to financial instruments admitted to trading on EU markets, including derivatives linked to them, and extends to spot commodity contracts, emission allowances, and auction platforms. It requires issuers to disclose inside information promptly, maintain insider lists, and report suspicious transactions. The Regulation was complemented by the Criminal Sanctions for Market Abuse Directive (2014/57/EU, or CSMAD), which obliges member states to make insider dealing and market manipulation criminal offences with minimum penalties.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for MAR was the 2008 global financial crisis and the subsequent G20 Pittsburgh Summit in September 2009, where world leaders — including EU heads of state — committed to "strengthen sanctions against market manipulation and insider trading." But the deeper motivation was a decade of frustration with the patchwork implementation of the Market Abuse Directive (MAD), which had been adopted in 2003 after the Enron and Parmalat scandals. By 2010, the European Commission's impact assessment found that MAD's transposition into national law was inconsistent: some member states imposed only administrative fines, while others had criminal penalties; the definition of "inside information" varied; and cross-border enforcement was virtually impossible.

The specific incident that broke the political deadlock was the **Libor scandal** of 2012. In June 2012, Barclays was fined £290 million by UK and US regulators for manipulating the London Interbank Offered Rate. The scandal spread to Deutsche Bank, UBS, and Rabobank, revealing that global benchmarks could be rigged with impunity. European Commissioner for Internal Market and Services Michel Barnier seized on the moment. On 25 July 2012, he proposed MAR alongside CSMAD, telling the European Parliament: "The integrity of our markets depends on trust. The Libor scandal showed that trust can evaporate in a day. We must ensure that anyone who breaks the rules faces real consequences — not just in one country, but across the entire EU."

The coalition that got MAR passed was unusual. The European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON) was led by rapporteur Arlene McCarthy (S&D, UK), who pushed for strong harmonisation. She built a coalition with the Greens' Sven Giegold (Germany), who wanted to extend MAR to spot commodity markets, and with centre-right MEPs like Corien Wortmann-Kool (EPP, Netherlands), who sought to protect emission allowance markets from manipulation. The main opposition came from the UK financial services industry, represented by the British Bankers' Association (BBA), which warned that MAR's requirement for issuers to delay disclosure of inside information only with explicit supervisory approval would "choke market liquidity." The City of London Corporation lobbied for a softer approach, but the Libor scandal weakened their credibility.

A key structural tension driving MAR was the fragmentation of enforcement under the old directive. Under MAD, national competent authorities (NCAs) had different powers: the UK's Financial Services Authority (FSA) could impose unlimited fines, while Germany's BaFin could only issue administrative sanctions up to €200,000. This created regulatory arbitrage, where firms could route trades through member states with weaker regimes. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA), established in 2011, was tasked with coordinating NCAs, but its powers remained limited. MAR solved this by making the rules directly applicable, removing national discretion.

Timing was critical. The 2014 European Parliament elections were approaching, and the Barroso II Commission needed a visible achievement in financial regulation to counter public anger over bank bailouts. Commissioner Barnier had already passed the Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive (AIFMD) and was pressing forward with the Banking Union. MAR was introduced in the first half of 2014, before the elections, and was adopted on 16 April 2014. It entered into force on 3 July 2016, giving firms and NCAs two years to prepare — a period that coincided with the implementation of Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II). The proximity to MiFID II was deliberate: the two frameworks were designed to be enforced together.

## Timeline

- **2003** — Market Abuse Directive (MAD, 2003/6/EC) adopted, requiring member states to prohibit insider dealing and market manipulation. Implementation is uneven.
- **2008-2009** — Financial crisis exposes gaps in market regulation. G20 Pittsburgh summit (September 2009) calls for stronger sanctions on market abuse.
- **2010** — European Commission impact assessment finds MAD transposition is inconsistent; only 12 member states have criminal penalties for insider dealing.
- **2011** — ESMA is established, inheriting coordination of market abuse enforcement from the Committee of European Securities Regulators (CESR).
- **2012, June** — Barclays fined £290 million in the Libor scandal. Commissioner Barnier accelerates plans for a stronger market abuse regime.
- **2012, July 25** — Barnier proposes MAR and CSMAD simultaneously, citing Libor as evidence that the existing framework is insufficient.
- **2013, September** — European Parliament's ECON committee votes 42-2 on the final compromise text, with support from S&D, EPP, ALDE, and Greens. The lone opponents are a small group of conservative MEPs.
- **2014, April 16** — MAR adopted by the European Parliament and Council as Regulation (EU) 596/2014.
- **2016, July 3** — MAR becomes applicable, replacing MAD and its implementing directives.
- **2018** — First major enforcement cases under MAR: the French AMF fines several firms for insider dealing, while the UK's FCA issues its first fine under MAR against a trader for market manipulation.
- **2021** — The European Commission publishes a review of MAR, noting that while enforcement has improved, smaller issuers struggle with the cost of compliance.
- **2023** — ESMA reports that over 200 administrative fines under MAR have been imposed across the EU, with total penalties exceeding €150 million.

## Impact and consequences

MAR fundamentally reshaped how market abuse is prosecuted in the EU. The shift from a directive to a regulation closed the loopholes from inconsistent national transposition. By 2021, according to a Commission report, all member states had harmonised definitions of inside information and market manipulation, and ESMA had issued binding technical standards.

The most contentious consequence was the impact on smaller listed companies. The requirement to maintain insider lists — recording every person with access to inside information — proved disproportionately costly for firms with limited compliance staff. The Commission's 2021 evaluation found that 73% of survey respondents from small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) reported that MAR compliance costs had increased by more than 20% since 2016. The European Association of Craft, Small and Medium-Sized Enterprises (UEAPME) lobbied for exemptions, leading the Commission to propose in 2023 a lighter regime for SME growth markets.

The Regulation also shifted enforcement dynamics. National competent authorities, now armed with harmonised sanctions, became more aggressive. The French Autorité des Marchés Financiers (AMF) fined investment firm Kepler Cheuvreux €20 million in 2019 for failing to report suspicious transactions — the largest MAR fine at the time. The UK's FCA (after Brexit, the UK retained its own version of MAR) fined a senior manager at a London-listed firm £1.2 million for unlawful disclosure of inside information.

The extension to commodity derivatives and emission allowances had practical effects. The European Energy Exchange (EEX) reported that the requirement to publish inside information on emission allowances forced some industrial firms to disclose operational data they previously treated as confidential. The European Federation of Energy Traders (EFET) warned that this could reduce liquidity in carbon markets, but ESMA's 2022 assessment found no evidence of such effects.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU switch from a directive to a regulation for market abuse under MAR?

The switch was a direct response to the failure of the Market Abuse Directive (MAD). Under MAD, each member state transposed the rules differently, creating a patchwork of definitions, sanctions, and enforcement powers. The Libor scandal in 2012 exposed how cross-border manipulation could exploit these gaps. A directly applicable Regulation removed national discretion, ensuring uniform rules and equal enforcement across all 28 member states (at the time).

### Why were emission allowances included in MAR?

Emission allowances under the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) were included because of evidence that the carbon market was vulnerable to manipulation. In 2009, a VAT fraud scheme involving emission allowances cost EU treasuries an estimated €5 billion. The Commission's impact assessment for MAR noted that carbon markets required the same integrity as financial markets, especially as their role grew in climate policy. The inclusion was championed by MEP Sven Giegold and supported by the European Commission's climate directorate (DG CLIMA).

### Why was a criminal sanctions directive adopted alongside MAR?

The Criminal Sanctions for Market Abuse Directive (CSMAD, 2014/57/EU) was proposed simultaneously because the Commission concluded that administrative fines alone were insufficient deterrents. The Libor and earlier scandals had shown that financial penalties were often treated as a cost of doing business. CSMAD required member states to make insider dealing and market manipulation criminal offences punishable by at least four years imprisonment for the most serious cases. However, the legal base forced the Commission to use a directive rather than a regulation, as criminal law is a national competence under the EU treaties.

### Why did UK financial firms oppose MAR so strongly?

The UK financial services industry, represented by the BBA and the City of London Corporation, opposed MAR primarily because of its strict rules on delay of disclosure. Under MAR, an issuer could only delay the publication of inside information if it had explicit permission from its national competent authority or had a "legitimate interest" — a much narrower exemption than under MAD. Firms argued that this would force premature disclosure of mergers and acquisitions, harming deal-making. The opposition weakened after the Libor scandal damaged the industry's credibility, but the UK's later Brexit decision was partly motivated by a desire to escape MAR's constraints.
