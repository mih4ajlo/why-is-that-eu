## What is it?

The Renewable Energy Directive III (RED III), formally Directive (EU) 2023/2413, is the third iteration of the EU's central legislative framework for promoting renewable energy. It entered into force on November 20, 2023, and member states must transpose its provisions into national law by May 21, 2025. Its core feature is a binding target for the EU to achieve at least a 42.5% share of energy from renewable sources in gross final energy consumption by 2030, with an additional 2.5% aspirational target that would bring the total to 45%.

The directive also introduces specific sub-targets for sectors like transport (a 29% greenhouse gas intensity reduction target from renewables), industry (a 1.6% annual increase in renewable energy use, with a 42% target for renewable hydrogen by 2030), and buildings (a 49% renewable energy share target by 2030). It overhauls permitting procedures by requiring member states to designate "renewables acceleration areas" where permitting for new projects must be completed within 12 months, and expands the "overriding public interest" presumption for renewable projects to further speed up approvals.

## Why was it introduced?

The most specific, dramatic element driving RED III was not a single scandal but a cascading energy crisis triggered by Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022. The invasion sent natural gas prices to record highs, reaching over €300 per megawatt-hour in August 2022, and forced the EU to confront its deep dependence on Russian fossil fuels—which accounted for 40% of EU gas imports in 2021, according to Eurostat data from that period. Overnight, energy security became a matter of existential urgency, and renewable energy was recast from a climate tool to a strategic necessity.

The structural tension that made a new directive inevitable was the gap between the EU's existing ambition under the European Green Deal and the reality of the REPowerEU plan. The Green Deal, announced in December 2019 by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, had set the 2030 emission reduction target to at least 55% (the "Fit for 55" package). Under this, the Commission's initial July 2021 proposal for RED III raised the 2030 renewable energy target from 32% (under RED II) to 40%. However, the REPowerEU plan of May 2022, designed to end Russian energy imports by 2027, proposed a further increase to 45%. The directive had to reconcile these two tracks: the climate ambition of the Green Deal and the security-driven urgency of REPowerEU.

The coalition dynamics were unusually robust. The invasion created a broad consensus across the European Parliament and Council. In Parliament, the key rapporteurs—Markus Pieper (EPP, Germany) for the main file and Morten Petersen (Renew Europe, Denmark) on the related EU solar strategy—pushed for higher ambition. The Council, too, was largely united, though some member states like Poland and Hungary, which remained heavily coal-dependent, expressed reservations about binding national targets and the speed of transition. The opposition was led by fossil fuel industry lobbies and some energy-intensive industries, such as the European Chemical Industry Council (Cefic), which warned that overly rapid deployment could disrupt energy markets and raise costs. However, the geopolitical shock of the war drowned out most of this opposition; the vote in Parliament on September 14, 2023, was a resounding 470 in favour to 120 against, with 42 abstentions.

The timing succeeded because the crisis broke a previous deadlock. Earlier attempts to strengthen the directive had stalled during the RED II negotiations in 2018, when the final target was set at 32%—the result of a hard-fought compromise between a more ambitious Parliament and a more cautious Council, with the Commission's initial proposal of 27% acting as a floor. What changed in 2022 was the war in Ukraine. The Commission's REPowerEU plan, announced on May 18, 2022, explicitly linked renewable energy deployment to ending Russian energy imports, giving the legislative process a speed and purpose it had lacked. The Commission also used the crisis to push through emergency permitting measures (the 2022 Regulation on emergency permitting for renewables, 2022/2577/EU) as a precursor to the permanent changes embedded in RED III.

## Timeline

- **December 11, 2019** — European Green Deal published, setting the 2050 climate neutrality goal and raising the 2030 emission reduction target.
- **July 14, 2021** — Commission proposes the "Fit for 55" package, including RED III with a 40% renewable energy target for 2030.
- **February 24, 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine, triggering the EU energy crisis.
- **May 18, 2022** — Commission publishes REPowerEU plan, proposing a 45% renewable energy target for 2030.
- **November 9, 2022** — Commission adopts emergency regulation (2022/2577/EU) to accelerate permitting for renewable projects.
- **March 30, 2023** — European Parliament and Council reach a provisional political agreement on RED III.
- **September 12, 2023** — Parliament formally adopts the directive (470 votes in favour, 120 against).
- **October 9, 2023** — Council formally adopts the directive.
- **November 20, 2023** — Directive (EU) 2023/2413 enters into force.
- **May 21, 2025** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence of RED III is the increased compliance burden on member states. According to the Commission's 2022 impact assessment for the Fit for 55 package, achieving the 42.5% target would require total additional investments of approximately €620 billion in renewable energy between 2021 and 2030, compared with the 40% target baseline. The European Investment Bank and national governments are the primary sources of financing.

Industry response has been mixed. The European solar sector welcomed the permitting reforms; SolarPower Europe, the EU trade association, noted in its 2023 Market Outlook that permitting delays had been the single largest barrier to installations. However, the wind industry, represented by WindEurope, warned in public statements that the directive's accelerated permitting targets might lead to lower quality projects and environmental challenges. Some energy-intensive industries, particularly steelmakers like ArcelorMittal and chemical producers like BASF, expressed concerns about the dedicated hydrogen targets, which require that 42% of hydrogen used in industry be renewable by 2030—a target they argued was unrealistic given the current lack of affordable green hydrogen supply.

In terms of enforcement, the Commission has already issued recommendations to several member states for delays in transposing RED II (2018/2001/EU), whose transposition deadline was June 2021. By the end of 2023, several member states, including Bulgaria, Poland, and Romania, had not fully transposed RED II. With RED III's tighter deadlines and more ambitious targets, the Commission has indicated it will use infringement procedures more aggressively; a 2024 Commission communication on enforcement of the Green Deal warned it would "not hesitate" to take legal action against member states failing to transpose RED III on time.

## Key questions answered

### Why is the target 42.5% instead of a round number like 45% in RED III?

The 42.5% target emerged from a compromise between the Parliament's ambition for 45% (driven by REPowerEU) and the Council's concern about national binding targets. In the March 2023 trilogue negotiations, the Parliament accepted a binding collective target of 42.5% while gaining an additional "aspirational" 2.5% top-up to reach 45%. This structure allowed both sides to claim victory: Parliament could point to the aspiration, while member states retained flexibility, as the top-up is not legally binding. The 32% target under RED II had also been a compromise between the 27% originally proposed by the Commission and the 35% sought by Parliament.

### Why did RED III introduce dedicated targets for industry and transport rather than a single overall target?

The introduction of sector-specific sub-targets reflects a structural issue that emerged during RED II's implementation: the overall renewable energy target masked very uneven progress across sectors. According to the Commission's 2020 Renewable Energy Progress Report, the share of renewables in electricity had reached 34% by 2018, but in transport it was only 8% and in heating and cooling it was just 22%. Industry and transport remained heavily fossil-dependent, particularly for hydrogen used in refining and steelmaking. The sector targets in RED III force action in these lagging areas, preventing countries from simply reaching the overall target through a massive build-out of cheap electricity alone.

### Why did the war in Ukraine trigger a faster permitting regime under RED III?

Before the war, permitting was a well-known barrier but lacked the political will to reform it. The Commission's 2020 study on permitting barriers found that wind projects in Germany took an average of 5-7 years to permit, while in Italy it could reach 10 years. These delays were widely seen as the single biggest bottleneck to deployment, but member states resisted reform because they feared losing control over local planning decisions. The 2022 energy crisis changed this calculation. When Russia cut gas flows via Nord Stream 1 in June 2022, the EU faced an immediate fuel shortage. The Commission's emergency regulation of November 2022, which introduced 6-month permitting deadlines for certain renewable projects, was explicitly justified under Article 122 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the EU, which allows emergency measures in times of crisis. RED III's permanent reforms codified these crisis-era measures into law.
