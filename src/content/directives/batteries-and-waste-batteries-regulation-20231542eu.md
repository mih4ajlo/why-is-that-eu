## What is it?

The Batteries Regulation (2023/1542/EU) overhauls all rules for batteries sold in the EU, covering the entire product lifecycle — from mining of raw materials like lithium and cobalt, through manufacturing, labelling, and use, to end-of-life collection and recycling. It introduces mandatory carbon footprint declarations, minimum recycled content requirements, performance and durability standards, and a unique "battery passport" for each industrial and electric-vehicle battery over 2 kWh. The regulation replaced the 2006 Batteries Directive (2006/66/EC), which focused narrowly on waste management and disposal.

The regulation applies to five categories: portable batteries (e.g., in phones, tools), light means of transport (LMT) batteries (e-bikes, scooters), starting-lighting-ignition (SLI) batteries (cars, machinery), industrial batteries (forklifts, backup power), and electric-vehicle (EV) batteries. By 2027, all batteries must carry a QR code linking to a digital passport with data on composition, origin, and repair history. A "battery passport" pilot ran in 2022, and the full system — the world’s first — takes effect in 2027 for EV and industrial batteries.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a perfect storm: a global battery supply crunch, growing environmental scandals in cobalt and lithium mining, Europe’s strategic fear of import dependence on China for electric-vehicle batteries, and a recognition that the 2006 Batteries Directive had failed to drive recycling or curb waste. The trigger moment was not a single scandal but a crescendo of events.

**The structural tension** was straightforward: the 2006 directive had set collection and recycling targets for portable batteries but lacked any rules on what happened *before* disposal — no minimum recycled content, no carbon footprint requirements, and no link to product design. By 2019, the Commission’s own evaluation found that the directive had "limited impact" on increasing recycling rates: only 45% of portable batteries placed on the EU market in 2018 were collected for recycling, far below the 50% target, and recycling processes for lithium-ion cells — now dominant — were virtually non-existent. Meanwhile, battery demand was exploding: the Commission’s 2020 Strategic Action Plan on Batteries estimated that global battery demand would grow 14-fold by 2030, and the EU risked becoming a dumping ground for imported batteries with unknown environmental footprints.

**The political coalition** that drove the regulation was unusual. It included environmental NGOs (ClientEarth, EEB) pushing for supply-chain due diligence, and the European automotive industry itself — companies like Volkswagen, Renault, and BMW — who feared being locked into dependence on Chinese battery giants (CATL, BYD) that controlled 70-80% of global battery cell production in 2020. In 2018, the European Battery Alliance, spearheaded by Commission Vice President Maroš Šefčovič, explicitly argued that the EU needed "a robust regulatory framework" to attract investment in domestic battery factories. The regulation was part of a broader industrial strategy, the Critical Raw Materials Act (2023), which aimed to secure supply chains.

**Opposition** came from some import-reliant manufacturers, non-EU producers, and member states with no domestic battery industry — notably Sweden, which argued for slower timelines on carbon footprint rules, and Poland, which hosted several new gigafactories and worried about compliance costs. Industry association Eurobat, representing battery makers, warned that the recycled content targets (e.g., 6% lithium and 16% cobalt by 2031) were "technically challenging" and could raise costs. The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur, MEP Simona Bonafè (S&D, Italy), later said the final compromise struck in December 2022 was "a delicate balance between ambition and feasibility."

**The timing** was critical: the regulation was proposed in December 2020, adopted in July 2023, and its timeline coincides with the EU’s 2030 climate target of reducing emissions by 55%. The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment projected that without new rules, 70% of EU battery demand would be met by imports by 2030, undermining the bloc’s strategic autonomy. The COVID-19 pandemic and supply-chain disruptions in 2020-2021 accelerated political urgency — the Commission’s President Ursula von der Leyen explicitly linked battery policy to European "open strategic autonomy" in her 2020 State of the Union speech.

**What changed** from earlier attempts was the shift from a waste-management directive to a full-product-regulation framework. The 2006 directive had no teeth on design or supply chains; the 2023 regulation does — including mandatory due diligence for social and environmental risks in the supply chain (for cobalt, lithium, nickel, and graphite), a first in EU product law. The battery passport concept was borrowed from the World Economic Forum’s "circular electronics" initiative and industrial pilots by Audi (2021) and the Global Battery Alliance (2019), which demonstrated that digital traceability could work in practice.

## Timeline

- **2006** — The original Batteries Directive (2006/66/EC) adopted, focusing on collection and recycling of portable batteries.
- **2015** — Commission evaluation finds the 2006 directive’s collection target (45%) was missed in most member states; recycling processes for lithium-ion batteries remain largely absent.
- **2017** — European Battery Alliance launched by Commission VP Šefčovič, bringing together 20+ companies to boost domestic production and push for new rules.
- **2019** — Public consultation begins; 2,692 responses show broad support for stricter rules on materials, carbon footprint, and supply-chain due diligence.
- **December 2020** — Commission publishes the proposed Batteries Regulation, replacing the 2006 directive.
- **March 2022** — European Parliament adopts its position (584 votes in favour, 67 against), adding mandatory due diligence requirements.
- **July 2022** — Council adopts its position; trilogue negotiations begin.
- **December 2022** — Provisional political agreement reached between Parliament and Council, including the battery passport and recycled content targets.
- **July 2023** — Final text adopted by Council (no vote split publicly recorded); published in Official Journal.
- **February 2024** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after publication).
- **August 2025** — First application date: mandatory carbon footprint declarations for EV and industrial batteries.
- **2027** — Battery passport mandatory for all EV and industrial batteries above 2 kWh; collection targets for portable batteries rise to 63% (portable) and 73% for LMT batteries.
- **2028** — Recycled content targets take effect for cobalt (16%), lead (85%), lithium (6%), and nickel (6%).
- **2031** — Targets increase: cobalt (26%), lithium (12%), nickel (15%).
- **2033** — Final law review by Commission.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects any company placing batteries on the EU market — roughly 300,000 tonnes of portable batteries annually (Eurostat 2022 estimate), plus millions of EV and industrial units. The consultancy McKinsey estimated in 2022 that compliance costs per battery could rise by 5-10%, mainly due to data collection and passport systems. Industry reaction has been split: EU-based gigafactories (Northvolt in Sweden, ACC in France, Volkswagen’s Salzgitter plant) welcomed the regulation as a competitive advantage, while Asian importers (CATL, LG Energy Solution) warned about "disproportionate administrative burden." In 2023, the Commission committed €3 billion via the European Battery Alliance to support recycling infrastructure.

The battery passport, built on a standardised data format, is the most innovative element: it will be accessible through a QR code and include the battery’s manufacturer, date of production, chemistry, carbon footprint, and a "recycling score." A pilot in 2022 involving Audi, Volvo, and the Global Battery Alliance demonstrated the model works. Environmental groups praised the due diligence requirement — ClientEarth called it "a historic step" — but noted that the regulation exempts small volumes of artisanal cobalt (under 2% of supply) from full traceability.

**First enforcement actions** will begin in 2025. The Commission has not yet fined any company, but the regulation gives national market surveillance authorities the power to impose fines of up to 4% of annual turnover for non-compliance. The first major test case could be over the carbon footprint declaration — companies that fail to verify claims by an accredited third party will face bans on selling affected batteries.

## Key questions answered

### Are companies that make batteries for phones, cars, or power tools all subject to the same rules?

No. The regulation creates five distinct categories with different timelines and requirements. Portable batteries (phones, tools) have the tightest collection targets (63% by 2027) but lighter due diligence rules. EV and industrial batteries must have a digital passport by 2027, while starting-lighting-ignition (car) batteries face separate design-for-recycling rules. Light means of transport (e-bike) batteries have specific removal-and-replacement standards. The carbon footprint declaration only applies to EV and industrial batteries above 2 kWh.

### Why does EU law now require a "battery passport" and what information does it contain?

The passport is a digital tool to make the entire battery lifecycle transparent — a response to the fact that batteries are complex products with long lives (10-20 years in a car) and unknown second-hand markets. For every battery over 2 kWh, the passport includes: manufacturer, date of production, battery chemistry, carbon footprint, recycled content percentage, repair history, and a "recycling score". It’s accessed via a QR code on the battery casing, using open-source software to allow service shops, recyclers, and buyers to verify data. The concept was piloted in 2022 by Audi, Volvo, and the Global Battery Alliance.

### What happens if a battery company refuses to disclose where its cobalt comes from?

It cannot sell that battery in the EU. The regulation makes supply-chain due diligence mandatory for cobalt, nickel, lithium, and graphite — the first EU product law to do so. Companies must submit to third-party audits of their entire supply chain, from mining to refining, and publish a "sustainable sourcing" report. The rule targets child labour in DRC cobalt mines and environmental damage in Australian lithium operations. Small-scale artisanal miners are exempt only if they supply under 2% of the total volume. In practice, major producers like Glencore (Switzerland) and Albemarle (US) have already set up traceability systems; the main impact will be on smaller, opaque suppliers in Asia and Africa.

### Is the regulation’s main effect about recycling or about preventing the EU from depending on Chinese battery imports?

Both, but the strategic independence argument was more important politically. The regulation emerged from the European Battery Alliance (2017) and the Critical Raw Materials Act (2023), both explicitly framed by the Commission as responses to China controlling 80% of world battery cell production (2020). By mandating recycled content (up to 26% cobalt by 2031) and carbon-footprint reporting, the EU aims to make European-made batteries cheaper and greener than imported ones, attracting gigafactory investments. The environmental side — recycling and toxics rules — was championed by the European Parliament, but the driving coalition was industrial policy: automotive companies like Volkswagen and Stellantis pushing to break Chinese supply dominance.
