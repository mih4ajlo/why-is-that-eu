## What is it?

The Data Act is an EU regulation that clarifies the rights of users — both individuals and businesses — to access the data generated by their use of smart devices, from tractors and industrial machinery to smartwatches and connected cars. It mandates that manufacturers cannot lock users out of the data their products produce, and must make such data available in real-time, free of charge. For business-to-business (B2B) and business-to-government (B2G) contexts, the Act sets rules on fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory (FRAND) terms for data-sharing contracts, including safeguards against abuse of contractual imbalances by dominant data holders.

The regulation also addresses technical and contractual barriers to switching between cloud and edge service providers, requiring the removal of switching charges and ensuring data portability within 30 days. It rebalances power in data-sharing agreements, nullifying unfair contractual terms imposed by a stronger party — notably in the context of "take-it-or-leave-it" contracts from dominant platforms or manufacturers. Critically, the Data Act does not alter the existing legal definition of who "owns" data; ownership remains a property law question not harmonised by this regulation. Instead, it focuses on access and use rights. Non-compliance can lead to fines of up to 20 million euros or 4% of a company's global annual turnover — the same maximum as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for the Data Act was not a single scandal but a structural failure: the vast majority of industrial and personal data generated in Europe was never used. The European Commission estimated in its 2022 impact assessment that 80% of industrial data collected in the EU was never even analysed, let alone shared or used to create new services. Meanwhile, a handful of large manufacturers and platform companies — in sectors from agricultural machinery (Deere & Company, CNH Industrial) to telecommunications (Ericsson, Nokia) — controlled access to this data, often using it to lock customers into proprietary ecosystems or extract monopoly rents.

The political coalition that pushed the Data Act was an unusual alliance of European industrial users and consumer advocates. The European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA) warned in 2021 that without clear rules, the EU would lose its competitive edge in connected services. Key driving figures included Commissioner for Internal Market Thierry Breton (France), who championed the Act as part of his EU sovereignty agenda, and the European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS), who warned in a 2021 opinion that data access imbalances had become systematic. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Pilar del Castillo Vera (Spain, EPP), worked closely with the Slovenian Presidency of the Council in 2021 to build consensus.

Opposition was significant and came from a powerful coalition. Manufacturers of complex machinery — especially agricultural equipment maker Deere & Company and its European dealers — warned that mandatory data-sharing would undermine intellectual property and business models built on aftermarket services. The German engineering association VDMA (Verband Deutscher Maschinen- und Anlagenbau) argued vocally that the rules would "socialise" private investment in sensor technology. The Computer & Communications Industry Association (CCIA Europe), representing companies including Google, Amazon, and Meta, claimed the Act would harm cloud competition by imposing undue switching costs. In the final plenary vote in the European Parliament on 14 November 2023, the Act passed 481-62, with 44 abstentions — a clear victory for the pro-regulation camp, but the 106 combined opposition and abstention showing that a significant minority remained unconvinced.

Timing was critical. The Data Act succeeded under the von der Leyen Commission because the geopolitical environment created urgency. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 had highlighted Europe's vulnerability in digital sovereignty — from cloud infrastructure to data access. The pandemic had exposed how proprietary data lock-in could delay medical research and supply-chain adjustments. Moreover, the Act complemented the Data Governance Act (2022/868/EU), adopted only months earlier, which created the broader framework for data altruism and data intermediaries. The Data Act was the missing piece: the binding rules that turned voluntary data-sharing into a legal right.

What changed from earlier attempts was the specific design of the "unfair contractual terms" mechanism. Earlier drafts faced accusations from industry that they would create legal uncertainty. In 2022, the European Commission's own Regulatory Scrutiny Board initially gave a negative opinion on the Act's impact assessment, demanding clearer cost-benefit analysis. The final version narrowed the scope — for example, explicitly exempting trade secrets unless absolutely necessary for data portability — and introduced a two-year transition period for existing contracts. These concessions secured the support of the German government, which had been wavering under industry pressure.

## Timeline

- **2017** — The European Commission's Communication on "Building a European Data Economy" first flags the problem of data access imbalances in the Internet of Things
- **2020 February** — The European Strategy for Data (COM/2020/66) announces the intention to propose a "Data Act" to address data access rights
- **2021 March** — Commission launches public consultation; receives over 300 responses from industry, civil society, and member states
- **2022 February 23** — The European Commission publishes the official proposal for the Data Act, alongside an impact assessment
- **2022 June** — The European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) publishes Opinion 3/2022, broadly supporting the proposal while calling for stronger safeguards for personal data
- **2022 November** — The European Parliament's Committee on Industry, Research and Energy (ITRE) adopts its report with amendments
- **2023 March** — The Council of the EU adopts its general approach; negotiations between Parliament, Council, and Commission begin
- **2023 June 27** — The trilogues conclude with a provisional political agreement
- **2023 November 9** — The European Parliament adopts the final text (481-62, 44 abstentions)
- **2023 November 27** — The Council formally adopts the regulation
- **2023 December 22** — The Data Act enters into force (20 days after publication in the Official Journal)
- **2025 September 12** — Full applicability date (two-year transition period ends); first compliance deadline

## Impact and consequences

The Data Act's full impact will only become clear after its 2025 applicability date, but early consequences are already visible. Several major manufacturers have begun restructuring their data-access models: John Deere announced in 2023 that it would expand its API access for independent repair shops in Europe — a shift that European right-to-repair advocates attribute to the looming Data Act. The tractor company had faced sustained criticism in the United States over repair monopolies, and the EU regulation created regulatory momentum across the Atlantic.

For cloud service providers, the Act's switching and portability requirements are forcing contract renegotiations. The European Cloud Alliance, a coalition of smaller providers, welcomed the ban on switching charges but expressed concern that the 30-day deadline for full portability would be technically challenging. The major cloud providers — AWS (Amazon), Azure (Microsoft), and Google Cloud — have all begun publishing compliance roadmaps, with Microsoft announcing in early 2024 a new "European Data Boundary" offering for customers.

The most contentious area remains B2B data-sharing for industrial machinery. German automotive supplier association GVA predicted in 2023 that the FRAND pricing framework could reduce manufacturer revenues from aftermarket services by up to 10%, though the Commission's impact assessment had estimated a net gain of €270 billion in additional GDP across sectors by 2028 through increased data use. The agricultural sector has seen early moves: the European Agricultural Machinery Association (CEMA) issued compliance guidelines in 2024, noting that 40% of its member companies had already begun redesigning data-sharing interfaces.

Member state enforcement authorities are preparing. By early 2025, 12 member states had designated their Data Act enforcement bodies — typically existing data protection authorities or competition agencies — while others, notably Poland and Bulgaria, had not yet completed the process, causing concern about uneven enforcement. The first legal challenge arrived in January 2024, when a coalition of German machinery manufacturers filed for partial annulment of the regulation at the Court of Justice of the European Union, arguing that its data-access provisions violated fundamental property rights; the case was pending as of early 2025.

## Key questions answered

### Does a company have to share data from my smart tractor or factory machine with me?

Yes, if you are the user of the product. Under the Data Act, anyone who owns, rents, or leases a connected device — whether a tractor, a factory robot, or a smartwatch — has the right to access the data generated by their use of that device in real-time, free of charge. The manufacturer must provide the data in a structured, commonly used format. This applies to "product data" and "related service data" — basically, the data that flows from your actual use of the device.

### Can I switch cloud providers without paying a fee?

From September 2025, yes. The Data Act bans switching charges for moving your data between cloud and edge service providers. It also requires the old provider to complete the migration within 30 days (with some exceptions for complex technical cases). This is designed to end the practice of "vendor lock-in" where customers stay with a cloud provider because the cost of leaving is too high. The rules apply to all cloud providers operating in the EU, including Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud.

### Does the Data Act change who owns data?

No. The Data Act does not create a new legal right of "ownership" over data. Instead, it focuses on access and use rights. This was a deliberate choice: ownership of data is a complex property law question that EU member states handle differently. The regulation leaves that untouched. What changes is your right to *use* the data — even if the manufacturer technically "owns" it for intellectual property purposes, you now have a legal right to access it.

### What happens if a manufacturer imposes unfair contractual terms for accessing data?

The Data Act makes such terms automatically null and void. It lists specific types of contractual clauses that are considered "unfair" — for example, clauses that give the stronger party the right to unilaterally interpret the contract, or that lock you into a data-sharing agreement indefinitely. Critically, the Act reverses the burden of proof: if there is a significant imbalance between the contracting parties, the presumption is that unfair terms exist. This was a major concession by industry, which had argued for a more limited list.
