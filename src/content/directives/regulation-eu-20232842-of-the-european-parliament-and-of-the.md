## What is it?

The Fisheries Control Regulation recast is the EU’s overhaul of the rulebook governing how fishing is monitored, reported, and enforced across Member States. It replaces and consolidates a patchwork of earlier rules — the original 2009 Control Regulation (EC) No 1224/2009 and subsequent amendments — into a single, digitally driven framework. The core change is mandatory electronic recording and reporting of catches for vessels down to 9 metres in length, real‑time transmission of vessel position data, and electronic traceability for all fishery and aquaculture products sold in the EU.

The regulation sets minimum standards for inspections, sanctions, and cross‑border cooperation. It also introduces a centralised EU database for catch data (the "Fisheries Monitoring and Control System") and gives the European Fisheries Control Agency (EFCA) a stronger role in coordinating joint deployment plans, especially in high‑risk areas. The recast was adopted in November 2023 and applies from 2024 onward, with a phased timeline for digital reporting obligations.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform was triggered not by a single scandal, but by a decade of accumulating evidence that the existing control system was structurally incapable of stopping illegal fishing and was failing to deliver reliable catch data. The most damning blow came from the European Court of Auditors (ECA) in a 2017 special report that found "significant weaknesses" in how Member States applied controls, inspected vessels, and reported information. The ECA calculated that the volume of catches reported by EU fleets was inconsistent with trade data; for some species, reported catches were less than half of what was being sold. The report noted that several Member States had not fully implemented electronic recording and reporting (ERS) as required by the 2009 regulation, and that the Commission had only initiated infringement proceedings in a handful of cases.

The political response crystallised under Commissioner for Environment, Oceans and Fisheries Virginijus Sinkevičius, who took office in 2019. The incoming European Green Deal and the Farm to Fork Strategy made sustainable fisheries a high‑profile priority. The Commission’s 2020 Sustainable Fisheries Partnership Agreements review showed that IUU fishing cost the EU fishing industry an estimated €400 million per year in lost revenue. Meanwhile, the European Parliament’s Fisheries Committee (PECH), led by rapporteur Clara Aguilera (S&D, Spain), pressed for a tighter system, arguing that without mandatory electronic logbooks for small‑scale fleets, weak spots would remain.

The decisive coalition was unusual: environmental NGOs such as Oceana and the Pew Charitable Trusts worked closely with PECH to demand remote electronic monitoring (REM), including mandatory CCTV on vessels that posed high compliance risks. The fishing industry, represented by Europêche, opposed CCTV as disproportionate and costly, but ultimately accepted the principle of mandatory electronic logbooks for vessels under 12 metres after the Commission phased the obligation in over three years and offered co‑financing through the European Maritime, Fisheries and Aquaculture Fund (EMFAF). A last‑minute flashpoint was the extension of traceability obligations to imported processed fish – EU countries with large canning sectors, especially Spain and Portugal, fought for exemptions, but they were overruled after the European Parliament threatened to reject the file. In the final vote in the Council on 20 October 2023, the regulation was adopted with only Romania and Poland abstaining.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Council Regulation (EC) No 1224/2009 establishes the first EU‑wide fisheries control system, introducing vessel monitoring systems (VMS) and electronic logbooks, but implementation is patchy.
- **2017** — European Court of Auditors special report "EU fisheries controls – more efforts needed" exposes critical gaps and recommends mandatory electronic reporting and a centralised data system.
- **2018** — The Commission publishes a legislative proposal for a recast, citing the ECA findings and the need to align with the reformed Common Fisheries Policy (CFP) (Regulation 1380/2013).
- **2020** — Commissioner Sinkevičius makes the recast part of the "EU Action Plan to combat IUU fishing".
- **2021** — The European Parliament adopts its position with a strong mandate for remote electronic monitoring (CCTV) on high‑risk vessels.
- **2022** — Trilogue negotiations begin; the Council resists mandatory CCTV, and the final compromise leaves REM optional for Member States.
- **20 October 2023** — Council formally adopts the recast regulation (with Poland and Romania abstaining).
- **15 November 2023** — Regulation published in the Official Journal; enters into force 20 days later.
- **10 January 2024** — First application date for most provisions, with phased deadlines: electronic logbooks for vessels ≥12 m by 2025, for vessels 9–12 m by 2026.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation changes daily practice for around 40,000 active EU fishing vessels. For small‑scale fleets (vessels under 12 m, which account for ≈75% of the fleet but only ≈6% of landings by volume), electronic logbooks replace paper forms for the first time. The Commission’s 2021 impact assessment estimated compliance costs at roughly €2,000 per vessel (including hardware and training) – a sum co‑financed at up to 75% by EMFAF. Opposition came mainly from artisanal fishermen in the Mediterranean and Baltic, who argued that low‑cost e‑logbooks were still a burden for owner‑operators making short day trips. Several Member States – notably Italy, Greece, and Croatia – warned of administrative bottlenecks and requested extended deadlines, which the regulation partially grants through a 2026 phase‑in for the smallest vessels.

On enforcement, the recast introduces a "points system" for the first time across all Member States: serious fisheries offences accumulate points, and a vessel that exceeds a threshold loses its fishing licence for a period. The system mirrors the one used in road transport. By mid‑2025, the Commission had not yet reported on the number of points assigned, but early reviews by EFCA highlighted slow uptake in national point registers. The greater impact may be on third‑country fleets: the regulation now requires electronic catch certificates for all imported fishery products, a move that the Commission says will close a loophole that allowed trans‑shipments at sea to avoid controls.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU track fishing boats and their catches?

Every EU fishing vessel, including small boats down to 9 metres, must now be equipped with an electronic logbook that transmits catch and effort data to national authorities in near real time. Vessels longer than 12 metres also carry a vessel monitoring system (VMS) sat‑tracker, while smaller ones can use newer, cheaper mobile‑based transmitters. All data flows into a centralised EU database accessible to the Commission and to Member State inspection services, enabling cross‑checks with port landing declarations and trade data.

### What happens to countries that allow illegal fishing?

The regulation strengthens the EU’s ability to penalise both vessels and Member States. For vessel operators, a points system for serious offences – such as false reporting, fishing in closed areas, or tampering with gear – can lead to licence suspension and eventual revocation. For Member States that fail to enforce the rules, the Commission can reduce or suspend EU fisheries funding. The recast also extends the EU’s IUU black‑listing powers: third countries that do not cooperate are subject to trade restrictions, including a ban on exporting fish into the European single market.

### Do EU fishing vessels have to use electronic logbooks?

Yes, with a phased timeline. As of 2025, all vessels 12 metres or longer must submit electronic logbooks. Vessels between 9 and 12 metres must do so by 2026. The Commission has committed to providing technical support and co‑funding to help the estimated 30 000 small boats make the switch. Only vessels under 9 m that operate exclusively within the baseline of a single Member State’s coastal waters and never land at a third country are temporarily exempt.
