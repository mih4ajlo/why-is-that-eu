## What is it?

The Energy Efficiency Directive 2023 (EED Recast) is the EU's primary legislative tool for reducing energy consumption across all sectors — buildings, industry, transport, and public services. It replaced the 2018 version and raised the bloc's energy efficiency ambition to align with the European Green Deal's goal of a 55% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030.

The directive sets a binding EU-level target to reduce final energy consumption by 11.7% by 2030, compared to the 2020 reference year projections. Member states must collectively ensure that annual final energy consumption does not exceed 763 million tonnes of oil equivalent (Mtoe) and primary energy consumption stays below 992.8 Mtoe, according to the Commission's impact assessment. It also introduces a strengthened annual energy savings obligation of 1.49% of final energy consumption from 2024 to 2030 — nearly double the previous 0.8% rate under the 2018 directive — and extends the requirement to cover the public sector, which must reduce its final energy consumption by 1.9% annually.

## Why was it introduced?

The Energy Efficiency Directive 2023 did not emerge from a single scandal or crisis. Instead, it was driven by a structural tension: the 2018 EED had set only an indicative EU target of 32.5% by 2030, which the Commission's 2020 Climate Target Plan found would lock in insufficient ambition. Member states were consistently falling short of their national contributions, and according to Eurostat data, the EU's primary energy consumption in 2021 was still 5.6% above the 2020 target trajectory.

The timing hinged on the Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Before that, the Commission had already proposed a recast in July 2021 as part of the Fit for 55 package, under Commissioner Kadri Simson. But the invasion transformed energy efficiency from a climate tool into an energy security imperative. The **REPowerEU plan**, launched in May 2022, identified energy savings as the fastest way to reduce dependence on Russian gas, calling for a short-term 5% reduction in gas consumption. This geopolitical shock broke a previous deadlock: Eastern European member states that had resisted binding targets — notably Poland, which had questioned the feasibility of national contributions — became more willing to accept higher obligations.

The coalition that passed the directive was built on the European Parliament's centre-right European People's Party (EPP) and the Socialists & Democrats (S&D), with key rapporteur Niels Fuglsang (S&D, Denmark) pushing for the 1.49% savings rate. Industry opposition came from sectors with high energy intensity — steelmakers like ArcelorMittal and the European Chemical Industry Council (Cefic) warned that accelerated efficiency obligations would require capital investments they could not recoup under energy price caps. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) supported the directive but pressed for just transition provisions. The final vote in the European Parliament on 11 July 2023 passed with 483 in favour to 98 against, with 56 abstentions — a comfortable margin, but with significant abstentions from the far-right Identity and Democracy (ID) group.

A specific event unblocked earlier disputes: in December 2022, the Council abandoned the principle of national binding targets, accepting instead an EU-level binding target with indicative national contributions. This concession, driven by Germany and France, who feared being locked into precise figures amid the energy crisis, paved the way for the final agreement.

- **25 July 2023** — Council adopts directive
- **20 September 2023** — Directive enters into force
- **11 October 2025** — Member states must transpose into national law
- **11 October 2026** — First compliance reports due to Commission

## Impact and consequences

Implementation of the 2023 EED requires member states to significantly ramp up national energy savings programmes. The **annual renovation rate of public buildings** must increase to 3% of total floor area, up from the previous 1% under the 2012 directive. A specific case: the European Commission estimated in its 2023 Implementation Report that the 1.49% obligation would require France to save an additional 38 TWh per year by 2030 — equivalent to powering roughly 3 million households.

Industry adaptation has been uneven. In April 2024, **Germany** submitted its revised National Energy and Climate Plan (NECP) projecting that the 1.49% obligation would require €25 billion in public investment through 2030, according to the German Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action. Steelmaker **Thyssenkrupp** announced a €1.5 billion investment in energy-efficient direct reduction iron technology at its Duisburg plant in January 2024, citing the directive's regulatory certainty.

In the **public sector**, the obligation for public entities to purchase only energy-efficient products (e.g., computers with high Energy Star ratings) has already reshaped procurement. The Commission's **Joint Research Centre** reported in October 2023 that 18 member states had updated their green public procurement criteria within one year of the directive's adoption.

First enforcement actions are emerging: in September 2024, the Commission sent letters of formal notice to **Bulgaria, Malta, and Poland** for failure to transpose the directive into national law by the October 2025 deadline (noting these were early warnings under Article 260(3) TFEU).

## Key questions answered

### Why does the Energy Efficiency Directive (Recast) shift from national binding targets to an EU-level binding target?

The shift was a political compromise. Germany and France argued during Council negotiations in late 2022 that setting binding national targets was too rigid given the energy crisis and volatile forecasts. They preferred indicative national contributions with a binding EU overall target — giving them flexibility to adjust through national plans while still holding the bloc collectively accountable. This model mirrors the approach used under the Effort Sharing Regulation.

### Why is the annual savings obligation nearly doubled to 1.49% in the Energy Efficiency Directive (Recast)?

The Commission's 2021 impact assessment modelled that achieving the original 32.5% target required a 0.8% savings rate, but reaching the new 55% emissions goal demanded deeper cuts. Commissioner Kadri Simson stated in the 14 July 2021 press conference that "efficiency is the hidden fuel of the energy transition." The 1.49% figure came from a linear interpolation between the 0.8% rate and what the Commission's modelling showed was needed to stay on the 1.5°C pathway under the Paris Agreement. The European Parliament's ITRE committee had pushed for 1.5%, settling on 1.49% during trilogue talks in March 2023.

### How does the Energy Efficiency Directive (Recast) interact with the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD)?

The EED 2023 sets horizontal efficiency obligations across all sectors, while the EPBD (2024/1275/EU) specifically governs building performance. The EED's 3% renovation rate for public buildings complements the EPBD's new requirement for zero-emission buildings from 2030. Both directives now share the same timeline for national transposition (October 2025), and member states are expected to submit joint implementation plans. The Commission's 2023 guidance document notes that overlap exists — for instance, public building renovations required under the EED must meet the EPBD's energy performance standards.
