## What is it?

The Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD) 2018/1808/EU is the third iteration of the EU's framework for regulating audiovisual content, revised to bring online video-sharing platforms (VSPs) under the same basic rules as traditional broadcasters. It covers three main areas: protecting minors from harmful content, banning hate speech and incitement to violence, and imposing stricter rules on commercial communications (advertising, product placement, sponsorship) in audiovisual media. Crucially, it also introduced a 30% quota for European works in on-demand catalogues and required VSPs to take "appropriate measures" against illegal and harmful content.

The directive was adopted on 14 November 2018, with member states required to transpose it into national law by 19 September 2020. It replaced the 2010 AVMSD, which had not anticipated the rise of user-generated content platforms.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a structural tension that became impossible to ignore: by 2015, YouTube had over a billion users and was generating more viewing time than any European broadcaster, yet it operated under zero liability for the content it hosted, thanks to the e-Commerce Directive's safe harbour. Meanwhile, traditional broadcasters faced stringent rules on hate speech, advertising limits, and children's protection. This created a glaring regulatory asymmetry that broadcasters—especially public service ones—argued was distorting competition.

The specific event that broke the deadlock was the European Commission's 2014 consultation on the AVMSD, which revealed overwhelming support (85% of respondents, per the Commission's 2015 impact assessment) for extending rules to VSPs. But political momentum only arrived after the November 2015 Paris attacks. In the aftermath, EU interior and digital ministers rushed to frame online radicalisation as an immediate threat. Commissioner Günther Oettinger (Digital Economy, 2014–2016) seized the moment, proposing in May 2016 to bring "video-sharing platforms" into scope. The Juncker Commission formally tabled the proposal in May 2016, citing the need to "create a level playing field between linear and non-linear services" and address "the growing risk of harmful content online."

The coalition that got it passed was unusual. It united the centre-right EPP (which wanted stronger child protection), the S&D (which wanted hate speech rules), and the Greens (which wanted European content quotas). The ALDE group was split, with Dutch and Nordic MEPs worried about free speech. The main opposition came from the European Digital Rights (EDRi) coalition and tech industry lobbyists from Google, Facebook, and smaller platforms, who argued that imposing broadcaster-style obligations would destroy user-generated content models. The Parliament's final vote on 2 October 2018 was 452 in favour, 132 against, with 56 abstentions—a comfortable majority.

A critical coalition element was the European content quota. French MEPs, led by Petra Kammerevert (S&D, Germany), pushed aggressively for the 30% on-demand quota, backed by the French film industry and the European Audiovisual Observatory. The Commission's own 2016 impact assessment estimated that European works made up only 21–27% of on-demand catalogues, far below what broadcasters already delivered (over 50%). This gave the quota a data-backed justification that was hard to dismiss.

Timing was decisive: the proposal was fast-tracked under the Juncker Commission's "Digital Single Market" strategy, which was itself a response to fears that Europe was losing its cultural sovereignty to US tech giants. The Brexit vote in June 2016 removed the UK—historically the strongest opponent of European content quotas and the staunchest defender of platform liability exemptions—from the Council's veto power. Without the UK, the balance shifted decisively in favour of intervention.

What changed specifically: earlier attempts to regulate online video, like the 2007 AVMSD, had explicitly exempted user-generated content. The 2018 revision overturned that exemption, creating a new legal category of "video-sharing platform" with direct obligations.

## Timeline

- **2007** — The first AVMSD (2007/65/EC) introduces the "country of origin" principle and rules for on-demand services, but explicitly exempts user-generated content.
- **2010** — The 2010 AVMSD (2010/13/EU) codifies the 2007 rules.
- **May 2015** — The Commission launches a public consultation on the AVMSD. 85% of respondents favour extending rules to VSPs (Commission impact assessment, 2016).
- **November 2015** — Paris terrorist attacks. Political urgency around online radicalisation spikes.
- **May 2016** — Commissioner Oettinger presents the Commission's AVMSD revision proposal.
- **October 2018** — European Parliament adopts the revised directive (452-132-56).
- **November 2018** — Council formally adopts the directive.
- **19 September 2020** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **2021-2023** — Infringement proceedings opened against several member states (including Poland and Hungary) for late transposition.
- **February 2022** — The Digital Services Act (DSA) proposal is published, which will later supersede some VSP obligations for very large platforms.

## Impact and consequences

Industry response was immediate and defensive. Google and Facebook lobbied heavily in Brussels, warning that the rules would "chill innovation" and "censor legitimate speech." The Computer & Communications Industry Association (CCIA) called the quota "protectionist." But the directive passed, and its real-world impact has been mixed.

On hate speech: member states implemented divergently. Germany's Network Enforcement Act (NetzDG, 2017) had already forced platforms to remove hate speech within 24 hours, a model that the AVMSD encouraged but did not mandate. By 2022, the European Commission's 2022 report on the AVMSD's application noted that most member states had transposed hate speech obligations, but enforcement varied wildly, with only 12 states having imposed fines by 2023.

On advertising: the rules on product placement and sponsorship for VSPs were largely ineffective, as most platforms already had their own policies. The Commission's 2022 report found that no major fines had been issued for advertising violations under the AVMSD.

On European content quotas: implementation has been the most controversial. By 2023, the European Audiovisual Observatory reported that on-demand catalogues in the EU averaged 25–30% European works, up from 21–27% in 2016, but progress was uneven. Netflix, which had lobbied against the quota, quietly complied, investing €1.2 billion in European production between 2018 and 2021 (Netflix's own 2021 European investment report). Smaller platforms like Amazon Prime and Disney+ lagged.

Countries that struggled most were those with smaller media markets (Malta, Luxembourg, Cyprus) which lacked enough European works to fill the quota without importing from larger neighbours. Poland and Hungary were cited by the Commission for late transposition, with Hungary's government explicitly resisting "Brussels-imposed content quotas" in 2021.

The biggest legacy of the AVMSD 2018 is that it paved the way for the Digital Services Act (DSA). The DSA, adopted in 2022, absorbed and expanded many of the AVMSD's VSP obligations—especially for very large platforms—creating a unified content moderation framework. In practice, the DSA now supersedes most of the AVMSD's platform-specific rules for users above 45 million in the EU.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a new AVMSD in 2018 instead of just updating the 2010 version?

The 2010 AVMSD was written for a world where broadcasters and on-demand services like Netflix dominated. It had no rules for user-generated platforms like YouTube, Facebook, or TikTok, which by 2016 had hundreds of millions of European users. The legal asymmetry between broadcasters (strict rules) and platforms (exempt from liability) was creating unfair competition, per the Commission's 2015 impact assessment. A full revision was needed because the earlier directive's scope could not be stretched to cover platforms through amendments.

### Why was the 30% European content quota so contentious?

It was seen by US tech companies and free-trade advocates as cultural protectionism. The European film and television industry, supported by the French government, argued it was essential to preserve European cultural diversity in the face of US-dominated streaming services. The quota was opposed by the UK (before Brexit), the Netherlands, and Sweden as a violation of free trade and internal market principles. The compromise was to make the quota for "on-demand" services (30%) much lower than the "must-carry" rules for broadcasters (over 50%, already required in most member states).

### Did the directive actually reduce hate speech and harmful content online?

The evidence is mixed. A 2022 European Commission report found that while 26 of 27 member states had transposed the hate speech obligations, only 12 had imposed any fines. The largest fine under the AVMSD was €4.5 million against Facebook (Ireland, 2021) for failing to remove hate speech within the required 24 hours. However, the directive's main mechanism—requiring platforms to have "appropriate measures"—was too vague. This weakness directly informed the DSA's more prescriptive transparency and enforcement obligations.
