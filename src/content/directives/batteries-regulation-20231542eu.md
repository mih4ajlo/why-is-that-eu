## What is it?

The Batteries Regulation (2023/1542/EU) sets mandatory requirements for all batteries placed on the EU market, covering the full lifecycle: sourcing of raw materials, carbon footprint declarations, performance and durability standards, collection and recycling targets, and a digital "battery passport" for every battery over 2 kWh. It replaces the 2006 Batteries Directive and applies to portable, automotive, industrial, electric vehicle (EV), and light means of transport (LMT) batteries — such as those in e-bikes and scooters.

The regulation introduces binding targets: by 2027, portable batteries must contain at least 50% recycled lithium and 90% recycled cobalt; by 2028, 70% of portable batteries must be collected; by 2030, EV batteries must have a minimum recycled content of 12% cobalt, 4% lithium, and 4% nickel. Producers are responsible for collection and recycling, with a phase-in of the battery passport starting 2026 for EV and industrial batteries.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation emerged from a structural tension: the 2006 Batteries Directive focused on end-of-life disposal but had no rules on what went *into* batteries or how they were used. By the late 2010s, Europe faced a surge in battery demand driven by the electric vehicle transition, combined with growing evidence of child labour in cobalt mines in the Democratic Republic of Congo and the EU’s near-total dependence on Chinese battery supply chains. A 2020 Commission study estimated that without intervention, the EU’s lithium demand would rise 18-fold and cobalt demand 5-fold by 2030 — creating both environmental and geopolitical vulnerabilities.

The timing was decisive: the COVID-19 pandemic exposed the fragility of global supply chains, and the European Green Deal — launched in December 2019 under Commission President Ursula von der Leyen — prioritised strategic autonomy in critical raw materials. Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton pushed for a regulatory framework that would turn batteries into a European industrial strength, while Environment Commissioner Virginijus Sinkevičius insisted on strict recycling and ethical sourcing rules. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, Dutch MEP Chantal van den Bossche (PES), helped build a broad coalition of Greens, Socialists, and centre-right EPP that favoured ambitious targets.

Industry reaction was split. European battery manufacturers like Northvolt and the European Battery Alliance welcomed regulations that would create a level playing field and customer trust. But automotive groups warned that recycled content mandates would be hard to meet given low recycling rates — only 47% of portable batteries were collected in 2020, according to Eurostat. The lithium and cobalt mining industry, alongside importers, argued targets were unachievable and would push up costs. China’s battery giant CATL warned it could shift investments elsewhere.

The proposal was also accelerated by the 2017 EU conflict minerals regulation and the 2018 cobalt scandal in the DRC, where Amnesty International documented child labour in artisanal mines supplying major electronics brands. The European Parliament voted 603-26 in favour in June 2023, reflecting near-unanimous political support.

What changed from earlier attempts was the scope: the 2006 directive had no rules on carbon footprint, ethical sourcing, or repairability. The new regulation shifted from a waste-focused approach to a lifecycle framework, inspired by the Ecodesign Directive (2009/125/EC) and the EU’s single-use plastics rules. The battery passport was a novel concept — modelled on similar passports for conflict minerals and later adopted in the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) in 2024.

## Timeline

- **2006** — Original Batteries Directive (2006/66/EC) adopted, focusing on collection and recycling of waste batteries.
- **2017** — EU conflict minerals regulation adopted, creating foundation for supply chain due diligence.
- **December 2019** — European Green Deal announced, including goal of strategic autonomy in batteries.
- **2020** — Commission publishes study on supply chain risks: EU dependent on China for over 80% of lithium-ion battery manufacturing.
- **December 2020** — Commission proposes the Batteries Regulation, replacing the 2006 directive, with lifecycle approach and digital passport.
- **June 2022** — European Parliament adopts its position (603-26), adding stricter recycled content targets and faster phase-in.
- **August 2022** — Council adopts its position, softening some deadlines.
- **December 2022** — Trilogue agreement reached, maintaining most ambitious targets.
- **July 2023** — Regulation published in Official Journal of the EU (2023/1542).
- **August 2023** — Regulation enters into force.
- **February 2024** — Mandatory carbon footprint declarations for EV batteries begin.
- **August 2025** — Recycling efficiency targets: minimum 65% for lithium-based batteries, 50% for others.
- **February 2026** — Battery passport required for EV and industrial batteries over 2 kWh.
- **December 2028** — Collection target for portable batteries reaches 70%.
- **December 2030** — Minimum recycled content mandates for all new batteries: 12% cobalt, 4% lithium, 4% nickel.
- **December 2035** — Collection target for portable batteries rises to 85%.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation immediately affected all battery producers selling in the EU, including European firms (Northvolt, Varta, Saft), Asian manufacturers (CATL, LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, Panasonic), and US companies (Tesla, which manufactures batteries at its Berlin Gigafactory). Smaller producers of LMT batteries for e-bikes and scooters — often SMEs — faced compliance costs for passport requirements.

Industry response was mixed. Northvolt — which opened Europe’s first home-grown lithium-ion gigafactory in Skellefteå, Sweden in 2022 — initially struggled to source enough recycled materials, but in 2023 announced a partnership with Norwegian recycling firm Hydrovolt to supply recycled cobalt and lithium. The European Battery Alliance, chaired by Commission Vice-President Maroš Šefčovič, lobbied for phased deadlines. The German automotive association VDA warned that rapid recycled content requirements could force temporary production cuts.

By 2024, early enforcement actions began: the Commission launched investigations into three Chinese battery makers for potential non-compliance with carbon footprint reporting rules. The first violations of collection targets were reported in five member states — including Italy and Poland — which had portable battery collection rates below 40%, according to the Commission’s 2024 implementation report. The regulation also created a new market for battery passport software providers, with firms like Circularise and Minespider developing systems to track materials from mine to recycling.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU require a digital passport for batteries?

The battery passport — a QR-code accessible digital record — was designed to solve two problems: first, to give recyclers and second-life users detailed information about battery chemistry, capacity, and safety that existing labels did not provide; second, to enforce ethical sourcing and recycled content claims throughout the supply chain. Without a passport, customs and enforcement authorities had no way to verify whether a battery met the regulation’s carbon footprint or recycled content requirements.

### What happens to batteries that do not meet EU recycling targets?

A battery that fails collection or recycling targets cannot be placed on the EU market. The regulation gives producers responsibility for organising and financing collection — so if a member state’s collection rate falls below 70% for portable batteries by 2028, producers in that country face penalties and corrective action plans. The Commission can fine companies up to 4% of annual turnover for non-compliance under standard EU market surveillance provisions.

### Can companies still use cobalt from artisanal mines in the EU?

Yes, but they must prove it was responsibly sourced under OECD due diligence guidelines. The regulation requires all batteries to include a supply chain due diligence policy that identifies and mitigates risks of child labour, forced labour, and conflict financing. If a company cannot prove its cobalt comes from verified sources — or if the Commission finds violations — it can be banned from the EU market. Amnesty International’s 2017 report on artisanal mines in the DRC directly motivated these rules.

### Does the regulation cover batteries in consumer electronics like phones and laptops?

Yes. All portable batteries — defined as batteries weighing under 5 kg and not designed for industrial use — are covered. This includes batteries in smartphones, laptops, power tools, cordless appliances, and wireless headphones. For these batteries, the key rules are: mandatory collection targets (70% by 2028, 85% by 2035), minimum recycled content for cobalt, lead, lithium, and nickel (phased in from 2026), and design requirements that allow easy removal and replacement by consumers (effective from 2027).
