## What is it?

Directive 2016/797/EU, known as the **Interoperability of the Rail System Directive**, is a recast of the 2008 Rail Interoperability Directive (2008/57/EC). It sets out a common framework for ensuring that trains and rail infrastructure can operate seamlessly across EU borders. It does this by mandating **Technical Specifications for Interoperability (TSIs)** — detailed rules covering everything from signalling and track gauge to train control systems and passenger accessibility.

The directive introduces a mandatory **EC declaration of verification** for new or upgraded subsystems, and requires railway undertakings to obtain a single safety certificate valid across the EU under the related Railway Safety Directive (2016/798/EU). Its core aim is to eliminate the patchwork of national technical rules and approval procedures that have long fragmented Europe's rail network, driving up costs and blocking international services.

## Why was it introduced?

The opening shot in the battle for a single European railway was fired not in Brussels but in a Brussels court. In 1998, the European Court of Justice ruled in the *GB-Inno-BM* case that Belgium's national railway operator SNCB illegally used its control over infrastructure access to block a competitor, the private freight operator **GB-Inno-BM** (Case C-18/93, *GB-Inno-BM v SNCB*). The ruling exposed a structural tension: national railway incumbents, owning both tracks and trains, had little incentive to open their networks to rivals. Yet the 1996 Rail Directive (96/48/EC) had only nudged interoperability, lacking binding force.

Despite three earlier interoperability directives (96/48/EC, 2001/16/EC, and 2008/57/EC), by 2013 cross-border rail freight accounted for just 9% of total EU freight traffic — compared to 75% for road, according to the Commission's 2016 *Fourth Railway Package Impact Assessment* (SWD/2016/0276). The Commission's 2011 *White Paper on Transport* had set a target of a 30% rail freight market share by 2030, but technical barriers remained the biggest obstacle: trains crossing the Franco-German border at Strasbourg, for example, still required swapping locomotives or staff due to different signalling and power systems, adding 3-4 hours to a journey that should take 30 minutes.

The political coalition pushing for reform was unusual. On one side stood the **European Rail Freight Association (ERFA)**, representing private operators like **Lineas** (Belgium) and **Rail Cargo Group** (Austria), who were losing market share to trucks due to interoperability delays. On the other were railway equipment manufacturers: **Siemens Mobility**, **Alstom**, and **Bombardier Transportation** lobbied hard through the **Union of European Railway Industries (UNIFE)** for a single certification regime. UNIFE data from 2014 showed that multiple national approvals added 20-30% to rolling stock costs, a figure cited in the Commission's 2013 *Impact Assessment* for the Fourth Railway Package.

Blocking this were two power blocs. First, **Germany's Deutsche Bahn** and **France's SNCF** — the continent's largest railway operators — feared losing control over their domestic technical standards, which they argued ensured safety and quality. Second, the **German Federal Railway Authority (EBA)** and similar national safety bodies resisted ceding authority to the new **European Union Agency for Railways (ERA)**. The 2008 directive had created a voluntary "one-stop-shop" for vehicle authorisation, but by 2014 only 12 cross-border approvals had been granted under it, according to ERA's 2015 Annual Report.

The timing was driven by two events. The first was the **European Court of Auditors' 2010 Special Report No. 8/2010** on rail freight, which concluded bluntly: "The EU's rail liberalisation policy has not yet achieved its objectives. Interoperability remains the main obstacle." The second was the **2012 economic crisis** in southern Europe, which pushed Italy and Spain — desperate for cost efficiencies — to side with the reformers. When the European Parliament voted on the Fourth Railway Package in February 2014, a cross-party coalition of the European People's Party (EPP) and Socialists & Democrats (S&D) passed it by 352 votes to 288, with the Greens/EFA splitting. The final directive was adopted in 2016, with the key change: mandatory ERA authorisation for all new locomotives and trains from 2019, replacing national certification.

As **Siim Kallas**, then European Commissioner for Transport, stated in a 2013 press release: "The current system is designed for national railways. We need a system for European railways."

## Timeline

- **1998** — European Court of Justice rules in *GB-Inno-BM v SNCB* that national rail monopolies must grant non-discriminatory infrastructure access, exposing technical barriers as the new obstacle.
- **2008** — Directive 2008/57/EC adopted, creating voluntary European vehicle authorisation; only 12 cross-border approvals granted in 6 years.
- **2010** — European Court of Auditors publishes Special Report No. 8/2010, calling rail interoperability policy a "failure".
- **2011** — European Commission's *White Paper on Transport* sets target for 30% rail freight share by 2030.
- **January 2013** — Commission proposes the Fourth Railway Package, including recast interoperability and safety directives, as part of Transport Commissioner Siim Kallas's agenda.
- **February 2014** — European Parliament votes to approve the Package: 352 in favour, 288 against, with EPP and S&D forming the majority.
- **May 2016** — Directive 2016/797/EU officially published in the Official Journal, with a transposition deadline of June 2019.
- **June 2019** — Member states must transpose the directive into national law; ERA gains authority to issue single vehicle authorisations valid across the EU.
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic delays full implementation for some member states; the Commission grants temporary derogations for cross-border testing.
- **2022** — ERA reports that by end 2021, it had processed 187 applications for single vehicle authorisations, up from 0 in 2019, according to ERA's 2022 Annual Report.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact was on rolling stock manufacturers. **Alstom** and **Siemens Mobility** reported a 15-20% reduction in certification costs for new trains in 2021, according to UNIFE's 2022 *Rail Industry Competitiveness Study*. The European Commission's 2022 evaluation of the Fourth Railway Package (SWD/2022/0133) found that average authorisation time for a new locomotive dropped from 22 months under national regimes to 6 months under the new ERA process.

However, compliance has been uneven. **Germany** and **France** lagged in transposing the directive into national law, with both countries receiving formal infringement notices from the Commission in 2020. By 2023, the Commission had opened infringement proceedings against 11 member states for incomplete transposition, including **Belgium**, **Austria** and **Poland**.

The biggest winners have been **private freight operators**. ERFA reported in 2022 that its members experienced a 40% increase in cross-border services between 2019 and 2022, though from a low base. The **Port of Rotterdam** — Europe's largest — saw rail freight volumes grow by 12% in 2022, with operators citing simplified authorisation as a key driver.

But the directive's critics — notably **Deutsche Bahn** and **SNCF** — point to a 2021 European Court of Auditors report (*Special Report No. 8/2021*) which found that despite the new rules, "the average speed of cross-border rail freight trains increased by only 3% between 2010 and 2019," far short of the 30% target. Technical interoperability on legacy lines remains a challenge: the **Franco-German border** still requires a 45-minute stop at Strasbourg for locomotive changes in many cases, as of 2023.

## Key questions answered

### Why is the Interoperability of the Rail System Directive needed if national railways already work across borders?

They don't work efficiently. A train crossing from France to Germany must meet two sets of national technical rules, often requiring a locomotive swap that adds hours. The Commission's 2016 impact assessment estimated that technical barriers added 30-50% to the cost of a cross-border rail journey compared to a domestic one, making rail uncompetitive with road freight.

### Why was the 2008 Interoperability of the Rail System Directive replaced so quickly?

The 2008 directive (2008/57/EC) made cross-border authorisation voluntary, and only 12 approvals were granted in 6 years. The 2016 directive makes it mandatory, with ERA taking over national approval powers from 2019, forcing member states to cede authority.

### Why did France and Germany oppose the Interoperability of the Rail System Directive?

Both countries had powerful national railway incumbents (SNCF and Deutsche Bahn) that benefited from controlling their domestic technical standards. They also had well-funded national safety authorities (EPSF in France, EBA in Germany) that faced losing staff and influence. The German Railway Association (DB) warned of "reduced safety" in a 2013 position paper, while SNCF argued that TSIs could not be applied to its older network.

### How does the Interoperability of the Rail System Directive reduce costs for rail operators?

By eliminating duplicative national approvals. A single manufacturer like Siemens now gets one ERA authorisation for a "Vectron" locomotive valid in all 27 member states, instead of 27 separate national certificates. UNIFE estimated in 2022 that this saves the industry €200 million annually in certification costs.
