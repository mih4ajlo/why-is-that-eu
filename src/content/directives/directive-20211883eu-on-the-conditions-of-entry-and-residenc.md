## What is it?

The recast EU Blue Card Directive (2021/1883/EU) is the European Union's revised framework for admitting highly qualified third-country nationals for work. It sets common minimum conditions for a fast-track, EU-wide work and residence permit, replacing the unsuccessful original 2009 Directive (2009/50/EC). Member States must transpose it into national law by 18 November 2023.

Key changes include lowering the minimum salary threshold from 1.5 times the average gross annual salary in the Member State to between 1.0 and 1.6 times, depending on the sector and national situation. It reduces the required job contract duration from twelve months to six months, and allows beneficiaries to move between employers and across Member States after six months, instead of requiring a new application. It also expands eligibility to beneficiaries of international protection and to workers in regulated professions like ICT specialists, and introduces a shorter processing deadline of 60 days.

## Why was it introduced?

The original 2009 Blue Card Directive (2009/50/EC) was a spectacular failure in its own terms. By 2016, the scheme had issued only 37,000 Blue Cards cumulatively across the EU — compared to over 60,000 under Germany's separate national "Blaue Karte EU" scheme alone in 2012. According to the European Commission's 2016 evaluation, Germany accounted for 86% of all Blue Cards issued, while countries like Poland, the Netherlands, and Sweden issued negligible numbers because their more permissive national schemes — often with lower salary thresholds or no cap — rendered the EU Blue Card irrelevant. The structural tension was clear: national systems designed by individual Member States to attract specific skills directly competed with the EU-level scheme intended to harmonise access, and the EU scheme lost.

The political coalition for a recast formed around a simple observation: Europe was ageing fast. Eurostat projections from 2017 showed the EU's working-age population would shrink by 48 million by 2070. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment stressed that without attracting more highly skilled migrants, Europe risked losing competitiveness to Canada, Australia, and the United States, all of which had far more efficient points-based or employer-sponsored systems. The business lobby was explicit: BusinessEurope, DIGITALEUROPE, and the American Chamber of Commerce to the EU all argued that fragmented national rules created administrative costs and deterred global talent.

Opposition came from two directions. A group of Member States led by Austria, the Netherlands, and Denmark — backed by their justice and interior ministries — feared losing control over migration policy. They argued for preserving national salary thresholds and opposed mandatory intra-EU mobility without new applications. In the European Parliament, the Greens and the Left criticised the directive as too restrictive for lower-paid workers and for failing to include family reunification improvements. The 2015 migration crisis, which saw over 1.3 million asylum applications, had politically poisoned any discussion of "economic migration" in many capitals. The original 2009 directive had been drafted in the pre-crisis era; by 2016, the political atmosphere was toxic.

What unblocked the file was timing: the appointment of Ylva Johansson as Commissioner for Home Affairs in December 2019. Johansson, a Swedish Social Democrat, made the Blue Card a personal priority. Her 2020 adoption of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum included a separate pillar on legal migration — deliberately decoupled from the asylum negotiations. She framed the Blue Card not as migration policy but as competitiveness policy, citing the EU's digital skills shortage — then estimated at over 700,000 unfilled ICT jobs by the Commission's 2020 Digital Economy and Society Index. The COVID-19 pandemic paradoxically helped: lockdowns convinced many governments that attracting global talent was an economic necessity, not a political concession.

The Council reached political agreement in June 2020, with a qualified majority of 26 Member States — only Austria opposed, arguing the threshold was too low and would undercut its national Red-White-Red Card. The European Parliament adopted its position in September 2020 with 437 votes in favour, 168 against, and 43 abstentions. The final text was adopted in May 2021 and entered into force on 18 November 2021.

## Timeline

- **October 2007** — European Commission proposes the original Blue Card Directive (2007/0233/COD) under Commissioner Franco Frattini
- **May 2009** — Directive 2009/50/EC adopted by Council and Parliament
- **June 2011** — Deadline for transposition; most Member States miss it; Germany, France, and the Netherlands implement by 2012
- **April 2016** — Commission publishes its evaluation under the Regulatory Fitness and Performance Programme (REFIT), finding the directive "ineffective" with only 37,000 Blue Cards issued
- **June 2016** — Commission launches public consultation on recast; over 400 responses received
- **September 2017** — The Court of Justice of the EU rules in case C-610/15 that Member States cannot impose additional conditions beyond those in the directive, limiting national discretion
- **December 2019** — Commission President Ursula von der Leyen appoints Ylva Johansson as Commissioner for Home Affairs, with a mandate to reform legal migration
- **September 2020** — Commission proposes the recast directive (COM/2020/638) as part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum
- **June 2021** — Council and European Parliament reach final agreement; Austria votes against
- **18 November 2021** — Directive enters into force
- **18 November 2023** — Deadline for Member States to transpose into national law; as of June 2024, 22 Member States had transposed; Commission opens infringement proceedings against Austria, Bulgaria, Romania, and Slovenia for late transposition

## Impact and consequences

The recast's most immediate effect has been the expansion of applicant eligibility. In Germany, the largest user, the Federal Office for Migration and Refugees reported a 45% increase in Blue Card applications in 2023 compared to 2022, reaching over 120,000 applications — though the national "Blaue Karte EU" remains dominant. In France, the number of Blue Cards nearly tripled from 1,200 in 2020 to 3,400 in 2023, according to the French interior ministry.

However, the scheme's uptake remains concentrated in a handful of countries. By the end of 2023, Germany, France, and the Netherlands accounted for over 80% of all EU Blue Cards issued. Smaller Member States like Latvia and Croatia continue to rely almost exclusively on their national permits. The Commission's 2024 EU Talent Attraction Report noted that the six-month intra-EU mobility provision had been used by fewer than 500 workers by late 2023 — largely because national transposition delays prevented it from being operational.

The industry response has been cautiously positive. DIGITALEUROPE and BusinessEurope urged Member States to set salary thresholds at the lower end of the allowed range. The German Association for IT, Telecommunications and New Media (Bitkom) argued the threshold was still too high for start-ups and small firms — a concern echoed by the European Startup Network. The Commission's 2023 impact assessment noted that the minimum threshold in Germany (€43,800 in 2024) had effectively excluded the ICT technician and junior developer roles most in demand.

A significant unintended consequence concerns family reunification. The 2023 guidelines from Member States show that several countries still apply separate waiting periods for family members that the directive does not override — prolonging the gap between the worker's arrival and family unification.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the original 2009 EU Blue Card for skilled foreign workers fail so badly?

The 2009 directive set a salary threshold of 1.5 times the national average gross salary, which in many Member States — especially Eastern European ones — created a de facto cap that excluded most high-value but not top-tier workers. Germany and a few others circumvented the EU scheme by designing national "Blue Card" programmes with lower thresholds (1.0 to 1.5 times the average), making the EU version redundant. The Commission's 2016 evaluation found that 13 Member States issued fewer than 100 Blue Cards total over the directive's lifespan.

### Why did the European Parliament accept looser rules for moving between EU countries than originally proposed?

The Parliament's Civil Liberties Committee had proposed a more ambitious text allowing mobility after only three months, with no employer notification requirement. But in trilogue negotiations in 2021, Member States — led by Germany's interior minister Horst Seehofer — insisted on six months and mandatory notification of the first Member State. The Parliament conceded because the alternative was no directive at all: the Austrian and Dutch interior ministers had explicitly threatened to veto the file if mobility rules were too permissive, citing national security concerns.

### Why was letting skilled foreign workers move between EU countries such a controversial issue?

The original 2009 directive had no intra-EU mobility provision at all — a Blue Card was valid only in the issuing Member State. Germany, France, and the Netherlands argued that without mobility, the Blue Card would never compete with national schemes that allowed workers to move between subsidiaries of multinational companies. But interior ministries in Austria, Denmark, and Poland resisted, warning that allowing workers to move between Member States without prior approval would create "forum shopping" where workers entered via a country with lower thresholds and then moved to a higher-wage country, bypassing that country's controls. The compromise — six months of work in the first Member State before any movement, plus mandatory employer reporting — was a political necessity to get the qualified majority.

### Did the updated EU Blue Card rules actually lead to more of these work permits being issued?

Yes, but unevenly. According to Eurostat, EU Blue Cards issued increased from 36,000 in 2021 to 72,000 in 2022 and 110,000 in 2023. However, the vast majority of this increase came from Germany (which accounted for 85% of 2023 issuances). The Commission's 2024 report noted that the recast had not yet changed the core imbalance: national schemes in Germany, France, and the Netherlands still attract more workers than the EU Blue Card, because they offer faster processing and lower thresholds than even the recast allows.
