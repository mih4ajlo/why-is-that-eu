## What is it?

The EU Taxonomy Regulation creates a common language for defining what counts as "green" or "sustainable" in economic activities. It sets six environmental objectives: climate change mitigation, climate change adaptation, sustainable use of water resources, transition to a circular economy, pollution prevention, and protection of biodiversity. An activity qualifies as "taxonomy-aligned" only if it substantially contributes to at least one objective, does no significant harm to the others, complies with minimum social safeguards, and meets technical screening criteria set by the Commission through delegated acts.

The regulation does not mandate what companies must do, but rather what they must disclose if they claim to be green. Financial market participants offering "sustainable investment" products must disclose the proportion of their portfolio aligned with the taxonomy. Large companies subject to the Non-Financial Reporting Directive must report their taxonomy-eligible and taxonomy-aligned turnover, capital expenditure, and operational expenditure. The regulation entered into force in 2020, with the climate objectives' technical criteria applying from 2022 and the remaining four objectives from 2023.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the European Commission's 2018 Action Plan on Financing Sustainable Growth, presented under Commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis (Financial Stability, Financial Services and Capital Markets Union) and Vice-President Jyrki Katainen. The Action Plan responded to a structural tension: the EU had committed to the Paris Agreement in 2015 and the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, but private capital flows remained overwhelmingly directed at fossil-fuel and high-carbon activities. The Commission's 2018 impact assessment found that the EU faced an annual investment gap of €180 billion for its 2030 climate and energy targets.

The political coalition that drove the Taxonomy Regulation was unusual. It brought together the European Parliament's Green group, the Socialists and Democrats, and parts of the centre-right European People's Party, with key rapporteurs like Sirpa Pietikäinen (EPP, Finland) and Bas Eickhout (Greens/EFA, Netherlands). The European Central Bank, under President Mario Draghi, and the European Investment Bank both provided technical support and public endorsements. Industry opposition was not monolithic: while some energy-intensive sectors warned of compliance costs, the Institutional Investors Group on Climate Change, representing €20 trillion in assets under management, actively lobbied for mandatory disclosure standards, as did the UN Principles for Responsible Investment.

Timing mattered enormously. The regulation was proposed in May 2018, negotiated through 2019, and adopted in June 2020 — in the middle of the COVID-19 pandemic. The pandemic broke a previous deadlock by demonstrating that large-scale state and private intervention could rapidly redirect capital flows. The €750 billion NextGenerationEU recovery package, launched in July 2020, explicitly tied funding to green investments, making a functional taxonomy essential for implementation. The Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, who took office in December 2019, made the European Green Deal her flagship policy, giving the Taxonomy sustained political backing.

What changed relative to earlier failed attempts was the development of a clear, legally enforceable classification system rather than voluntary guidelines. Previous efforts — the 2014 Non-Financial Reporting Directive, the 2017 High-Level Expert Group on Sustainable Finance — had relied on voluntary disclosure and had failed to prevent widespread greenwashing. A 2019 Commission study found that 42% of self-declared "green" investment funds contained companies with significant fossil-fuel exposure. The Taxonomy Regulation closed this loophole by mandating precise technical criteria and making green claims legally verifiable.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Paris Agreement adopted; EU commits to climate neutrality by 2050
- **2016** — G20 leaders request the Financial Stability Board to develop climate-related financial disclosures (TCFD)
- **2017** — Commission establishes the High-Level Expert Group on Sustainable Finance, led by Christian Thimann (former AXA executive)
- **March 2018** — Commission publishes the Action Plan on Financing Sustainable Growth, announcing a taxonomy for sustainable activities
- **May 2018** — Commission publishes the legislative proposal for the Taxonomy Regulation
- **March 2019** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position; Council reaches general approach
- **June 2020** — Regulation adopted by the European Parliament and the Council; published in the Official Journal
- **July 2020** — Commission establishes the Platform on Sustainable Finance to advise on technical screening criteria
- **January 2022** — First delegated act on climate mitigation and adaptation applies
- **February 2022** — Commission publishes Complementary Climate Delegated Act controversially including nuclear and natural gas under conditions
- **January 2023** — Remaining four environmental objectives' technical criteria apply
- **March 2023** — First major enforcement action: Commission opens infringement procedures against 17 Member States for failing to transpose the Taxonomy disclosure requirements on time

## Impact and consequences

Financial market participants managing over €8 trillion in assets under management in the EU were directly affected. The regulation reshaped product labelling: the proportion of Article 8 funds (promoting environmental characteristics) and Article 9 funds (with sustainable investment as an objective) under the related Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation surged from 30% to 65% of EU fund assets between 2021 and 2023, according to Morningstar data cited in a 2024 Commission review. However, the same data showed that 40% of Article 9 funds had less than 50% of their investments taxonomy-aligned, raising questions about the criteria's stringency.

Non-financial companies faced significant reporting burdens. A 2023 survey by the European Reporting Lab found that taxonomy reporting required an average of 3,500 hours of internal work per company for the first year, with costs ranging from €50,000 to €1 million depending on company size and sector. The Commission's 2024 review acknowledged that small and medium-sized enterprises indirectly subject to the regulation through supply-chain reporting obligations had struggled with compliance.

The controversial inclusion of nuclear and natural gas in the Complementary Delegated Act of February 2022 created political battles. The European Parliament's resolution to object to the delegated act failed by only 76 votes (328 for, 278 against, with 33 abstentions), reflecting deep divisions. Nine Member States, including Austria and Luxembourg, announced legal challenges, while France, Poland, and several central European countries had lobbied heavily for inclusion. The European Commission was sued by ClientEarth, Greenpeace, and WWF over the decision, with proceedings ongoing as of 2024.

Market uptake of the Taxonomy proved slower than anticipated. The Commission's 2024 review found that only 10% of EU-listed companies reported any taxonomy-alignment data, and only 5% aligned full turnover. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) reported that 60% of green bond issuers referenced the Taxonomy but few provided the detailed data required. The review identified data availability, complexity of technical criteria, and lack of assurance standards as key barriers.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU need a taxonomy instead of relying on private green labels?

Private green labels proliferated in the 2010s without a common standard, creating confusion and enabling greenwashing. A 2020 study by the European Securities and Markets Authority found that there were 68 different green bond frameworks globally, with no consistent criteria for what counted as "green." The EU's 2019 Behavioural Insights study showed that 89% of EU consumers considered it difficult to identify genuinely sustainable investments. The Taxonomy creates a single, legally enforceable standard that regulators can audit and that investors can rely on.

### Why was nuclear energy included in the Taxonomy?

Nuclear was included through the Complementary Climate Delegated Act (February 2022) after intensive lobbying by France, which generates 70% of its electricity from nuclear power, and by a coalition of central European countries with existing nuclear fleets. The Commission's own Joint Research Centre had concluded in 2021 that nuclear "does not cause more harm to human health or the environment than other electricity generation technologies" when lifecycle impacts are considered. Inclusion was opposed by Austria, Luxembourg, Germany, and environmental NGOs, who argued that nuclear creates long-term radioactive waste storage risks. The legal basis for inclusion is Article 10(2) of the Taxonomy Regulation, which allows the Commission to include activities that support the transition to climate neutrality, provided they do not lock in fossil-fuel assets.

### What are the consequences for a company that claims to be taxonomy-aligned but is not?

The Taxonomy Regulation itself does not impose fines for incorrect self-declaration, but it creates liability through the broader EU regulatory framework. Article 23 of the Taxonomy Regulation requires Member States to establish effective penalties for misleading taxonomy-related claims. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) has issued enforcement priorities each year since 2022, and the 2023 coordinated enforcement action by national competent authorities resulted in 47 investigations into potential greenwashing by fund managers. Additionally, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (2022/2464/EU), which amends the Taxonomy's disclosure framework, requires limited assurance from statutory auditors on taxonomy-related data from 2024 and reasonable assurance from 2026. Fines under national transpositions vary: the German financial regulator BaFin can impose penalties of up to €5 million or 5% of annual turnover for misleading sustainability claims.

### How does the Taxonomy relate to the EU's broader sustainability reporting requirements?

The Taxonomy Regulation sits within a pyramid of linked legislation. At its base is the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (2019/2088/EU), which requires financial market participants to disclose how they integrate sustainability risks. The Taxonomy provides the definitional framework for those disclosures. Above it sits the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, 2022/2464/EU), which requires large companies to report according to European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) — and the ESRS explicitly reference Taxonomy-alignment data. At the apex is the European Green Deal, whose 2050 climate-neutrality target sets the overarching policy goal. The European Commission's 2024 review proposed a "simplification package" to harmonise reporting deadlines across these instruments, acknowledging that overlapping requirements had created compliance burdens.
