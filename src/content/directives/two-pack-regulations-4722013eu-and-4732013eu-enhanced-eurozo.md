## What is it?

The Two-Pack is a set of two EU regulations — 472/2013/EU and 473/2013/EU — adopted in May 2013 to strengthen economic and fiscal surveillance within the Eurozone. Regulation 473/2013 applies to all Eurozone members and requires them to submit draft budgets to the European Commission for scrutiny each autumn, with the Commission empowered to request revisions if the budget breaches EU fiscal rules. Regulation 472/2013 applies to Eurozone states receiving financial assistance (e.g., from the European Stability Mechanism, or ESM) or facing serious financial difficulties, imposing enhanced monitoring and regular reporting obligations.

Together, the Two-Pack created a tighter fiscal oversight regime for the 17 (now 20) Eurozone countries, supplementing earlier rules like the Stability and Growth Pact and the Six-Pack reforms of 2011. It was designed to prevent the kind of sovereign debt crises that rocked Greece, Ireland, Portugal, and Spain from 2009 onwards.

## Why was it introduced?

The Two-Pack was a direct response to the Eurozone debt crisis that erupted in late 2009 when the newly elected Greek government, led by Prime Minister George Papandreou, revised the 2009 budget deficit estimate from 3.7% to 12.7% of GDP — later revised further to 15.1%. This revelation exposed structural flaws in the EU’s economic governance system. The original Stability and Growth Pact (1997) had empowered the Commission to monitor deficits and debt, but it lacked enforcement teeth: the 2003 suspension of the Excessive Deficit Procedure against France and Germany after both exceeded the 3%-of-GDP deficit limit demonstrated political capture of enforcement.

The crisis deepened in 2010. On 2 May 2010, Eurozone finance ministers and the International Monetary Fund agreed a €110 billion bailout for Greece, the first in a series of rescue packages. But the Commission, the European Central Bank, and national treasuries discovered they had no legal framework to force fiscal discipline on countries receiving aid. Surveillance of programme countries was ad hoc, negotiated case-by-case through “Memoranda of Understanding” that lacked democratic accountability or judicial oversight. Meanwhile, non-programme Eurozone countries like Italy and Spain showed deficit risks that could trigger contagion.

The political impetus for reform came from two directions. First, creditor states — led by Germany under Chancellor Angela Merkel and Finance Minister Wolfgang Schäuble — demanded stricter oversight as a condition for further bailout funds. The German Bundestag, which held veto power over ESM contributions, insisted on binding budget rules to protect German taxpayers. Second, the European Commission under President José Manuel Barroso, particularly Economic and Monetary Affairs Commissioner Olli Rehn, argued that the single currency required a central budget authority with real powers. Rehn’s June 2010 proposal for a “European Semester” — a annual cycle of economic policy coordination — had already passed in September 2010 (Regulation 1175/2011).

The Two-Pack faced strong opposition. The European Parliament, where the centre-left S&D group and Greens worried about austerity imposed unilaterally by Brussels, fought for parliamentary oversight of programme countries. The Parliament demanded that the Commission report quarterly to the European Parliament on programme implementation. The Council, representing member states, resisted ceding budget sovereignty. The compromise, reached in February 2013 after months of negotiation, gave the Commission powers to reject national budgets but left final budget authority with national parliaments. The Parliament secured the quarterly reporting requirement.

The timing was critical. By late 2012, the crisis had spread: Spain received a €100 billion bank bailout in June 2012; Cyprus sought assistance in July 2012; Greece received a second €130 billion bailout in March 2012. The European Stability Mechanism (ESM) became operational in October 2012, replacing the temporary European Financial Stability Facility. The Two-Pack passed in May 2013, just as Ireland prepared to exit its €85 billion bailout programme in December 2013, and Greece faced continued programme reviews.

The regulations entered into force on 30 May 2013, with budget monitoring provisions applying from the 2014 budget cycle. The Commission’s first draft budget assessment under the Two-Pack came in November 2013, when it reviewed the 2014 budgets of 16 Eurozone countries and requested revisions from seven — including France, Italy, and Spain — for insufficiently ambitious deficit reduction.

## Impact and consequences

The Two-Pack fundamentally altered the fiscal governance of the Eurozone. For the first time, the European Commission gained the power to review and — in effect — reject national budget plans before they passed through national parliaments. Between 2013 and 2022, the Commission assessed over 200 draft budgets and requested revisions from roughly 40% of them, according to Commission annual reports. For example, in 2017, Italy was required to revise its 2018 budget to reduce the structural deficit by 0.3 percentage points; in 2019, the Commission rejected Italy’s draft budget entirely for breaching the 3%-of-GDP deficit rule, triggering months of standoff between Rome and Brussels.

For programme countries, Regulation 472/2013 imposed quarterly reporting to the Commission, the ECB, and the ESM. Greece, under its three bailout programmes (2010-2018), submitted over 100 quarterly reports on fiscal, structural, and financial sector reforms. The regulation also required programme countries to establish independent fiscal councils and conduct comprehensive audits of public finances. The Commission noted in its 2018 evaluation that these requirements improved transparency but imposed heavy administrative burdens on already overstretched state apparatuses.

Industry and market reaction was mixed. Bond markets welcomed the enhanced credibility: by 2014, yield spreads between Italian and German 10-year bonds fell from 550 basis points (2012 peak) to around 150 basis points, according to ECB data. However, national governments — particularly in France, Italy, and Spain — chafed at perceived loss of sovereignty. French President François Hollande, a Socialist, argued in 2014 that the Commission’s budget assessments were “technocratic interference” with democratic decisions. The European Trade Union Confederation warned that the Two-Pack locked in austerity, reducing public investment.

The most consequential application came in 2015-2018 with Greece. Regulation 472/2013 required Greece to seek prior Commission approval for any significant economic policy change during its programme. This included privatisations, pension reforms, and labour market deregulation. Critics — including the International Labour Organization — argued that the enhanced oversight violated collective bargaining rights and democratic accountability. The Commission, in its 2018 post-programme evaluation, acknowledged that “the intensity of the reform conditionality was unprecedented” but argued it was necessary to restore market confidence and fiscal sustainability.

A key unintended consequence: the Two-Pack’s eight-month budget cycle (November approval, full-year execution) proved rigid during crises. In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic forced the Commission to activate the “general escape clause” of the Stability and Growth Pact, suspending Two-Pack budget assessments for two years. The clause was reactivated in 2023, and the Commission proposed a reform of the fiscal rules in April 2023, partly to address Two-Pack rigidities.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU force countries to submit their budgets to Brussels before parliaments could vote on them?

The 2009 Greek debt crisis revealed that Eurozone countries could hide massive deficits and run unsustainable fiscal policies without detection until a market panic or bailout was forced. The Two-Pack’s prior review requirement was designed to allow the Commission to catch incipient imbalances — like Italy’s rising debt-to-GDP ratio approaching 150% — before they triggered a crisis. Creditor states, particularly Germany, insisted on this as a condition for further ESM bailout funds.

### How was a country like Greece treated differently from, say, Germany under the Two-Pack rules?

Regulation 472/2013 applied only to countries receiving financial assistance or deemed at serious risk of financial difficulties. Greece, under its three bailout programmes (2010-2018), was subject to quarterly Commission-ECB-IMF reviews of every major policy change — tax rates, pension ages, public sector wages, privatisation decisions. These reports were sent to the Eurogroup and the European Parliament. Germany, by contrast, was subject only to Regulation 473/2013’s annual budget assessment, which reviewed but could not block its budget.

### Did the Two-Pack actually prevent another Eurozone debt crisis?

The Two-Pack contributed to reducing bond yield spreads and improving fiscal discipline in non-programme countries between 2014 and 2019. However, it did not prevent the 2020 pandemic-induced economic collapse, nor did it stop Italy’s debt-to-GDP ratio from rising from 135% (2013) to 144% (2023). A 2021 European Fiscal Board report found that the Two-Pack’s monitoring “did not significantly alter the trajectory of structural deficits” in most countries. The ultimate test — a sudden market crisis — did not occur during its first decade of operation, partly due to the ECB’s 2012 “whatever it takes” commitment.
