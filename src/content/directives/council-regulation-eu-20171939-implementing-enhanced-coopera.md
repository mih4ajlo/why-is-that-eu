## What is it?

The European Public Prosecutor’s Office (EPPO) is an independent EU body created to investigate, prosecute, and bring to judgment crimes affecting the EU’s financial interests. It operates as a single office across participating member states, with a European Chief Prosecutor in Luxembourg and European Delegated Prosecutors embedded in national systems. Rather than replacing national prosecutors, the EPPO takes over cases that involve damage to the EU budget — including fraud in EU funds, customs duties evasion, and cross-border VAT carousel fraud exceeding €10 million.

Before the EPPO, these crimes were handled exclusively by national authorities, which often lacked the resources, incentives, or jurisdiction to pursue transnational cases effectively. The EPPO can issue European arrest warrants, request asset freezes, and bring cases directly to national courts. It started operations on 1 June 2021 with 22 participating member states; five states (Poland, Hungary, Sweden, Denmark, and Ireland) remain outside, each for different political or legal reasons.

## Why was it introduced?

The EPPO was born from a spectacular failure: member states were barely recovering any money stolen from the EU budget. The European Commission’s own 2013 impact assessment estimated that fraud cost the EU budget at least €500 million per year — and national prosecutors only recovered a tiny fraction. A 2012 European Court of Auditors report found that customs fraud alone cost the EU €2.7 billion annually in lost revenue, with member states recovering almost nothing.

The structural tension was simple: the EU budget was spent cross-border — a Polish contractor building a road with regional funds, a German company evading customs duties on Chinese imports — but prosecution remained purely national. National prosecutors had little incentive to prioritise complex, expensive cross-border fraud cases against the EU budget, especially when the victim was a faraway institution in Brussels rather than local taxpayers.

The political trigger came through a series of scandals. In 2013, the OLAF Supervisory Committee reported that OLAF — the EU’s anti-fraud office — had no judicial powers: it could investigate and recommend, but it could not prosecute. Cases involving organised crime groups exploiting VAT carousel schemes across multiple member states routinely died because no single country could pursue them. The 2014 European Parliament elections brought a wave of MEPs, particularly from the liberal and centre-right groups, who made the EPPO a priority.

The coalition that pushed it through was unusual. On one side were EU institutions — Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker (2014-2019), who made the EPPO a signature project of his "political Commission", and First Vice-President Frans Timmermans, who championed it as a rule-of-law issue. On the other side were large member states like France, Germany, Italy, and Spain, along with smaller states like Belgium and Luxembourg, who wanted stronger EU enforcement. Their argument: the EU budget was €155 billion annually by 2020, and member states were failing to protect it.

Opposition was fierce and nearly succeeded. The UK, Sweden, Denmark, Hungary, Ireland, and Poland blocked unanimity. The UK’s House of Commons European Scrutiny Committee called the EPPO a "massive transfer of criminal justice sovereignty" — a line that resonated in several capitals. The Netherlands and Finland were deeply sceptical. For two years, the proposal was stuck.

The breakthrough came in 2017 under the "enhanced cooperation" procedure — used only a few times in EU history — which allowed a group of at least nine member states to proceed without the holdouts. The Commission’s legal service confirmed it was possible. In March 2017, 16 member states wrote to the Council requesting enhanced cooperation. The European Parliament approved with 456 votes in favour, 163 against, and 60 abstentions on 5 October 2017. The Council adopted the regulation on 12 October 2017.

The timing mattered hugely. The 2016 Panama Papers leaks had exposed massive cross-border tax evasion, and the 2017 Paradise Papers followed. Public anger was high. The Commission’s 2017 anti-fraud report showed that member states had recovered only €1.7 billion of €9.5 billion in recommended recoveries from 2012-2016. That 18% recovery rate was politically unsustainable.

What changed was the procedural path. Enhanced cooperation let the willing states proceed. The holdouts — particularly Poland and Hungary, which had both been accused of rule-of-law backsliding and were sensitive to any EU body with prosecutorial power — were left behind. The EPPO became the first EU body with direct criminal prosecution powers, a constitutional milestone that would have been impossible without the enhanced cooperation workaround.

## Timeline

- **1999** — The Tampere European Council first proposes creating a European Public Prosecutor to combat fraud against the EU budget
- **2001** — The Commission issues a Green Paper on criminal-law protection of the financial interests of the EU, proposing a European prosecutor
- **2003** — The draft Constitutional Treaty includes provisions for a European Public Prosecutor, but the treaty collapses after French and Dutch referendums in 2005
- **2009** — The Lisbon Treaty enters into force; Article 86 explicitly provides for establishing a European Public Prosecutor's Office "from Eurojust" by unanimous Council vote
- **2013** — The Commission publishes a legislative proposal for the EPPO; the European Parliament supports it; the UK and several other member states block it
- **2014** — Jean-Claude Juncker takes office as Commission President and makes the EPPO a priority; the Parliament names Eva Joly (Greens/EFA) as rapporteur
- **2016** — The Commission presents a revised proposal; the Netherlands and Finland remain opposed; the UK announces it will not participate after Brexit
- **March 2017** — 16 member states request enhanced cooperation to establish the EPPO
- **5 October 2017** — The European Parliament approves the regulation (456 votes in favour, 163 against, 60 abstentions)
- **12 October 2017** — The Council adopts the regulation
- **20 November 2017** — The regulation enters into force
- **31 October 2019** — Laura Codruța Kövesi (Romania) is appointed as the first European Chief Prosecutor
- **1 June 2021** — The EPPO begins operations with 22 participating member states
- **2022** — The EPPO opens over 1,000 investigations; by 2023, it has frozen over €540 million in criminal assets
- **2023** — The EPPO reports investigating over 1,900 cases with alleged damages of over €14 billion

## Impact and consequences

The EPPO’s impact has been immediate and measurable. By the end of its first operational year (June 2022), it had opened 929 investigations involving damages of €8.3 billion. By December 2024, the EPPO reported it had over 1,900 active cases and had frozen assets worth over €540 million. The Commission’s 2023 anti-fraud report noted that EPPO investigations had already led to court decisions in several member states, with actual convictions and asset confiscations.

The national prosecutors most affected have been in smaller member states. In the Baltic states, Slovenia, and Croatia, the EPPO has taken over cases that would have overwhelmed national capacity — typically cross-border VAT fraud involving multiple EU countries. Large member states like Germany and France have seen more friction, with tensions over case-allocation between national prosecutors and European Delegated Prosecutors.

Industry has responded with caution. Law firms specialised in white-collar crime report a "new enforcement architecture" where companies can face simultaneous actions from the EPPO, national prosecutors, and the Commission. Compliance costs have risen for companies handling EU funds — construction firms, agricultural cooperatives, research institutions — who now face a body with direct arrest and asset-freeze powers.

The legacy is the EPPO itself: the first criminal prosecution body in the EU with operational independence from member states. Its creation through enhanced cooperation, rather than unanimous agreement, has created a two-tier Europe in criminal justice — a precedent that may be used again for other sensitive areas like company law or taxation.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU have a prosecutor who can arrest people across borders?

Yes. The European Public Prosecutor's Office (EPPO) can issue European arrest warrants and request asset freezes across all 22 participating member states. It can order seizures of property, freeze bank accounts, and demand documents — but it cannot conduct arrests directly; national police execute its warrants. It started work in June 2021 and by 2023 was handling over 1,900 cases.

### Why can’t national police handle EU fraud on their own?

They often cannot, because the fraud is cross-border. A VAT carousel scheme might involve companies in five different EU countries, with the stolen money moving through banks in a sixth. National prosecutors lack jurisdiction outside their own country and often lack the resources to coordinate complex multi-country investigations. The European Commission’s own statistics showed that before the EPPO, member states recovered only about 18% of the money stolen from the EU budget between 2012 and 2016 — the rest was lost.

### Is every EU country part of the European cross-border fraud prosecutor's office?

No. Only 22 of the 27 EU member states participate. Poland, Hungary, Sweden, Denmark, and Ireland opted out, though for very different reasons: Poland and Hungary opposed it on sovereignty grounds as their rule-of-law disputes with the Commission worsened; Sweden and Denmark have constitutional concerns about transferring criminal jurisdiction to an EU body; Ireland, like Denmark, has an opt-out from justice and home affairs cooperation. Participating and non-participating states still cooperate through Eurojust.
