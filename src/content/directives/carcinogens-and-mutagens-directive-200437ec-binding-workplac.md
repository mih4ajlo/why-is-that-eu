## What is it?

The Carcinogens and Mutagens Directive (CMD) establishes a harmonised framework for protecting workers from exposure to substances that cause cancer or genetic mutations. It requires employers to assess and eliminate or reduce exposure, and it sets binding occupational exposure limit values (OELs) for specific substances, including asbestos and benzene. These limits are maximum allowable concentrations in workplace air over a defined period (typically an 8-hour shift or a short-term exposure).

The directive applies across all EU member states, covering more than 200 million workers. It was adopted in 2004 as a recast of earlier, weaker directives from the 1990s, and has been amended multiple times to add new substances and lower limits as scientific evidence emerged.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive’s origin lies in a grim reality: by the 1990s, over 100,000 workers in the EU were dying each year from occupational cancers, according to the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC). The European Commission’s 2001 impact assessment estimated that workplace cancer accounted for 32% of all registered occupational diseases in the EU, with asbestos alone linked to 20,000 deaths annually across the bloc. Yet national exposure limits varied wildly: Germany’s limit for benzene was 1 ppm, while Italy’s was 10 ppm — a tenfold difference that created a race to the bottom.

The immediate trigger for the 2004 recast was a series of failures in the earlier 1990/394/EEC directive. That directive allowed member states to set their own OELs, leading to fragmentation that frustrated both trade unions and the European Parliament. The Parliament, led by its Committee on Employment and Social Affairs under rapporteurs like Stephen Hughes (UK, PES), had repeatedly called for binding EU-wide limits after national scandals — including Italy’s asbestos industry, where courts in the 1990s convicted executives of Enel and Eternit for failing to protect workers, and the UK’s failure to enforce limits on benzene in chemical plants.

The pivotal moment came in 2002, when the European Court of Justice ruled in *Commission v. France* (Case C-155/01) that France had failed to transpose the 1990 directive correctly for benzene exposure. The court found that France’s national limits were not based on the best available scientific evidence and that the directive’s vague wording allowed systematic under-enforcement. This was not an isolated case — the Commission had by then launched infringement proceedings against 12 of the then-15 member states for inadequate implementation of the 1990 directive.

In response, Commissioner for Employment and Social Affairs Anna Diamantopoulou (2001–2004) pushed for a recast that would centralise limit-setting in the EU. The Commission’s 2003 proposal drew on input from the Scientific Committee on Occupational Exposure Limits (SCOEL), whose 2002 opinion classified benzene as a proven human carcinogen with no safe threshold. Business lobbyists — including CEFIC (chemical industry), the International Confederation of European Asbestos Producers (ICEA), and employer group BusinessEurope — argued that binding EU limits would cost jobs and competitiveness. CEFIC’s 2003 position paper claimed that a 1 ppm limit for benzene would cost the chemical sector €500 million annually. Yet the trade unions responded with data showing that occupational cancers cost the EU economy an estimated €4.5 billion per year in lost productivity, healthcare, and compensation.

The European Parliament’s first reading in March 2004, led by Spanish MEP Manuel Medina Ortega (PES), voted 456 in favour to 89 against to set the benzene OEL at 1 ppm (8-hour TWA) and asbestos at 0.1 fibres/cm³. The Council adopted it with a qualified majority in April 2004, with Greece, Poland, and Portugal initially opposing on cost grounds but later joining after transitional periods were added.

## Timeline

- **1990** — Original Carcinogens Directive (90/394/EEC) adopted, but leaves OELs to member states, leading to fragmentation.
- **2001** — Commission report shows 12 member states failing to implement 90/394/EEC properly; European Parliament calls for binding limits.
- **2002** — ECJ ruling in *Commission v. France* (C-155/01) finds France’s benzene limits inadequate.
- **2003** — SCOEL confirms benzene as proven carcinogen; Commission proposal published under Commissioner Diamantopoulou.
- **March 2004** — European Parliament adopts text with binding limits of 1 ppm benzene and 0.1 fibres/cm³ asbestos.
- **April 2004** — Council adopts directive with qualified majority; Greece, Poland, Portugal get transitional periods.
- **June 2004** — Directive transposed into national law deadline set for 2006.
- **2009** — First amendment (2009/90/EC) adds cadmium and chromium limits.
- **2017** — Amendment (2017/2398 EU) lowers benzene limit to 0.5 ppm and adds new limits for respirable crystalline silica.
- **2022** — Amendment (2022/431 EU) further lowers asbestos limit to 0.01 fibres/cm³ for the most common type.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s most immediate effect was harmonisation: by 2010, all member states had adopted binding OELs for benzene at 1 ppm at minimum, closing the earlier 10-fold gap. The European Agency for Safety and Health at Work (EU-OSHA) reported in 2017 that the directive reduced average benzene exposure levels in the EU chemical sector by 40% compared to 2004 levels.

For asbestos, the impact was similarly strong. The 2004 limit of 0.1 fibres/cm³ forced the closure of multiple asbestos-processing plants in southern Europe, particularly in Greece, where the Asvestochori factory near Thessaloniki shut down in 2005 after failing to meet the limit. In Italy, the directive accelerated the phase-out of asbestos production, which had already been banned under national law in 1992 but where enforcement had been lax. However, compliance challenges persisted: a 2020 European Commission report found that 8 member states still had deficient transposition for key provisions, including France, Poland, and Romania.

The economic cost to industry was significant. CEFIC estimated in 2008 that compliance with the benzene limit cost the EU chemical industry €200–300 million in the first five years, far lower than the €500 million it had predicted. Meanwhile, the 2017 amendment adding respirable crystalline silica — which affects millions of construction workers — triggered major investment in dust-control equipment by companies like HeidelbergCement, Lafarge Holcim, and Saint-Gobain.

The directive remains a living instrument. The 2022 amendment lowered asbestos limits to 0.01 fibres/cm³, a level that the European Parliament’s Environment Committee called "the only scientifically defensible threshold" but which has faced pushback from construction and waste-treatment industries in Poland, Bulgaria, and Romania, where large amounts of asbestos-containing materials remain in buildings.

## Key questions answered

### Why does EU law regulate how much cancer-causing dust workers can breathe at their jobs?

Before the 2004 directive, each EU country set its own limits — Germany had strict limits, while Italy and Greece had much looser ones. This meant workers in one country were dying of cancers that could have been prevented if the same company moved operations to a laxer member state. The EU stepped in to create a level playing field, with the European Court of Justice ruling in 2002 that national limits must be based on science, not politics.

### Did European industry successfully block stronger exposure limits for benzene?

Initially, yes. CEFIC, the chemical industry lobby, argued that a 1 ppm limit would cost €500 million. But trade unions countered with data showing occupational cancers cost €4.5 billion per year. After the 2002 ECJ ruling against France, the Parliament passed the limit with 456 votes in favour, and industry ultimately spent far less than predicted — around €200–300 million.

### Why did the EU take 17 years to lower asbestos limits after the 2004 directive?

The 2004 limit of 0.1 fibres/cm³ was based on 1990s science. Medical evidence advanced: the World Health Organization declared in 2006 that no safe threshold exists for asbestos. However, construction and waste-treatment lobbies warned of massive cleanup costs, especially in eastern Europe where asbestos remains in millions of buildings. Only after the European Parliament’s Environment Committee pushed hard, and the European Commission’s 2020 Health and Safety Strategy set a goal of eliminating occupational cancers, did the limit drop to 0.01 fibres/cm³ in 2022.

### Can EU workers sue their employers for getting cancer from benzene exposure at work?

Yes, under the directive, employers are required to eliminate or reduce exposure to the binding limits. If an employer fails to meet the limit and a worker develops cancer, the worker can pursue compensation under national civil law. Key cases include a 2011 Italian court ruling against Enel for benzene-related leukemia at a chemical plant, where the court cited the directive as establishing a clear duty of care that Enel had violated.
