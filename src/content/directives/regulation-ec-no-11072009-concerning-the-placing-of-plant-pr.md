## What is it?

Regulation (EC) No 1107/2009 sets the EU‑wide rules for the approval of active substances (the chemical or biological ingredients of pesticides) and the authorisation of plant protection products (the final formulations sold to farmers). It replaced the earlier Directive 91/414/EEC, which had led to inconsistent national decisions and a backlog of unapproved substances.

Under the regulation, the European Commission, supported by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), decides whether to approve an active substance at EU level. Member states then authorise the sale and use of products containing that substance within their territory, following common criteria. The framework includes “cut-off criteria” that automatically exclude carcinogens, mutagens, reproductive toxicants, endocrine disruptors, and persistent organic pollutants, unless exposure is negligible.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation’s adoption in 2009 was driven by a structural failure: the old system (Directive 91/414/EEC) had produced a huge backlog. By the mid‑2000s, over 800 active substances were still pending review, according to the Commission’s 2006 impact assessment. National authorisation procedures varied wildly, creating trade barriers and legal uncertainty. The 2004 enlargement into Central and Eastern Europe worsened the fragmentation, as new member states brought in different lists of approved products.

Coalition dynamics were unusually tense. The dominant industry group, the European Crop Protection Association (now CropLife Europe), pushed for a streamlined, science‑based system that would speed approvals and maintain a large toolbox of substances. Environmental and consumer organisations, led by Pesticide Action Network (PAN) Europe and supported by the European Parliament’s Environment Committee, demanded stricter “hazard‑based” cut‑off criteria — meaning that certain dangerous properties alone should disqualify a substance, regardless of exposure levels. The European Commission under President José Manuel Barroso and Commissioner for Health Markos Kyprianou proposed a compromise in July 2006 that included cut‑off criteria but allowed exemptions for “essential uses.”

Timing was crucial. The 2006 proposal came just after the publication of the REACH chemicals regulation (1907/2006/EC), which had established a new paradigm for managing chemical risks. The same political appetite for stringent chemical control spilled over into pesticides. In April 2007, the Parliament adopted a tough position with strong cut‑off criteria, alarming industry. The Council — led by agriculture ministers — watered them down, but a final compromise in 2008 kept the cut‑off criteria intact while introducing a “negligible exposure” derogation for certain substances. The regulation was adopted on 21 October 2009 and entered into force on 14 June 2011.

What changed in later years was the glyphosate crisis. The regulation’s system had never been designed for a substance as ubiquitous and politically charged as glyphosate. When IARC (the World Health Organization’s cancer agency) classified glyphosate as “probably carcinogenic” in March 2015, it directly contradicted EFSA’s January 2015 conclusion that glyphosate was unlikely to pose a carcinogenic risk. Under normal procedure, the Commission proposes a renewal based on EFSA’s assessment. But the IARC finding turned the routine re‑approval into a political firestorm. The Commission could not obtain a qualified majority in the Standing Committee on Plants, Animals, Food and Feed (PAFF) for either renewal or rejection. In 2016, it granted only an 18‑month extension, then in 2017 a five‑year renewal after the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) confirmed glyphosate’s non‑carcinogenicity. The “Glyphosate Files” investigative journalism (2017) revealed that the Commission had copied large sections of text from a Monsanto submission into its own assessment report — directly undermining the regulation’s claim to independent science. This scandal broke the deadlock on the long‑stalled proposal for a Transparency Regulation (2019/1381/EU), which now requires the Commission to publish all studies submitted by applicants.

## Timeline

- **1991** — Directive 91/414/EEC adopted; establishes a 10‑year programme to review existing active substances.
- **2000‑2005** — Reviews fall far behind schedule; over 700 substances still not evaluated by 2005.
- **July 2006** — European Commission proposes new regulation (COM(2006) 388), including cut‑off criteria.
- **October 2007** — European Parliament adopts first‑reading position with strong cut‑off criteria and no exemption for endocrine disruptors.
- **2008** — Council and Parliament reach compromise: cut‑off criteria retained, but “negligible exposure” exemption allowed for some substances.
- **January 2009** — Parliament approves final text on 13 January; Council adopts on 23 February.
- **21 October 2009** — Regulation (EC) No 1107/2009 published in Official Journal.
- **14 June 2011** — Regulation becomes fully applicable, replacing 91/414/EEC.
- **2012** — Glyphosate’s approval expires; the Commission renews it for ten years (until 2022) under the old rules.
- **March 2015** — IARC classifies glyphosate as “probably carcinogenic.”
- **November 2015** — EFSA concludes glyphosate is unlikely to be carcinogenic.
- **2016** — Commission fails to get a qualified majority for renewal; grants an 18‑month extension.
- **2017** — “Glyphosate Files” published; Commission’s rapporteur (Germany) was found to have copied from Monsanto’s submission. ECHA concludes glyphosate is not carcinogenic. Commission renews approval for five years (until 2022).
- **2019** — Transparency Regulation (EU 2019/1381) adopted; requires publication of all pesticide‑related studies.
- **2023** — Commission renews glyphosate for another ten years after EFSA finds no critical areas of concern; only nine member states vote against.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation sharply reduced the number of approved active substances in the EU. According to the Commission’s 2018 REFIT evaluation, the number fell from over 800 in the 1990s to around 450 by 2016. Many older, more controversial substances were removed, but the cut‑off criteria also blocked newer ones, particularly endocrine disruptors. Industry warned of a declining pesticide toolbox, but environmental groups argued the remaining products were safer.

The glyphosate episode severely damaged trust in the authorisation system. The European Ombudsman opened an inquiry into the Commission’s handling of the re‑approval, and the European Court of Justice annulled part of the renewal decision in 2019 due to procedural errors. However, the court did not question the scientific conclusions. The Transparency Regulation now forces applicants to submit full study data, which must be made public, addressing one of the core criticisms raised during the glyphosate crisis.

Member states remain deeply divided. France, Austria, and Germany (partially) have moved to restrict or ban glyphosate nationally, but under the regulation they cannot unilaterally ban an EU‑approved active substance without notifying the Commission. This has created tensions between national and EU authority. The regulation’s “zonal” authorisation system (dividing the EU into three zones) has improved product access but still suffers from inconsistent national conditions of use.

## Key questions answered

### Is glyphosate banned in the EU?

No. Glyphosate is approved until 15 December 2033 under Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2023/2660. Member states can restrict or phase out its use nationally under the regulation’s safeguard clause, and several (Austria, France, Germany) have done so, but they cannot ban it outright without a change in the EU approval. Consumer groups and some MEPs continue to demand a full ban, citing IARC’s classification.

### Why did EU regulators re‑approve glyphosate despite IARC’s cancer finding?

EFSA and ECHA both concluded, after reviewing all available studies, that glyphosate is unlikely to be carcinogenic to humans. IARC’s classification is a hazard assessment (identifying potential risk under any exposure), while EU law requires a risk assessment (probable harm under realistic conditions). The Commission and member states followed EFSA’s and ECHA’s assessments, as the regulation requires. However, the 2017 “Glyphosate Files” revealed that parts of the Commission’s draft assessment were copied from a Monsanto submission, leading to accusations of industry bias.

### What are the cut‑off criteria and why are they controversial?

The regulation’s cut‑off criteria automatically disqualify active substances that are carcinogens, mutagens, reproductive toxicants, endocrine disruptors, or persistent organic pollutants, unless exposure is negligible. Industry argues these criteria are too rigid and ignore safe‑use scenarios, potentially removing valuable tools for pest control. Environmental advocates say they embody the precautionary principle. The criteria were a major point of negotiation in 2007‑2008; they survived in the final text, but the “negligible exposure” exemption has been used to keep some borderline substances on the market.

### Could the EU’s pesticide authorisation system be reformed again?

Yes. The Commission’s 2020 “Farm to Fork” strategy proposed a legally binding target to reduce the use of chemical pesticides by 50% by 2030, which would require fundamental changes to the authorisation system. A proposed Sustainable Use of Plant Protection Products Regulation (SUR), tabled in June 2022, would ban all pesticides in sensitive areas and enforce integrated pest management. However, the SUR suffered a surprise rejection in the European Parliament in November 2023 amid strong opposition from farming lobbies and centre‑right MEPs. The file is pending further negotiation.
