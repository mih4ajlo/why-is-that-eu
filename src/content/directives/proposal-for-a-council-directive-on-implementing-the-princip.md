## What is it?

The Horizontal Anti-Discrimination Directive (HADD), proposed by the European Commission on 2 July 2008, would extend EU-level protection against discrimination on grounds of religion or belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation to areas outside the labour market. These areas include social protection (including social security and healthcare), education, and access to goods and services available to the public, such as housing, banking, and transport.

The directive mirrors the scope already covered by the Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC), which prohibits discrimination based on race or ethnic origin in the same non-employment fields. HADD would close the gap in EU anti-discrimination law, where some grounds are protected in employment only (via the Employment Equality Directive, 2000/78/EC), while others — notably disability and age — would gain broader protection under the new instrument.

## Why was it introduced?

The proposal emerged directly from the 2007 Intergovernmental Conference that drafted the Lisbon Treaty, where France — then holding the EU presidency — pushed for a "new dynamic" in social policy. Commissioner Vladimír Špidla (Employment, Social Affairs and Equal Opportunities) had been working on a horizontal directive since 2005, after the European Parliament adopted a resolution calling for one, but the political will crystallised only in 2007-2008.

The immediate trigger was the 2005 European Year of Citizenship through Education and the 2007 European Year of Equal Opportunities for All, which produced Commission-commissioned surveys showing that 84% of Europeans thought discrimination based on sexual orientation was "widespread" (Eurobarometer 263, 2007). The same survey found that 64% of Europeans favoured EU-level action to protect sexual minorities outside work.

The structural tension was clear: the Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) protected people from discrimination in hiring, promotion, and dismissal based on religion, disability, age, or sexual orientation, but left them unprotected when renting an apartment, buying a meal, or seeking medical care. A gay man could be fired for his orientation in most member states after 2003 (when the directive took effect for age and disability), but could still be refused service at a restaurant or denied a mortgage — and the EU had no legal basis to intervene. The Racial Equality Directive had closed that gap for race and ethnicity in 2000; the other four grounds remained two-tier.

The coalition that got the proposal through the Commission was led by Špidla and supported by the European Parliament's Socialist and Green groups, along with civil society organisations including the European Disability Forum, ILGA-Europe, AGE Platform Europe, and the European Network Against Racism. The European Trade Union Confederation also backed the proposal. Business lobbies were divided: BusinessEurope expressed concern about additional costs, but the European Services Forum was broadly supportive.

Opposition came primarily from four member states: Germany, under Chancellor Angela Merkel (CDU), raised subsidiarity concerns; Poland, under Prime Minister Donald Tusk (Civic Platform), objected to sexual orientation protection, reflecting the influence of the Catholic Church and Law and Justice opposition; Malta, then socially conservative, also resisted; and the Netherlands, where the issue of protection for religious organisations' exemptions became a sticking point. The Netherlands' objection was particularly consequential: Dutch Christian parties had long secured exemptions for faith-based schools and hospitals from anti-discrimination rules, and they feared the horizontal directive would remove them.

The timing mattered. The proposal landed just as the Lisbon Treaty was being ratified — a moment of reformist optimism — but before the 2008 financial crisis shifted EU agendas to economic survival. The crisis meant member states were less willing to take on new social obligations. The Commission's 2008 impact assessment estimated the directive would cost EU businesses €23-79 million annually, mostly in adapting buildings for accessibility — a sum industry called unacceptably high, but which the Commission argued was outweighed by the benefits of increased participation.

What changed to block it? In 2009, the Lisbon Treaty came into force, creating a new legal base (Article 19 TFEU) requiring unanimity in the Council for anti-discrimination measures — not qualified majority voting. This raised the bar dramatically. Previously, the Commission had hoped the measure would pass under qualified majority; the Lisbon Treaty's entry made that impossible. From 2009 onward, a single member state could veto.

By 2012, the Polish government under Donald Tusk had shifted its position and became an opponent of sexual orientation protections; Poland formally blocked progress. In 2015, the Juncker Commission tried to break the deadlock by narrowing the directive's scope — limiting it to disability and age — but this was rejected by the European Parliament as discriminatory. In 2018, the Commission's 2019-2024 priorities included a revived effort, but the Council has not debated the proposal substantively since 2013.

As of 2025, the directive remains formally on the Council table, with 12 member states in support under the rotating presidency system — but unanimity is not achievable with Poland, Hungary, Bulgaria, Slovakia, and Romania opposed to including sexual orientation. The European Parliament's Civil Liberties Committee has passed resolutions calling for its adoption in 2019, 2021, and 2023, all ignored by the Council.

## Timeline

- **2000** — Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) and Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) adopted, both under Article 13 TEC
- **2005** — European Parliament resolution calls for a horizontal anti-discrimination directive; Commission begins preparatory work
- **2007** — Eurobarometer 263 finds 84% of EU citizens think sexual orientation discrimination is widespread; 64% support EU action
- **2 July 2008** — Commission proposes the Horizontal Anti-Discrimination Directive (COM(2008) 426)
- **2 April 2009** — European Parliament adopts first-reading position, supporting the proposal with amendments
- **1 December 2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force; anti-discrimination measures now require unanimous Council approval (Art. 19 TFEU)
- **2011** — Polish presidency fails to achieve compromise; Germany signals unwillingness to proceed
- **2013** — Last substantive Council debate under Irish presidency; no progress
- **2015** — Juncker Commission attempts to narrow scope to disability and age only; Parliament rejects
- **2019-2024** — Von der Leyen Commission includes HADD in its priority list but takes no legislative action
- **2023** — Council legal service confirms the directive remains blocked; no scheduled debate

## Impact and consequences

The directive's indefinite blockage has created a tiered anti-discrimination system in the EU: EU citizens are protected against racial discrimination in all areas of life, but against sexual orientation, age, or disability discrimination only in employment. This means, for example, a visibly Roma person cannot be refused a hotel room under EU law; a disabled person can be. A Black person cannot be denied a mortgage on racial grounds; an older person can be told to come back with a younger co-signer. A Muslim woman cannot be refused healthcare because of her headscarf; a gay man can be refused an HIV test.

Legal cases have tested the limits. In *P v. S. and Cornwall County Council* (1996), the CJEU extended the Gender Equality Directive to protect trans people; in *Asociatia Accept v. Consiliul National pentru Combaterea Discriminării* (2013), the CJEU ruled that homophobic statements in the media can constitute discrimination in employment. But no CJEU case has been able to close the horizontal gap because no legal base exists outside employment for these grounds.

The impact has been hardest on LGBTQ+ people and disabled persons. The European Disability Forum's 2022 report found that disabled people were 28% less likely to have accessible housing; while EU law requires workplace accessibility, it does not require shops, banks, or restaurants to be accessible. ILGA-Europe's 2024 Rainbow Map shows that the 11 member states with the lowest legal protection for LGBTQ+ people — including Poland, Hungary, Bulgaria, Romania, Slovakia, Latvia, and Lithuania — would be hardest hit by compliance, and are also the ones blocking the directive.

The financial cost of non-adoption is difficult to quantify, but the European Commission's 2019 evaluation of the Employment Equality Directive noted that nearly one-third of discrimination complaints under that directive go to non-employment settings (housing, services, goods), where complainants have no EU-level recourse. National courts in 18 member states have partially filled the gap with domestic legislation; 9 member states (including Poland, Hungary, Bulgaria, Slovakia, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Romania, and Croatia) still have no comprehensive horizontal protection for sexual orientation.

The directive's failure has also affected EU enlargement. As a candidate country, Serbia adopted a Law on Anti-Discrimination in 2009 that covers all grounds horizontally — meaning its citizens have broader protection than EU citizens in nine member states. This paradox has been cited by the European Parliament as "unacceptable" in multiple resolutions.

## Key questions answered

### Why are some types of discrimination still legal in some EU countries?

EU law prohibits discrimination based on race and ethnicity everywhere — in jobs, housing, healthcare, education, banking, and public services — under the 2000 Racial Equality Directive. But for other grounds (sexual orientation, religion, age, disability), EU law only covers the workplace. This is because a proposed directive to extend those protections was proposed in 2008 but never adopted. The Lisbon Treaty raised the voting threshold to unanimity, and a handful of member states — led by Poland and Hungary — have vetoed it ever since. As a result, in nine EU countries, a landlord can legally refuse to rent to a same-sex couple, and no EU law exists to stop it.

### Can a disabled person in the EU be refused access to a bank or a restaurant?

Yes, in most EU countries. Under the Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC), employers must provide reasonable accommodation for disabled employees, and cannot discriminate in hiring, promotion, or dismissal. But outside of work — in banks, restaurants, shops, cinemas, public transport, or housing — there is no EU-wide requirement to provide reasonable accommodation. Some member states have passed national laws to fill this gap (like Germany's AGG or France's disability laws), but nine have not. The 2008 horizontal directive would have closed this gap, but it remains blocked.

### Why did it take so long to propose EU-wide anti-discrimination rules for LGBTQ+ people?

The Racial Equality Directive was adopted in 2000, a year after the Employment Equality Directive covering sexual orientation. The political window for extending LGBTQ+ protections outside work opened only in 2005-2008, driven by the European Year of Equal Opportunities (2007) and a Commission survey showing that over 80% of Europeans thought sexual orientation discrimination was widespread. Commissioner Špidla proposed the horizontal directive in 2008, but the Lisbon Treaty's unanimity requirement let a minority of member states — notably Poland and Hungary — block it. Earlier attempts in the 1990s failed because the Treaty of Amsterdam (1997) had only just given the EU new legal competence to combat discrimination.

### What would change if EU-wide rules against discrimination based on religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation were adopted tomorrow?

If adopted, the directive would require all EU countries to prohibit discrimination based on sexual orientation, age, disability, and religion in housing, healthcare, education, social protection, and access to goods and services. Landlords could not refuse to rent to a gay couple; banks could not reject a loan to an older person solely because of age; restaurants could not refuse service to a Muslim woman wearing a headscarf; and shops would need to provide reasonable accommodation for disabled customers. The directive also includes provisions for recognising same-sex partners in inheritance, joint tenancy, and survivor benefits — though with a long transition period to respect national family law.
