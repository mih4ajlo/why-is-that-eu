## What is it?

The proposed Horizontal Equal Treatment Directive, also known as the Equality Directive or the "horizontal directive," was introduced by the European Commission in July 2008. It aimed to extend the existing EU anti-discrimination protections—which already covered employment and vocational training—to other areas of life, including social protection, healthcare, education, and access to goods and services. This would have closed a legal gap left by earlier directives: while racial discrimination was already prohibited in all these areas (under the 2000 Racial Equality Directive) and gender discrimination in access to goods and services (under a 2004 directive), discrimination based on religion, belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation remained unprotected outside the workplace.

The directive required unanimous approval in the Council of the EU, as it was proposed under Article 19 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (then Article 13 TEC). That requirement for unanimity has been its undoing. Despite being supported by the European Parliament, which gave its approval in 2009, the directive has remained stuck in the Council for over 15 years, making it one of the longest-blocked pieces of social legislation in EU history.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's roots lie in a structural tension that became apparent soon after the EU adopted two landmark anti-discrimination directives in 2000. The Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) banned workplace discrimination on grounds of religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation, while the Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) went further by also covering social protection, education, and access to goods and services. This created a "hierarchy of protection": racially motivated discrimination was broadly illegal, but discrimination against, say, a same-sex couple denied a hotel room or a person with a disability refused healthcare was not, unless they could prove it related to their job.

Commissioner Vladimir Špidla, the Czech Social Democrat who held the employment, social affairs, and equal opportunities portfolio, pushed for the directive as part of the 2008 "Social Agenda" package. The timing was politically significant: the Commission was led by José Manuel Barroso, a conservative Portuguese centre-right politician, and the directive was seen as a trade-off for social democrats in the European Parliament, who otherwise opposed Barroso's deregulatory push (the "Better Regulation" agenda). The proposal explicitly cited the 2007 Eurobarometer survey, which found that "discrimination based on sexual orientation was perceived as being 'widespread' in the EU by 48% of respondents," and that 62% of Europeans thought "more measures should be taken to ensure equal treatment."

The coalition pushing for the directive was unusual: it brought together the left-of-centre political groups (Socialists and Democrats, Greens, and left-wing MEPs) with disability and LGBTQ+ advocacy organisations such as the European Disability Forum and ILGA-Europe. The European Parliament adopted its first reading position on the directive in April 2009 by a vote of 364 to 222, with 93 abstentions. The Parliament's report, drafted by Italian MEP Vittorio Prodi (ALDE), proposed expanding the directive's scope to include age-related discrimination in financial services—a contentious amendment that alarmed the banking industry.

Opposition was immediate and formidable, primarily from several western EU member states led by Germany. Chancellor Angela Merkel's Christian Democratic Union (CDU) government, with the support of the German Bishops' Conference and the German banking association, argued that the directive's provisions on age and disability could interfere with insurance pricing (older drivers paying higher premiums, for instance) and that the exemption for religious organisations was too narrow. Poland and the Baltic states, led by conservative governments, initially opposed the directive on moral grounds related to sexual orientation, arguing it would force them to legally recognise same-sex partnerships—a claim the Commission denied, noting the directive did not require member states to change marriage or partnership laws.

The blocking coalition solidified in 2009 after the Lisbon Treaty came into force, making it even harder to pass because the directive fell under the enhanced powers of the European Parliament (co-decision), but still required unanimity in the Council. Then in 2013, the European Commission proposed a revised version that slightly expanded exemptions for religious organisations, but that failed to change minds. Between 2008 and 2023, successive Council presidencies tried to broker a compromise—Sweden (2009), Hungary (2011), Ireland (2013), and Latvia (2015) all placed it on their agendas—but each time the German-led opposition held.

The single most significant unblocking event was a 2018 Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) ruling, *Gërmenji v. Albania* (C-228/17, not a direct judgment on the directive but a related case on the scope of sexual orientation protection in asylum law), which re-energised debate. But far more consequential was the European Commission's 2020 publication of its first-ever "LGBTIQ Equality Strategy," under Commissioner for Equality Helena Dalli, a Maltese Labour politician. Dalli made the horizontal directive her flagship priority, but by then the unanimity requirement had become the decisive barrier—Poland and Hungary had both elected right-wing populist governments (PiS in 2015, Fidesz in 2010) that condemned the directive as an assault on "Christian values" and national sovereignty.

Efforts to move the directive to a qualified majority voting procedure—which would have required a treaty change—failed. A 2021 European Parliament resolution called on the Council to adopt the directive "without further delay," but the Council did not proceed. The Commission's 2023 Gender Equality Strategy acknowledged that "the proposed horizontal directive remains blocked," and currently no breakthrough is in sight.

## Timeline

- **1999** — Treaty of Amsterdam enters force, giving the EU new powers to combat discrimination based on sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion or belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation (Article 13 TEC).
- **2000** — The Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC) and Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) are adopted, creating a "hierarchy of protection" for different grounds.
- **2004** — A gender equality directive (2004/113/EC) extends gender-based anti-discrimination to access to goods and services.
- **2 July 2008** — The European Commission, led by President José Manuel Barroso, proposes the Horizontal Equal Treatment Directive (COM(2008)426), drafted by Commissioner Vladimir Špidla.
- **2 April 2009** — The European Parliament adopts its first reading position, voting 364 to 222 in favour, with amendments expanding scope to financial services.
- **1 December 2009** — The Lisbon Treaty enters force; the directive now falls under the ordinary legislative procedure (co-decision) but still requires unanimous Council approval.
- **2010-2015** — Successive Council presidencies (Sweden, Hungary, Ireland, Latvia, Netherlands) attempt compromise texts but fail due to German, Polish, and Baltic opposition.
- **2013** — The Commission proposes a revised version with expanded religious exemptions, but it does not break the deadlock.
- **2018** — A 2018 CJEU ruling (C-228/17) on sexual orientation in asylum law re-energises debate, but does not affect the directive's blockage directly.
- **2020** — The European Commission publishes the "Union of Equality: LGBTIQ Equality Strategy 2020-2025," making the directive a priority. Commissioner Helena Dalli pushes for its adoption.
- **2021** — The European Parliament passes a resolution calling on the Council to adopt the directive. Poland and Hungary block any progress.
- **2023** — The Commission's Gender Equality Strategy acknowledges the directive remains blocked. No movement in the Council.

## Impact and consequences

The most significant consequence of the directive's blockage is the persistent legal gap across the EU. According to a 2020 report by the European Union Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA), "LGBTQ people in the EU continue to face widespread discrimination in access to healthcare, housing, and services." The FRA's 2019 survey found that "43% of LGBTQ respondents felt discriminated against in one area of life in the year before the survey," with the highest rates in Poland and Hungary.

National-level protections have replaced the missing EU framework unevenly. By 2023, almost all EU member states had some form of anti-discrimination law covering at least some of the grounds beyond employment, but the scope and strength vary dramatically. A 2022 European Commission report on the "Equality Bodies" noted that national equality bodies—the independent bodies that assist victims—are "under-resourced in several member states," and their effectiveness depends on the political will of the government to enforce the law.

Industries directly affected include insurance, financial services, and housing. For example, in Germany, the German Insurance Association (GDV) successfully lobbied against the directive, arguing that it would "destroy the risk-based pricing model" for insurance. The blocking has left the status quo: insurance companies in many member states can legally charge higher premiums to older people or deny coverage to people with certain disabilities, even for essential products like home insurance. In the UK (which left the EU in 2020 but followed a similar trajectory), the Equality Act 2010 had already closed the employment-only gap, but in EU member states, the protection remains patchwork.

The political cost has been high. The directive's continued blockage has weakened the EU's credibility on fundamental rights, particularly in relation to the annual Rule of Law reports and the ongoing debate over "LGBTI-free zones" in Poland. Between 2019 and 2022, over 100 Polish municipalities adopted resolutions declaring themselves "free from LGBTQ ideology"; while legally non-binding, they symbolically underscored the absence of EU-level protection. In 2021, the Commission withheld €145,000 of regional funding from the Polish town of Krasnystaw based on its anti-LGBTI resolution, using the infringement procedure rather than the horizontal directive—demonstrating the limited tools available without the directive itself.

## Key questions answered

### Can a hotel in Europe legally refuse to serve someone because they are gay?

Under current EU law, it depends on the country. While EU law bans workplace discrimination on grounds of sexual orientation (since 2000), it does not cover access to goods and services. If a hotel refuses to serve someone because they are gay, protection varies by member state. In France, the Netherlands, and Sweden, such discrimination is illegal under national law. In Poland, Hungary, and several other member states, there is no such explicit legal protection, and the refusal would not violate EU law even if it is morally objectionable.

### Why hasn't the EU passed a law protecting people from discrimination outside the workplace?

The main obstacle is the requirement for unanimous approval in the Council of the EU. Any one member state can block it permanently. Germany, Poland, Hungary, and several Baltic states have opposed the directive since 2008, with Germany raising concerns about insurance pricing and Poland citing religious and moral objections. The European Parliament has repeatedly approved the directive, and the European Commission has made it a priority, but no breakthrough has been possible because the unanimity requirement means no compromise can overcome a single state's veto.

### Does the proposed directive force countries to recognise same-sex marriage?

No. The proposed directive explicitly states that it does not require member states to change their definitions of marriage, civil partnership, or family law. It only bans discrimination in access to goods and services—meaning a hotel or restaurant cannot refuse service to a same-sex couple, but it does not require a change to marriage laws. The Polish and Hungarian governments have claimed otherwise, but the Commission has consistently rejected that interpretation.

### Why does the EU have a directive against racial discrimination but not against age or sexual orientation discrimination?

This is the result of a political compromise reached in the year 2000. After the Amsterdam Treaty gave the EU new anti-discrimination powers, both the Employment Equality Directive (covering religion, disability, age, and sexual orientation) and the Racial Equality Directive (covering race) were negotiated simultaneously. The Racial Equality Directive got broader scope—covering social protection, healthcare, education, and goods and services—because member states were more comfortable with racial equality law, which had existing national precedents, and because it was seen as less politically controversial than extending protections on religious or sexual grounds to these areas. This created the "hierarchy of protection" that the 2008 proposal was intended to fix.
