## What is it?

The Port State Control Directive (2009/16/EC) sets up a mandatory, risk-based inspection regime for all foreign ships calling at EU ports. Under this system, national maritime authorities inspect vessels to verify compliance with international conventions covering safety (SOLAS), pollution prevention (MARPOL), seafarer living and working conditions (Maritime Labour Convention), and crew competency (STCW). Ships with a higher risk profile — older vessels, poor flag state records, or previous detentions — are inspected more frequently.

The directive created a "New Inspection Regime" (NIR) that replaced an earlier rule requiring every ship to be inspected at least once a year. Instead, a targeting system calculates a "ship risk profile" based on parameters like age, flag, recognised organisation performance, and company history. High-risk ships must be inspected every six months; standard-risk ships every 10-12 months; and low-risk ships every 24-36 months. This shift from quantitative to risk-based inspections is the directive's core innovation.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger for the directive was a series of high-profile maritime disasters — notably the *Erika* (1999, off Brittany, 12,000 tonnes of heavy fuel oil spilled) and the *Prestige* (2002, off Galicia, 63,000 tonnes spilled, devastating Spain's coastline). The *Erika* sank on 12 December 1999, leading to an enormous environmental clean-up cost of approximately €1.5 billion according to the French government's estimate. The *Prestige* broke apart on 19 November 2002, causing an estimated €2.5 billion in damages to fisheries and tourism, according to a 2005 European Commission report.

Both disasters exposed a fundamental structural tension in the existing system: flag states (the country where a ship is registered) had weak incentives to enforce safety standards, as shipowners could easily re-flag to "flags of convenience" with poor safety records. A 2001 European Commission communication noted that the EU had tried to address this through the "Port State Control" directive (95/21/EC), but inspections remained inconsistent across member states and too few high-risk vessels were targeted.

The coalition for stronger rules formed around two key groups. On one side, coastal states like France, Spain, and Portugal — whose economies and environments bore the direct cost of spills — pushed for tougher enforcement. On the other, the European Parliament's Transport and Tourism Committee, led by rapporteur Dirk Sterckx (Belgium, ALDE), argued for a risk-based system that would focus inspections on the worst-performing ships, rather than wasting resources on well-run vessels. The European Community Shipowners' Association (ECSA) initially opposed more frequent inspections, warning of increased port delays and costs for reputable operators. However, the shipping industry's own internal logic — that bad actors undercut good ones on safety — eventually aligned many shipowners with reformers.

The Commission's 2005 impact assessment found that the existing system inspected only about 25% of ships in EU ports each year (the Paris MOU target at the time), and that many substandard ships were escaping detection. Maritime safety commissioner Loyola de Palacio (2002 proposal) and later Jacques Barrot (2008 adoption) made the directive a priority. The Parliament and Council reached first-reading agreement in April 2009, with strong support from the EPP and S&D groups.

Timing was critical. The *Prestige* disaster in 2002 had broken a long deadlock in the Council, where member states like Greece (a major flag state) had previously blocked stricter controls. The EU's "Third Maritime Safety Package" — also known as "Erika III" — was adopted in 2008-2009, bundling seven directives and regulations. Port State Control was the package's enforcement pillar. The 2002 European Court of Justice ruling in Case C-221/00 (Commission v Austria) had also confirmed that EU law could impose binding inspection standards on member states, limiting their discretion.

## Timeline

- **12 December 1999** — *Erika* tanker sinks off Brittany, spilling 12,000 tonnes of heavy fuel oil. The disaster kills 150,000 seabirds and devastates local oyster farming.
- **19 November 2002** — *Prestige* breaks apart off Galicia, spilling 63,000 tonnes of oil. The spill forces the closure of Spain's richest fishing grounds.
- **6 December 2001** — European Commission issues its "White Paper on European Transport Policy" calling for a single EU maritime safety area and stronger port state controls.
- **23 November 2005** — Commission proposes the "Third Maritime Safety Package" including the new Port State Control Directive (COM(2005) 588 final).
- **23 April 2009** — Directive 2009/16/EC formally adopted by the European Parliament and Council.
- **1 January 2011** — Directive applies in all member states, replacing the earlier 95/21/EC regime. Paris MOU 2010 targets already influenced by the new approach.
- **15 August 2013** — Commission reports first positive results: the detention rate of substandard ships in EU ports rises to 3.5% (from 2.8% under the old system), and high-risk ship inspections increase by 40%.
- **13 May 2019** — Commission amends the directive through Regulation (EU) 2019/219, updating ship risk profiles and inspection criteria to include environmental and labour conditions.

## Impact and consequences

For ship operators, the directive increased inspection frequency for high-risk vessels but reduced it for low-risk ones. A European Commission 2013 implementation report found that the proportion of high-risk ships inspected went from under 30% in 2009 to over 60% by 2013. The number of ships detained in EU ports increased from about 1,200 in 2009 to roughly 1,500 in 2012, according to the Commission's report.

Flag states responded: the Paris MOU's "white list" (flags with the best safety records) expanded, while flags like Bolivia, Cambodia, and Libya were placed on the "black list" and faced near-comprehensive inspections in EU ports. The directive effectively created an EU-wide deterrent: shipowners could no longer count on "port shopping" to avoid scrutiny, as a detention in one EU port automatically triggered a risk rating update for all other member states.

Environmental groups (e.g., Seas At Risk, the European Environmental Bureau) praised the directive, though they pushed for even stricter criteria. The European Parliament's 2013 report on maritime safety noted that some members (notably from Eastern European states with smaller maritime sectors) expressed concern about cost burdens for their national administrations.

The directive's most visible consequence has been the "banning" of persistently poor-performing ships. Under the 2009 regime, a ship detained multiple times can be banned from all EU ports. The Commission's 2018 overview (EMSA, 2018) reported that 162 ships were banned from EU ports between 2011 and 2017. This has forced some of the worst operators to either improve or exit the European market entirely.

## Key questions answered

### How does a ship get inspected or detained in an EU port?

National port authorities calculate a "Ship Risk Profile" using an EU-wide information system, THETIS. If a ship receives a high risk score — due to its age, flag state record, or history of deficiencies — it must be inspected every six months. If inspectors find critical deficiencies in safety, pollution prevention, or crew working conditions, they can detain the ship until the problems are fixed. The detention and the reason are recorded in THETIS, visible to all EU member states.

### Can a ship be banned from all EU ports?

Yes. A ship can be banned from all EU ports if it has been detained three times in the previous 36 months, or if it fails to call at a designated repair yard after a detention. The ban applies to all EU member states simultaneously. The ship can only return after a detailed inspection in its home port and written confirmation from its flag state that it now complies.

### What changes were made after the 2019 amendments?

The 2019 amendments expanded the ship risk profile to include environmental and labour conditions more explicitly. Ships with poor energy efficiency or emissions records now face higher inspection scores. The amendments also integrated the Maritime Labour Convention (2006) checks, so inspectors can now detain ships for poor crew accommodation or unpaid wages, not just safety and pollution issues. The target inspection rate for low-risk ships was reduced from every 12-36 months to allow more resources for high-risk ones.

### Why do some countries' ships get inspected more often in EU ports?

The system scores ships partially based on their flag state's performance. Flags on the Paris MOU "black list" — typically states with poor inspection records — trigger the highest inspection frequency. This creates a strong economic incentive for shipowners to register under better-performing flags. For example, flags like Panama (grey list) and Liberia (white list) face much less scrutiny than flags like Bolivia or Tanzania (black list), according to Paris MOU data.
