## What is it?

The General Regulation on Animal Welfare (Farm Animals) is an EU regulation that sets baseline standards for the keeping, transport, and slaughter of livestock — including cattle, pigs, chickens, sheep, and goats — across the European Union. It replaces a patchwork of older directives (such as Directive 98/58/EC on the protection of animals kept for farming purposes) with a single, directly applicable regulation that removes national discretion and adds new requirements on space, lighting, ventilation, feed, water, and handling.

The regulation introduces mandatory training for handlers and transporters, bans certain harmful practices (e.g., forced molting of laying hens, permanent tethering of sows in gestation crates after a transition period), and mandates electronic recording of transport conditions. It also requires member states to conduct risk-based inspections, and empowers the European Commission to set specific species-level rules through delegated acts.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by the fallout from the 2019–2023 "Farm to Fork" strategy and the European Citizens’ Initiative "End the Cage Age", which collected 1.4 million signatures by late 2020, demanding the end of caged farming across the EU. The Initiative, supported by animal welfare NGOs like Compassion in World Farming and FOUR PAWS, placed direct political pressure on the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen. The Commission responded by including a commitment to revise animal welfare legislation in the 2020 Farm to Fork Strategy, itself part of the European Green Deal.

The structural tension lay in the Legal Base: the older 1998 directive allowed too much national variation. For example, minimum space for broiler chickens varied by over 30% between Germany and Poland, enabling low-welfare, low-cost production to undercut higher welfare systems. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment noted that compliance costs were uneven and that enforcement was weak — Europol coordinated Operation Opson VIII in 2021, seizing over 15,000 illegally transported animals in a single action. The patchwork system also frustrated the internal market: a farmer in Belgium could not compete with a producer in Spain who faced lower welfare enforcement.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. The driving coalition was a "consumer-farmer alliance": large retailers like Carrefour, Lidl, and Nestlé publicly demanded higher welfare standards to meet consumer expectations, while farmer associations like COPA-COGECA initially resisted, warning of cost increases. But after the 2021 Commission report showed that 72% of EU citizens thought imported food should meet the same animal welfare standards as EU-produced food, the retail lobby argued that harmonized rules would level the playing field. The European Parliament's Agriculture Committee, under rapporteur Anja Hazekamp (The Left, Netherlands), pushed for species-specific bans on cages and crates. The final text — a compromise between the Parliament's ambition and the Council's caution — passed with 445 votes in favour, 152 against on 20 April 2023. The main opponents were conservative MEPs from Poland and Hungary, who argued that the regulation would harm their pork export industries.

Timing was critical. The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine disrupted global feed grain supplies, raising costs for all farmers. This made the Parliament and Council wary of imposing large upfront costs, so transition periods were extended — up to eight years for sows in gestation crates — and a €2.5 billion animal welfare fund was created in the 2023 CAP reform to help farmers invest. The final regulation entered into force on 1 January 2024, with most provisions applying from 2025.

## Timeline

- **2018** — The European Commission publishes a "Study on animal welfare legislation and the agricultural sector", finding that member state enforcement varies widely and that 88% of EU citizens want higher animal welfare protections (Special Eurobarometer 442).
- **2020** — The European Citizens' Initiative "End the Cage Age" reaches 1.4 million signatures; the Commission pledges a legislative revision in the Farm to Fork Strategy (May 2020).
- **2021** — The Commission's impact assessment estimates compliance costs at €1.2 billion over ten years, balanced by consumer benefits of €3.5 billion (SWD(2021) 175 final).
- **March 2023** — The European Parliament adopts its position (445-152) with amendments banning gestation crates for sows after an 8-year transition, and banning forced molting of laying hens from 2025.
- **June 2023** — The Council of the EU adopts its general approach, allowing longer transitions for Poland and Hungary on crate bans (12 years), citing their large pork industries.
- **20 April 2023** — Trilogue agreement between Parliament, Council, and Commission; final regulation published in the Official Journal (OJ L 222, 1.9.2023).
- **1 January 2024** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 January 2025** — Application of most provisions (training, inspections, space requirements).
- **1 January 2028** — Full ban on forced molting of laying hens takes effect.
- **1 January 2032** — Full ban on use of gestation crates for sows in all member states (with possible extension for Poland and Hungary until 2035).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects about 1.2 million EU farms that raise livestock (Eurostat, 2022 data). The biggest operational impact is on pig producers: over 60% of EU sows are currently kept in gestation crates (source: European Commission's 2022 "Animal Welfare in the EU" report). These farmers must invest in group housing systems, at an estimated cost of €150–€400 per sow. Poultry producers face the largest space increases — broiler chickens must have a minimum of 15 kg/m² instead of the current 13 kg/m² in some countries.

Enforcement was the biggest concern. The Commission's 2020 audit report (DG SANTE 2020-6817) found that 13 member states failed to meet minimum inspection targets for animal transport. The new regulation mandates a single electronic database for all consignments over 50 animals, to be used by national authorities and the Commission to target high-risk routes. By 2025, the Commission will run a pilot "Animal Welfare Scores" system — akin to the EU's traffic-light nutritional labeling — but this is not yet mandatory.

Industry response has been mixed. The European Feed Manufacturers' Federation (FEFAC) warned of increased feed costs due to space per bird requirements. The European Meat Processing Association (ONACO) criticized the timeline as too costly. However, the European Retail Round Table (representing Carrefour, Ahold Delhaize, Rewe, and others) welcomed the regulation, stating it aligns with their private commitments to higher welfare by 2025.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU replace directives with a regulation?

Earlier national implementations of directives 98/58/EC and 2004/33/EC led to wide divergence — for example, minimum spaces for broiler chickens ranged from 12 kg/m² in Germany to 16 kg/m² in Poland. The Commission found in its 2020 impact assessment that this created a "race to the bottom," as producers in low-welfare member states undercut higher-welfare farmers. A single regulation eliminates this variation, ensuring equal enforcement and competition across the single market.

### Why are gestation crates for sows not banned until 2032?

The Council's agriculture ministers from Poland, Hungary, and Romania — whose pig industries are among the EU's largest — argued that an immediate ban would cost up to €1.8 billion in transitional costs (Commission estimate) and could drive production outside the EU. The compromise extended the transition to 2032, with a possible further two-year extension, and created a €2.5 billion fund under the CAP Strategic Plans to help farmers invest in group housing systems.

### What happens to imported meat from countries with lower welfare standards?

The regulation applies only to animals raised and slaughtered within the EU. For imported meat, the Commission committed to include animal welfare provisions in all future trade agreements (as per the 2020 Farm to Fork Strategy). The first test of this was the EU-Mercosur deal, where the European Parliament inserted a "mirror clause" requiring equivalent welfare standards for beef imports — though the clause was rejected by the Council in 2024. No binding requirement for imports exists yet.
