## What is it?

The Asylum Procedures Regulation (APR) is a core component of the EU’s 2024 Pact on Migration and Asylum, replacing the fragmented national asylum procedures that had led to wildly different recognition rates across member states. It creates a single, mandatory EU-wide procedure for processing asylum claims, from initial registration to final decision and appeal.

Under the APR, applicants will have their claims assessed by the member state of first entry or relocation, using a standardised process that includes a personal interview, assessment under both the Qualification Regulation and national law, and a right to an effective remedy before a court or tribunal. Crucially, the regulation makes border procedures mandatory for applicants from countries with low EU-wide recognition rates (under 20%), and optional for others. These border procedures can last up to 12 weeks and may include detention.

## Why was it introduced?

The most specific, dramatic element that broke the decade-long deadlock on EU asylum reform was the 2015–16 migration crisis, during which over 1.3 million asylum applications were lodged in the EU in 2015 alone, according to Eurostat. The existing Dublin III Regulation, which made the country of first entry responsible for processing claims, collapsed under the strain. Greece processed over 870,000 arrivals in 2015; Germany received over 890,000 asylum applications that year. The system was structurally broken: frontline states bore overwhelming responsibility while others paid lip service to solidarity.

A 2016 Commission proposal to reform the Dublin system failed in the Council, blocked by a coalition led by Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic, who refused mandatory relocation quotas. The European Court of Justice in Joined Cases C-643/15 and C-647/15 (2017) upheld the quotas, but Hungary, Poland, and the Czech Republic ignored the ruling, and the Commission’s infringement proceedings stalled.

The timing shifted decisively in 2020, when Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, a German Christian Democrat, made the Pact on Migration and Asylum a flagship of her mandate. The COVID-19 pandemic had frozen crossings temporarily, but by 2022, irregular arrivals via the Western Balkans and Central Mediterranean surged again. The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment noted that "on average, around 30% of asylum decisions at first instance are overturned on appeal" across the EU, indicating systemic procedural failures.

The coalition that passed the Pact in 2024 was unusual. Von der Leyen brokered a deal with the European Parliament’s centre-right EPP, Socialists & Democrats, and Renew groups, while the Greens/EFA reluctantly abstained and the far-right ID group opposed. In the Council, the opposition from Poland and Hungary was neutralised by a qualified majority vote in May 2024, after Germany’s coalition government, led by Chancellor Olaf Scholz, dropped its earlier resistance to mandatory border procedures in exchange for a crisis mechanism allowing flexibility during mass arrivals.

What changed in the final 2023–24 negotiations was the introduction of a "mandatory screening" at entry, including identity verification and health checks, followed by a border procedure for applicants from countries with less than 20% recognition rates. This was a concession to member states like Austria and Denmark that had demanded more restrictive rules. Human rights organisations, including Amnesty International and the European Council on Refugees and Exiles, warned that the border procedures risked "automatic detention and inadequate legal guarantees," but the political centre held.

## Timeline

- **2015** — The migration crisis overwhelms the Dublin system; over 1.3 million asylum applications in the EU (Eurostat).
- **2016** — Commission proposes reform of the Dublin system; blocked in Council by Visegrád group.
- **2017** — CJEU upholds relocation quotas in joined cases C-643/15 and C-647/15; Poland, Hungary, and Czechia refuse compliance.
- **2020** — Commission President von der Leyen presents the Pact on Migration and Asylum, including a proposal for a single Asylum Procedures Regulation.
- **2022** — Russia’s invasion of Ukraine triggers the Temporary Protection Directive; Commission revises the Pact to include crisis mechanisms.
- **2023** — European Parliament and Council reach provisional agreement on the APR in December.
- **2024, April** — European Parliament adopts the entire Pact with 322 votes in favour, 266 against, and 70 abstentions.
- **2024, May** — Council adopts the Regulation by qualified majority; Poland and Hungary vote against.
- **2024, June** — Regulation enters into force.
- **2026, June** — Full application date (two-year transition for member states to implement).

## Impact and consequences

The APR creates the most centralised asylum procedure in EU history. It standardises timelines: an applicant must be registered within 3 days, and the first-instance procedure completed within 6 months in normal procedures, or 12 weeks for border procedures. Member states must establish or designate a "determining authority" with trained staff and interpreters.

The border procedure is expected to affect around 30-40% of applicants based on 2023 recognition rates, according to the European Asylum Support Office (now EUAA). For example, applicants from India, Vietnam, and the Western Balkans, whose EU-wide recognition rates were below 20% in 2023, will face mandatory border processing. Human rights groups fear this will lead to prolonged detention in overcrowded facilities, particularly in Greece and Italy, where border reception capacity is already strained.

The regulation also introduces a "safe third country" concept, allowing member states to reject claims if the applicant had a connection to a country deemed safe, even if not an EU member. The Commission estimated in 2024 that this could reduce asylum applications by up to 25% over five years, though critics argue it merely shifts processing burdens to non-EU states with weaker standards.

Industry response has been muted; the main lobbying came from NGOs like Caritas Europa and the International Rescue Committee, which argued for exemptions for vulnerable groups. The European Parliament’s LIBE Committee inserted provisions for unaccompanied minors, who will not be subject to mandatory border procedures.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need a common set of asylum rules when each country already had its own way of handling asylum claims?

The pre-existing system produced huge disparities: in 2023, the recognition rate for Syrian applicants was 98% in Sweden but 62% in Bulgaria, according to Eurostat data. This created "asylum shopping" — secondary movements within the EU — and undermined trust in the external border. The APR mandates identical procedures and deadlines, reducing incentives for forum-shopping and ensuring that applicants are assessed on the merits of their case, not the member state they reach.

### Why did the EU decide that some asylum applications must be processed at the border instead of inside the country?

The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment found that "secondary movements remain a major challenge" — approximately 20% of applicants in 2019 had lodged claims in multiple member states. The border procedure is designed to deter unfounded applications by enabling fast-track decisions at the external border, with detention permitted to prevent absconding. It was a direct response to the 2015 crisis, where applicants could vanish into the Schengen area before their claims were processed. Critics argue it sacrifices due process for deterrence, but the political calculus was that only such measures could secure the necessary support from Austria, Denmark, and other restrictive states.

### Why did Germany change its mind and start supporting the idea of processing asylum claims at the border?

Germany’s coalition government (SPD, Greens, FDP) was internally divided. The Greens opposed border detention, while the FDP demanded stricter rules. In 2023, German Interior Minister Nancy Faeser (SPD) brokered a compromise: Germany would support mandatory border procedures in exchange for a "crisis mechanism" that allows member states under mass arrival pressure to extend procedures or derogate from standards. This deal also included a "solidarity pool" of 30,000 relocations per year, although this is voluntary. Without Germany’s flip, the Pact would have collapsed in the Council.

### What happens to the current rules that decide which EU country is responsible for an asylum claim?

The APR effectively replaces the Dublin rules for first-instance procedures. Under the new system, the member state that conducts the screening at entry is responsible for the procedure, but the APR introduces a "relocation mechanism" that can transfer applicants to other member states if the first country is overwhelmed. The old Dublin Regulation (604/2013) is repealed by the Asylum and Migration Management Regulation (2024/1351/EU), which is part of the same Pact.
