## What is it?

The CAP 2023-2027 is the latest iteration of the EU's oldest and largest common policy, accounting for roughly 31% of the EU budget (€387 billion in 2021 prices, including €264 billion for direct payments and €95 billion for rural development). It operates through 28 national CAP Strategic Plans (one per member state, plus two for Belgium and Finland's autonomous regions), approved by the European Commission in 2022-2023.

Its core innovation is the **new green architecture**: a mandatory "enhanced conditionality" scheme called GAEC (Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition) standards, coupled with voluntary **eco-schemes** that reward farmers for practices like organic farming, carbon sequestration, and precision agriculture. At least 25% of direct payment budgets must fund eco-schemes, and 35% of rural development funds must go to environmental and climate measures. The policy also introduces a **social conditionality** component, linking some payments to compliance with EU labour laws, and a **redistributive payment** requiring member states to shift at least 10% of direct payments from larger to smaller farms.

## Why was it introduced?

The CAP's reform was driven by a contradiction that became impossible to ignore by the late 2010s: the policy continued to pay billions to large landowners regardless of environmental outcomes, while the EU's own Green Deal set binding targets for halving pesticide use, cutting fertiliser runoff by 50%, and expanding organic farming to 25% of farmland by 2030. The Commission's 2019 impact assessment found that 80% of nitrogen surplus came from just 20% of farms — a concentration of pollution that direct payments had done nothing to address.

The political breakthrough came in December 2019, when the von der Leyen Commission published the **European Green Deal** and its **Farm to Fork Strategy** (May 2020). These set legally non-binding but politically powerful targets that the CAP would need to deliver. Agriculture Commissioner **Janusz Wojciechowski** (Poland, ECR), a former farmer himself, initially resisted strong environmental conditionality, arguing it would hurt small Polish farms. But he was outflanked by European Commission President **Ursula von der Leyen** and by the Parliament's Agriculture Committee chair **Norbert Lins** (EPP, Germany), who pushed for the new green architecture as a condition for Parliament's approval.

The European Parliament vote on 23 October 2020 was the decisive moment. The centre-right EPP and centre-left S&D formed a coalition with centrist Renew Europe, approving the basic architecture (425 in favour, 247 against). The main opposition came from the far-right Identity and Democracy group (ID) and the left-wing GUE/NGL, who argued the reforms would either hurt farmers (ID) or not go far enough (GUE/NGL). The Greens/EFA group, holding 74 seats, pushed for harder targets — including a mandatory 30% eco-scheme budget floor — but settled for the 25% after the Commission threatened to withdraw the proposal if it was made too ambitious.

The reform's timing was shaped by two crises. First, the **COVID-19 pandemic** (2020) disrupted food supply chains and exposed the fragility of just-in-time agriculture; the Commission used this to argue that a greener, more resilient CAP was essential for food security. Second, Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 — during the policy's final approval phase — triggered a temporary suspension of greening requirements (the "derogation" allowing planting on fallow land), creating tension between short-term food production and long-term sustainability goals. The Commission, under agriculture DGs **Wolfgang Burtscher** and **Tassos Haniotis**, had to negotiate a compromise: the derogation would last one year, but the green architecture itself would remain intact.

Key external actors included **Copa-Cogeca** (the main farm lobby), which warned in its 2021 position paper of "unprecedented administrative burden" and successfully watered down the eco-scheme from mandatory to optional for member states. Environmental NGOs like **BirdLife Europe** and **WWF** countered with a 2020 study claiming the 25% eco-scheme floor would still cover only 8% of farmland; the Commission - in its 2021 impact assessment - estimated 35% of farmland would be covered by 2027, but independent analysis by the **Institute for European Environmental Policy** (IEEP) in 2022 put the figure at 15-20%.

## Timeline

- **2018** — The Commission publishes its first legislative proposals for the 2021-2027 CAP, but negotiations stall over budgetary disputes with the European Council.
- **May 2020** — The Farm to Fork Strategy is published, setting specific environmental targets that the new CAP must deliver.
- **23 October 2020** — European Parliament adopts its position on the three CAP reform regulations, including the 25% eco-scheme floor.
- **December 2021** — Political agreement reached between Parliament, Council, and Commission after two years of trilogue negotiations.
- **2 December 2021** — The three CAP regulations (2021/2115, 2021/2116, 2021/2117) are formally adopted in the Official Journal.
- **January 2022** — Russia's invasion of Ukraine prompts the Commission to grant a one-year derogation from GAEC 7 (fallow land requirement).
- **September 2022** — The Commission approves all 28 CAP Strategic Plans, the last being for Belgium (Wallonia) in December.
- **1 January 2023** — CAP 2023-2027 enters into application; member states begin issuing payments under the new rules.
- **April 2023** — The Commission launches the first audit of eco-scheme implementation, noting that only 12 member states met the 25% budget allocation by the deadline.
- **March 2024** — EU farmers' protests in France, Germany, Poland, and the Netherlands lead to the Commission proposing a "simplification package," including exemptions from certain GAEC standards (e.g., GAEC 8 on landscape features).

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate impact has been administrative. Member states were required to redesign their entire payment systems within 18 months, and some — notably Italy and Poland — submitted strategic plans that the Commission sent back for revision due to insufficient environmental ambition. The Commission's **Annual Activity Report for 2023** noted that €2.3 billion in payments were delayed for 15 member states due to IT system failures in tracking the new conditionality rules.

The redistribution mechanism has been controversial. Data from the Commission's own **CAP Indicators Dashboard** (2024) showed that the 10% redistributive payment requirement actually reduced payments to the largest farms by only 1.2% on average, because member states used loopholes: they could cap payments per-farm rather than per-hectare, and many applied the cap at €100,000 — affecting only the top 2% of farms. The **European Court of Auditors** is currently conducting a performance audit; its preliminary findings (February 2024) suggested that eco-schemes were "poorly targeted" and that member states had designed them to maximise uptake rather than environmental outcome.

Farmers have responded with widespread protests across Europe in early 2024 — involving as many as 40,000 tractors in Brussels alone in February 2024 — forcing the Commission to roll back some GAEC standards. On 15 March 2024, the Commission proposed exempting small farms (under 10 hectares) from all conditionality requirements, affecting about 65% of farms but only 9% of farmland. Environmental NGOs, including **Greenpeace** and **Friends of the Earth**, called this a "surrender to the farm lobby."

On social conditionality, the first enforcement case emerged in Spain: in October 2023, the Spanish government withheld €1.8 million in payments from a large company operating in the strawberry fields of Huelva after labour inspectors found systematic violations of the Seasonal Workers Directive, using the new CAP labour conditionality rule for the first time.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the CAP need reform if it already had greening measures?

The 2013 CAP reform introduced "greening" — crop diversification, permanent grassland, and ecological focus areas — but the European Court of Auditors' 2017 special report found it was "not delivering on the environment": 80% of farms were exempt from greening rules (mainly because they were below the 10-hectare threshold or they already met other obligations), and the rules themselves were so weak that compliance was automatic for most farms. The 2023 reform replaces this with a "new green architecture" that is mandatory and progressively stricter.

### Why did farmers protest, and was their opposition successful?

Farmers objected primarily to three things: the administrative burden of the new conditionality tracking (especially the AIMO soil sampling system required in the Netherlands), the lower water and higher input costs under GAEC standards, and the loss of direct payments from the redistributive mechanism. Protests escalated in early 2024, culminating in the February-March tractors protest in Brussels. The protests were partly successful: the Commission proposed exempting small farms from conditionality on 15 March 2024, but the core green architecture — eco-schemes and mandatory GAEC standards — remains intact.

### How does the CAP interact with the EU's climate targets?

The CAP is the largest single source of EU climate funding, with at least 40% of its budget (€155 billion) allocated to climate-relevant measures. But the Commission's own 2021 Fitness Check found that only 8% of CAP spending actively contributed to climate mitigation; the rest was passive or even harmful (e.g., livestock subsidies in some member states). The post-2023 CAP aims to increase this to 25% of direct payments through eco-schemes, but the European Environment Agency's 2023 report on agricultural greenhouse gas emissions noted that EU farm emissions fell by only 2.1% between 2005 and 2021 — far below the Green Deal's 55% target.
