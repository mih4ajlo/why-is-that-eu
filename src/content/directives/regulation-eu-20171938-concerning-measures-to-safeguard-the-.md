## What is it?

This 2017 regulation overhauls the EU's approach to gas supply security after the 2009 Russia-Ukraine transit crisis exposed critical vulnerabilities. It replaces the 2010 regulation (994/2010/EU) with stricter requirements: mandatory joint risk assessments by groups of member states, binding infrastructure standards, and a novel "solidarity mechanism" that forces countries to supply gas to their neighbours' protected customers during severe shortages.

The regulation sets two key supply standards: the "N-1" rule requiring infrastructure capacity to meet extreme demand even if the largest single piece of infrastructure fails, and the "protection of customers" standard guaranteeing gas to households and essential social services during a gas supply crisis. Crucially, it introduces a three-tier crisis framework — early warning, alert, emergency — and requires countries to have preventive action plans and emergency plans approved by the European Commission.

## Why was it introduced?

The January 2009 Russia-Ukraine gas cutoff was the immediate trigger. For 20 days in winter, Russia's Gazprom halted all gas flows through Ukraine's Naftogaz transit system, cutting off 18 EU member states — some entirely, like Bulgaria and Slovakia, which lost 100% of their supply. The European Commission's then-Energy Commissioner Andris Piebalgs called it "a wake-up call" for an EU that had grown complacent about Russian gas dependence.

The 2010 regulation that followed was a first step, but it had fatal weaknesses. It left too much discretion to national governments, allowed countries to keep their infrastructure plans secret, and had no mechanism for cross-border solidarity when one member state's crisis threatened others. The 2014 annexation of Crimea and Russia's weaponisation of energy against Ukraine — cutting gas supplies in June 2014 over a payment dispute — shattered any remaining illusion that Russia was a reliable supplier.

Energy Commissioner Günther Oettinger (2014-2016) drove the revision, arguing the EU needed "more than national plans for national emergencies — we need a European energy union." The European Parliament's industry committee, led by Polish MEP Jerzy Buzek, pushed hard for mandatory solidarity agreements. The Baltic states and Poland, which had experienced Russian pressure since the 1990s, formed the most vocal coalition for strong rules, while Germany and the Netherlands initially resisted, fearing loss of control over their own gas storage and supply contracts.

The decisive break came in April 2015 when the European Council — under pressure from Eastern members — explicitly endorsed a "solidarity clause" for gas security as part of the Energy Union strategy. By 2016, the Commission's impact assessment estimated that EU countries had between them 334 billion cubic metres of gas storage capacity but that capacity was unevenly distributed: countries like Latvia could store 100% of annual demand, while Greece and Portugal had near-zero storage. The assessment warned that without coordination, a severe January cold snap combined with a Russian cutoff could leave 12 countries unable to supply households for more than two weeks.

Opposition came heavily from industry associations. Gas Infrastructure Europe (GIE), representing storage operators, argued that mandatory storage filling targets would force companies to buy gas at peak summer prices, raising consumer costs by an estimated €2-5 billion annually. The German industry association BDI warned the solidarity mechanism could violate commercial contract law. The UK, in 2016 during the Brexit referendum campaign, signalled it would opt out of any solidarity mechanism that might force it to ship its North Sea gas to continental Europe.

The final regulation emerged after 18 months of tense trilogue negotiations between Council and Parliament. The key compromise: solidarity agreements would be voluntary in form but mandatory in principle — if a country lacked a bilateral agreement, the Commission could impose "default solidarity rules" during an emergency. Storage mandates were watered down from fixed filling targets to "best-effort" obligations, a victory for industry lobbyists.

## Timeline

- **January 2009** — Russia cuts gas flows through Ukraine for 20 days; 18 EU members affected. Slovakia declares a state of emergency.
- **October 2010** — Original Gas Security Regulation (994/2010/EU) adopted, requiring national risk assessments and emergency plans.
- **June 2014** — Russia cuts gas to Ukraine over a debt dispute, stoking fears of another winter cutoff.
- **February 2015** — European Council endorses Energy Union strategy including solidarity principle for gas.
- **February 2016** — European Commission publishes proposal for revised regulation (COM/2016/52), introducing solidarity mechanism and regional risk assessments.
- **April 2017** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement after trilogue negotiations.
- **November 2017** — Regulation (EU) 2017/1938 published in Official Journal, entering into force 20 days later.
- **November 2018** — Deadline for EU countries to submit national risk assessments and emergency plans to Commission.
- **September 2022** — In response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine and gas supply cuts, Commission proposes mandatory 80% storage fill target (adopted as Regulation 2022/1854/EU), temporarily overriding the previous voluntary approach.
- **February 2023** — EU achieves 56% storage fill target by summer ahead of schedule, avoiding worst-case winter shortages.
- **October 2023** — Commission reports that 20 member states have concluded bilateral solidarity agreements, with 7 still pending.
- **March 2024** — Commission proposes revision of the regulation to make storage filling obligations permanent and add binding demand-reduction targets.

## Impact and consequences

The 2017 regulation dramatically reshaped how EU countries prepare for gas emergencies. By 2019, the Commission had approved 24 group-level risk assessments covering all member states, replacing the previous patchwork of national plans that ignored cross-border dependencies. The "Protected Customers" concept — households, hospitals, schools — became a binding legal category, meaning that during the 2022 crisis, EU countries could not cut gas to these customers even under extreme conditions.

The real test came in 2022. When Russia cut flows through Nord Stream 1 from 53 million cubic metres per day in June to zero by September, the solidarity mechanism was invoked for the first time. Germany activated its emergency plan in June 2022 at the "alert" phase. Finland reported in May that Russia stopped supplies after it refused to pay in roubles. Bulgaria and Poland were cut off entirely in April 2022.

The Commission's 2022 winter preparedness measures, building on this regulation, required member states to fill storage to 80% by November 2022. Against expectations, the EU reached 95% storage fill by November, largely through coordinated purchasing and demand reduction that cut gas use in the EU by 18% compared to the previous five-year average (Eurostat data). Germany alone spent €13.4 billion on its storage filling programme, using the regulatory framework of the 2017 regulation to justify emergency state intervention in gas markets.

However, the regulation's weakness was exposed in the winter of 2022-23. Its voluntary storage provisions proved insufficient — the 2022 crisis forced the EU to adopt temporary mandatory targets under Regulation 2022/1854/EU, which required member states to fill storage to 80% by November 2022 and 90% by November 2023. The Commission's March 2024 proposal for a permanent revision would make these storage obligations binding, and add binding 15% demand-reduction targets — lessons directly drawn from the 2022-23 crisis.

## Key questions answered

### Can one EU country be forced to send its gas to another during a crisis?

Yes, under the solidarity mechanism. If a country faces a severe gas shortage threatening its "protected customers" (households, hospitals, schools), it can demand gas from neighbouring member states. The supplying country must prioritise this request over its own non-protected customers — including industry. Solidarity must be agreed bilaterally, but the Commission can impose default rules if no agreement exists. This was tested in 2022 when several countries invoked the mechanism as Russia cut supplies.

### Does every EU country have to maintain gas storage?

Not directly under this regulation — originally, the 2017 version only required countries to ensure they could meet peak demand, with storage as one option alongside LNG terminals and pipeline diversity. But after Russia's 2022 cuts, the EU adopted temporary mandatory fill targets (80% by November 2022, 90% by November 2023) under separate legislation (2022/1854/EU). The Commission's 2024 proposal would make these storage requirements permanent for all member states that have storage capacity.

### What happens if a country ignores the emergency planning requirements?

The Commission can issue a formal opinion demanding corrective action, and ultimately open infringement proceedings before the European Court of Justice. In practice, the Commission has used political pressure and the threat of financial penalties through the European Energy Community — in 2019, it formally requested 12 member states to submit missing emergency plans, and seven complied within three months. No fines have been imposed specifically under this regulation, but the credibility of enforcement increased significantly after 2022.

### Why did the EU need such detailed rules — wasn't the 2010 regulation enough?

The 2010 regulation gave countries too much flexibility. It allowed them to keep their risk assessments secret, had no mechanism for cross-border coordination, and critically lacked a solidarity mechanism. The 2014 Russian annexation of Crimea and subsequent gas cuts to Ukraine revealed that a purely national approach left the EU dangerously fragmented. The Baltic countries, which had no storage and only one gas source — Russia — were especially vulnerable. The 2017 regulation was designed to create a genuine security union, not just a collection of national emergency plans.
