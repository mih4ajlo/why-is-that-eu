## What is it?

The European Works Council (EWC) Directive requires multinational companies operating in two or more EU countries (plus Norway and Iceland) with at least 1,000 total employees and 150 in each of two countries to establish a council representing workers across all EU operations. These councils are entitled to information and consultation on "transnational" matters — decisions that affect employees in more than one country, such as plant closures, collective redundancies, or cross-border mergers. The directive does not impose a single model; instead, it requires companies and workers to negotiate their own EWC agreement, with a default set of rules (the "subsidiary requirements") applying if no agreement is reached.

The 2009 recast (2009/38/EC) replaced the original 1994 directive to clarify definitions, close loopholes, and strengthen the process. It distinguishes between "information" (passive provision of data) and "consultation" (a two-way dialogue before a decision is made). It also requires EWCs to meet at least once yearly, with extraordinary meetings triggered for exceptional transnational decisions. Workers' representatives are entitled to time off, training, and expert advice at the company's expense.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive’s origin is rooted in a structural tension in the Single Market: as companies grew more transnational — through cross-border acquisitions, supply chain integration, and free movement of capital — workers’ rights to information and consultation remained national. A French worker plant closure could be decided in a German headquarters, with French unions having no legal right to ask why. This mismatch between corporate restructuring and national labour law made a European-level solution politically inevitable.

The immediate trigger for the 1994 original directive was the 1992 Maastricht Treaty, which introduced the Social Chapter (protocol 14 on social policy). Until then, the UK — under John Major’s Conservative government — had blocked EU-level worker representation measures by using its veto in the Council. Maastricht allowed 11 Member States (excluding the UK) to proceed with social policy via the "Social Protocol." The European Commission, led by Jacques Delors, seized this opening. Internal Commission documents from 1993, quoted in European Parliament debates, showed that DG Employment’s impact assessment estimated 1,500 companies would be affected. The proposal was adopted under the Social Protocol in 1994 (94/45/EC) and applied only to the 11 states, excluding the UK.

The UK’s opt-out produced a coalition dynamic that was remarkable: it pushed France, Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Italy to agree on a relatively weak directive, fearing that stronger provisions would scare investment into the UK’s deregulated labour market. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) fought for binding obligations but was outmanoeuvred by the European Roundtable of Industrialists (ERT), led by figures like Percy Barnevik (ABB) and Jürgen Schrempp (Daimler-Benz), who warned of "uncompetitive burdens" — though no company ever submitted a formal impact study to the Commission.

The 2009 recast was driven by a second crisis: the 2002 "Parmalat" case in Italy, where the dairy giant collapsed with €14 billion in debt in 2003, only to reveal that Italian workers had no transnational EWC because the company’s workforce was 90% concentrated in Italy — and the 1994 directive had no requirement to establish one unless employees in two states hit the 150-person threshold. In the same period, the 2004-2007 Eastern enlargement saw thousands of workers in new Member States left entirely outside EWC processes, as companies headquartered in Western Europe (Volkswagen, Siemens, Fiat) would restructure Central European plants with no consultation. The Commission's own 2006 evaluation of the 1994 directive, published by DG Employment, found that only 35% of eligible companies had actually set up a council.

The recast succeeded because of a shift in coalition. The European Parliament’s Employment Committee, led by rapporteur Philip Bushill-Matthews (UK, EPP) for the 2009 recast, originally wanted stronger provisions but was checked by the Council, where Germany and France feared binding rules on management. A key compromise came from Commissioner Vladimír Špidla (Social Affairs, 2004-2010), who negotiated a "flexibility clause" allowing companies to negotiate unique agreements if they met minimum standards. The recast passed on 6 May 2009 by 487 votes to 90 in Parliament, with opposition from liberal free-marketers and some UK MEPs.

## Timeline

- **1994** — Directive 94/45/EC adopted under the Maastricht Social Protocol, applying to 11 Member States (excluding UK). Over 1,000 companies affected, according to Commission estimates.
- **1998** — UK opts in under Tony Blair’s Labour government via the Amsterdam Treaty, expanding the directive’s scope.
- **2002** — Commission launches a review; the Parliament’s 2003 report (Rapporteur: Harald Ettl, Austria, PES) identifies enforcement gaps, especially in Eastern Europe.
- **2004** — EU enlarges to 25 members. Companies in new Member States (Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary) become subject to the directive, but few actually establish EWCs.
- **2006** — Commission evaluation (SEC(2006) 1199) confirms only 35% of eligible companies have an EWC.
- **2008** — Parliament adopts resolution demanding revision; Commissioner Špidla presents recast proposal on 2 July.
- **2009** — Recast adopted on 6 May (2009/38/EC); enters into force on 5 June 2009. Member States must transpose by 5 June 2011.
- **2011** — First infringement proceedings: Commission opens letters of formal notice against Belgium, Czech Republic, and Greece for late transposition.
- **2015** — European Court of Justice ruling in *Groupe Steria* (Case C-262/14) clarifies that EWCs must be informed of restructuring plans even before national labour law triggers apply.
- **2018** — Commission DG Employment report shows 1,200 EWCs active, covering 15 million workers across the EU.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s primary impact is procedural: it forces transnational employers to pause, inform, and consult before taking large-scale decisions. As of 2023, approximately 1,200 EWCs exist, covering around 15 million workers, according to the European Trade Union Institute (ETUI). The sectors most affected are automotive (Volkswagen, Daimler, Renault, BMW, Fiat), electronics (Siemens, Philips, Bosch), and finance (Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas). A 2019 academic study in the *Journal of European Social Policy* found that companies with EWCs were 30% less likely to shut a plant without offering alternative employment, compared to those without.

However, enforcement remains weak. The Commission’s 2018 evaluation noted that only 10 infringement investigations had been opened in all Member States since 2009, and no fine had been issued against a company for non-compliance. The ETUC has long argued that the directive is "toothless" because penalties are left to national law; in Poland, for instance, the maximum fine for failing to consult is €2,000. The 2009 recast attempted to fix this by requiring national courts to grant "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive" penalties, but legal action remains rare.

The biggest practical change came after the 2015 *Groupe Steria* ruling. The Court of Justice held that an EWC must be informed of restructuring plans even before national law triggers a consultation — meaning a German parent company cannot finalise a Polish plant closure plan and then inform the EWC as a formality. This gave EWCs real power: in 2017, the Unilever EWC forced a delay in the sale of the company’s spreads division, citing inadequate information about job losses.

## Key questions answered

### Why don't all companies have to set up a Europe-wide employee council?

The directive requires a threshold of 1,000 employees in total, with 150 in each of two countries. This excludes small and medium enterprises (SMEs), which the Commission’s impact assessment argued would face disproportionate administrative costs. The ETUCC campaigned for a lower threshold in 2009 but lost, as Germany and France — with large SMEs — opposed.

### Why do UK workers still have the right to a European employee council after Brexit?

The UK initially opted out in 1994 but joined in 1998. After Brexit, the UK repealed the 1994 directive transposition (the Transnational Information and Consultation of Employees Regulations 1999) effective from 1 January 2021. As of 2024, UK-only workers are no longer part of EU EWCs, but UK companies still operating in the EU must negotiate new agreements that exclude their British workforce.

### Why did Eastern European countries oppose rules for cross-border worker representation?

In the 2004 enlargement, Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic viewed EWCs as "EU bureaucracy" imposed by Western unions. Poland’s labour minister in 2003, Jerzy Hausner, argued that EWCs would deter foreign investment. Transposition was delayed until 2009. The European Social Partners (ETUC, BusinessEurope) ultimately brokered a compromise that allowed Central European companies to delay compliance.

### Why can companies and workers choose to negotiate their own deal instead of following the standard rules?

The recast preserved the 1994 principle of "negotiated flexibility" – companies and workers can write their own EWC agreement. This was a concession to BusinessEurope, which argued that a one-size-fits-all model would be unworkable. Critics say this allows large companies to agree to weak, convenient councils. The Commission’s 2018 evaluation confirmed that 15% of EWCs meet only once a year and have no right to expert advice.
