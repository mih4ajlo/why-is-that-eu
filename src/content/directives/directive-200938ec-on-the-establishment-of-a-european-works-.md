## What is it?

Directive 2009/38/EC requires every multinational operating in the European Economic Area with at least 1,000 employees in the EEA and at least 150 employees in each of two or more Member States to establish a European Works Council (EWC) — or an equivalent information and consultation procedure — at the request of workers or management. The EWC is a standing forum of employee representatives drawn from across the company's European operations. Management must brief it on the group's economic situation, restructuring plans, mergers, mass redundancies, and any other cross-border developments likely to affect workers.

The 2009 recast replaced the original 1994 directive (94/45/EC). It tightened the definitions of "information" and "consultation", clarified that consultation must take place at a time, in a manner and with content that allows the EWC to actually influence decisions, gave EWC members an explicit right to training paid by the employer, and obliged national and European trade union federations to be notified when negotiations to set up a council begin. It also clarified the responsibility of "central management" and made clear that EWCs and national-level information bodies must be linked.

Roughly 1,000 European Works Councils were operating across the EU when the recast was adopted, covering several million workers in firms such as Volkswagen, Unilever, Airbus, Siemens and Nestlé — figures regularly cited by the European Trade Union Institute (ETUI), which maintains the EWC database on behalf of the European Trade Union Confederation.

## Why was it introduced?

On 27 February 1997, Renault's chief executive Louis Schweitzer announced in Paris that the company's profitable Vilvoorde assembly plant in Belgium, employing more than 3,000 workers, would close. Belgian managers, Belgian unions and the existing Renault European group works council learned the news from the press. The plant had been built by Renault in 1926; the closure was decided without any prior consultation of the European-level body Renault had voluntarily set up under French law two years earlier.

The political reaction was immediate and pan-European. Tens of thousands marched in Brussels in a "Euro-demonstration" on 16 March 1997. Belgian Prime Minister Jean-Luc Dehaene and French Prime Minister Alain Juppé both publicly condemned Renault. A Belgian labour court in Brussels ruled in April 1997 that Renault had breached Belgian information-and-consultation law; a French court in Nanterre ruled in the same period that the company had violated the 1994 EWC directive by failing to consult its European group works council before taking the decision. Those were the first major court rulings under directive 94/45/EC, and they exposed a structural weakness: the directive obliged companies to "inform and consult", but did not define those terms with enough precision to force consultation *before* a decision was final. Renault closed the plant anyway in July 1997.

Vilvoorde turned EWCs from a niche social-policy file into a political imperative. The Commission, then under Jacques Santer, immediately announced a review of directive 94/45/EC. Progress stalled, however, throughout the early 2000s: employer federation UNICE (now BusinessEurope) resisted reopening the directive, arguing that EWCs were a young institution that needed time to bed in, while ETUC demanded a full overhaul. In April 2008 Commissioner for Employment Vladimír Špidla formally launched a second-stage consultation of the social partners under Article 138 TEC. When the social partners failed to open autonomous negotiations, the Commission tabled a recast proposal in July 2008. The European social partners — ETUC, BusinessEurope, CEEP and UEAPME — then produced a joint advice in August 2008 identifying points where they agreed the text could be improved, and several of their suggestions were incorporated by the Parliament and Council.

Three further pressures aligned in 2008–2009 to break the deadlock. First, the financial crisis triggered a wave of cross-border restructuring announcements (in automotive, steel and banking), reviving the political salience of Vilvoorde-type failures. Second, a body of case law — including rulings of national courts in cases involving Marks & Spencer's 2001 announcement of store closures across continental Europe and Alstom's restructurings — had repeatedly shown that "consultation after the fact" was the norm rather than the exception. Third, the French Presidency of the Council in the second half of 2008 pushed the file as a priority. The Parliament adopted the recast at first reading in December 2008 and the Council followed shortly after; the directive entered into force on 5 June 2009 with a transposition deadline of 5 June 2011.

## Timeline

- **1994** — Directive 94/45/EC adopted under the Maastricht Social Protocol; the UK initially opted out.
- **27 February 1997** — Renault announces closure of the Vilvoorde plant without prior consultation of its European group works council.
- **March–April 1997** — Mass demonstrations in Brussels; Belgian and French courts rule Renault breached information and consultation obligations.
- **1997** — UK Labour government ends the Social Chapter opt-out; directive 97/74/EC extends 94/45/EC to the UK.
- **2000** — Commission publishes its first implementation report, identifying gaps in definitions and enforcement.
- **April 2008** — Commissioner Špidla opens second-stage social-partner consultation.
- **July 2008** — Commission proposes the recast directive.
- **August 2008** — ETUC, BusinessEurope, CEEP and UEAPME issue a joint advice.
- **16 December 2008** — European Parliament adopts the recast at first reading.
- **6 May 2009** — Directive 2009/38/EC signed.
- **5 June 2009** — Entry into force.
- **5 June 2011** — Transposition deadline in Member States.
- **2018** — Commission evaluation under the REFIT programme finds the directive working but flags enforcement and sanctions as weak points.
- **2023** — European Parliament adopts a legislative-initiative resolution (Radtke report) calling on the Commission to revise the directive again, particularly on sanctions and access to justice.
- **January 2024** — Commission tables a proposal to amend the directive, addressing sanctions, the definition of transnational matters and confidentiality.

## Impact and consequences

According to the ETUI's EWC database, well over 1,000 European Works Councils are active today, covering several million workers in multinationals headquartered both inside and outside the EU (US, Swiss, Japanese and Korean groups with sufficient European workforces are caught). The recast significantly increased the share of agreements that explicitly cite tighter definitions of information and consultation: the Commission's 2018 evaluation reported that most new or renegotiated agreements signed after 2011 incorporated the directive's stronger definitions, though many older "Article 13"/"Article 14" agreements remain in force unchanged.

Enforcement, however, has remained the directive's persistent weakness. Sanctions are set by Member States and vary enormously — from symbolic fines in some jurisdictions to court injunctions in France and Germany capable of suspending corporate decisions. Litigation has clustered in France, Belgium, Germany and the Netherlands. High-profile cases have included disputes at Gaz de France-Suez, Visteon, Nokia and, more recently, restructurings at Caterpillar's Gosselies plant in Belgium in 2016 — which Belgian unions and commentators explicitly compared to Vilvoorde almost twenty years on. The Commission's 2018 REFIT evaluation concluded that the directive had produced a stable institutional framework but that confidentiality clauses, weak national sanctions and the cost of litigation for worker representatives continued to limit its bite — findings that underpinned the Commission's January 2024 proposal for a further revision.

## Key questions answered

### Can a European multinational close a factory without telling its workers first?

Not legally, if it has a European Works Council and the closure affects workers in at least two Member States. The company must inform and consult the EWC at a time and in a manner that allows employee representatives to express an opinion that can still influence the decision. The Renault Vilvoorde case in 1997, where 3,000 Belgian workers learned of their plant's closure from the press, is precisely what the rules — and the 2009 recast — were designed to prevent.

### Do EWC rules apply to American or Japanese companies operating in Europe?

Yes. The trigger is the workforce in the EEA, not the location of the parent company. Any group employing at least 1,000 workers in the EEA, with at least 150 in each of two or more Member States, falls within scope — meaning US firms like Ford, IBM and McDonald's, Japanese firms like Toyota, and Swiss groups like Nestlé all run European Works Councils. The directive designates a "representative agent" in the EU when the parent is based abroad.

### What happens if a company ignores its European works council?

That depends on the country: sanctions are national, and they range from modest administrative fines to court orders suspending corporate decisions. French and Belgian courts have been the most willing to grant injunctions; in other Member States, penalties are widely regarded as too low to deter large multinationals. This enforcement gap is the central reason the Commission tabled a further revision in January 2024.

### Why were these rules tightened in 2009 instead of 1997, right after Renault?

Because the Commission initially hoped that the courts and social partners would close the gaps in the 1994 directive on their own, and because employer federation UNICE/BusinessEurope resisted reopening the text throughout the early 2000s. It took a decade of unsatisfactory case law, a series of further high-profile restructurings, and a determined French Council Presidency in 2008 to assemble the political coalition — Commissioner Vladimír Špidla, the Parliament and most national governments — needed to push the recast through.
