## What is it?

The Medical Devices Regulation (MDR) is the EU's legal framework governing the design, clinical evaluation, certification, and post-market surveillance of all medical devices sold in the European Union — from hip replacements and pacemakers to surgical gloves and in-vitro diagnostic tests. It replaced two older directives: the Medical Devices Directive (93/42/EEC) and the Active Implantable Medical Devices Directive (90/385/EEC).

Under the MDR, manufacturers must provide far more rigorous clinical evidence to demonstrate safety and performance before a device can receive CE marking and be placed on the market. High-risk devices (Class III and certain Class IIb implantables) now face mandatory scrutiny by special expert panels before certification. All devices must be registered in the European Database on Medical Devices (EUDAMED), which gives regulators and the public access to safety alerts, clinical investigation summaries, and certificates.

The regulation imposed stricter obligations on "notified bodies" — the private, for-profit organisations that audit and certify medical devices — requiring them to employ sufficient qualified specialists and be subject to joint audits by national authorities and the European Commission. It also introduced a new role: the "person responsible for regulatory compliance" within each manufacturer.

## Why was it introduced?

The MDR was a direct consequence of the Poly Implant Prothèse (PIP) breast implant scandal — one of the largest public health frauds in European medical history. Between 2001 and 2010, the French company PIP manufactured and sold breast implants containing unauthorised industrial-grade silicone, not the medical-grade silicone their CE certificate required. When the implants ruptured — at rates far higher than legitimate devices — they caused severe inflammation, chronic pain, and required repeat surgeries. The French health agency AFSSAPS estimated that over 400,000 women worldwide had received PIP implants, including an estimated 30,000 to 40,000 in the UK alone, according to a 2012 UK government review chaired by Sir Bruce Keogh.

The scandal revealed a catastrophic regulatory failure. PIP's CE certificate had been issued and maintained by TÜV Rheinland, a German private notified body that was authorised to certify medical devices under the existing directives. Investigators found that TÜV Rheinland had not conducted unannounced factory inspections, had accepted falsified documentation from PIP without verification, and had continued to recertify the company's devices even after a Spanish regulator, in 2006, raised the alarm that PIP implants were rupturing at abnormally high rates. A 2012 French Senate report by Senator Alain Milon described the system as one of "structural weakness" where manufacturers could "shop around" for lenient notified bodies across EU borders.

The European Parliament launched a special inquiry. In its 2013 report, MEPs were scathing: the existing directives had allowed "regulatory competition" between notified bodies that drove down standards, and the Commission had known about systemic problems since at least 2005 but failed to act. The 2013 Parliament report explicitly named the pressures that had blocked reform: a powerful medical devices industry lobbied through MedTech Europe, which argued that tighter rules would slow innovation and hurt European competitiveness against the US and China.

Coalition dynamics were unusual. The scandal created a rare alignment between patient safety NGOs (like BEUC, the European Consumers' Organisation), women's health groups, several national health ministries (particularly France, Germany, and the UK), and — crucially — the European Parliament's centre-left and liberal blocs. Industry positions fractured: large manufacturers with established reputations saw an opportunity to squeeze out smaller competitors by raising compliance costs, while the SME-heavy sector of device makers (the Commission's own 2010 impact assessment noted that 95% of medical device companies were SMEs) warned of "devastating" costs. The Parliament's final text, adopted in April 2017 by a large majority (449 to 113), was significantly tougher than the Council's original position.

Timing mattered. The scandal broke in 2010 at the peak of the post-financial crisis anti-deregulation mood. The first draft of the MDR was published by the Barroso II Commission in September 2012, with Health Commissioner Tonio Borg making it a personal priority. The final text was agreed in 2016 under the Juncker Commission, with Commissioner for Health and Food Safety Vytenis Andriukaitis pushing hard for transparency provisions.

## Timeline

- **2001–2010** — PIP manufactures and sells breast implants using unauthorised industrial silicone, certified by TÜV Rheinland
- **2010** — French health authorities discover the fraud; global recall begins; hundreds of thousands of women affected
- **2012** — French Senate report (Milon report) published, calling the device certification system "structurally weak"
- **September 2012** — European Commission publishes first draft of MDR proposal, alongside proposal for In Vitro Diagnostics Regulation
- **2013** — European Parliament inquiry report published, condemning "regulatory competition" and naming notified bodies as the weakest link
- **March 2013** — European Parliament votes in favour of significantly tougher oversight than Commission proposal
- **2014–2016** — Trilogue negotiations; industry (MedTech Europe) lobbies against mandatory scrutiny of high-risk devices; Parliament resists Council attempts to water down provisions
- **April 5, 2017** — European Parliament adopts MDR (449 votes in favour, 113 against, 26 abstentions)
- **May 25, 2017** — MDR enters into force, with a three-year transition period
- **May 26, 2020** — Original application date; delayed due to COVID-19 pandemic
- **May 26, 2021** — MDR becomes fully applicable (original deadline)
- **2022** — Commission reports that notified bodies and manufacturers are struggling to meet deadlines; many legacy devices lose certification, creating shortages in patient-critical areas
- **March 2023** — Commission amends transition periods to phase out legacy devices more gradually, following warnings from the EMA and national hospitals about shortages

## Impact and consequences

The MDR's most immediate impact was a dramatic reduction in the number of notified bodies. In 2018, there were over 80 notified bodies under the old directives across the EU. By 2023, only 39 had been designated under the MDR, according to the Commission's public list. Some of the largest — including TÜV Rheinland, which had certified PIP — initially failed the new stricter designation criteria and had to be reassessed.

The cost of compliance soared. A 2021 survey by MedTech Europe reported that its members spent an average of €13.5 million per company on MDR-related compliance, with smaller firms hit disproportionately. The Commission's own 2022 report noted that 400–500 medical device patents were being withdrawn annually because manufacturers could not afford the new clinical evidence requirements.

The transition was chaotic. By May 2021, thousands of devices certificated under the old directives had not yet been re-certified under the MDR. The UK's Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) warned in 2022 that some devices had "completely disappeared from the European market." Paediatric devices, rare-disease technologies, and custom-made implants were particularly affected. The European Society of Cardiology formally complained that several CE-marked heart devices were no longer available.

National health systems scrambled. Germany, France, and the Netherlands introduced national provisions to allow continued use of non-MDR-certified devices for "public health reasons" — a move critics said undermined the regulation's purpose.

The PIP scandal's direct victims received little compensation from the EU system. A French court in 2013 sentenced PIP founder Jean-Claude Mas to four years in prison. TÜV Rheinland reached a settlement with French victims in 2019, after a decade of litigation, but the company admitted no liability. The MDR did not create a victim compensation fund — something the Parliament's 2013 report had called for but industry successfully blocked.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take a breast implant scandal to overhaul medical device safety in Europe?

Europe's old device directives were built on voluntary standards and self-declaration by manufacturers, with oversight outsourced to private, for-profit companies (notified bodies). The system assumed that industry could regulate itself. PIP implants were certified by TÜV Rheinland — a German company that received fees from PIP — and those certifications were accepted by all EU countries without question. The fraud continued for nearly a decade because no national regulator had the authority or resources to inspect a certified manufacturer in another country. The scandal directly proved that the system's core assumption was false.

### Does the MDR require more testing than the old rules?

Yes, substantially more. Under the old directives, manufacturers could claim "equivalence" to an existing device to avoid conducting new clinical investigations — a loophole often exploited. The MDR requires manufacturers of implantable and Class III devices to conduct clinical investigations unless they can demonstrate that using existing clinical data is scientifically justified. For the first time, high-risk devices must be reviewed by independent expert panels before certification. The regulation also requires manufacturers to show not just safety but "clinical performance" — meaning a device must actually do what it claims.

### Can a device still be certified if it doesn't meet MDR requirements?

No — and that is the point. But the transition was messy. Because notified bodies were so slow to be designated, and because certification processes took 12–18 months, many perfectly safe legacy devices (including essential items like defibrillators, hip implants, and surgical instruments) lost their certificates temporarily. The Commission was forced to introduce grace periods and phase-out extensions in 2023 to avoid shortages. Critics like the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) argued the Commission was reopening dangerous loopholes — the Commission insisted the delays were procedural, not about safety.

### Are there still problems with EU medical device regulation today?

Yes, multiple. As of 2024, EUDAMED — the central public database meant to provide full transparency — still has not been fully implemented. Only its first two modules (actor registration and device registration) are operational; the modules for clinical investigations and vigilance are delayed. The MDR also did not address the fundamental profit incentive of notified bodies — they are still paid by the manufacturers they certify, creating the same conflict of interest as before. The number of notified bodies has stabilised but remains low, causing bottlenecks. Some patient groups and MEPs have called for a fully public, EU-run certification authority, but the Commission proposed only further reforms in its 2023 MDR evaluation, not a fundamental redesign.
