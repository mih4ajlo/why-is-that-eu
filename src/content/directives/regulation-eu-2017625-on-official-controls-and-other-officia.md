## What is it?

The Official Controls Regulation (OCR), formally Regulation (EU) 2017/625, is a comprehensive EU law that sets out the rules for how national authorities must plan, conduct, and report on official controls across the entire agri-food chain. It replaced a patchwork of earlier sector-specific regulations—most notably Regulation (EC) 882/2004 on feed and food controls and Regulation (EC) 854/2004 on products of animal origin—with a single, risk-based framework.

The OCR covers controls on food and feed safety, animal health and welfare, plant health, plant protection products (pesticides), organic production, and protected geographical indications. It requires member states to establish multi-annual national control plans, charge fees for certain controls (such as meat inspections) to ensure cost recovery, and provides the European Commission with stronger oversight, including audit powers and the ability to impose corrective actions. The regulation also set up the Administrative Assistance and Cooperation (AAC) system to improve cross-border information sharing, and introduced rules for official laboratories and border controls on imports.

## Why was it introduced?

The OCR was driven by a series of structural failures in the EU's food safety enforcement system, exposed most vividly by the 2013 horse meat scandal. In January 2013, Irish food safety authorities announced they had found horse DNA in beef burgers sold by major retailers, including Tesco, Burger King, and Aldi. The scandal quickly spread across the EU, revealing that horse meat labelled as beef had entered the supply chain through a complex web of traders in Romania, the Netherlands, Cyprus, and France, bypassing national control systems. The European Commission’s subsequent investigation found that national authorities had failed to share information and that controls were fragmented across different legal bases.

Structural tension within the existing system was acute. National enforcement was governed by Regulation (EC) 882/2004, which had been adopted in 2004 but did not cover all sectors uniformly. Rules for animal health and welfare controls remained in separate directives, and border controls on plant and animal products were handled under different regulations. The Commission's 2013 fitness check of the official controls framework, published by DG Health and Food Safety (then DG SANCO) under Commissioner Tonio Borg, concluded that the system was "fragmented, inconsistent, and insufficiently risk-based," according to the Commission’s own impact assessment.

The coalition that pushed the OCR through was led by the European Parliament's Environment, Public Health and Food Safety (ENVI) Committee, where rapporteur Karin Kadenbach (S&D, Austria) made enforcement reform a priority. Consumer organisations like BEUC and member states with robust food safety systems—notably Denmark, the Netherlands, and Ireland—supported stronger rules. The meat processing industry, represented by FoodDrinkEurope and the European Meat Association, initially warned of higher costs for mandatory fee charging but ultimately accepted the reform as a way to restore consumer trust. Opposition came from several eastern European countries—including Poland, Hungary, and Romania—who argued that the fee provisions would burden smaller producers and that the Commission’s audit powers infringed on national sovereignty. In Council negotiations, these states succeeded in watering down some provisions: final fees remained capped at cost-recovery levels rather than becoming fully harmonised, and the regulation included transitional periods for smaller slaughterhouses.

Timing was critical. The 2013 horse meat scandal had shattered consumer confidence, and subsequent contamination incidents—such as the 2011 E. coli outbreak from fenugreek sprouts in Germany (which killed 53 people) and the 2014 listeria outbreak in Denmark linked to contaminated pork—kept food safety high on the political agenda. The Juncker Commission, which took office in November 2014, made simplification of existing legislation a priority under its Better Regulation agenda. Commissioner for Health and Food Safety Vytenis Andriukaitis, a former Lithuanian health minister, personally championed the OCR as a "single, clear rulebook" during his 2014-2019 mandate.

What changed was the political will to override previous objections. Earlier attempts to consolidate rules—such as the 2010 proposal for a new food hygiene regulation—had stalled over disagreements on inspection fees and the role of private standards. The horse meat scandal broke that deadlock. European Parliament hearings in 2013 saw MEPs grill national authorities, and the EP’s own inquiry report, drafted by MEP Esther de Lange (EPP, Netherlands), called for mandatory origin labelling and stronger enforcement. The Commission’s 2013 legislative proposal for the OCR incorporated many of these demands, and after three years of negotiations, the regulation was adopted in March 2017 by a qualified majority in the Council, with Poland and Hungary voting against on the fee provisions.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Regulation (EC) 882/2004 on official feed and food controls enters into force, establishing a common framework but leaving sector-specific rules for animal health, plant health, and welfare.
- **2010** — European Commission launches a "fitness check" of the official controls system under DG SANCO, finding fragmentation and inconsistent enforcement.
- **January 2013** — Irish authorities discover horse DNA in beef burgers; the scandal spreads across the EU, revealing cross-border fraud.
- **2013** — European Parliament holds hearings and adopts a resolution (P7_TA(2013)0229) calling for stronger enforcement and a single regulatory framework.
- **May 2013** — Commission publishes its legislative proposal for the OCR (COM/2013/0265 final).
- **2014-2016** — Trilogue negotiations between Commission, Parliament, and Council. Key disagreements on fees, audit powers, and transitional periods for small businesses.
- **March 2017** — Regulation (EU) 2017/625 adopted by the European Parliament and Council. Vote: 589 in favour, 26 against (including Polish and Hungarian MEPs).
- **December 2019** — OCR enters into force for most provisions, replacing previous sectoral regulations.
- **April 2021** — New rules on border controls for imported products apply, requiring all consignments of animals and products of animal origin to enter via designated Border Control Posts (BCPs).
- **2022** — European Commission issues first report on member state implementation, noting persistent delays in BCP upgrades in Belgium, Germany, and the Netherlands.

## Impact and consequences

The OCR significantly changed how member states enforce food safety. Countries had to reorganise their inspection services to adopt a risk-based approach, focusing resources on higher-risk operators rather than routine visits. The Commission's 2022 report on implementation found that 22 of 27 member states had updated their multi-annual national control plans, but that smaller member states—particularly Malta, Luxembourg, and Cyprus—struggled with the cost of upgrading border control posts, with some delays lasting into 2023.

For industry, the largest impact was the mandatory charging of fees for official controls at slaughterhouses (a requirement that came into force in December 2019). The European Meat Association estimated that this increased costs for small slaughterhouses by 15-20% in some countries, according to industry statements cited in the Commission's 2021 impact assessment. The regulation also introduced stricter rules for organic producers: controls on organic operators must now be carried out at least once a year, up from previous risk-based intervals. The organic farming sector, represented by IFOAM Organics Europe, supported this as a way to protect organic integrity against fraud.

Cross-border cooperation improved through the AAC system, which in 2020 processed over 2,000 notifications of non-compliance between member states, according to the Commission's 2021 annual report. The horse meat scandal that had triggered the regulation resulted in fines and convictions: in 2017, a French court fined three companies (Spanghéro, TTD, and Comigel) a total of €500,000 for fraud; in 2021, a Romanian trader received a 3-year suspended sentence.

The OCR also gave the Commission stronger enforcement tools. In 2020, the Commission used its audit powers to conduct unannounced inspections in seven member states, identifying weaknesses in border control procedures. The most significant enforcement action came in 2022, when the Commission launched infringement proceedings against Poland for failing to implement the regulation's animal welfare control requirements at slaughterhouses, following the 2021 undercover investigation by animal welfare groups like Compassion in World Farming.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the horse meat scandal specifically trigger this regulation?

The scandal exposed fundamental flaws in the enforcement framework: controls were fragmented across different regulations (one for feed, another for animal products, another for fraud), and national authorities had no mechanism to share information across borders. The Commission's investigation found that horse meat had travelled through six member states before reaching UK shelves, but no single authority had the full picture. The scandal broke the political deadlock because consumer outrage was so widespread—polling by the European Commission in 2013 showed that 89% of EU citizens considered food safety a "very important" issue—forcing member states to accept stronger EU coordination.

### Why did some member states oppose the fee provisions?

Poland, Hungary, and Romania argued that mandatory fees for official controls would disproportionately burden small and medium-sized slaughterhouses and farms, which operate on thin margins. In Poland, the meat sector employs over 100,000 workers, mostly in small family-run operations. These countries also viewed fees as transferring costs from the national budget to producers, which they argued would undermine competitiveness within the single market. The compromise reached in the final text allowed member states to exempt small-scale slaughterhouses from fee charging by up to 40%, and introduced a transitional period until 2024 for those meeting certain criteria.

### How does the OCR differ from the previous Regulation (EC) 882/2004?

The OCR is broader in scope, covering not just food and feed safety but also animal health, animal welfare, plant health, plant protection products, organic production, and geographical indications. It introduced mandatory risk-based inspection frequency (replacing the previous discretion for national authorities), strengthened traceability requirements (including origin labelling for fresh, chilled, and frozen meat of pigs, sheep, goats, and poultry, introduced through a separate 2013 regulation), and created a formal system for administrative assistance and cooperation (AAC). The OCR also gave the Commission the power to conduct unannounced audits and to require corrective actions from member states, which was previously limited to recommendations.
