## What is it?

The 2023 recast of the Energy Efficiency Directive (EED) raises the EU's binding energy efficiency target from 32.5% to 11.7% final energy consumption reduction by 2030 compared to projections from 2020. This is a legal obligation, not a political aspiration: the target is now collectively binding on member states, and the European Commission has the power to assess national contributions and demand upward revisions.

The recast also introduces the "energy efficiency first" principle as a binding legal obligation in all policy and investment decisions across the energy system, rather than a voluntary guideline. Other key novelties include: a nearly doubled annual energy savings obligation (from 0.8% to 1.5% per year until 2030), a 1.9% annual energy consumption reduction requirement for the entire public sector, and a new requirement that large companies (with average annual energy consumption above a threshold) implement an energy management system.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for this recast was the 2021 energy price crisis and Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, which exposed the EU's structural vulnerability to fossil fuel supply shocks. Before the crisis, the 2018 EED (amended as part of the Clean Energy for All Europeans package) had set a non-binding 32.5% energy savings target by 2030. The Commission's 2020 impact assessment for the Climate Target Plan had already identified a gap: even achieving the existing target would leave the EU short of the 55% greenhouse gas reduction needed by 2030 under the European Green Deal.

The specific event that broke the previous political deadlock on binding targets was the 2022 energy crisis. In March 2022, the European Council's Versailles Declaration committed member states to reducing energy dependence on Russia. By April 2022, the Commission proposed a tightening of the efficiency target and binding obligations as part of the "REPowerEU" plan, explicitly linking energy efficiency to energy security. Commissioner for Energy Kadri Simson stated in the proposal's presentation: "Saving energy is the cheapest, safest and cleanest way to reduce our dependence on Russian gas."

The coalition that got this passed was the same "Fit for 55" majority: the Commission under Ursula von der Leyen, the European Parliament's centre-right to centre-left coalition (EPP, S&D, Renew), and a qualified majority of member states led by France, Germany, and Spain. The main opposition came from some Eastern member states (notably Poland and Hungary) who argued binding targets would impose disproportionate costs on coal-dependent economies, and from industry associations like BusinessEurope and European Aluminium who warned that mandatory energy management systems would create administrative burdens on small-to-medium enterprises.

The recast was adopted on 13 September 2023 after trilogue negotiations that ran from June 2022 to March 2023. The key compromise was increasing the target from the Commission's initial 9% proposal to 11.7% — a middle ground between the Parliament's demand for 14% and the Council's initial 9% position.

## Timeline

- **2012** — First Energy Efficiency Directive (2012/27/EU) adopted, setting a non-binding 20% energy savings target by 2020
- **2018** — Amending directive (2018/2002/EU) set 32.5% target by 2030, still non-binding
- **July 2020** — European Commission launches its "Fit for 55" package, proposing to upgrade the 2030 target
- **February 2022** — Russia's invasion of Ukraine sparks energy crisis, European gas prices spike by over 400% from 2021 levels according to the Commission
- **March 2022** — European Council's Versailles Declaration commits EU to ending reliance on Russian gas
- **April 2022** — Commission proposes REPowerEU plan, including accelerated EED revision with binding obligations
- **June 2022** — Commission releases formal recast proposal with 9% binding target
- **July 2023** — European Parliament (471 votes in favour, 147 against, 134 abstentions) and Council adopt the final text
- **13 September 2023** — Directive 2023/1791/EU enters into force
- **11 October 2025** — Member states must transpose the directive into national law
- **2027** — First deadline for member states to demonstrate compliance with the 1.5% annual savings obligation

## Impact and consequences

The recast directly affects EU governments, large corporations, and public institutions. For member states, the binding target means the Commission can now open infringement proceedings against those that do not submit adequate National Energy and Climate Plans (NECPs) showing how they will meet their contribution. In its 2023 assessment of draft NECPs, the Commission found that collectively, member states' planned contributions fell 5 percentage points short of the 11.7% target, requiring upward revisions from 13 of 27 countries.

For the public sector, the 1.9% annual energy consumption reduction requirement is a paradigm shift. Under the previous directive, public bodies were only subject to a general renovation obligation for 3% of central government buildings annually. The new obligation applies to all levels of public administration and covers total energy consumption, not just buildings.

The requirement for large companies to implement energy management systems has an estimated compliance cost of €3,000–€5,000 per company according to the Commission's impact assessment, but the same assessment projected energy savings of €200 million annually from this measure alone by 2030. Industry associations representing energy-intensive sectors (aluminium, steel, chemicals) argued that many of their members already operate such systems, but small-to-medium enterprises with 250-500 employees would face disproportionate costs.

Implementation has been uneven. According to the European Environment Agency's 2024 report, 14 member states had not yet fully transposed the directive by the October 2024 deadline, including Bulgaria, Romania, and Poland. The Commission has initiated pre-infringement dialogues with several of these countries.

## Key questions answered

### Does EU law force countries to save a specific amount of energy every year?

Yes. The Energy Efficiency Directive sets a binding target for the EU as a whole to reduce final energy consumption by 11.7% by 2030 compared to 2020 projections. Each member state must contribute through its National Energy and Climate Plan, though the law allows for flexibility: if the EU as a whole is off-track, the Commission can require individual countries to increase their contributions. The law also requires each country to achieve new annual energy savings of 1.5% of final energy consumption from 2025 through 2030.

### What does "energy efficiency first" mean in practice?

It means that when making investment, policy, or planning decisions, public authorities must consider energy efficiency alternatives before building new energy supply or infrastructure. For example, before approving a new gas power plant, a national regulator must assess whether demand-side measures like building insulation or industrial efficiency upgrades could achieve the same results at lower cost. This principle was previously voluntary; the recast makes it a binding legal requirement in all energy-related decisions.

### Are companies in Europe forced to monitor their energy use?

Large companies are. The directive requires any company with an average annual energy consumption above a threshold (the Commission is to define this by December 2024) to implement an energy management system certified to European standards such as ISO 50001. This covers energy audits, monitoring, and reduction targets in manufacturing, logistics, and buildings. Companies below the threshold are still subject to mandatory energy audits at least every four years.

### Can the EU fine a country for not saving enough energy?

Yes, potentially. The 11.7% target is legally binding at the EU level. If a member state fails to include adequate measures in its National Energy and Climate Plan, or fails to implement those measures, the European Commission can open infringement proceedings under Article 258 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the EU. This could ultimately lead to the Court of Justice of the EU imposing fines. However, the target is collective rather than individually binding on each country, meaning the Commission focuses on assessing and correcting national contributions rather than fining countries solely for missing the target.
