## What is it?

The Right to a Lawyer Directive (2013/48/EU) establishes a common minimum standard across the European Union: anyone arrested or detained must be allowed to consult a lawyer before any police interrogation and to have that lawyer present during questioning. The right applies from the moment a person is deprived of liberty — whether through arrest, detention, or surrender under a European Arrest Warrant (EAW). It also covers the right of the suspect to communicate confidentially with their lawyer, and to have their lawyer attend procedural steps such as identification parades or reconstruction of the crime scene.

The directive does not replace national laws that provide stronger protections; it only sets a floor. Member states may temporarily derogate from the right in exceptional circumstances — for example, if there is an urgent need to prevent serious prejudice to a criminal investigation — but even then the restriction must be proportionate, justified, and subject to judicial review. The EAW part of the directive ensures that a person arrested on a European arrest warrant has the same right to a lawyer in the executing state and in the issuing state, with a lawyer present during the surrender hearing.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was driven by a specific shock from Europe’s human rights court and by a structural hole in EU criminal law. In November 2008, the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) issued its landmark judgment in *Salduz v. Turkey*. A 17‑year‑old had been taken into police custody, questioned without a lawyer, and made incriminating statements that were later used to convict him. The ECtHR ruled unanimously that denying access to a lawyer during the first police interrogation violates Article 6 (right to a fair trial) of the European Convention on Human Rights. The judgment sent a clear signal to all 47 Council of Europe states, including the 27 member states of the EU: their existing systems were falling short.

At the time, EU law had no binding rules on the right to a lawyer in criminal proceedings. The EU had tried to adopt a Framework Decision on procedural rights in 2004, but it collapsed because the United Kingdom, Ireland, and several other member states refused to accept a common text — the UK argued that such matters should remain under national sovereignty. The attempt was formally withdrawn in 2005. The structural tension was clear: the EU was deepening police and judicial cooperation — the European Arrest Warrant had been in force since 2004 — but there was no corresponding floor of rights for the suspects and accused persons swept across borders.

The Lisbon Treaty, effective December 2009, changed the game. Article 82(2) TFEU gave the EU explicit competence to adopt minimum rules on procedural rights. The Stockholm Programme, adopted by the European Council in December 2009, revived the stalled agenda. Under the Barroso II Commission, Justice Commissioner Viviane Reding made procedural rights a priority. She presented a “Roadmap for strengthening procedural rights of suspected or accused persons” in 2009, which the Council endorsed. The Roadmap listed six measures, with the right to a lawyer as measure C.

In June 2011, the Commission proposed the directive. The European Parliament’s rapporteur in the LIBE committee was Jan Philipp Albrecht (Greens/EFA, Germany), who pushed for broad coverage including the right to communicate with a lawyer before any questioning, and for limits on derogations. The Parliament adopted its first‑reading position in October 2011. The Council, still containing sceptics, took two years to reach agreement. The UK and Ireland initially opted in to the negotiations under Protocol 21, eventually deciding to opt in; Denmark stayed out.

The directive was adopted on 22 October 2013. The key political trade‑off was between the Commission/Parliament’s ambition for strong, unconditional rights and member states’ demands for flexibility to restrict access in terrorism or organised crime cases. The final text allowed derogations but required them to be justified in writing and subject to judicial review. The timing — after Salduz, after Lisbon, and during a period of heightened terrorism fears — made the compromise possible.

## Impact and consequences

The directive compelled every member state (except Denmark) to review its criminal procedure. In countries where police questioning was routinely conducted without a lawyer — such as some post‑communist states with long pre‑trial detention traditions — the change was significant. Italy, for example, extended the right to all stages of investigation, including to persons under formal investigation before arrest. France amended its code of criminal procedure to allow a lawyer to be present from the start of police custody (garde à vue), reversing previous restrictions for certain offences.

Implementation, however, has been uneven. The European Commission’s 2019 report on the directive found that while all member states had transposed the text, many struggled with the guarantee of confidentiality of lawyer‑client communications. Several states also lacked effective remedies when the right was breached — the directive requires that evidence obtained in violation of the right to a lawyer must not be used to secure a conviction unless its use would not prejudice the overall fairness of the proceedings. The Commission launched infringement proceedings against Greece, Poland, and Malta for late or incomplete transposition.

Civil society organisations such as Fair Trials International and the European Criminal Bar Association welcomed the directive but have criticised the derogation clause, arguing that prosecutors can too easily justify excluding a lawyer. In its 2019 report, the Commission noted that the derogation powers were “rarely used” in practice, suggesting that most member states had absorbed the rule as the default.

The directive also affected European Arrest Warrant proceedings: a person facing surrender now must be granted a lawyer in the executing state and given the opportunity to consult a lawyer in the issuing state before the hearing. This has reduced the number of contested EAW cases where suspects complained about lack of representation.

## Key questions answered

### What are my rights to a lawyer when I’m arrested in an EU country?

From the moment you are arrested or detained in any EU member state (except Denmark), you have the right to consult a lawyer in private before the police start questioning you. Your lawyer can be present during all interrogations, during identification procedures, and during any reconstruction of events. You also have the right to have your lawyer notified of your arrest if you are unable to inform a relative yourself.

### Can the police question me without a lawyer if it’s an emergency?

Only in exceptional circumstances, such as an immediate risk of serious harm to someone or an urgent need to prevent the destruction of key evidence. Even then, the restriction must be specifically justified in writing, approved by a judge or other judicial authority, and as short as possible. Any statements you make under such a restriction cannot automatically be used as evidence; the court must assess whether admitting them would make the trial unfair.

### Does this right apply if I’m wanted in another EU country with a European Arrest Warrant?

Yes. The directive explicitly covers European Arrest Warrant proceedings. If you are arrested in one EU country on a warrant issued by another, you have the right to a lawyer in the country where you are arrested (the executing state) for the surrender hearing, and also the right to a lawyer in the country that issued the warrant (the issuing state) to advise you on the underlying proceedings. The lawyer in the executing state must have access to the relevant documents and be able to speak with you confidentially.

### Why did the EU adopt this directive instead of leaving it to national law?

The main reason was the gap created by the European Arrest Warrant. Before 2013, a person could be transferred from one EU country to another without any EU‑wide guarantee that they had had a lawyer in the first country. This created a two‑tier system: strong rights in some states, weak rights in others. The *Salduz* judgment showed that the European Convention alone was not enough to force harmonisation. After the Lisbon Treaty gave the EU the power to set minimum rules, the Commission and Parliament seized the moment to ensure that no one would be extradited or convicted within the EU without having had a real chance to speak to a lawyer.
