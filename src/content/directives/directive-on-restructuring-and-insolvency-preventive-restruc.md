## What is it?

The Preventive Restructuring Directive requires all EU member states to put in place a legal framework that allows a company in financial difficulty to restructure its debts — with creditors bound by a court-approved plan — **before** it becomes insolvent. Previously, in many countries the only option was a formal insolvency procedure that often led to liquidation instead of rescue.

The directive also mandates a **full discharge of debt for honest but over-indebted entrepreneurs** within a maximum of three years. This “second chance” provision is aimed at removing the stigma and legal barriers that prevent people from restarting a business after failure. Member states were required to transpose the directive into national law by July 2021.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive’s real trigger was not a single scandal but a **structural tension** inside the EU’s single market. By the 2010s it was clear that wildly divergent insolvency regimes were distorting cross-border investment. A viable German company that hit trouble could restructure under one set of rules; a similar Italian company could be forced into liquidation. Creditors in other member states could not reliably predict what would happen if a debtor failed. In its 2014 Communication on the Capital Markets Union, the Commission explicitly identified the absence of harmonised insolvency rules as a barrier to cross-border lending and investment.

The immediate political window opened under the Juncker Commission (2014–2019). Commissioner Věra Jourová, responsible for Justice, and Vice-President Frans Timmermans pushed the file as part of a broader “second chance” agenda. A preliminary step was the **2014 Commission Recommendation on a new approach to business failure and insolvency** — a non-binding text that tested the water. When few member states implemented it, the Commission concluded that only a binding directive would work.

The legislative proposal was tabled on **22 November 2016** (COM(2016)723). The European Parliament’s lead rapporteur was the Spanish MEP Enrique Guerrero Salom (S&D). The main fault line was over the duration of the debt discharge period. Several member states — notably Germany, Austria, and Finland — wanted to keep longer exclusion periods (up to seven years) for entrepreneurs they considered reckless. The Parliament pushed for a maximum of three years. In the final compromise, the directive sets three years but allows member states to extend to three years from a different starting point if they can justify it. The vote in the Parliament’s JURI committee was close: 15 in favour, 10 against, with the main opposition coming from the EPP and ECR groups, who argued that the rules would undermine creditor protection.

A secondary controversy was the **stay on individual enforcement actions**. The directive allows a debtor to obtain a temporary moratorium on debt collection while the restructuring plan is negotiated. The banking industry, represented by the European Banking Federation, warned that this could destabilise secured lending. In the end, secured creditors retained a veto over the stay in most cases.

Another specific incident that shaped the text was the **2017 Cobelfret case** (C‑138/16, CJEU). Although not directly about the directive, the Court of Justice ruled that a Belgian rule discriminating against foreign creditors in insolvency proceedings violated the free movement of capital. This gave the Commission a powerful legal precedent to argue that national insolvency regimes could no longer be left unharmonised.

During the trilogues, the position of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) was heavily debated. The Commission’s impact assessment had estimated that 50% of cross-border insolvencies involved SMEs, yet the cost of formal restructuring was often prohibitive for them. The directive therefore includes provisions for simplified procedures and online filing, a concession to SME associations like UEAPME that had lobbied for lower thresholds.

## Timeline

- **2014** — Commission publishes a non-binding Recommendation on a new approach to business failure and insolvency; most member states ignore it.
- **22 November 2016** — Commission proposes the Preventive Restructuring Directive as part of the Capital Markets Union package.
- **2017** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position; rapporteur Guerrero Salom secures a three-year maximum discharge period and stronger debtor protections.
- **June 2019** — Directive formally adopted by the Council and Parliament after trilogues.
- **17 July 2019** — Directive enters into force.
- **17 July 2021** — Transposition deadline. Only a handful of member states meet it.
- **2022–2023** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against 15 member states for late or incomplete transposition, including Germany, Poland, and Ireland.
- **2024** — Commission publishes a report on implementation, finding that most member states have now transposed but the quality of frameworks varies significantly.

## Impact and consequences

The directive has forced fundamental rewrites of insolvency law in several member states. **Poland**, for example, introduced a completely new restructuring law in 2020, creating four separate procedures. **Germany** amended its Insolvency Code (InsO) to introduce a preventive restructuring procedure, but only after a threat of infringement proceedings. According to a 2023 study by the European Law Institute, the transposition was incomplete in at least five member states two years after the deadline.

The “second chance” provision has had the most visible effect. In **Portugal**, the number of entrepreneurs obtaining a full discharge jumped from fewer than 200 per year before the directive to over 1,200 in 2023, according to the country’s insolvency statistics. In **Belgium**, the maximum discharge period was cut from seven to three years, leading to a surge in applications.

However, the directive has also been criticised for not going far enough. The **European Insolvency and Restructuring Association** noted in 2022 that the directive does not harmonise ranking of creditors, priority of claims, or avoidance actions — leaving the core differences that distort the single market in place. The Commission’s 2020 evaluation of the Business Statistics database (Eurostat) showed that only 8% of companies in the EU survive 10 years, with bankruptcy and insolvency a major cause, but it remains difficult to attribute changes directly to the directive given the COVID-19 pandemic’s distorting effect.

The banking sector adapted by tightening lending terms in some countries, fearing the new moratoriums would delay enforcement. The **European Banking Authority** found in a 2022 report that secured creditors in member states with creditor-friendly traditions (e.g., the Netherlands) had largely negotiated contractual safeguards outside the directive’s scope.

## Key questions answered

### Can a struggling business in the EU avoid bankruptcy by restructuring debts?

Yes, but the exact mechanism depends on the member state. The directive requires every country to offer at least one court-confirmed “preventive restructuring” procedure where a majority of creditors can bind the minority to a plan that cuts debt, converts it to equity, or extends maturities. The company must be viable — the restructing plan must be confirmed by a court only if it passes a “best interest of creditors” test. Many countries, including France and Luxembourg, already had such procedures; others, like Slovenia and Croatia, built them from scratch after 2019.

### Does every EU country give entrepreneurs a second chance after bankruptcy?

Not exactly — the directive sets a **maximum of three years** to obtain a full discharge of remaining debts after a bankruptcy procedure, but member states may exclude debts arising from fraud, gross negligence, or certain taxes. Practically, all 27 member states now have some form of early discharge, but the number of conditions and exclusions varies. For example, Austria excludes debts from gambling, while Germany only excludes intentional tort claims. The Commission has noted that claims that the three-year limit is too generous have not materialised — the vast majority of discharged entrepreneurs remain small traders, not large-scale fraudsters.

### What is a “pre-pack” and does the directive require it?

A pre-pack is a sale of a company’s business or assets arranged before formal insolvency proceedings begin. The directive does not require pre-packs outright, but it encourages member states to allow their use as part of a restructuring plan. Several countries (e.g., the Netherlands, the UK before Brexit) had developed pre-pack practice before the directive; others, like Italy, introduced explicit pre-pack rules during transposition in 2021. Critics say the directive misses the chance to harmonise pre-pack rules, leaving a patchwork that benefits forum shopping.

### How does the directive affect creditors in another EU country?

Creditors from other member states benefit from the same treatment as domestic creditors during restructuring — the directive prohibits discrimination based on the creditor’s seat or nationality. In practice, cross-border enforcement remains messy because the directive does not harmonise priority rules or avoidance actions. A secured creditor in Germany may still find that its collateral is subordinated to employee claims under Polish law if the debtor’s centre of main interest (COMI) is in Poland. The Commission has signalled that further harmonisation of substantive insolvency law is on the agenda for the 2025–2029 period.
