## What is it?

The Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) is the EU's dedicated funding instrument for large-scale infrastructure projects that link member states across borders. Established in 2013 and revised in 2021, it covers three sectors: transport (including the Trans-European Transport Network, TEN-T), energy (trans-European energy networks, TEN-E), and digital (cross-border broadband and 5G corridors). The regulation sets out the budget, eligibility rules, co-financing rates, and selection criteria for projects of common interest.

Under the 2021 regulation, CEF has a budget of €33.71 billion for 2021–2027, broken down into €25.81 billion for transport, €5.84 billion for energy, and €2.07 billion for digital (Article 5 of Regulation 2021/1153). The programme operates through calls for proposals managed by the European Climate, Infrastructure and Environment Executive Agency (CINEA) and the European Health and Digital Executive Agency (HaDEA) for digital actions. Co-financing rates can reach up to 85% for cross-border transport projects in cohesion countries and 50% for energy and digital projects.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2021 revision of the CEF was driven by four intersecting pressures: the European Green Deal's requirement to decarbonise transport and energy grids, the Digital Decade ambition to connect every European to gigabit networks, the post-pandemic recovery imperative, and persistent structural weaknesses in the 2014–2020 programme.

The original CEF, adopted under the Barroso II Commission in 2013, had a budget of €33.25 billion. Its main weakness, identified in the European Court of Auditors' 2018 special report, was the low absorption rate: only 72% of the transport budget had been committed by mid-2017, and cross-border rail projects faced chronic delays due to member states' inability to complete national sections. The Commission's 2018 mid-term evaluation also noted that the energy window had overly focused on gas infrastructure, while the digital window was underfunded relative to demand.

When the Juncker Commission' s successor, the von der Leyen Commission, took office in December 2019, it made the Green Deal its centrepiece. On 11 December 2019, President Ursula von der Leyen presented the European Green Deal Communication, which explicitly called for a "sustainable and smart mobility strategy" and a "clean energy transition." That same month, the Commission published a new TEN-T policy review, signalling that infrastructure funding would need to align with climate neutrality by 2050.

The initial Commission proposal for the 2021–2027 CEF, published on 6 June 2018 as part of the Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) package, requested €42.3 billion (in 2018 prices). This was quickly attacked by net-contributing member states, notably Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden, and Austria, who argued the budget increase was excessive. During the MFF negotiations in 2019–2020, the European Parliament pushed for a higher digital budget, while the Council sought overall cuts. A key unblocking moment came in July 2020, when the European Council agreed on an MFF of €1.074 trillion (including NextGenerationEU), and the CEF budget was settled at €33.71 billion – a 19% cut from the Commission's proposal but still above the original 2014–2020 programme.

The timing accelerated with the COVID-19 pandemic. In May 2020, the Commission's recovery plan, NextGenerationEU, emphasised investment in green and digital infrastructure. The CEF was positioned as a key vehicle for channelling funds. Adina Vălean, Commissioner for Transport, argued in a June 2020 speech that "the crisis makes cross-border connectivity more important, not less." Meanwhile, energy Commissioner Kadri Simson pushed for hydrogen and smart grid projects, and digital Commissioner Thierry Breton called for 5G corridors along major transport routes.

Environmental groups, notably Transport & Environment, criticised the original proposal for not excluding fossil fuel infrastructure from transport and energy eligibility. The final regulation introduced stronger sustainability criteria: energy projects must contribute to decarbonisation, and road projects are no longer eligible for CEF funding (except for smart charging points and safety upgrades). Gas infrastructure was restricted to projects that are compatible with the 2050 climate neutrality goal.

## Timeline

- **2013** — Original CEF Regulation (1316/2013) adopted under Barroso II with €33.25 billion for 2014–2020.
- **2016** — European Court of Auditors initiates an audit of CEF transport spending; publishes critical report in 2018.
- **June 2018** — European Commission publishes its proposal for the 2021–2027 CEF as part of the MFF package, requesting €42.3 billion.
- **December 2019** — von der Leyen Commission presents the European Green Deal, setting new climate objectives for EU infrastructure spending.
- **July 2020** — European Council agrees on the MFF envelope; CEF budget fixed at €33.71 billion.
- **April 2021** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council; regulation adopted by the European Parliament on 19 May 2021 by 576 votes to 22, with 79 abstentions.
- **14 July 2021** — Regulation (EU) 2021/1153 enters into force, with retroactive application from 1 January 2021.
- **2022** — First calls under the new CEF published, including dedicated 'Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Facility' (€1.5 billion) and 'Cross-border Digital Connectivity' (€200 million).
- **2023** — CINEA reports that CEF 2021–2023 has committed approximately 50% of the total transport budget.

## Impact and consequences

The 2021 regulation fundamentally shifted CEF's focus from bridging physical gaps to enabling the green and digital transitions. In transport, coalitions of member states and railway undertakings like Rail Baltica and the Lyon-Turin high-speed line benefited from higher co-financing rates. In energy, the new regulation prioritised smart grids, cross-border renewable energy projects, and hydrogen infrastructure, effectively ending support for new natural gas pipelines (except those with a clear decarbonisation path). The digital window, previously marginal, was expanded to fund 5G corridors along the TEN-T core network, with a target of seamless connectivity by 2025.

Industry reactions were mixed. The European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA) welcomed the alternative fuels infrastructure facility but warned that €1.5 billion was insufficient to meet the 2025 charging targets. The European association of electricity transmission system operators, ENTSO-E, praised the focus on smart grids but noted that the energy budget was too small to modernise the entire European grid — the Commission's own 2020 impact assessment estimated a need of €100 billion in grid investments by 2030.

Member states with lower absorption capacity, such as Romania, Bulgaria, and Greece, continued to face challenges in designing projects that met the higher sustainability criteria. The Commission introduced technical assistance measures, but the European Court of Auditors flagged in a 2022 review that project preparation remained a bottleneck.

By the end of 2023, CEF had funded over 500 projects across the three sectors. Notable examples include: the Spain-France interconnector (energy), the 5G Corridor on the Rotterdam-Frankfurt-Vienna axis (digital), and the Rail Baltica high-speed railway (transport). The programme's leverage effect, according to the Commission's 2023 annual activity report, attracted an additional €15.5 billion in private and national co-financing.

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU decide which cross-border infrastructure projects to fund?

Projects must be 'projects of common interest' (PCIs) identified through the TEN-T, TEN-E, or Digital Decade policy frameworks. Calls for proposals are evaluated by executive agencies (CINEA or HaDEA) based on European added value, maturity, and contribution to Green Deal and digital targets. Co-financing rates depend on project type and location, with cohesion countries eligible for higher shares.

### Why did the EU cut the CEF budget from the Commission's proposed €42 billion to €33.7 billion?

Net-contributing member states, led by Germany and the Netherlands, argued during the 2018–2020 MFF negotiations that the budget was too large, especially given low absorption rates in the previous programme. The European Council's July 2020 agreement imposed a 19% reduction, which the Parliament accepted only after securing more flexibility in transferring unspent funds between sectors.

### What is the link between the CEF and the European Green Deal?

The Green Deal set a target of climate neutrality by 2050, requiring a fundamental shift in how infrastructure is funded. The 2021 regulation made sustainability a core criterion: road projects are no longer eligible (except for recharging stations), energy projects must be consistent with the 2050 decarbonisation pathway, and digital projects must reduce connectivity gaps. Additionally, at least 60% of the CEF transport budget is reserved for projects that directly contribute to climate objectives, as stated in Article 7.

### Did the CEF include funding for gas infrastructure under the new rules?

Only under strict conditions. The 2021 regulation limits gas projects to those that are 'compatible with the decarbonisation objectives', meaning they must be capable of carrying hydrogen or biomethane and must replace higher-emission alternatives. No new pure natural gas pipelines are eligible. This was a significant change from the 2014–2020 programme, under which several major gas interconnectors were funded, including the Greece-Bulgaria Interconnector (IGB).
