## What is it?

The European Crowdfunding Service Provider (ECSP) Regulation, in force since November 2021, creates a single EU-wide passport for crowdfunding platforms. It replaces a patchwork of 27 national regimes that had forced platforms to seek separate licences in each member state. The regulation covers two types of crowdfunding: investment-based (selling securities like shares or bonds to investors) and lending-based (loans from multiple investors to businesses).

Platforms authorised under the ECSP Regulation can market their services across the entire EU using a single approval from their home-country regulator. The regulation caps individual investments at €5 million per project and imposes tiered disclosure rules — requiring platforms to provide standardised risk warnings and key investment information sheets. Platforms are banned from directly soliciting retail investors and must offer a "cooling-off period" of 14 days. As of early 2025, the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) maintains a public register of authorised platforms, numbering roughly 400.

## Why was it introduced?

The ECSP Regulation was born from the collapse of a single German crowdfunding platform — but the deeper failure was structural. By 2017, Europe had over 600 crowdfunding platforms, yet fewer than 5% operated in more than one member state, according to the European Commission's 2018 impact assessment. The reason: each country had its own licensing regime, disclosure rules, and investor caps, making cross-border expansion prohibitively expensive for small platforms.

The political trigger was the failure of the German platform **Companisto** in 2016 — not as a formal bankruptcy, but as a crisis of confidence. Companisto had raised €30 million for a single real estate project, then failed to deliver returns. German retail investors, many unaware of the risks, were left with worthless claims. German regulator BaFin responded by tightening national rules, but this only deepened the fragmentation — a French platform authorised in France could not easily raise money from German investors under BaFin's stricter regime.

The coalition behind the regulation was unusual. On one side stood the **European Commission** under Jean-Claude Juncker, whose Capital Markets Union project (launched in 2015) needed a concrete win to demonstrate the EU's relevance to small businesses. On the other side stood a coalition of small platforms — **Companisto**, **Seedrs**, and **Lendix** (later renamed October) — that had lobbied the Commission's DG FISMA since 2016. They formed the **European Crowdfunding Network** and submitted a joint letter in 2017 demanding a single passport, warning that without it, platforms would relocate to the UK or US.

The Commission's first draft in March 2018 proposed a voluntary regime — platforms could choose either a national or EU passport. The European Parliament under EPP rapporteur **Markus Ferber** pushed for a mandatory regime, arguing voluntariness would maintain fragmentation. The key vote in the Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON) in December 2018 was 34-14 in favour of mandatory rules, with the Greens and Socialists joining the EPP, opposed by some liberal MEPs who feared overregulation.

The **timing was critical**. The June 2016 Brexit referendum had removed the UK block — London's Platform Black and Crowdcube had been the most vocal opponents of EU harmonisation, preferring a competitive race to the bottom. With the UK leaving, German and French platforms became dominant voices. The 2018 European Parliament election campaign also forced the issue: the Commission needed to deliver on Capital Markets Union before the new Commission took office.

**What changed** from earlier failed attempts was the **financial crisis of 2008**. Before 2008, crowdfunding was a marginal hobbyist activity. The crisis choked bank lending to SMEs — according to the European Central Bank's 2016 Survey on Access to Finance, 18% of eurozone SMEs cited access to finance as their top problem, up from 8% in 2007. Crowdfunding platforms became a genuine alternative, and their failure to scale cross-border became an economic problem, not just a regulatory one.

## Timeline

- **2013** — European Commission publishes Green Paper on crowdfunding, launching consultation; only 37 responses received, mostly from platforms
- **2014** — UK's Crowdcube and Seedrs start cross-border operations; German BaFin warns that platforms may violate securities laws
- **2015** — Capital Markets Union action plan includes crowdfunding passport as a long-term ambition
- **2016** — Companisto crisis in Germany; BaFin imposes €2 million fine on the platform for misleading investors; European Crowdfunding Network formally requests EU regulation
- **2017** — Commission impact assessment finds €2.5 billion lost annually due to fragmentation
- **March 2018** — Commission publishes first draft (voluntary regime)
- **December 2018** — European Parliament ECON committee votes 34-14 for mandatory regime
- **March 2020** — Final trilogue agreement, delayed four months by COVID-19 negotiations
- **November 2021** — Regulation applies; platforms have 12 months to either seek EU authorisation or keep national only
- **November 2023** — ESMA publishes first annual report; 120 platforms authorised under ECSP, 400 more remain national-only
- **2024** — First enforcement actions: Spanish regulator CNMV fines two platforms €150,000 each for failing to provide key investment information sheets

## Impact and consequences

The ECSP Regulation reshaped Europe's crowdfunding market — but more slowly than optimists hoped. By end-2024, ESMA reported 420 authorised platforms, but Germany's BaFin alone had approved only 35, while smaller regulators like Malta's MFSA had greenlit 60 — raising concerns about regulatory tourism. The €5 million per-project cap proved controversial: platforms complained it excluded large infrastructure projects, but the Commission's 2023 review found that 92% of all campaigns were below €2 million.

The regulation forced consolidation. In 2022, Seedrs acquired rival German platform Companisto (the same one that sparked the crisis) after both received ECSP authorisation. **Lendix** (now October) expanded from France to seven other EU countries within 18 months of authorisation, raising €400 million across its combined markets. But the biggest winner was **ESMA**, whose authority over crowdfunding was a modest but concrete expansion into direct supervision.

Investor protection improved measurably. The Commission's 2024 consumer survey found that 68% of crowdfunding investors now received a key information sheet before investing, up from 12% in 2018. Default rates on lending platforms, however, remained higher than national regimes predicted — ESMA's 2024 report noted that 14% of loans on ECSP-authorised platforms were in arrears, compared to 8% on national-only platforms, suggesting the single passport allowed riskier projects to find unsuspecting cross-border investors.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the UK oppose the ECSP Regulation?

The UK's platform sector — led by Crowdcube and Funding Circle — had built a global hub around light-touch regulation under the Financial Conduct Authority. They opposed mandatory EU harmonisation because it would impose higher disclosure costs and cap investment sizes, reducing the attractiveness of UK platforms to international investors. After Brexit, their opposition evaporated, and EU-based platforms became the dominant lobbying force.

### Why did the €5 million cap exist?

The cap was a compromise between the Commission (which wanted no cap to maximise funding) and the European Parliament (which feared retail investors would pour savings into risky projects). The €5 million figure was drawn from the existing Prospectus Regulation (2017/1129/EU), which exempted offerings under that threshold from full prospectus requirements. The intention was to keep crowdfunding for genuinely small businesses, not to compete with traditional capital markets.

### Why did the regulation take so long to pass?

Three reasons: First, the UK blocked progress until the Brexit referendum made its interests irrelevant. Second, the 2014-2019 Commission was focused on GDPR and digital single market rather than financial legislation. Third, national regulators — especially Germany's BaFin and France's AMF — initially opposed losing their gatekeeping power, but relented after the 2018 Parliament vote made regulation inevitable.
