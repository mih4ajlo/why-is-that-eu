## What is it?

The Consumer Credit Directive 2023 (CCD II) is a recast of the 2008 Consumer Credit Directive (2008/48/EC). It sets EU-wide rules for consumer credit agreements — loans, credit cards, overdrafts, and buy-now-pay-later schemes — covering amounts from €200 to €100,000. Its core purpose is to ensure consumers receive clear, standardised information before signing, and that lenders assess creditworthiness responsibly. The directive introduces new rules for digital credit advertising, caps on late-payment charges, and stronger rights for consumers to withdraw from linked purchases.

The directive replaces the 2008 version, which was widely seen as outdated for the age of smartphone apps, marketplace lending, and embedded finance. It applies to all creditors and credit intermediaries, including new players like crowdfunding platforms, and extends to loans associated with the purchase of goods or services (e.g., buy-now-pay-later at checkout). Member States have until 20 November 2025 to transpose it into national law, with application from 20 November 2026.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger for the 2023 directive was a combination of a specific scandal and a broader structural failure. In 2021, the European Commission’s own evaluation of the 2008 directive concluded that it had "not prevented the recurrence of over-indebtedness in the EU," with one in four consumers reporting difficulty meeting credit payments, according to a 2018 Eurobarometer survey. But the political momentum to act was accelerated by a high-profile case: the collapse of the German payments firm Wirecard in June 2020, which exposed how lightly regulated digital credit and payment products could enable fraud and consumer harm. The scandal, which involved €1.9 billion in missing funds and led to the arrest of former CEO Markus Braun, prompted the Commission to push for tighter rules on credit advertising and digital lending practices.

The structural tension was clear: the 2008 directive had been written before smartphones, buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) platforms, or algorithmic credit scoring were common. By 2019, the BNPL market in Europe had grown to over €30 billion annually, according to an industry report by Credit Suisse, yet these products were often exempt from the directive because they were structured as interest-free instalment plans under national thresholds. The European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee (ECON) repeatedly warned that consumers were taking out multiple small loans with no creditworthiness check, driving over-indebtedness, particularly among young people.

The coalition dynamics that got the directive passed were unusual. The Commission proposal, released on 30 June 2021 under Commissioner for Justice Didier Reynders, was supported by the European Parliament’s centre-right EPP and centre-left S&D groups, backed by consumer organisations like BEUC and the European Consumer Organisation. The main opposition came from the European Banking Federation (EBF) and trade associations representing fintechs and BNPL providers, who argued that extending full credit rules to short-term, zero-interest loans would stifle innovation and increase compliance costs for small startups. The German government, home to major BNPL player Klarna, initially sought a lighter-touch approach, proposing exemptions for loans under €200 during Council negotiations. But the European Parliament, led by rapporteur Kateřina Konečná (GUE/NGL, Czechia), pushed back, citing evidence that 40% of BNPL users in Sweden — Klarna’s home market — reported financial stress, according to a 2020 study by the Swedish Consumer Agency.

The timing was decisive. The COVID-19 pandemic had driven a surge in online lending and BNPL, while also increasing household debt across the EU. The Commission’s 2021 impact assessment noted that consumer credit complaints had risen 25% in 2020 compared to 2019, according to national ombudsman data. The political will to act was further strengthened by the EU’s broader digital finance strategy, unveiled in September 2020, which explicitly called for updated consumer credit rules as part of a package to make digital financial services safer.

What changed the outcome of earlier stalled attempts was the Wirecard scandal and the pandemic. Previous attempts to revise the directive in 2015 and 2017 had failed due to industry lobbying and member state disagreements over thresholds. But the Wirecard collapse made regulators more willing to impose oversight on non-bank lenders, while the pandemic’s economic shock made consumer protection a higher priority for EU voters. The final directive, adopted by the Council on 23 October 2023 with 27 member states in favour (no formal opposition), included a mandatory creditworthiness assessment for all credit agreements, including BNPL, and a ban on unsolicited credit limit increases. The Parliament vote on 12 September 2023 saw 505 in favour, 90 against, and 75 abstentions, with opposition from a handful of MEPs from the far-right Identity and Democracy group who argued it was an overreach.

## Timeline

- **2008** — Original Consumer Credit Directive (2008/48/EC) enters into force.
- **2015** — Commission evaluation finds directive outdated for digital lending; first revision attempt stalls over industry opposition.
- **2017** — Second revision attempt fails due to disagreements on scope and thresholds.
- **June 2020** — Wirecard collapse in Germany exposes regulatory gaps in digital credit; EU calls for tighter rules.
- **September 2020** — EU Digital Finance Strategy published, commits to revising consumer credit rules.
- **June 2021** — Commission proposal for new directive released by Justice Commissioner Didier Reynders.
- **September 2023** — European Parliament adopts directive with 505 votes in favour, 90 against.
- **October 2023** — Council adopts directive unanimously.
- **November 2023** — Official publication in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **November 2025** — Deadline for member states to transpose into national law.
- **November 2026** — Directive applies in all member states.

## Impact and consequences

The directive is expected to affect over 1,000 fintech firms across the EU, particularly BNPL providers like Klarna, PayPal, and Clearpay, which will now have to conduct creditworthiness checks on all consumers, even for loans under €200. The Commission estimates the directive will reduce over-indebtedness by up to 15% in member states with high BNPL usage, according to its 2021 impact assessment. Industry groups, including the European Fintech Association, warned that compliance costs could reach €50,000 per company, potentially driving small players out of the market.

Consumer groups have broadly welcomed the directive, but BEUC noted in a September 2023 press release that "the ban on unsolicited credit limit increases is a crucial step, but the directive does not go far enough on capping interest rates," as the final text left rate caps to member states. The European Banking Federation expressed concern that the rules would "disproportionately burden traditional banks" compared to unregulated lenders in non-EU markets, but the directive includes provisions for third-country firms offering services to EU consumers.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the directive cover buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) even though it’s often interest-free?

BNPL products were historically exempt from the 2008 directive because they were short-term and interest-free. But the Commission’s 2021 impact assessment found that BNPL users defaulted at rates similar to high-interest credit cards, and that the ease of sign-up led to multiple overlapping loans. The directive now covers any credit agreement where the consumer repays in more than four instalments, averaging to roughly three months, closing the loophole.

### Why is there no mandatory interest rate cap in the directive?

The European Parliament pushed for an EU-wide interest rate cap during negotiations, but member states resisted, arguing that credit markets vary widely — for example, 30% APR caps in France versus 100% in some Eastern European states. The final directive leaves rate caps to national laws, but requires member states to justify any decision not to introduce a cap based on consumer protection data.

### How does the directive affect digital advertising of credit?

A key new provision is that all credit advertisements, including online banners and in-app pop-ups, must include a clear standardised warning: "Borrowing money costs money." The Commission mandated this after studies showed 60% of BNPL users in the UK (pre-Brexit, using similar marketing) did not realise they were taking out credit, according to a 2020 Financial Conduct Authority analysis. The warning must be displayed prominently and not buried in terms and conditions.
