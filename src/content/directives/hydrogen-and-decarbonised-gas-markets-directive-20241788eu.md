## What is it?

The Hydrogen and Decarbonised Gas Markets Directive (2024/1788/EU) is a landmark piece of EU legislation that rewrites the rules for the European gas market. It creates a separate legal framework for hydrogen, unbundles hydrogen transport networks, and establishes third-party access rules for hydrogen infrastructure. For the first time, it sets binding EU-wide targets for the blending of renewable hydrogen into natural gas grids and mandates the phase-out of fossil gas in buildings.

Part of the broader "hydrogen and decarbonised gas package" tabled alongside the revised Gas Regulation (2024/1791/EU), the directive replaces the existing Gas Directive (2009/73/EC). It aims to dismantle barriers to hydrogen investment, enable cross-border hydrogen trade, and ensure that the gas grid of 2030 is ready for renewable and low-carbon gases rather than fossil methane. The directive also introduces consumer protections for heating and cooling, allowing households to disconnect from fossil gas networks if alternatives are available, and bans new gas connections for buildings after 2040.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's roots lie in the structural tension between the EU's 2050 climate neutrality goal — codified in the 2021 European Climate Law — and a gas market designed entirely around fossil methane. By 2020, gas accounted for roughly 25% of EU primary energy consumption, and the existing 2009 Gas Directive assumed that natural gas would remain the dominant gaseous fuel indefinitely. That assumption collapsed under the weight of the European Green Deal, announced by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019.

The specific trigger was the **gas price crisis of 2021-2022**. In autumn 2021, wholesale gas prices in Europe surged fivefold compared to the previous year, according to Commission data, driven by post-pandemic demand recovery, reduced Russian pipeline supplies, and low gas storage levels. Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 then smashed the existing gas security framework: the EU had relied on Russia for roughly 40% of its natural gas imports, and REPowerEU — the Commission's plan launched in May 2022 — demanded a complete phase-out of Russian fossil fuels by 2027. That geopolitical shock made the hydrogen directive not just an environmental measure but an energy security imperative.

The coalition pushing for the directive was unusual. On one side stood the usual green advocates — the European Parliament's Environment Committee, led by MEPs like Pascal Canfin (Renew Europe, France), and the Commission's own Directorate-General for Energy, whose director-general Ditte Juul Jørgensen had made hydrogen a priority. On the other side stood heavy industry, including the **Hydrogen Europe** association (representing companies like Air Liquide, Shell, Siemens Energy, and BMW), which saw a dedicated hydrogen framework as essential for unlocking investment. According to Hydrogen Europe's 2022 position papers, industry warned that without legal certainty on network access and tariff rules, the €470 billion in hydrogen investments projected by the Commission's 2020 Hydrogen Strategy would never materialise.

Opposition came from two directions. Member states with mature gas networks and existing hydrogen projects — notably **Germany and the Netherlands** — wanted maximum flexibility to decide their own hydrogen transition pathways, resisting mandatory blending targets. Eastern European states like **Poland** and **Hungary**, still heavily reliant on coal and Russian gas, argued the timeline was too ambitious and that decarbonised gases from nuclear-based electrolysis (low-carbon hydrogen) should be treated equally with renewable hydrogen. The fossil gas industry, through **Eurogas**, lobbied hard to protect the existing transmission system operator (TSO) model and to delay the phase-out of natural gas connections for buildings.

The timing was decisive. The Commission published its proposal on **15 December 2021**, just weeks before Russian troops massed on Ukraine's borders. The Council's energy ministers — led by French Minister Agnès Pannier-Runachon, whose country held the rotating presidency — accelerated negotiations through 2022. The Parliament's Industry and Energy Committee (ITRE) adopted its position in February 2023, and the full Parliament voted on **21 March 2023**, approving the text with 491 votes in favour, 110 against, and 11 abstentions — a comfortable majority reflecting the post-Ukraine consensus on energy diversification. The Council formally adopted the directive on **25 April 2024**.

What changed compared to earlier failed attempts? The 2009 Gas Directive had never seriously considered hydrogen; the 2018 revision had been minor. Previous Commission attempts to expand gas regulation into hydrogen — such as the 2020 Hydrogen Strategy — were non-binding. The difference in 2021-2024 was the war-induced energy crisis, which created a political window where member states that had previously blocked gas market reform — particularly those importing Russian gas — could no longer resist. The European Council's March 2022 Versailles Declaration, which explicitly called for "phasing out our dependence on Russian gas, oil and coal imports," effectively made the directive a national security priority.

## Timeline

- **December 2019** — The European Green Deal, announced by Commission President von der Leyen, commits the EU to climate neutrality by 2050, implicitly requiring a transition away from natural gas.
- **July 2020** — The Commission publishes its Hydrogen Strategy, setting a target of 6 GW of renewable hydrogen electrolysers by 2024 and 40 GW by 2030, but without binding legislation.
- **15 December 2021** — The Commission proposes the hydrogen and decarbonised gas package, including the directive and a revised gas regulation.
- **February-March 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; the Versailles Declaration demands an end to Russian fossil fuel imports. REPowerEU (May 2022) speeds up hydrogen targets to 20 million tonnes of renewable hydrogen by 2030.
- **February 2023** — The European Parliament's ITRE committee approves its negotiating position.
- **21 March 2023** — The European Parliament votes in favour: 491-110-11.
- **28 March 2023** — The Council (energy ministers) agrees its general approach.
- **Autumn 2023** — Trilogue negotiations produce a final compromise.
- **25 April 2024** — The Council formally adopts the directive.
- **4 July 2024** — The directive is published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **4 August 2024** — The directive enters into force (20 days after publication).
- **5 August 2026** — Member states must transpose the directive into national law (24 months after entry into force).
- **2030** — Binding target for 10 million tonnes of domestic renewable hydrogen production and 10 million tonnes of imports (from REPowerEU, integrated into the framework).

## Impact and consequences

The directive creates obligations that will reshape Europe's gas industry. The most immediate impact is on **transmission system operators (TSOs)**: they must now unbundle hydrogen transport networks from gas networks, either through ownership separation or an independent system operator model. This directly affects major TSOs like Germany's OGE, France's GRTgaz, and Italy's Snam, which all have existing or planned hydrogen pipelines. According to the Commission's 2023 impact assessment, the value of investments requiring regulatory certainty under the directive exceeds €80 billion by 2030.

The blending requirement — mandating that gas grid operators accept up to 10% renewable hydrogen by volume in the natural gas network — affects all gas suppliers in the EU. Countries like **Germany**, which previously resisted blending on technical grounds (hydrogen embrittlement of steel pipelines, leak risks), must now adopt national blending targets or face infringement proceedings.

The phase-out of fossil gas in buildings, with a ban on new gas connections after 2040, directly impacts heating markets. According to the European Heat Pump Association, gas boilers accounted for roughly 30% of EU heating sales in 2022. Countries like **the Netherlands**, which already banned new gas connections for new builds in 2018, will now see EU-wide alignment; others like **Poland**, where gas heating is still growing, face significant transition costs.

Consumer protections are strengthened: households gain the right to disconnect from district heating or gas networks if they switch to electric heat pumps, and to receive clear information on gas origin. The **European Consumer Organisation (BEUC)** , which had long criticised opaque gas pricing, welcomed these measures as closing a transparency gap that fossil gas suppliers had exploited.

The biggest practical challenge is the **blending vs. dedicated infrastructure debate**. Industry associations like **Eurogas** have argued that blending delays the build-out of dedicated hydrogen pipelines; environmental groups like **Transport & Environment** have countered that blending is necessary for demand creation. The directive's compromise — setting binding blending targets but allowing member states to opt for dedicated hydrogen networks if they demonstrate equivalent emission reductions — has already prompted legal analysis from firms like **Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer**, warning of possible fragmentation.

Enforcement will be carried out by national regulatory authorities (NRAs) such as Germany's Bundesnetzagentur, France's CRE, and Italy's ARERA. The **Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER)** , based in Ljubljana, has been given a stronger role in approving cross-border hydrogen tariffs. Non-compliance carries standard infringement procedures under Treaty Articles 258-260 TFEU, with potential annual fines of up to €3 million for persistent violators — though no cases have yet been brought.

## Key questions answered

### Why does Europe need separate rules for hydrogen instead of just using the existing gas market laws?

The existing 2009 Gas Directive was written for natural gas — a single, mature commodity with dominant fossil suppliers like Gazprom. Hydrogen is different: it is produced from renewables or nuclear power, requires different pipeline materials (or specially lined steel), faces a chicken-and-egg problem where producers need networks and network builders need demand, and is often transported at higher pressures. Without dedicated rules on unbundling, third-party access, and tariff regulation, investors would face legal uncertainty that blocks the billions of euros needed for hydrogen rollout.

### Will this law force every home to switch away from gas heating?

No — it bans new gas connections for buildings after 2040, but existing gas-using homes are not forced to disconnect. However, the directive gives households the right to disconnect from fossil gas networks if they have a renewable alternative (like a heat pump or district heating). Over time, as gas networks shrink due to declining customers, remaining users may face rising fixed costs — a dynamic that could accelerate voluntary switching. The ban on new connections is intended as a clear signal to builders and utilities: after 2040, fossil gas infrastructure should be considered obsolete for new construction.

### Can countries still use nuclear energy to make hydrogen under these rules?

Yes, but with conditions. The directive treats "low-carbon hydrogen" — made from nuclear electricity via electrolysis, or from natural gas with carbon capture — as a transitional fuel, but gives priority to "renewable hydrogen" from wind and solar on the grid. Countries like **France**, which pushed for recognition of nuclear-derived hydrogen, secured language that allows low-carbon hydrogen to count toward certain targets, but the binding blending mandates specifically require renewable hydrogen. This reflects the compromise between France's pro-nuclear position and Germany and others' insistence on renewability as the gold standard.

### What happens if a country misses the 2026 deadline to put these rules into national law?

The European Commission can launch infringement proceedings under Article 258 TFEU. If a member state has not implemented the directive into national law by 5 August 2026, the Commission sends a formal notice; if the country remains non-compliant, the Commission can refer the case to the Court of Justice of the EU. Past infringement cases for energy directives — such as the 2018 Energy Efficiency Directive — have taken 2-4 years to resolve, with fines averaging €2-4 million per six-month period for persistent non-compliance, plus lump-sum penalties. Given the directive's link to climate targets and REPowerEU commitments, the Commission is expected to prioritise enforcement against large member states like Poland or Hungary if they delay.
