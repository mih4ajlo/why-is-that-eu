## What is it?

The InvestEU Programme is the European Union's flagship investment support mechanism for the 2021-2027 budget period. It pools the EU budget guarantee (€26.2 billion) to back loans, guarantees, and equity investments made by financial partners such as the European Investment Bank (EIB) and national promotional banks. The goal is to de-risk projects that are strategically important for the Union but struggle to attract private financing on their own.

The programme operates through four policy "windows": sustainable infrastructure (covering climate, energy, transport, and circular economy); research, innovation, and digitisation; small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs); and social investment and skills. A fifth cross-cutting window addresses strategic European autonomy, added in 2024. The European Commission manages the overall framework, while the EIB implements roughly 75% of the guarantee; national promotional banks handle the remainder via a "Member State compartment". InvestEU also runs an advisory hub that provides technical assistance to project promoters.

## Why was it introduced?

InvestEU is the permanent successor to the European Fund for Strategic Investments (EFSI), better known as the **Juncker Plan**, which ran from 2015 to 2020. That temporary instrument was created in the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis to revive investment in the EU, which had fallen by 20% from its pre-crisis peak. By mid-2020, EFSI had mobilised €524.4 billion in total investment, according to the European Commission's own evaluation — above its initial target of €500 billion. But it was always designed as a crisis-response tool with a fixed end date, and its legal basis (Regulation 2015/1017/EU) expired in 2020.

The structural problem was fragmentation. Before InvestEU, the EU budget operated 13 different financial instruments across 15 funding programmes, each with separate rules, approval processes, and implementing partners. This duplication raised transaction costs for both the Commission and final beneficiaries. The European Court of Auditors had repeatedly flagged inefficiencies, notably in a 2016 special report that found up to 40% of projects were "non-additional" — meaning they would have been financed anyway without the EU guarantee. The Commission's own 2018 impact assessment for InvestEU estimated that streamlining could reduce administrative costs by €200–€300 million per year.

The **timing** was shaped by two events. First, the June 2018 proposal for InvestEU was part of the European Commission's broader package for the 2021–2027 Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF). But negotiations stalled over the UK's exit from the EU, which left a hole in the budget, and over demands from the so-called "Frugal Four" (Netherlands, Austria, Denmark, Sweden) to cap overall spending. The deadlock broke only in the summer of 2020, when the COVID-19 pandemic swept across Europe. The economic shock — the EU economy shrank by 6.1% in 2020, the deepest recession in its history — made a rapid, large-scale investment programme politically irresistible. The European Council agreed a €1.8 trillion package (MFF plus NextGenerationEU) at a summit on 21 July 2020. InvestEU’s budget guarantee was increased from an originally proposed €15.2 billion to €26.2 billion, partly to cover a new "Strategic European Investment Window" that the Commission added in April 2020 in response to the pandemic’s disruption of supply chains.

The **coalition** that pushed InvestEU through was unusual. The European Parliament's lead rapporteur, MEP **José Manuel Fernandes** (EPP, Portugal), worked with shadow rapporteurs from the S&D and Renew Europe groups to secure broad cross-party support. The main opposition came from the European Greens, who argued that the criteria for "green" spending were too weak and that fossil fuel projects could still qualify. The Greens tabled several amendments to tighten sustainability requirements; none passed. In the Council, the decisive bargain was struck by Economy Commissioner **Paolo Gentiloni** and German Finance Minister **Olaf Scholz** (then also holding the rotating Council presidency in the second half of 2020). Scholz pushed for the inclusion of climate spending targets — 30% of InvestEU's overall portfolio must contribute to climate objectives — to align with the European Green Deal announced by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019.

A key **unblocking moment** occurred in October 2020, when the European Parliament's ECON committee rejected a compromise on the size of the guarantee pool. The committee chair, **Roberto Gualtieri** (S&D, Italy), brokered a last-minute deal that raised the guarantee to €26.2 billion by drawing on unused margins from the MFF. The final trilogue agreement was reached on 9 December 2020, less than three weeks before the Juncker Plan was due to expire.

## Timeline

- **2014-2015** — Juncker Plan (EFSI) launched after the investment crisis; initial capital of €21 billion from EU budget and EIB.
- **2017** — EFSI extended to cover social investment and agriculture.
- **June 2018** — European Commission proposes InvestEU as part of the 2021-2027 MFF package; budget guarantee of €15.2 billion.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 pandemic declared a global emergency; Commission revises InvestEU proposal, increases guarantee to €26.2 billion and adds a strategic window.
- **21 July 2020** — European Council agrees €1.8 trillion recovery package; InvestEU embedded in the MFF.
- **9 December 2020** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council.
- **24 March 2021** — Regulation (EU) 2021/523 adopted by the Council of the European Union.
- **1 January 2021** — Regulation applies retroactively from this date.
- **2022** — First InvestEU guarantee agreements signed with EIB and national promotional banks.
- **2024** — Commission activates the Strategic European Investment Window to support critical raw materials, deep tech, and defence manufacturing.

## Impact and consequences

The direct impact on the EU's investment landscape has been slow to materialise. By the end of 2023, the Commission reported that InvestEU had mobilised €72 billion in investment — far below the €372 billion target for the end of 2027. The main obstacle has been the concurrent NextGenerationEU recovery funds, which many member states preferred to use for direct grants rather than guarantee-backed loans. The European Court of Auditors, in a September 2022 review, noted that the "additionality" criterion — a requirement that projects would not have happened without EU support — remains difficult to verify, a persistent problem inherited from the Juncker Plan.

For SMEs, the impact has been more visible. The programme's SME window, backed by €5.3 billion in guarantees, allowed partner banks to issue up to €90 billion in new loans at reduced interest rates. The European Investment Fund (EIF), which manages this window, reported that 90% of supported firms had fewer than 50 employees. In France, Bpifrance used the InvestEU guarantee to launch a €2 billion innovation loan line in 2022. In Italy, Cassa Depositi e Prestiti set up a €1.5 billion guarantee scheme for female-led startups.

The largest institutional impact has been on the EIB itself. The bank shifted its lending model from pure market-rate loans to riskier equity and quasi-equity instruments, enabled by the InvestEU guarantee. EIB Vice-President **Ambroise Fayolle** stated in a 2023 interview that InvestEU allowed the bank to finance "first-of-a-kind" technologies, such as the Northvolt battery gigafactory in Sweden, which received a €350 million guaranteed loan. However, environmental groups criticised the programme for still counting some gas infrastructure projects — such as the Greece-Bulgaria interconnector — as "sustainable" under the taxonomy regs.

## Key questions answered

### What is the EU’s main investment programme and how does it work?

The EU's central investment programme for 2021–2027 is InvestEU. It uses a €26.2 billion EU budget guarantee to encourage banks and the European Investment Bank to lend to projects that are too risky or untested for normal commercial financing. The guarantee covers a portion of potential losses, so lenders can offer cheaper loans. The programme targets four areas: sustainable infrastructure, research and innovation, small business lending, and social projects like affordable housing and education.

### How does InvestEU differ from the earlier Juncker Plan?

The Juncker Plan (EFSI) was a temporary crisis-response fund created after the 2008 financial crash. It ran until 2020 and was governed by a single €21 billion guarantee managed solely by the European Investment Bank. InvestEU is permanent, has a larger guarantee (€26.2 billion), and includes national promotional banks as direct partners. It also consolidates 13 previous EU financial instruments into one set of rules, making it simpler for companies to apply.

### Does InvestEU support climate and green projects?

Yes, the regulation requires that at least 30% of InvestEU-backed investments contribute to climate objectives — such as renewable energy, energy efficiency, clean transport, and circular economy projects. This target aligns with the European Green Deal’s goal of net-zero emissions by 2050. However, some fossil-fuel-backed projects (like gas infrastructure) have been contested by environmental groups, who argue the programme’s "do no significant harm" screening is not strict enough.

### How much has actually been invested so far?

By the end of 2023, the European Commission reported that InvestEU had mobilised roughly €72 billion in total investment — about 19% of the €372 billion target for the full seven-year period. The slower-than-expected start is partly because many EU countries prioritised direct grants from the €800 billion NextGenerationEU recovery fund over using loan guarantees. The programme is expected to accelerate after 2025 as unspent MFF resources are reallocated.
