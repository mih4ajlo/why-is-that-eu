## What is it?

The Victims’ Rights Directive establishes legally binding minimum standards across all EU member states for how crime victims must be treated by police, prosecutors, courts, and support services. It covers three core areas: the right to information (victims must be told about their case, available support, and how to complain), the right to support (access to free, confidential victim support services), and the right to protection (individual assessment of each victim's risk of repeat victimisation or intimidation, with appropriate measures like testifying behind a screen or via video link).

The directive applies to all victims of crime, regardless of their nationality or residence status, and covers all stages of the criminal justice process — from the moment a victim first contacts the police through to the conclusion of proceedings. It replaced the earlier, much weaker 2001 Framework Decision on the standing of victims in criminal proceedings, which had been widely criticised for being non-binding and poorly implemented.

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst was a 2009 ruling by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) in *Case C‑205/08, Umweltanwalt von Kärnten*, which exposed glaring gaps in how victims were treated across EU borders. The case involved a victim of environmental crime in Austria who was denied access to information about the proceedings because Austrian law did not recognise him as a "victim" under domestic definitions. The Court found that the 2001 Framework Decision — a non-binding instrument — did not give victims enforceable rights. The ruling triggered a political crisis for the European Commission: it had spent a decade touting a "victims' rights" agenda, but the CJEU showed the framework was toothless.

The structural tension was that the EU had built an elaborate system of mutual recognition of criminal judgments — via the European Arrest Warrant and the European Investigation Order — but had no corresponding minimum standards for the treatment of victims. Criminals had enforceable cross-border rights; victims did not. This asymmetry was politically unsustainable, especially after the 2009 Lisbon Treaty gave the EU explicit competence in criminal justice and made the Charter of Fundamental Rights legally binding (Article 47 on fair trial and Article 4 on prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment implicitly covered victims).

The coalition that drove the directive was unusual. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Teresa Jiménez-Becerril (EPP, Spain) — whose brother was murdered by ETA — pushed for strong, enforceable rights. The Commission, under Justice Commissioner Viviane Reding (Luxembourg, EPP), made it a flagship of her "Procedural Rights Package". Reding had previously been a vocal critic of the 2001 Framework Decision, calling it "a paper tiger" in a 2010 speech. The Council of the EU, representing member states, was initially resistant, fearing costs and national discretion. But a series of high-profile cases — including the 2006 murder of Meredith Kercher in Italy and the 2008 death of a British student in France who was denied victim support — created media pressure that made it politically toxic to oppose.

Opposition came mainly from governments in eastern Europe (Czech Republic, Poland, Hungary) and some northern countries (Finland, Sweden) who argued that victim support was a national social service, not an EU competence. The UK government under David Cameron threatened a veto, insisting on a full opt-out from all justice and home affairs measures after the Lisbon Treaty. A compromise was reached in June 2011 where the UK secured an opt-out, but the directive proceeded without them. The final vote in the Council was unanimous among participating member states, with 26 of 27 supporting (the UK abstaining). In the Parliament, the vote was 614 in favour, 12 against, and 56 abstentions, passing on 12 September 2012.

The timing was propitious. The 2010 Stockholm Programme — the EU's five-year justice plan — had prioritised victims' rights. The 2011 Council of Europe Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence (Istanbul Convention) was creating parallel momentum. And the Commission's own 2009 evaluation of the 2001 Framework Decision had found "serious shortcomings" in implementation in 22 of 27 member states, with some countries providing no victim support at all. The CJEU ruling in 2009 broke the deadlock by making clear that without a binding directive, the EU's entire mutual recognition system rested on an unstable base.

## Timeline

- **2001** — Council Framework Decision 2001/220/JHA on the standing of victims in criminal proceedings adopted, but as a non-binding soft-law instrument
- **2009** — CJEU ruling in *Case C‑205/08* (Umweltanwalt von Kärnten) finds the 2001 Framework Decision does not confer enforceable rights on victims
- **2010** — Commission publishes an evaluation finding "serious shortcomings" in 22 of 27 member states' implementation of the 2001 Framework Decision
- **2011, May** — Commission proposes the Victims' Rights Directive (COM(2011) 275 final)
- **2011, June** — UK secures opt-out from the directive during Justice and Home Affairs Council negotiations
- **2012, September 12** — European Parliament approves the directive (614 in favour, 12 against, 56 abstentions)
- **2012, October 25** — Council formally adopts the directive
- **2012, November 15** — Directive published in the Official Journal
- **2015, November 16** — Member states must transpose the directive into national law
- **2016** — Commission launches infringement proceedings against 22 member states for late or incomplete transposition
- **2022** — Commission publishes evaluation report finding that while awareness has improved, transposition remains uneven across member states

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate consequence was that millions of crime victims across the EU gained enforceable rights they previously lacked. The European Commission's 2022 evaluation report found that all member states (except Denmark, which did not opt in) had transposed the directive, but the quality of transposition varied widely. Six member states — including Bulgaria, Romania, and Poland — were referred to the CJEU for failing to implement the individual risk assessment requirement properly.

The directive created a new market for victim support services. Since the directive requires member states to ensure access to free, confidential victim support, many countries that had no dedicated services — such as Malta, Cyprus, and several eastern European states — had to establish them from scratch. The Commission estimated that transposition costs averaged €2-5 million per member state, with ongoing annual costs of €1-3 million, but noted that these were offset by savings in police and court time from better-informed and more cooperative victims.

The directive had a particularly significant impact on victims of domestic violence. For the first time, member states were required to conduct individual risk assessments for victims at risk of repeat victimisation — a provision that was deliberately worded to include victims of intimate partner violence. Women's rights NGOs, particularly the European Women's Lobby, had lobbied heavily for this provision, arguing that domestic violence victims were routinely re-victimised because police did not assess risk before releasing perpetrators.

However, the directive's most controversial element was the right to review a decision not to prosecute. Under Article 11, victims have the right to challenge a prosecutor's decision to drop a case. This created tension in several member states — notably France and Germany — where the principle of prosecutorial discretion (the *opportunité des poursuites* or *Legalitätsprinzip*) clashed with the directive's requirement. France was referred to the CJEU in 2017 for failing to provide an effective remedy for victims whose cases were dropped.

The UK's decision to opt out meant that British victims could not rely on the directive's protections when travelling in the EU, and victims from other EU countries were not guaranteed the same rights in the UK. After Brexit, this asymmetry became permanent, with the UK's own Victims' Code (2013) offering weaker protections.

## Key questions answered

### Can a crime victim get information about their case in another EU country?

Yes. Under the directive, every victim has the right to receive information about their case in a language they understand from their first contact with authorities, including about available support services, how to make a complaint, and the progress of the investigation. This applies regardless of where in the EU the crime occurred.

### Does EU law require police to protect a domestic violence victim from the perpetrator?

Yes. The directive requires police to conduct an individual risk assessment for every victim who may be at risk of repeat victimisation, intimidation, or retaliation. For domestic violence victims, this assessment must specifically address the risk from the intimate partner. Based on the assessment, authorities must provide protective measures such as restraining orders, separate waiting rooms in courts, or special interview arrangements.

### Can a victim appeal if a prosecutor decides not to press charges?

Yes. Article 11 of the directive gives victims the right to have a decision not to prosecute reviewed by a competent authority. This does not mean victims can force a prosecution, but they must have access to a mechanism (administrative, judicial, or quasi-judicial) to challenge the decision. Several member states — including France and Germany — struggled with this provision because it conflicts with their traditional prosecutorial discretion.
