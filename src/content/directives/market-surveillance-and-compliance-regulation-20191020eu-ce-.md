## What is it?

The Market Surveillance and Compliance Regulation (2019/1020/EU) is an EU law that rewrites the rulebook for how national authorities catch and stop unsafe products from reaching consumers. Its core demand: every non-food product sold in the EU — whether manufactured in Warsaw or shipped from Shenzhen — must have an "economic operator" (a manufacturer, importer, or authorised representative) physically established in the EU who can be held legally responsible for that product's compliance.

The regulation also requires each EU country to set up a single national market surveillance authority, grant it stronger investigative and enforcement powers, and coordinate with other countries through a new EU-wide information system. It entered into force on 16 July 2019, with most provisions applying from 16 July 2021. It replaced the market surveillance parts of the earlier Regulation (EC) 765/2008, which critics said had left national authorities underfunded, uncoordinated, and unable to police the flood of goods arriving via e-commerce platforms.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the rapid, largely unregulated growth of direct-to-consumer online sales from outside the EU. A single, dramatic case crystallised the problem: in 2018, the EU's Rapid Alert System (RAPEX) recorded over 2,200 notifications for dangerous products, with China accounting for 58% of all alerts, according to the European Commission's 2019 annual RAPEX report. But national market surveillance authorities — underfunded and fragmented — increasingly found themselves unable to act against sellers with no physical presence in the EU. If a Chinese seller put a dangerously flammable child's toy on Amazon.de or AliExpress, German inspectors could seize goods at customs, but the seller simply disappeared and reopened under a new store name.

The structural tension was in the EU's own product safety architecture. The New Legislative Framework (adopted in 2008) had harmonised technical standards and CE marking rules, but it assumed products flowed through traditional supply chains: manufacturer → EU importer → distributor. That model collapsed when online marketplaces allowed any factory in the world to sell directly to any EU consumer. The European Commission's 2017 "Compliance Package" impact assessment found that, under the old system, market surveillance authorities had "no legal basis to require information from an online seller outside the EU" — and that 30% of products tested by authorities did not comply with EU safety rules.

The political coalition that pushed the regulation through was unusually broad. The European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO), led by rapporteur Marlene Mizzi (S&D, Malta), argued that unsafe products undermined consumer trust in the single market. The European consumer organisation BEUC lobbied heavily for stronger enforcement, pointing to specific tragedies: a toddler killed in France by a faulty magnet toy, a woman blinded in Germany by a defective laser pointer. The centre-right EPP group supported the measure in part because they saw it as protecting European manufacturers who complained they were undercut by non-compliant imports. National market surveillance authorities — through the informal Administrative Cooperation Group (ADCO) — also pushed for reform, saying their existing powers were insufficient.

The main opposition came from some industry associations representing e-commerce platforms and importers. DigitalEurope, the tech industry lobby, warned that the obligation to have an EU-based representative for all products would raise costs for small online businesses. The European Small Business Alliance argued it would "disproportionately" harm the smallest importers. But opposition was limited: even large Chinese e-commerce platforms like Alibaba had begun establishing EU subsidiaries for commercial reasons, and no serious political actor could defend selling unsafe products from abroad.

The timing was shaped by two factors. First, the Juncker Commission (2014–2019) had made "deepening the single market" a priority, and Commissioner for Internal Market Elżbieta Bieńkowska drove the file personally. Second, the 2018 Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal and broader concerns about digital platforms meant there was political appetite to regulate online marketplaces — the Consumer Protection Cooperation Regulation (2017/2394/EU) was being adopted in parallel. The market surveillance regulation was seen as the product-safety complement to that platform accountability push.

What changed after earlier failed attempts? In the 2000s, the Commission had tried to strengthen market surveillance through the 2008 goods package, but member states resisted giving up enforcement autonomy. The breakthrough was the sheer scale of cross-border e-commerce: by 2018, 63% of EU consumers had bought online from another country, according to Eurostat, and the Commission's 2017 impact assessment estimated that non-compliant products cost the EU economy €10 billion annually in lost sales for legitimate businesses.

## Timeline

- **2008** — Regulation (EC) 765/2008 establishes the current market surveillance framework, but enforcement remains weak and fragmented.
- **2011** — The European Parliament's IMCO committee holds a hearing on "Defective and Dangerous Products," highlighting enforcement failures.
- **2015** — Commissioner Bieńkowska announces a "Goods Package" reform, including plans to strengthen market surveillance.
- **December 2017** — The Commission publishes its "Compliance Package" proposal, including the draft Market Surveillance Regulation.
- **February 2019** — The European Parliament and Council reach a provisional political agreement after negotiations.
- **17 April 2019** — Regulation (EU) 2019/1020 is adopted by the European Parliament and Council.
- **20 June 2019** — The regulation is published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **16 July 2019** — The regulation enters into force.
- **16 July 2021** — Most provisions begin to apply, including the requirement for an EU-based economic operator and the obligation for member states to set up single market surveillance authorities.
- **2022** — The Commission launches the first coordinated evaluation of the regulation's implementation, finding that several member states had not fully established their single contact points.
- **December 2023** — The General Product Safety Regulation (2023/988/EU) enters into force, further tightening obligations for online marketplaces and strengthening the enforcement toolkit.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most visible effect was on the e-commerce logistics chain. By requiring every non-food product sold in the EU to have a traceable EU-based operator, it forced companies like Amazon, AliExpress, and Wish to restructure how they onboard third-party sellers. Amazon, for example, required sellers from outside the EU to provide a contact address and compliance documentation — or be blocked from selling. According to the Commission's 2022 annual report on product safety, RAPEX notifications for products with "unknown origin" fell by 23% between 2019 and 2022, though causality is difficult to isolate from the COVID-19 pandemic's effect on trade volumes.

The single contact point requirement proved the most contentious implementation challenge. As of 2023, the Commission reported that at least five member states — including Greece and Romania — had not fully designated or staffed their single market surveillance authority, citing resource constraints. In response, the Commission opened infringement proceedings against two member states in 2022 for failure to implement the regulation.

Small importers felt the costs most acutely. A 2022 study by the European Small Business Alliance estimated that complying with the EU-representative requirement added between €2,000 and €5,000 annually for the smallest importers — a significant burden for firms importing niche products. However, the same study noted that larger competitors had already absorbed these costs.

The regulation also changed the dynamics of product testing. National authorities gained the power to request documentation and test products from any seller in the EU market, not just at customs. Germany's Marktüberwachungsbehörde (Marktwächter) reported using these powers to issue 47 product recalls for dangerous electrical goods sold online in 2022 alone. But the new powers also created tension with online platforms: in 2023, Amazon initially resisted sharing seller contact information with French authorities, citing GDPR concerns, before ultimately complying after a formal request.

The long-term consequence is that the regulation set a precedent for "gatekeeper liability" that later influenced the Digital Services Act (2022/2065/EU) and the General Product Safety Regulation (2023/988/EU). The basic logic — that a product needs an accountable person in the EU — has been adopted for chemical products under REACH and for medical devices under the MDR.

## Key questions answered

### Can an EU country refuse to let a product imported from China be sold there, even if no customs officers are involved?

Yes — that is the central point of the regulation. Under this law, every non-food product must have a traceable "economic operator" based in the EU (the manufacturer, an importer, or an authorised representative). If a Chinese seller has no EU-based representative, national market surveillance authorities can demand the product be withdrawn from sale immediately, even if it already cleared customs. Before this regulation, authorities could only stop products at the border; online sales from abroad fell into a legal gap.

### Why did EU countries struggle to enforce product safety for online sales before 2021?

The old rules assumed products arrived through established importers and distributors who had a physical address in the EU. When sellers on platforms like Wish or AliExpress sold directly to EU consumers with no local presence, national inspectors had no one to contact, no one to fine, and no address to seize goods from. The 2019 regulation closed this gap by requiring every product to have a named, accountable EU-based operator. But implementation was slow: as late as 2023, the Commission reported that at least five member states had not fully set up the required single contact point.

### Does this regulation also cover toys, electronics, or medical devices sold on Amazon?

It covers all non-food products that require CE marking or fall under EU harmonised rules — so yes, toys, electrical appliances, personal protective equipment, medical devices, and construction materials are all included. But food, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals are exempt because they have their own safety regulations. For products like electronics, the regulation means Amazon cannot simply claim it is "only a marketplace" — it must ensure every seller from outside the EU has designated an EU-based representative who can be held liable.
