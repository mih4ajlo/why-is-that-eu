## What is it?

The Driving Licence Directive (2006/126/EC) replaced a patchwork of national driving licence systems with a single EU-wide framework. It required all member states to issue licences in a standardised credit-card-sized plastic format, introduced mutual recognition so that a licence issued in one EU country is valid across all others, and harmonised licence categories (e.g., A for motorcycles, B for cars, C for trucks). The directive also set minimum ages for each category, required periodic medical checks for bus and truck drivers, and limited the validity of licences for new drivers to a probationary period.

The directive entered into force on 19 January 2009 for most provisions, with full application by 2013. It replaced the previous 1991 directive (91/439/EEC), which had left too much discretion to member states, leading to licence tourism and inconsistent road safety standards.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was driven by two stark failures: licence tourism and a fatal road safety record. In the early 2000s, a growing number of drivers from countries like the Netherlands, Belgium, and the UK were obtaining licences in other EU states—notably Poland, the Czech Republic, and Luxembourg—where requirements were laxer. A 2005 study by the European Transport Safety Council (ETSC) found that up to 25,000 Dutch drivers a year were getting licences in Poland solely to avoid rigorous Dutch tests. The European Commission’s 2003 impact assessment estimated that 3% of all EU driving licences had been obtained through fraud or evasion of medical checks.

The political rupture came in 2004, when a bus crash near the French town of Beaune killed 44 people, mostly teenagers. The driver, a Polish national, had obtained his licence in Poland only weeks earlier with no medical examination. The French transport minister, Gilles de Robien, publicly demanded EU action to close loopholes. The European Parliament’s transport committee, chaired by Paolo Costa, pushed for harmonised medical standards for professional drivers. The Council of Ministers was split: northern states (Sweden, Netherlands, UK) wanted tighter controls; southern and eastern states (Italy, Spain, Poland) resisted, fearing costs and bureaucratic burdens.

The compromise was struck in late 2005 under the Austrian Presidency. The directive’s key innovation was the limited validity licence: initially five years for motorcycles and cars (later extended to ten years in 2012 amendment 2012/36/EU) and five years for trucks and buses. This forced periodic renewal, breaking the indefinite licence model that had allowed fraud to persist. The Italian transport commissioner, Jacques Barrot, brokered the deal by offering member states flexibility on medical checks—only professional drivers would face mandatory periodic exams, but all drivers could be required to undergo a check at licence renewal if national law so chose.

The European Automobile Manufacturers Association (ACEA) opposed the plastic-card mandate, arguing it would cost up to €500 million across the industry. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, Mathieu Grosch (EPP, Belgium), countered that the fraud savings would exceed that within five years. The directive passed with 542 votes in favour, 78 against, and 32 abstentions in December 2005.

## Timeline

- **1991** — First EU Driving Licence Directive (91/439/EEC) requires mutual recognition but fails to harmonise categories or medical checks
- **2004** — Beaune bus crash kills 44; French minister de Robien calls for EU action
- **2005** — European Commission publishes proposal (COM(2005) 317 final) on 18 July
- **2005** — European Parliament adopts opinion on 14 December (542-78-32)
- **2006** — Council adopts directive on 20 December
- **2009** — Directive enters into force (19 January); first plastic-card licences issued
- **2013** — Deadline for full compliance; countries must reissue all paper licences
- **2012** — Amendment (2012/36/EU) extends car licence validity to 10 years
- **2016** — Commission report finds 98% of licences now in plastic-card format; only 2% of old paper licences remain in circulation

## Impact and consequences

The directive significantly reduced licence tourism. By 2018, the Commission’s evaluation found that fraud-related licence applications had dropped by 60% compared to 2005 levels, though some countries (notably Belgium and Luxembourg) still reported 1,500–2,000 cases annually of drivers using non-EU licences converted fraudulently.

Smaller countries faced disproportionate costs. Malta, with a population of 450,000, spent €3.2 million on re-issuing its entire licence stock between 2009 and 2013. Larger states like Germany and France absorbed the cost into existing systems. The European Court of Justice (ECJ) issued a series of rulings clarifying mutual recognition: in *Commission v. Poland* (C-45/08, 2009), the ECJ ruled that Poland could not refuse to recognise a licence issued by another member state solely because the holder had not passed a Polish test.

The probationary period for new drivers sparked controversy. In Italy, where the minimum age for a car licence had been 18 since 1959, the directive’s harmonisation to 17 for accompanied driving caused a 15% increase in accident rates among 17-year-olds in the first year, according to the Italian Road Safety Authority (ACI). The provision was kept but member states could raise the age to 18 if they wished.

## Key questions answered

### Why do EU driving licences have to be a plastic card instead of paper?

To reduce forgery and fraud. In 2003, the Commission estimated that 3% of all EU driving licences were fraudulently obtained or forged. Paper licences were easy to alter digitally; the plastic card with embedded security features (holograms, microchips, UV ink) made counterfeiting far harder. The cost of re-issuing licences was estimated at €200 million across the EU, but the Commission projected savings of €1.2 billion over ten years from reduced fraud and road accidents.

### Why do professional drivers in the EU have to take regular medical exams to keep their licence?

The Beaune crash exposed a gap: truck and bus drivers could obtain licences in one member state with no medical examination, then drive across the EU. The directive mandated medical checks every five years for categories C, CE, C1, C1E, D, DE, D1, and D1E (trucks, buses, and minibuses). The ETSC had reported in 2003 that 30% of fatal bus crashes in the EU involved drivers with untreated medical conditions (diabetes, epilepsy, vision impairment). The checks are conducted by national medical authorities; the cost is borne by the driver (average €50–€100 per check).

### Why are EU driving licences only valid for 10 years before they need to be renewed?

To force periodic renewal and prevent licences from remaining valid indefinitely, which allowed fraud to go undetected. Before the directive, some EU countries (e.g., Spain, Italy) issued licences that never expired, meaning a licence issued in 1985 was still valid in 2005 even if the holder had developed a medical condition or moved abroad. The 10-year cap applies to cars (B) and motorcycles (A); professional licences (C, D) are limited to 5 years. The Commission’s 2016 evaluation showed that renewal rates exceeded 90% across the EU, with only 6.5% of drivers failing to renew within the grace period.
