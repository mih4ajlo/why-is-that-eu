## What is it?

Directive 2011/95/EU defines, across the EU, who is a refugee under the 1951 Geneva Convention and who qualifies for "subsidiary protection" — a separate EU status for people who do not meet the refugee definition but would face a real risk of serious harm (the death penalty, torture, or indiscriminate violence in armed conflict) if returned home. It also lays down the content of those statuses: residence permits, access to employment, education, healthcare, social welfare, and family reunification.

The directive is one of the three "second-generation" instruments of the Common European Asylum System (CEAS), alongside the recast Asylum Procedures Directive (2013/32/EU) and the recast Reception Conditions Directive (2013/33/EU). It applies in all member states except Denmark and Ireland, which opted out of the recast and remain bound by the 2004 original.

Crucially, the 2011 recast narrowed — but did not eliminate — the gap between refugee status and subsidiary protection. Beneficiaries of both now get residence permits of comparable length and broadly equivalent social rights, ending a two-tier system in which Syrians, Somalis or Afghans granted only subsidiary protection received markedly worse treatment than those granted full refugee status.

## Why was it introduced?

By the late 2000s, an Iraqi asylum seeker's chance of being recognised as a refugee depended almost entirely on which EU country processed the claim. The Commission's 2010 impact assessment accompanying the recast proposal documented recognition-rate spreads of dozens of percentage points between member states for nationals of the same country of origin — a pattern UNHCR and the European Council on Refugees and Exiles (ECRE) had been denouncing for years. For a "Common" European Asylum System resting on the Dublin Regulation's premise that any member state was a safe place to claim asylum, this was an existential embarrassment.

The 2004 Qualification Directive (2004/83/EC) had been a minimum-harmonisation instrument negotiated under unanimity in the Council, and member states had used the flexibility to entrench their pre-existing national doctrines. Germany retained a restrictive reading of "non-state actors of persecution"; France interpreted the "internal flight alternative" generously to the state; subsidiary protection beneficiaries in some countries received one-year permits with no family reunification rights, while refugees in others received three-year permits with full social access. The Court of Justice's 2010 ruling in *Abdulla* (C-175/08) on cessation of refugee status, and *Bolbol* and *El Kott* on Palestinian refugees, also exposed how loosely member states were interpreting the Geneva Convention's terms.

The political timing reflected three pressures. First, the 2008 European Pact on Immigration and Asylum, pushed by the French Presidency under Brice Hortefeux, committed member states to completing the CEAS by 2012. Second, the Lisbon Treaty's entry into force in December 2009 moved asylum from unanimity to ordinary legislative procedure, giving the European Parliament — where the S&D, Greens, ALDE and GUE/NGL were broadly aligned with NGO positions — real co-legislative power for the first time. Third, Commissioner Cecilia Malmström, who took over the Home Affairs portfolio in 2010, made completion of the CEAS a personal priority and tabled the recast as part of a package.

The negotiation was bruising. The Commission's original 2009 proposal would have fully equalised refugee and subsidiary protection statuses; member states including Germany, France, the UK and the Netherlands resisted, fearing a "pull factor" and the budgetary cost of extending family reunification and integration support to subsidiary protection beneficiaries. The final text, agreed under the Hungarian and Polish Presidencies in 2011, was a compromise: rights were largely aligned, but member states retained discretion on the length of residence permits and certain integration entitlements. The directive was adopted in December 2011 with transposition due by 21 December 2013 — just as the Syrian war was about to test the system as never before.

## Timeline

- **2004** — Council adopts the original Qualification Directive (2004/83/EC) under unanimity.
- **2007** — Commission Green Paper on the future of the CEAS identifies divergent recognition rates as a core failure.
- **2008** — European Pact on Immigration and Asylum commits member states to completing the CEAS by 2012.
- **2009** — Commission tables recast proposal (COM(2009) 551) alongside revisions to the Procedures and Reception directives.
- **2009** — Lisbon Treaty enters into force, bringing asylum under ordinary legislative procedure.
- **2010** — CJEU rulings in *Abdulla* and *Bolbol* clarify Geneva Convention concepts.
- **2011** — European Parliament and Council adopt Directive 2011/95/EU on 13 December.
- **2013** — Transposition deadline (21 December); recast Procedures and Reception directives adopted.
- **2014–2016** — Directive applied to the Syrian, Iraqi and Afghan caseloads of the migration crisis; recognition-rate divergences resurface.
- **2016** — Commission proposes replacing the directive with a directly applicable Qualification Regulation as part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum.
- **2024** — Qualification Regulation (EU) 2024/1347 adopted, set to replace the directive from 2026.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most concrete achievement was lifting subsidiary protection beneficiaries — predominantly Syrians, Afghans and Somalis through the 2010s — closer to refugee-equivalent rights in most member states. Family reunification, employment access and healthcare entitlements were broadly aligned, though Germany controversially suspended family reunification for subsidiary protection holders in 2016 in response to the Syrian arrivals, exploiting the directive's residual flexibility.

Eurostat data through the migration crisis nonetheless continued to show striking divergence in first-instance recognition rates for the same nationalities: Afghan recognition rates, for example, ranged from near-total acceptance in some member states to near-total rejection in others in the same year. The Commission cited this persistent divergence as the main justification for converting the directive into a regulation under the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, arguing that only a directly applicable instrument leaving no transposition discretion could close the gap.

The Court of Justice has built an extensive case law on the directive's provisions — on cessation, exclusion (including the *Lounani* and *B and D* rulings on terrorism-related exclusion), on gender-based persecution, and on the meaning of "indiscriminate violence" in *Elgafaji* and *Diakité*. These rulings have done at least as much as the text itself to harmonise practice across national asylum authorities.

## Key questions answered

### Why does an Afghan get refugee status in one EU country but not another?

Despite EU-wide rules defining who qualifies as a refugee, national asylum authorities and courts retain significant interpretive latitude — on what counts as persecution, when internal relocation within the home country is "reasonable", and how to assess country-of-origin information. Eurostat data has repeatedly shown recognition rates for the same nationality varying by tens of percentage points between member states, which is why the Commission proposed in 2016 to replace the directive with a directly applicable regulation.

### What is the difference between refugee status and "subsidiary protection" in the EU?

Refugee status applies to people facing persecution on grounds of race, religion, nationality, political opinion or membership of a particular social group — the 1951 Geneva Convention definition. Subsidiary protection is an EU-specific status for people who would face the death penalty, torture, or serious harm from indiscriminate violence in armed conflict but do not fit the persecution definition. Since the 2011 recast, the two statuses carry broadly similar rights, though member states retain some discretion over residence permit length and family reunification.

### Can EU countries refuse asylum to people fleeing war?

Not lawfully, where the situation meets the threshold of indiscriminate violence. The Court of Justice clarified in *Elgafaji* (2009) and *Diakité* (2014) that civilians fleeing armed conflict can qualify for subsidiary protection without having to prove they are individually targeted, provided the level of violence in the country or region is sufficiently severe. This was the legal basis for mass grants of protection to Syrians from 2013 onwards.

### Why is the EU replacing the directive with a regulation?

Directives must be transposed into national law and leave member states room for interpretation; regulations apply directly and uniformly. Because the 2011 directive failed to eliminate recognition-rate divergences across the EU, the Commission proposed in 2016, and EU legislators adopted in 2024, a Qualification Regulation (EU 2024/1347) that will replace the directive in 2026 as part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum.
