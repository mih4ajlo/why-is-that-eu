## What is it?

The Gender Goods and Services Directive (2004/113/EC) prohibits direct and indirect sex-based discrimination in the access to and supply of goods and services available to the public, regardless of the economic sector or whether they are provided for free or for payment. It applies to areas like housing, banking, insurance, transport, and retail — but not to the content of media, advertising, or education.

The directive’s most controversial provision was Article 5, which initially allowed member states to permit sex-based actuarial factors in insurance pricing (e.g., charging women less for car insurance because of lower statistical accident rates, or men more for life insurance). This opt-out was later struck down by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) in the 2011 *Test-Achats* ruling, mandating unisex insurance premiums and benefits after 21 December 2012.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was driven by a structural tension: EU law had comprehensively tackled gender equality in employment (through the 1976 Equal Treatment Directive and subsequent case law), but left a glaring gap in the everyday economic life of citizens — buying a house, getting a loan, or taking out insurance. A 2003 Eurobarometer survey found that 84% of EU citizens believed it was unacceptable to treat men and women differently when offering services. Yet national laws varied wildly: some countries permitted gender-based insurance pricing, others banned it, creating an uneven playing field for cross-border businesses.

The political coalition was a classic left-centre alliance in the European Parliament and Council, pushed by the then-Equality Commissioner Anna Diamantopoulou (Greece, Socialist). The proposal, tabled by the European Commission on 5 November 2003, was part of a broader push by the Prodi Commission to modernise EU anti-discrimination law, following the 2000 Race Equality Directive and the 2000 Employment Equality Directive. The European Women's Lobby strongly supported it, while the insurance industry — represented by the Comité Européen des Assurances (CEA, now Insurance Europe) — mounted a fierce lobbying campaign to preserve the use of gender as a risk factor, arguing it was based on sound actuarial science.

Timing was crucial: the directive succeeded under the 2004 Irish Presidency, in the wake of the 2004 Eastern enlargement. The accession of 10 new member states, many with weaker gender equality records, paradoxically created a sense of urgency to codify minimum standards before the EU's diversity expanded. The European Parliament backed the proposal by a large majority (306 votes in favour, 127 against, with 43 abstentions) in March 2004. The Council adopted it unanimously on 13 December 2004 — a rare achievement for a directive that had divided the insurance sector and several governments (notably the UK and Germany, which had argued for maintaining actuarial differentiation).

What changed the dynamic was a successful legal challenge: the Belgian consumer group Test-Achats brought a case before the CJEU, arguing that Article 5(2)'s opt-out violated the fundamental right to equal treatment enshrined in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights. On 1 March 2011, the CJEU ruled the opt-out invalid from 21 December 2012, forcing a rapid shift to unisex pricing across the EU — a decision that the Commission's own 2019 report estimated led to an average 5-10% increase in premiums for young women (who had previously benefited from lower car insurance rates), but a corresponding decrease for young men.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam introduces explicit gender equality competence in all areas of EU law (Article 141 TEC)
- **2000** — European Commission adopts a "Framework Strategy on Gender Equality" signalling a move beyond employment
- **5 November 2003** — European Commission proposes the directive under Anna Diamantopoulou
- **30 March 2004** — European Parliament adopts its first-reading position with 306 votes in favour
- **13 December 2004** — Council adopts the directive unanimously during the Dutch Presidency
- **21 December 2004** — Directive enters into force
- **21 December 2007** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law
- **1 March 2011** — CJEU ruling in *Test-Achats ASBL v Conseil des ministres* (Case C-236/09) invalidates Article 5(2), banning gender-based insurance premiums from 21 December 2012
- **21 December 2012** — Unisex pricing becomes mandatory across the EU for all new insurance contracts
- **2019** — European Commission publishes a report on the directive's implementation, noting "satisfactory" transposition but persistent gaps in enforcement, particularly in insurance and housing

## Impact and consequences

The directive had its most visible impact on the insurance sector. The 2011 *Test-Achats* ruling forced insurers across the EU to abandon sex-based pricing for products like car, life, and health insurance. A 2012 study commissioned by the European Commission found that the impact on premiums was modest: young women's car insurance rose by about €50–100 per year on average, while young men's fell by a similar amount. Life insurance premiums adjusted more slowly, but the overall market disruption was contained.

Beyond insurance, the directive influenced housing policies: several member states revised laws permitting landlords to set different rent levels or deposit requirements based on gender. In banking, the directive reinforced the post-2008 financial crisis trend toward risk-neutral pricing, though the Commission's 2019 report noted some banks still used indirect gender proxies (e.g., asking about marital status in loan applications).

Enforcement has been uneven. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against 15 member states for late or incomplete transposition by 2008. By 2020, the European Commission's 2020 Gender Equality Strategy acknowledged that "persistent gaps" remained, particularly in the enforcement of the burden of proof (shifting to the defendant once the complainant establishes a prima facie case) and in the availability of effective remedies.

The directive was the first EU instrument to explicitly address third-country nationals' access to goods and services, though its territorial scope remains limited to the EU. It has been cited in CJEU case law on intersectional discrimination (e.g., *Belgische Vereniging van Artsen-Specialisten*), but no major fines or enforcement actions have reached the level of the *Test-Achats* ruling.

## Key questions answered

### Can an insurance company charge different rates for men and women in Europe?

No, not since 21 December 2012. The 2004 directive initially allowed an opt-out, but the 2011 *Test-Achats* CJEU ruling struck it down. Since then, EU law requires unisex premiums and benefits for all new insurance contracts, regardless of gender. The Court reasoned that using gender as a risk factor contravened the fundamental right to equal treatment, even if backed by statistical evidence.

### Does EU law ban gender discrimination in housing and banking?

Yes. The directive covers access to housing (rentals, mortgages) and banking services (loans, accounts) if they are offered to the public. A landlord cannot refuse to rent to someone because of their gender, nor can a bank impose different interest rates or loan conditions. EU member states must provide legal remedies — including compensation — for victims of such discrimination.

### Why are there still gender pay gaps in Europe despite these laws?

The directive only covers goods and services, not employment. The gender pay gap falls under separate EU legislation: the 2006 Equal Treatment Directive (2006/54/EC) and the 2023 Pay Transparency Directive (2023/970/EU). However, the gap persists because of structural factors (occupational segregation, care responsibilities, negotiating differences) that anti-discrimination law alone cannot solve — as the European Commission's 2021 report on the pay gap made clear.

### What happened to countries that didn't apply the ban on gender-based insurance?

The Commission opened infringement proceedings against 15 member states between 2008 and 2012 for failing to transpose the directive on time. Most cases were resolved before reaching the CJEU. The most significant legal impact was the CJEU's *Test-Achats* ruling, which invalidated the opt-out and forced all member states to apply unisex pricing by December 2012 — a direct consequence of the directive's initial weak design.
