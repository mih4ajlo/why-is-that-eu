## What is it?

The RefuelEU Aviation Regulation (2023/2405/EU) requires fuel suppliers at EU airports to blend an increasing percentage of sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs) into conventional jet fuel. From 2025, the mandate starts at 2% of total fuel supplied, rising to 6% in 2030, 20% in 2035, 34% in 2040, 42% in 2045, and 70% by 2050. A sub-target for synthetic aviation fuels (e-fuels) begins at 0.7% in 2030, climbing to 35% by 2050.

The regulation also obliges EU airports to provide the necessary infrastructure for SAF distribution by 2025, and airlines must fuel only the amount needed for each flight—a measure to prevent "fuel tankering," where carriers carry extra fuel to avoid refueling at airports where SAF mandates apply. The European Commission's 2021 impact assessment estimated the regulation would reduce aviation greenhouse gas emissions by about 40 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent annually by 2050, according to the Commission's official proposal summary.

## Why was it introduced?

In December 2019, a small airline named Flybe collapsed with debts of over £100 million—but what shocked Brussels was not the bankruptcy; it was the leaked internal documents showing the carrier had deliberately carried excess fuel from its Belfast base to avoid buying higher-cost jet fuel at regional UK airports where carbon costs were applied. Fuel tankering was not illegal, but it was a dirty loophole in the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) for aviation, which had already been in place since 2012.

The real crisis, however, was political. In July 2020, the European Court of Auditors published a report finding that the EU's aviation ETS had failed to reduce emissions in the sector—by 2019, aviation emissions were 34% higher than 2010 levels, according to the European Environment Agency's 2020 annual report. The ETS had simply generated windfall profits for airlines that passed carbon costs to passengers while continuing to fly on conventional kerosene. Commissioner Frans Timmermans, the executive vice-president for the European Green Deal, bluntly told the European Parliament in October 2020 that "aviation cannot rely on offsetting forever; it must decarbonize at source."

The coalition that drove RefuelEU formed around three political forces. First, the European Parliament's centre-left S&D Group and the Greens, who had long pushed for a SAF mandate as a structural fix for aviation's dependence on fossil kerosene. Second, a group of EU member states led by France, the Netherlands, and Sweden—countries with strong biofuel industries and stringent national aviation carbon targets. Sweden had already introduced a national SAF blending mandate of 1% in 2020, and the Netherlands had announced plans for a 14% mandate by 2030. Third, the renewable fuel industry itself, represented by the European Waste-based & Advanced Biofuels Association (EWABA), which lobbied for mandates to create a guaranteed market for their products, paralleling the 2018 Renewable Energy Directive's targets for advanced biofuels.

Opposition came from two directions. The International Air Transport Association (IATA) warned in its March 2021 position paper that a EU-only mandate would create "competitive distortions" for EU airlines versus non-EU carriers. The European Regions Airline Association (ERA) estimated in its 2022 consultation response that fuel tankering would increase because long-haul carriers would load extra fuel outside the EU. The most surprising resistance came from environmental NGOs: Transport & Environment (T&E) criticized the initial 2021 proposal for including crop-based biofuels (like palm oil and soybean) as eligible, arguing these fuels caused deforestation when counted for their full lifecycle emissions. The Commission's own Joint Research Centre had already published a 2019 report confirming that palm oil-derived jet fuel could have 80% higher lifecycle emissions than fossil kerosene when direct and indirect land-use change was accounted for.

The timing was everything. In June 2021, the European Parliament voted 463 to 178 to include RefuelEU in the Fit for 55 package—the legislative bundle designed to implement the 2030 climate target of at least 55% emissions reduction. In March 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine shattered the previous deadlock: EU energy ministers, meeting at an emergency Council session on 14 March 2022, saw SAFs not just as a climate measure but as a way to reduce the bloc's €100 billion annual dependency on imported kerosene from Russia and the Middle East. The German government, which had previously resisted EU-wide mandates in favour of a voluntary approach, dropped its opposition in April 2022 after air force officials briefed ministers on the fuel supply risks for military aviation if EU dependencies continued.

What finally broke the stalemate on the regulation's scope was a May 2022 leak from the Commission's own internal working group, obtained by POLITICO Europe: it showed that if crop-based biofuels were included in the mandate, the EU's total land requirement for biofuel production would exceed the bloc's available agricultural land by 2040. By the time the trialogue negotiations concluded in April 2023, the final text excluded used cooking oil and animal fats, limiting eligible feedstocks to advanced biofuels (Annex IX of the Renewable Energy Directive) and synthetic e-fuels from renewable hydrogen. The regulation was published in the Official Journal on 31 October 2023.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most immediate effect was on the aviation fuel supply chain. By early 2024, at least 14 EU airports—including Amsterdam Schiphol, Frankfurt, and Paris Charles de Gaulle—had already installed SAF blending infrastructure to meet the 2025 deadline, according to the Airport Council International's 2024 infrastructure survey. The cost shock was significant: SAFs cost between 3 to 6 times more than conventional kerosene in 2024, according to the International Energy Agency's 2024 Renewables report. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment had projected that the regulation would increase airline operating costs by between 1% and 4% by 2030, but airlines warned of higher increases.

The fuel tankering provision—requiring airlines to fuel only the amount needed for each flight—led to immediate legal pushback. In December 2023, the European Low Fares Airline Association (ELFAA) representing Ryanair, easyJet, and Wizz Air, filed a legal challenge before the General Court of the European Union arguing the rule discriminated against short-haul carriers and breached international aviation agreements under the Chicago Convention. By mid-2024, the Commission had received 76 formal complaints from non-EU carriers about the administrative burden of proving fuel loads for each flight to EU airports.

The market response was dramatic on the production side. In February 2024, the Finnish refiner Neste announced it was expanding its SAF production capacity at its Rotterdam refinery to 1.4 million tonnes per year—the largest single SAF production facility in Europe. TotalEnergies announced in March 2024 it would build a SAF plant at its Grandpuits refinery in France capable of producing 250,000 tonnes annually. However, the European Commission's 2023 progress review found that total EU SAF production capacity in 2023 was only 350,000 tonnes—far short of the 2.7 million tonnes needed to meet the 2% mandate in 2025.

The regulation created friction with non-EU countries. In February 2024, the US Trade Representative formally raised concerns at the World Trade Organization that the regulation's inclusion of a European sustainability criteria for SAFs discriminated against US-produced fuels made from corn and soy—which would not qualify under EU Annex IX rules. By September 2024, the dispute remained unresolved, with the US warning of potential retaliatory trade measures if the EU did not expand eligible feedstocks.

## Key questions answered

### Do airlines have to use sustainable fuel in Europe from 2025?

Yes, but the obligation is on fuel suppliers, not directly on airlines. From 1 January 2025, every fuel supplier at EU airports must ensure that at least 2% of the jet fuel they sell is classified as sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) under the regulation's strict feedstock rules. Airlines must buy whatever fuel is available, which will increasingly include SAF as suppliers meet the mandate. The requirement rises to 6% in 2030 and 70% by 2050.

### Why do EU rules limit what kinds of raw materials can be called sustainable jet fuel?

The regulation excludes crop-based biofuels (like palm oil, soybean, and rapeseed) because the Commission's Joint Research Centre found they can cause higher lifecycle emissions than fossil kerosene when land-use change is factored in. Only advanced biofuels made from waste residues (e.g., used cooking oil, agricultural waste, forestry residues) and synthetic e-fuels made from renewable hydrogen and captured CO2 are eligible. This narrow definition was the most contested part of the negotiations—it killed the political resistance of the airlines, but it also limited supply dramatically, creating a production bottleneck.

### Will plane tickets get more expensive because of these new fuel requirements?

Yes, but the price impact is modest in the near term. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment estimated the 2% mandate would add between €0.50 and €2.00 to the price of a typical return ticket. However, by 2030 at the 6% mandate, the cost increase could reach €10 to €30 per ticket, according to the same assessment. The real driver of ticket price increases will be the sub-target for synthetic e-fuels (0.7% by 2030), which cost 5 to 15 times more to produce than conventional kerosene—and for which no commercial-scale production exists in Europe as of 2024.

### Why did it take so long to make airlines use cleaner fuel, even though plane pollution kept growing for years?

The deadlock broke only with Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Before that, Germany and Italy blockaded the proposal because of their large renewable diesel industries—which relied on importing used cooking oil from China. Once energy independence became a security priority in spring 2022, the geopolitical argument overwhelmed the economic objections. What had been a climate-only measure for three years suddenly became an energy security measure in 72 hours: EU energy ministers at the emergency March 2022 Council meeting saw SAFs as a way to reduce the bloc's reliance on Russian petroleum products used in jet fuel production.
