## What is it?

The Asylum and Migration Management Regulation (AMMR) replaces the Dublin III Regulation (604/2013/EU) as the core legal framework for determining which EU member state is responsible for examining an asylum application. It was adopted alongside five other pieces of legislation as part of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, which the European Commission proposed in September 2020 and which the European Parliament and Council approved in May 2024.

Under the AMMR, the old rule that the first country of entry bears primary responsibility is maintained, but it is now coupled with a binding solidarity system. If a member state faces disproportionate pressure (e.g. high arrivals at an external border), other states must contribute either by relocating asylum seekers, paying a financial contribution (set at €20,000 per relocation slot), or providing operational support. A "fast-track" border procedure is introduced for people arriving from countries with a low recognition rate of international protection (below 20% at EU level), which can last up to 12 weeks. The regulation also establishes a voluntary, then mandatory, annual solidarity pool of at least 30,000 relocations. Application will start from July 2026, giving member states two years to implement national changes.

## Why was it introduced?

The Dublin system collapsed during the 2015–2016 migration crisis. Greece, Italy, and other frontline states processed over a million arrivals in 2015 alone, while countries further north — notably Germany and Sweden — initially suspended Dublin transfers and later demanded a permanent burden-sharing mechanism. The system's principle that the first country of entry is responsible placed an unsustainable weight on southern borders, and the 2015–2017 emergency relocation decisions (Council Decisions 2015/1523 and 2015/1601) were met with fierce refusal by Hungary, Poland, and the Czech Republic, who challenged them in court. The Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU) upheld the relocation scheme in 2017 (Cases C‑643/15 and C‑647/15), but compliance was negligible: by end-2017 only about 33,000 of the planned 160,000 relocations had taken place, according to the European Commission's 2018 progress report.

The structural tension was clear: no member state could be forced to relocate under the existing treaties — or so argued Poland and Hungary. The 2018 European Council agreed on a voluntary solidarity model, but it produced almost no results. By 2019, only Malta and Cyprus had received any relocations under that voluntary scheme.

The political deadlock was broken by a series of crises and institutional shifts. In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic temporarily shut down all arrivals but also exposed how quickly emergency border closures could fragment the Schengen zone. Later that year, Belarus began artificially funneling migrants from the Middle East and Africa to the Polish, Lithuanian, and Latvian borders, weaponising irregular migration as a hybrid threat. This forced even the most reluctant eastern states to accept some form of EU-level crisis management. Meanwhile, the September 2020 New Pact proposal by Commissioner for Home Affairs Ylva Johansson, with strong backing from Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, shifted the debate from "mandatory versus voluntary" to "mandatory but flexible" — each state could choose its contribution type.

The final legislative agreement in December 2023 was a delicately balanced compromise. Southern states (Italy, Greece, Malta, Spain, Cyprus) got the binding solidarity obligation they wanted; northern and eastern states (Germany, the Netherlands) secured stricter border procedures and faster returns; the Visegrád group (Poland, Hungary, Czechia) — though still opposing any mandatory relocation — accepted the financial contribution option as a way out. Poland and Hungary were the only member states to vote against the entire Pact in the Council. The European Parliament's rapporteur was Tomas Tobé (EPP, Sweden) who steered the file through months of trilogues. The regulation passed with the required qualified majority in the Council (25 out of 27 states, representing over 65% of the EU population) and a clear majority in the Parliament (301 in favour, 272 against, 52 abstentions).

## Timeline

- **2013** — Dublin III Regulation enters into force, continuing the "first-country-of-entry" rule despite criticism from frontline states.
- **2015–2016** — Over 1.3 million asylum seekers arrive in the EU. Emergency relocation decisions adopted, but Hungary, Poland, and Czechia refuse to implement them.
- **2016** — European Commission proposes a reform of the Dublin system, including a mandatory corrective allocation mechanism. Talks stall.
- **2017** — CJEU upholds the legality of the relocation scheme. Non-compliance remains widespread.
- **2018** — European Council endorses a voluntary solidarity model; voluntarily offers total fewer than 2,000 relocations.
- **September 2020** — Commission unveils the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, including a new Regulation on Asylum and Migration Management with a "mandatory but flexible" solidarity mechanism.
- **2021–2022** — Belarus crisis pushes eastern states to accept crisis-management provisions for instrumentalisation.
- **June 2023** — Greek migrant shipwreck (Pylos) kills over 600 people, increasing political pressure for a deal.
- **December 2023** — European Parliament and Council reach a provisional agreement on the entire Pact.
- **10 April 2024** — European Parliament votes in favour (301 for, 272 against, 52 abstentions).
- **14 May 2024** — Council adopts the Pact. Poland and Hungary vote against.
- **July 2024** — Regulation published in Official Journal; enters into force.
- **1 July 2026** — Regulation becomes applicable; member states must have national implementation measures in place.

## Impact and consequences

The AMMR does not set a fixed quota; instead it creates a dynamic, annual solidarity pool. For the first time, financial contributions (€20,000 per unfilled relocation slot) are legally mandated, providing a clear deterrent against free-riding. According to the Commission's 2024 implementation roadmap, the scheme expects at least 30,000 relocations per year from 2026, though critics — including the European Council on Refugees and Exiles — question whether frontline states can effectively identify eligible applicants within the tight border procedure timelines.

The regulation introduces a screening regulation (2024/1348/EU) that requires all irregular arrivals to be photographed, fingerprinted (Eurodac database), and health/security-checked within seven days, with a potential 10-day extension. If an applicant comes from a country with an EU-wide recognition rate under 20%, they can be held at the border for a fast-track procedure. This is a major change: under Dublin III, such fast-track procedures were optional and rarely used. Italy, Greece, and Malta now have legal cover to detain people at borders longer, which NGOs like Médecins Sans Frontières argue risks violating the right to non-refoulement and due process.

Industry impact is limited, but transport carriers (airlines, ferries) face tightened liability rules: they must now ensure all passengers have valid travel documents or face fines of up to €5,000 per person, harmonising an earlier optional rule. No fines have yet been issued under the new regime, as the regulation does not fully apply until 2026.

Countries that consistently refused relocation — Hungary, Poland — will now either have to accept relocations or pay the financial contribution. If they refuse both, the Commission can initiate infringement proceedings, potentially leading to fines by the Court of Justice. As of mid-2025, neither country has indicated compliance.

## Key questions answered

### Does every EU country have to accept asylum seekers under the new rules?

No, but each country must choose either to relocate a share of asylum seekers (drawn from a yearly pool of at least 30,000 people) or to pay a €20,000 contribution per unfilled relocation slot. A third option is to provide operational support such as border guards or reception facilities. The mechanism is mandatory but flexible.

### What happens if a country refuses to participate in the solidarity scheme?

If a member state rejects both relocations and financial contributions, the European Commission can launch infringement proceedings. The Court of Justice of the EU can then impose lump-sum or penalty fines. Poland and Hungary voted against the entire Pact and have indicated they will not comply, setting up a likely legal confrontation after 2026.

### Why did the EU need to replace the Dublin system after nearly a decade of failed attempts?

The Dublin system placed all responsibility on first-entry countries, which created an asymmetric burden on southern Europe. The 2015–2016 crisis demonstrated that voluntary solidarity did not work. The New Pact succeeded because a coalition of southern states demanded binding rules, while Belarus's hybrid attacks on eastern borders convinced other reluctant states that a predictable, flexible scheme was better than chaotic ad hoc crisis management.
