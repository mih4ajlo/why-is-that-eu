## What is it?

The Renewable Energy Directive III (RED III) is the third iteration of the EU's primary legislative framework for renewable energy. It sets a legally binding target for the EU to source 42.5% of its gross final energy consumption from renewables by 2030, with an aspirational top-up to 45%. This is a near-doubling from the previous target of 32%, and represents the most significant increase in the EU's renewable ambition since the first directive in 2009.

The directive also introduces mandatory measures to accelerate permitting for renewable energy projects, designating them as "overriding public interest" which limits legal challenges, and creates "go-to areas" where environmental assessments are streamlined. For the first time, the directive includes binding sub-targets for specific sectors: transport (29% renewable energy by 2030), industry (1.6% annual increase in renewable use), and buildings (49% renewable share by 2030). A key innovation is a target for 42% of hydrogen used in industry to come from renewable fuels of non-biological origin by 2030, rising to 60% by 2035.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's dramatic ambition leap is directly traceable to a single event: Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Before that, the European Commission under Ursula von der Leyen had proposed, in July 2021 as part of the "Fit for 55" package, raising the 2030 target to 40% from the 32% set in RED II. This was considered ambitious but politically achievable.

The invasion shattered that calculus. On 8 March 2022, the Commission published its "REPowerEU" communication, explicitly framing renewable energy as a matter of energy security, not just climate policy. Russia had supplied roughly 40% of EU gas imports before the war, and the Kremlin's weaponisation of energy supplies — cutting gas flows through Nord Stream 1 in stages from June 2022 — created an immediate, acute crisis. By September 2022, European gas prices had spiked over tenfold from 2020 lows. The political logic of the renewable transition flipped overnight: it was no longer about meeting Paris Agreement obligations but about preventing economic collapse and geopolitical blackmail.

The coalition that passed the directive was a new, wartime alliance. The European Parliament's lead rapporteur, Markus Pieper (EPP, Germany), pushed for the higher 45% target, finding support from the Socialists & Democrats, Renew Europe, and the Greens — an unusual cross-spectrum coalition that would not have formed on pre-war climate politics alone. In the Council, Germany and the Netherlands, previously cautious on binding targets, shifted their positions dramatically as their economies faced deindustrialisation from high gas prices. The only significant opposition came from Poland and Hungary, which argued that higher targets would raise costs for coal-dependent economies; Poland's opposition weakened after the Commission's "REPowerEU" chapters offered additional funding for energy transition.

The 42.5% binding target finalised in the trilogue on 30 March 2023 was a compromise between Parliament's demand for 45% and the Council's preference for 40%. The inclusion of the aspirational "top-up" to 45% was the specific mechanism that broke the deadlock: it gave Parliament a political win while Council members could point to the legally binding figure being lower.

What changed from RED II's 2018 negotiations was the introduction of mandatory permitting time limits. Under RED II, the Commission had merely "encouraged" member states to streamline permits; by 2022, the European Court of Auditors had found that permitting delays of 4-7 years were the single biggest bottleneck for renewable deployment, and the EESC had documented cases of wind farms being blocked for over a decade. The new rules — a maximum of 12 months in "go-to areas" and 24 months elsewhere, renewable projects declared "overriding public interest" — were an explicit response to industry pressure from WindEurope and SolarPower Europe, which had lobbied the Commission's Energy Platform Task Force in 2022 with data showing that 80 GW of wind projects were stuck in permitting queues.

- **Feb 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; EU gas prices begin explosive rise
- **Mar 2022** — Commission issues REPowerEU communication, reframing renewables as security imperative
- **Jun 2022** — Parliament votes in committee to raise target to 45%
- **Dec 2022** — Council agrees common position for 40% target; formal trilogues begin
- **Mar 2023** — Political agreement reached in trilogue: 42.5% binding target with 45% aspirational goal
- **Sep 2023** — Directive formally adopted by Council and Parliament
- **Nov 2023** — Published in Official Journal of the European Union; entered into force (20 days after publication)
- **May 2024** — Member states due to transpose the directive into national law (18-month deadline)
- **2025-2026** — First reporting deadlines for member states on permitting acceleration and sub-targets
- **2030** — Deadline to meet the 42.5% binding target

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact has been on the EU's regulatory environment. By declaring renewable projects "overriding public interest," member states can now override many environmental impact assessment requirements for projects within "go-to areas" — a provision that has been controversial with environmental NGOs, with ClientEarth arguing in a December 2023 legal analysis that it could undermine the Birds and Habitats Directives.

Industry response has been strongly positive. By early 2024, the Commission reported that nine member states — including Germany, France, and Spain — had already transposed the fast-track permitting rules into national law, ahead of the 2025 deadline. WindEurope's 2024 annual report noted that permitting times for onshore wind had fallen from an average of 5.4 years to under 2 years in several states that adopted the "go-to area" concept early.

However, the directive's success is uneven. The 42% renewable hydrogen target for industry by 2030 is widely seen as unrealistic: as of early 2024, Europe had less than 0.3 GW of electrolyser capacity operational, against the 40 GW the Commission projected in REPowerEU would be needed. Carbon capture and storage projects, which are competing for the same finance, have also raised questions about the hydrogen target's feasibility.

The transport sector's 29% renewable target was partially met in 2023 (at roughly 18%), but progress depends heavily on e-fuels and advanced biofuels, production of which remains at demonstration scale. The Commission's 2023 State of the Energy Union report warned that Italy, Poland, and four other member states were "off-track" for meeting the 2030 target largely due to underdeveloped transport decarbonisation strategies.

The directive has also had significant distributional consequences. Higher renewable targets increase electricity costs in the short term for member states with less-developed grid infrastructure. Romania and Bulgaria, which rely on coal for 30% and 40% of generation respectively, received specific transition funding under the Social Climate Fund to offset these impacts, but the EIB's 2023 analysis found that the 12 Cohesion member states face renewable deployment costs equivalent to 2.3% of GDP by 2030, compared to 1.1% for wealthier states.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU suddenly set much higher renewable energy targets in 2023?

Russia's February 2022 invasion of Ukraine transformed renewable energy from a climate issue into a security imperative. With 40% of EU gas imports coming from Russia, the Kremlin's weaponisation of energy supplies caused prices to spike tenfold, threatening economic collapse. The EU's previous target of 32% had been set in 2018 during normal geopolitical conditions; the 2023 crisis made a dramatic increase politically unavoidable.

### How fast do renewable projects need to get built under the new rules?

The directive mandates maximum permitting times of 12 months for projects in designated "go-to areas" and 24 months elsewhere. Before this, permitting delays of 4-7 years were common across the EU. Projects are also declared "overriding public interest," which limits legal challenges. Member states must identify go-to areas by 2025, and the Commission reported in early 2024 that several countries had already cut permitting times by more than half.

### Does every EU country have to meet the same renewable target individually?

No. The 42.5% target is binding for the EU as a whole, but each member state has a negotiated individual contribution based on its GDP, renewable potential, and starting point. Wealthier states like Sweden (already at over 60% renewables) have higher targets, while coal-dependent states like Poland have lower ones. However, the Commission can enforce compliance through infringement proceedings if the collective trajectory is missed.

### Can countries ignore the fast-track permitting rules if they don't want more renewables?

No. The permitting acceleration measures are mandatory for all member states. If a country fails to designate "go-to areas" or fails to meet the 12/24-month deadlines, the Commission can open infringement proceedings. By early 2024, the Commission was already issuing letters of formal notice to several member states that had not begun transposition. The Council's legal service confirmed in a 2023 opinion that the permitting rules are directly binding.
