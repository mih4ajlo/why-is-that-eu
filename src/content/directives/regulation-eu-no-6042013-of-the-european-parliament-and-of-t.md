## What is it?

The Dublin III Regulation (604/2013/EU) is the legal framework that determines which EU Member State is responsible for examining an application for international protection. Its core principle, the "first country of entry" rule, dictates that the Member State through which an asylum seeker first entered the EU irregularly is responsible for processing their claim. The regulation also includes criteria based on family unity, visa issuance, and unaccompanied minors' best interests.

Recast from the earlier Dublin II Regulation (343/2003/EC), Dublin III introduced procedural safeguards, including a mandatory personal interview for applicants, the right to an effective remedy against transfer decisions, and a mechanism for early warning and preparedness to prevent systemic failures. Despite these improvements, the regulation's design assumed a relatively balanced distribution of asylum seekers—a premise that collapsed during the 2015 migration crisis.

## Why was it introduced?

The Dublin III Regulation was the direct result of the Greek asylum system's collapse. In January 2011, the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) ruled in M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece that transferring asylum seekers back to Greece under Dublin II violated Article 3 of the European Convention on Human Rights due to degrading detention conditions and systemic deficiencies in Greece's asylum procedure. The Court found that Belgium knew or should have known that Greece could not provide adequate protection. This landmark ruling effectively shattered the presumption that all EU Member States provided equivalent protection—the very foundation of the Dublin system.

The European Commission, then under President José Manuel Barroso and Home Affairs Commissioner Cecilia Malmström (a Swedish liberal), had already tabled a recast proposal in December 2008. But the legislative process had stalled. The M.S.S. ruling in January 2011, followed by the Court of Justice of the European Union's (CJEU) similar ruling in N.S. v. Secretary of State for the Home Department in December 2011 (Case C-411/10), broke the deadlock. Both courts declared that transfers could not continue to Member States where systemic deficiencies existed. The Commission responded by accelerating the recast, and the Regulation was adopted by the European Parliament and Council in June 2013, entering force on 19 July 2013.

The political coalition that supported Dublin III was fragile. Southern Member States, led by Greece and Italy, demanded solidarity mechanisms, arguing they bore disproportionate responsibility as first-entry countries. Northern states, particularly Germany and Sweden under Angela Merkel's and Fredrik Reinfeldt's governments, opposed mandatory burden-sharing but supported procedural safeguards to avoid ECtHR censure. The Visegrád group (Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic, Slovakia) was largely silent during negotiations but would later become a vocal opposition block. The final text included an "early warning, preparedness and crisis management mechanism" (Article 33) as a compromise—a non-binding solidarity clause that satisfied no one fully.

## Timeline

- **1990** — The Dublin Convention is signed, establishing the principle that the first EU country of asylum is responsible for examining an application
- **2003** — Dublin II Regulation (343/2003/EC) replaces the Convention, codifying the system into EU law
- **21 January 2011** — ECtHR rules in M.S.S. v. Belgium and Greece that transfers to Greece violate the ECHR; the Irish government immediately suspends all Dublin transfers to Greece
- **21 December 2011** — CJEU ruling in N.S. v. Home Secretary (UK) confirms that Member States cannot transfer asylum seekers where systemic deficiencies exist, and must examine applications themselves if necessary
- **December 2008** — Commission (Malmström) publishes original recast proposal; negotiations stall until court rulings
- **11 June 2013** — Regulation 604/2013/EU (Dublin III) is adopted by Parliament and Council
- **19 July 2013** — Dublin III enters into force, applying to all Member States (except Denmark and Ireland initially opting out, though Ireland later opted in)
- **14 June 2015** — The Greek-Turkish border route surges; in August 2015, Germany suspends Dublin transfers for Syrians, effectively abandoning the system
- **2016** — Commission proposes a Dublin IV recast (September 2016) with mandatory relocation quotas; it remains blocked in Council to this day
- **2020** — The New Pact on Migration and Asylum proposes a "mandatory solidarity" mechanism but fails to achieve consensus
- **2023** — EU negotiates a Crisis and Force Majeure Regulation to modify Dublin rules during emergencies, agreed in December 2023 but not yet in force

## Impact and consequences

The Dublin III Regulation has actually reduced asylum transfers, not enabled them. According to Eurostat data cited in the Commission's 2019 evaluation, only about 15% of asylum seekers eligible for Dublin transfer are actually transferred, and the success rate of transfers—when carried out—is below 30% due to absconding, legal appeals, and national court suspensions. During the 2015 crisis, Greece received 857,000 arrivals but processed only a fraction of Dublin requests; between 2015 and 2018, Greece lodged 63,000 incoming transfer requests but only 6,000 transfers to other Member States were executed.

The regulation's most visible consequence was its de facto suspension during 2015. On 25 August 2015, Germany—then receiving an estimated 7,000 asylum seekers daily—unilaterally stopped all Dublin transfers for Syrians, effectively declaring the regulation inoperable. The Commission's emergency relocation decisions (2015/1523 and 2015/1601), which proposed relocating 160,000 asylum seekers from Greece and Italy, were based on treaty Article 78(3) and bypassed Dublin rules entirely. Hungary, the Czech Republic, Poland, and Slovakia challenged these relocation decisions before the CJEU; the Court upheld them in September 2017, but compliance remained minimal.

Member State courts and national human rights bodies have systematically blocked transfers. The German Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG) in 2014 ruled that Dublin transfers to Greece were unconstitutional absent proof of systemic improvement. The Italian Supreme Court of Cassation in 2019 ordered suspension of transfers to Bulgaria due to detention conditions. By 2023, over 40 ECtHR interim measures halting Dublin transfers were in effect.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU still use the rule that forces asylum seekers to apply in the first country they enter, even though it clearly broke down during the 2015 migration crisis?

The Dublin III Regulation has survived not because it works, but because Member States cannot agree on a replacement. The Commission's 2016 Dublin IV proposal, which sought mandatory relocation quotas, was blocked by the Visegrád group, particularly Hungary's Viktor Orbán and Poland's Mateusz Morawiecki, who argued that forced relocations violated national sovereignty. The 2020 New Pact on Migration and Asylum tried a different approach—"mandatory solidarity" with a choice between relocation, financial contributions, or operational support—but remained deadlocked. The regulation's survival represents a political failure, not a technical one.

### How do EU asylum rules that share personal data between countries interact with Europe's data privacy laws?

Dublin III requires systematic sharing of biometric data (fingerprints) through the EURODAC system, which the regulation governs. This creates a direct tension with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the EU's Charter of Fundamental Rights. In 2022, the European Data Protection Board (EDPB) issued guidelines noting that Dublin III's data retention periods (up to 10 years for asylum seekers) may violate the proportionality principle under Article 5(1)(c) GDPR. Several national data protection authorities, including the Irish DPC, have raised concerns, but no court has yet ruled on the conflict.

### Why did the UK keep following EU rules that send asylum seekers back to the first EU country they entered, all the way until 2020?

The United Kingdom had an opt-out under Protocol 21 of the Treaty of Lisbon but chose to opt into Dublin III via a Council Decision in 2014. This was because the UK saw the regulation as a tool to return asylum seekers to the first Member State of entry, primarily France through the Channel crossings. After Brexit, the UK left the Dublin system on 31 January 2020, though the transition period maintained cooperation until December 2020. Since then, the UK and EU have reached no replacement agreement, leading to the current Channel crossing crisis.

### Can EU countries send asylum seekers back to Turkey or other non-EU countries under the current asylum responsibility rules?

No. The Dublin system only applies between EU Member States plus Norway, Iceland, Switzerland, and Liechtenstein (associate countries). The EU-Turkey Statement of March 2016, which allowed Greece to return migrants to Turkey as a "safe third country," was not a Dublin transfer but an informal political agreement. In 2020, the CJEU ruled in Case C-540/19 that Turkey could not be considered a "first country of asylum" under Dublin for Greece, effectively limiting the statement's legal force.
