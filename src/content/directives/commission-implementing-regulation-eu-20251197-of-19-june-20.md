## What is it?

On 19 June 2025, the European Commission adopted the first-ever measure under its International Procurement Instrument (IPI), a regulation designed to pressure non-EU countries into opening their public procurement markets. This specific measure targets economic operators and medical devices from the People’s Republic of China, restricting their access to EU public procurement contracts for medical devices.

Practically, this means that Chinese companies bidding for EU public tenders to supply items like surgical masks, ventilators, MRI machines, or hospital beds will face a price-adjustment penalty of up to 20-30% on their bids, effectively making them less competitive. The measure applies to procurement above a certain threshold and covers all EU member states. It does not apply to private-sector procurement.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was not a single scandal but a decade of structural imbalance. The EU’s public procurement market, worth roughly €2 trillion annually or about 14% of EU GDP according to the Commission, is legally open to foreign bidders under the WTO’s Government Procurement Agreement (GPA). China, however, became a signatory to the GPA in 2014 but secured extensive exemptions—covering only central government contracts, not sub-central bodies, and excluding state-owned enterprises and major sectors like medical devices.

The concrete breaking point came during the COVID-19 pandemic. In early 2020, as EU member states scrambled to procure ventilators, PPE, and vaccines, Chinese companies aggressively bid for EU contracts while China’s own public procurement market remained largely closed to European suppliers. According to the Commission’s 2021 impact assessment, between 2017 and 2020, Chinese companies won EU public procurement contracts worth €1.3 billion, while EU companies secured less than €50 million in Chinese public contracts—a 26:1 imbalance.

Political momentum built under Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, who made “Open Strategic Autonomy” a centrepiece of her 2019-2024 mandate. Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton, a former French finance minister and tech CEO, was the driving force. He argued publicly that the EU’s openness was being exploited by competitors who “keep their markets under lock and key.” The European Parliament backed the IPI regulation by a wide margin in June 2022—524 votes in favour, 99 against, with 38 abstentions.

The coalitions were unusual. Pro-trade groups like BusinessEurope supported the IPI as a tool to force market opening, while protectionist industries in steel and construction had long demanded retaliation against China. Opposition came from a coalition of free-trade Northern European states (Sweden, Netherlands, Denmark) and the European Services Forum, which feared retaliation against European service providers in China. The German automotive industry also lobbied heavily against the IPI, warning that Chinese retaliation could hurt BMW and Volkswagen in the Chinese market—Germany’s largest export destination.

The deadlock was broken by the pandemic. Before COVID, the Commission had proposed an IPI in 2012 and again in 2016, but both attempts stalled due to member state divisions. The pandemic made the procurement imbalance a visible political liability. In June 2021, the Council reached a general approach, and the final regulation was adopted in June 2022. The implementing regulation for medical devices followed three years later, after a 10-month investigation (June 2024 to April 2025) confirmed that China had not taken sufficient steps to open its market.

## Timeline

- **2012** — European Commission first proposes an International Procurement Instrument; proposal stalls due to member state opposition.
- **2014** — China joins the WTO Government Procurement Agreement with extensive exemptions excluding medical devices and sub-central procurement.
- **2016** — Commission revives the IPI proposal; again fails to get Council approval.
- **2020** — COVID-19 pandemic reveals massive asymmetry: Chinese companies win EU health procurement while EU firms are locked out of China.
- **June 2021** — EU Council reaches a general approach on the IPI, breaking years of deadlock.
- **June 2022** — European Parliament adopts the IPI Regulation (2022/1031/EU) with 524 votes in favour, 99 against.
- **June 2024** — Commission opens an investigation into Chinese procurement practices for medical devices under the IPI.
- **April 2025** — Commission concludes China has taken “no meaningful steps” to open procurement; recommends a measure.
- **19 June 2025** — Commission adopts Implementing Regulation 2025/1197, imposing the first IPI measure on Chinese medical devices.

## Impact and consequences

The immediate impact is on Chinese medical device exporters and EU hospitals. Chinese firms like Mindray (patient monitors, ultrasound), Shandong Weigao (IV catheters, dialysis supplies), and Haier Biomedical (vaccine refrigerators) will now face a significant price penalty in EU public tenders. The regulation’s preamble explicitly notes that the measure applies to all procurement above €5 million for works and €1 million for supplies and services.

EU hospitals and health systems face a trade-off: they will pay more for medical devices if Chinese suppliers were the lowest bidder. The Commission’s impact assessment projected an average 3-8% increase in procurement costs for affected contracts, though it argued that domestic producers and alternative suppliers (e.g., from the US, Japan, or Germany) would benefit.

The broader geopolitical impact is significant. China immediately condemned the measure as “discriminatory and protectionist” through its Mission to the EU. Beijing has threatened retaliatory measures against European medical device exports to China, which were worth €4.2 billion in 2024 according to Eurostat. The EU’s medical device industry, led by companies like Siemens Healthineers, Philips, and GE HealthCare, now faces potential exclusion from the Chinese market.

Legal challenges are expected. The regulation will likely be contested at the WTO, where China may argue it violates the non-discrimination principle of the GPA. However, since China’s GPA commitments explicitly exempt medical devices, the EU argues the IPI measure is a lawful countermeasure under the relevant GPA provisions.

## Key questions answered

### Why was the first IPI measure targeted at medical devices and not steel or construction?

Medical devices were chosen for three reasons. First, the COVID-19 pandemic made the vulnerability of EU supply chains in this sector politically salient—voters remember the scramble for masks and ventilators. Second, China’s exclusion of medical devices from its GPA commitments was unambiguous, making a strong legal case. Third, the healthcare sector had broad political support across EU member states, unlike steel, where large consumers (automotive, construction) would have fiercely opposed restrictions that raised input costs.

### Why did the IPI take over a decade to pass, and what finally broke the deadlock?

The IPI failed twice (2012, 2016) due to a coalition of free-trade Northern European states and export-dependent German industry, who feared Chinese retaliation. The deadlock was broken by the COVID-19 pandemic, which turned a technical trade issue into a visible political crisis. The pandemic showed EU citizens that their tax money was being used to buy Chinese medical equipment while European firms could not access the Chinese market. Commission President von der Leyen’s “Open Strategic Autonomy” agenda provided the political framework. Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton personally pushed the dossier through the Council.

### Will this measure be expanded to other sectors or countries?

Yes, the IPI regulation is a framework tool, not a one-off. The Commission has signalled it will investigate other sectors where procurement imbalance exists, including construction, railway equipment, and information technology. The regulation also applies to other non-GPA countries beyond China, such as India, Brazil, and Turkey. However, each measure requires a full investigation and implementing regulation, so expansion will be gradual. Any escalation depends on the targeted country’s willingness to negotiate market access.
