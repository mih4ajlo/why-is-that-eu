## What is it?

Regulation (EU) No 1151/2012 consolidates and strengthens the EU's quality schemes for agricultural products and foodstuffs, primarily the Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) and Protected Geographical Indication (PGI) labels. It also covers Traditional Specialities Guaranteed (TSG) and optional quality terms like "mountain product." PDO requires that every stage of production, processing, and preparation occurs in the specific region, while PGI requires only that at least one stage links to the geographical area.

The regulation applies to cheeses, meats, wines, spirits, fruits, vegetables, and other agricultural products. It sets out a two-tier application process: national authorities first examine a product's application, then the European Commission—after a three-month opposition period—approves or rejects the registration. Once registered, the name is protected across all 27 EU member states, and producers must follow a binding product specification, including geographical boundaries, production methods, and raw material sourcing.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation's most dramatic trigger was not a food scandal but a structural tension in EU agricultural trade policy that dated back to the 1992 MacSharry reforms. By the early 2000s, the European Commission—under Agriculture Commissioner Franz Fischler (Austrian, 1995-2004)—recognised that the EU's quality label system, first set up in 1992 via Regulation 2081/92, was riddled with legal vulnerabilities. The system had survived a 1999 World Trade Organisation (WTO) dispute brought by the United States and Australia (DS174), where a WTO panel ruled in 2005 that the EU's registration system discriminated against non-European producers. Commissioner Mariann Fischer Boel (Danish, 2004-2010) oversaw a 2006 revision (Regulation 510/2006) that opened the system to non-EU countries, but the core problem remained: small producers struggled to defend their names against industrial imitators, and consumers had little trust in the labelling.

The political coalition that pushed for the 2012 reform was unusual. On one side, Southern European member states—Italy, France, Spain, Greece—saw geographical indications as a bulwark against globalisation and a tool to protect rural economies. Italy's agriculture minister, Giancarlo Galan (2010-2011), and his successor, Mario Catania (2011-2013), were vocal supporters, pointing to studies showing that PDO/PGI products commanded price premiums of 20-50% over generic equivalents. On the other side, Northern European countries—notably the UK, Netherlands, and Germany—initially resisted, viewing the system as protectionist. However, the 2008 financial crisis shifted the debate: as commodity prices collapsed, farmers demanded higher value-added channels. The European Parliament's Agriculture Committee, chaired by Paolo De Castro (Italian, S&D), drafted a report in 2011 that argued GI products could create 2-3 times more rural employment than conventional agriculture.

The final text, proposed by Agriculture Commissioner Dacian Cioloș (Romanian, 2010-2014) in December 2010, was adopted by the European Parliament on 14 June 2012 with 559 votes in favour, 47 against, and 31 abstentions. The Council adopted it on 12 October 2012. Key concessions included simplifying the registration process for small producers and allowing collective trademark protections alongside GI registrations. The UK and Netherlands extracted a promise that the system would not block trade liberalisation talks.

The specific event that unblocked earlier deadlocks was the 2010 "Parmesan" crisis. German retailer Aldi had sold a block of hard cheese labelled "Parmesan" that was not produced in Parma. The Consorzio del Parmigiano-Reggiano sued in German courts, but the case exposed that the existing 2006 regulation provided weak enforcement—German courts allowed the label because it was considered a "generic" term. The Commission's 2010 impact assessment found that counterfeit food products cost EU producers €2.3 billion annually, with 8-10% of GI products sold in third countries being fakes. This commercial damage, combined with the Parmigiano-Reggiano case, made the political case for stronger enforcement.

## Timeline

- **1992** — Regulation 2081/92 establishes first EU PDO/PGI system for agricultural products
- **1999** — US and Australia file WTO complaint (DS174) against EU's GI system
- **2005** — WTO panel rules EU system discriminates against non-EU countries
- **2006** — Regulation 510/2006 replaces 1992 system, opens to non-EU applicants
- **December 2010** — European Commission proposes Regulation 1151/2012 under Commissioner Cioloș
- **14 June 2012** — European Parliament adopts text (559-47-31)
- **12 October 2012** — Council adopts regulation
- **3 January 2013** — Regulation enters into force (20 days after Official Journal publication)
- **3 January 2014** — Application date for most provisions; new simplified application procedures begin
- **2015** — First major test: Commission rejects Romania's application for "Telemea de Ibănești" PGI due to insufficient geographical link, upheld by EU General Court in 2017
- **2023** — Commission publishes evaluation report (SWD(2023) 431) finding 3,500+ registered PDO/PGI names, with estimated sales value of €77 billion in 2022

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed the EU food landscape. By 2023, over 3,500 PDO/PGI names were registered, with the Commission's 2023 evaluation reporting sales valued at €77 billion in 2022—up from €54.3 billion in 2017. Italy dominated with over 300 registrations (Parmigiano-Reggiano alone generating €4.2 billion annually, according to the Consorzio), followed by France (264) and Spain (198). The average PDO/PGI product sold for a 23% premium over non-GI equivalents, according to the Commission's 2022 "Study on economic value of EU quality schemes."

Enforcement remains uneven. The regulation gave national authorities stronger powers: in 2021, Italian customs seized 2,100 tonnes of counterfeit "Mozzarella di Bufala Campana" PDO, worth estimated €70 million. However, a 2022 Commission report found that only 12 member states had fully implemented the enforcement mechanisms by 2020. The UK's withdrawal from the EU in 2020 created a gap: while UK producers could no longer apply for new EU GIs, the Withdrawal Agreement protected existing UK-registered names like Scotch Whisky and Melton Mowbray Pork Pies.

Industry response was mixed. Large producers welcomed the clarity: Nestlé and Lactalis developed dedicated GI supply chains. But small artisan producers struggled with the bureaucracy—a 2019 European Court of Auditors report found that application times averaged 2.9 years, triple the legal maximum. The 2012 regulation attempted to fix this by introducing a "simplified" online application system, but the Court of Auditors found it still required 30+ annexes per application.

## Key questions answered

### Why are the rules for protected food names different for wines and spirits compared to other foods?

Wines and spirits are covered by separate legislation—Regulation 1308/2013 (Common Market Organisation for wines) and Regulation 2019/787 (spirit drinks)—because they have historically distinct production systems and EU market structures. The 2012 regulation specifically excludes them from its scope. Wine GIs date back to the 1970s EU wine regime, while spirits gained protection in 1989. The separation allows tailored rules: wine GIs focus on vintage and grape variety, while food GIs focus on production methods and recipes.

### Why did the EU reject Canada's list of protected regional food names during trade talks?

During CETA negotiations (2009-2016), the EU insisted Canada protect around 200 European GIs, but Canada initially offered only 50. The deadlock broke in 2013 when the EU's Agriculture Commissioner, Dacian Cioloș, and Canada's Trade Minister, Ed Fast, reached a compromise: Canada protected 145 "high-value" GIs (like Parmigiano-Reggiano and Prosciutto di Parma) while allowing "semi-generic" terms like feta and asiago to remain unenforceable in Canada. This deal was critical for securing Southern European support for the final CETA trade agreement.

### Why didn't EU rules on protected regional foods prevent fights over food names after Brexit?

When the UK left the EU in 2020, the Withdrawal Agreement preserved existing UK-registered GIs, but the UK government decided in 2021 to create its own GI system, modelled on the EU's but with faster processing. The result was confusion: UK producers like Cornish Clotted Cream (PGI) retained EU protection, but new UK applications could not be recognised in the EU without trade negotiations. The UK's Geographical Indications (Amendment) Regulations 2023 created a parallel system, but no mutual recognition agreement exists with the EU as of 2024.
