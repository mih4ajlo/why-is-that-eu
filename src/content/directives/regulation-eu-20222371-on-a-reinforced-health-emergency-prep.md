## What is it?

The HERA Regulation creates a permanent European Health Emergency Preparedness and Response Authority — a Directorate-General-level body within the European Commission, operational from early 2022. Its mandate is to prevent, detect, and respond rapidly to cross-border health emergencies by coordinating the development, procurement, and equitable distribution of medical countermeasures (vaccines, therapeutics, diagnostics, personal protective equipment).

HERA does not replace national health systems but acts as a central coordinator: it conducts threat assessments (the "EU Health Threats Radar Board"), builds strategic stockpiles (resCEU), co-funds research and production capacity for priority threats (over 40 million doses of monkeypox vaccine secured in 2022), and can trigger emergency funding and joint procurement mechanisms under the EU Health Union framework.

## Why was it introduced?

The initial shock came on 30 January 2020, when the World Health Organization declared COVID-19 a Public Health Emergency of International Concern. Within weeks, European governments discovered that the existing system — the Health Security Committee, the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC), and voluntary joint procurement under the 2013 Decision on Serious Cross-Border Health Threats — was catastrophically inadequate. No EU body had the mandate or resources to buy masks, ventilators, or vaccines at scale. Member states scrambled unilaterally, triggering export bans (Germany on 4 March 2020, France on medical masks) and price spirals.

The structural tension was clear: EU treaties assign public health primarily to member states, but a pandemic respects no borders. The single market allowed free movement of goods and people, yet when crisis hit, capitals retreated behind national borders, fragmenting supply chains and destroying negotiating leverage.

The political coalition for change formed slowly. In March 2020, European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen established a temporary "Coronavirus Task Force" under Stella Kyriakides (Commissioner for Health). But real momentum came in September 2020, when von der Leyen's State of the Union address proposed a permanent "European Biomedical Research and Development Agency" — the seed of HERA. Germany's Presidency of the Council (July–December 2020) pushed the concept harder, shocked by the April 2020 "mask scandal" where intergovernmental bidding wars drove N95 prices from €0.50 to over €10 in days.

The decisive trigger was the vaccine procurement fiasco of January 2021. The Commission had contracted Pfizer-BioNTech (November 2020) for 200 million doses, but AstraZeneca announced on 22 January 2021 that it would deliver only 31 million of the promised 120 million doses by March. The EU had no legal mechanism to enforce production or threaten redirection of manufacturing capacity. Member states erupted in fury — Belgium, the Netherlands, and Denmark called for immediate export controls, while France's President Macron warned of "European solidarity failures." The Commission scrambled, creating a temporary Vaccine Sharing Mechanism and invoking Article 122 TFEU for centralised procurement, but the damage was done.

The timing was ripe: the Portuguese Presidency (January–June 2021) made Health Union a priority, the European Parliament passed resolutions on 10 July 2020 (498 votes in favour) and 19 June 2020 calling for a permanent health response structure. The final legislative proposal was published on 11 November 2020 as part of the EU4Health programme package, with an initial budget of €2.7 billion (2021–2027). The Council adopted its position on 23 March 2021, and after a swift trilogue, the regulation passed with overwhelming support — 567 votes for, 26 against in Parliament on 5 October 2022.

Two earlier attempts had failed: the 2013 proposal to strengthen ECDC's mandate (to include threat response) was blocked by member states fearing sovereignty loss, and the 2016 "Joint Procurement Agreement" for medical countermeasures remained voluntary and underfunded. What broke the deadlock was COVID-19 — a crisis so severe that even traditionally sovereignty-sensitive health ministries accepted the need for a central EU authority with real powers, including the ability to invoke the Single Market Emergency Instrument (SMEI) and cooperate with NATO on strategic stockpiling.

## Timeline

- **March 2020** — Commission creates temporary Coronavirus Task Force under Stella Kyriakides; first EU-wide joint procurement of PPE begins
- **September 2020** — von der Leyen proposes permanent European Biomedical R&D Agency in State of the Union
- **11 November 2020** — Commission publishes legislative proposal for HERA as part of EU4Health, budget €2.7 billion
- **December 2020** — Council (German Presidency) adopts partial general approach, supports creation of HERA
- **January 2021** — AstraZeneca delivery shortfall triggers political crisis; calls for binding EU procurement powers intensify
- **March 2021** — Council (Portuguese Presidency) finalises its position on HERA Regulation
- **September 2021** — Commission launches interim HERA (before legal adoption) to start threat monitoring and stockpiling
- **5 October 2022** — European Parliament adopts Regulation 2022/2371 (567 votes for, 26 against, 26 abstentions)
- **28 December 2022** — Regulation enters into force; HERA becomes permanent Commission body
- **January 2023** — HERA adopts first annual work programme; identifies 15 priority health threats and 5 categories of medical countermeasures
- **July 2024** — HERA coordinates EU response to Mpox (monkeypox) outbreak, securing 215,000 doses of Jynneos vaccine through joint procurement

## Impact and consequences

The immediate impact was institutional: HERA merged and formalised the temporary COVID-19 structures, giving the Commission a permanent health crisis unit with around 200 staff and a mandate that includes threat anticipation, stockpiling, and production capacity monitoring. The first test came with the 2022–2023 Mpox outbreak, where HERA's early threat detection (via the Health Threats Radar Board on 7 May 2022) allowed joint procurement of 215,000 vaccine doses within weeks — a speed impossible under the pre-2021 system.

However, member states retained veto power over many decisions. The Regulation explicitly states that HERA cannot compel production or redistribute strategic stocks without Council approval — a compromise demanded by Germany and the Netherlands during trilogue negotiations. The Commission's original proposal to give HERA direct "mandatory licensing" powers during emergencies was removed after heavy lobbying by pharmaceutical industry associations (EFPIA, Vaccines Europe) and opposition from 12 member states.

The budget remains relatively small for the ambition: €2.7 billion over 7 years (2021–2027), compared to the €120 billion the EU spent on COVID-19 vaccines alone. The European Court of Auditors warned in a September 2022 report that HERA's "resource constraints and coordination gaps" could hinder its effectiveness.

The Regulation also changed the relationship between EU institutions and industry. HERA now publishes a "priority threat list" every two years and can sign "advance purchase agreements" with manufacturers — a model learned from the €2.4 billion Pfizer-BioNTech contract. Critics argue this creates dependency on a few large producers; the Parliament's INTA committee noted in a 2022 brief that only 4 manufacturers supply 90% of EU vaccine production.

## Key questions answered

### How did COVID-19 change Europe's ability to handle health emergencies?

Before March 2020, the EU had no power to buy masks, ventilators, or vaccines for member states. Each country negotiated alone, leading to price spikes (PPE costs rose 20x in weeks) and national export bans that broke single market rules. After COVID-19, the EU created a permanent Health Emergency Preparedness and Response Authority (HERA) that can jointly purchase medical countermeasures, maintain strategic stockpiles, and coordinate threat monitoring across all 27 member states.

### Can the EU force a pharmaceutical company to produce vaccines during a pandemic?

No — not directly. The HERA Regulation does not give the Commission power to mandate production or redirect manufacturing lines during an emergency, despite early proposals to do so. Pharmaceutical industry lobbying (by EFPIA, Vaccines Europe) and resistance from 12 member states (led by Germany and the Netherlands) removed these powers in trilogue negotiations. The EU can use the Single Market Emergency Instrument (SMEI) to require information from companies, but cannot compel production.

### Who pays for Europe's health crisis stockpiles?

Member states pay through national budgets for their own strategic stockpiles. The EU funds a central "resCEU" reserve (part of the EU Civil Protection Mechanism) and HERA's threat monitoring and procurement coordination — budgeted at €2.7 billion for 2021–2027. When HERA procures vaccines or treatments jointly, it typically uses a mix: EU funds for the contract (advance purchase agreements) and member states pay per dose (the "pull" mechanism). The EU also contributed €1.7 billion to COVAX for global equity, but the main financing for national stockpiles remains at member state level.

### Does the new EU health crisis authority replace national health agencies like France's ANSM or Germany's RKI?

No — HERA coordinates but does not replace. National health agencies remain responsible for domestic disease surveillance, clinical guidelines, and procurement. HERA's role is supranational: threat scanning across the continent, joint procurement negotiation to improve buying power, and maintaining EU-level strategic reserves. The Regulation explicitly respects Article 168 of the TFEU, which gives member states primary responsibility for health policy. The compromise was deliberately crafted so no country would feel its health sovereignty was transferred to Brussels.
