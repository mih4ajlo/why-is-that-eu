## What is it?

MiCA (Regulation 2023/1114) is the EU's bespoke rulebook for crypto-assets that fall outside existing financial services law. It covers three categories: asset-referenced tokens (stablecoins backed by baskets of currencies, commodities, or other crypto), e-money tokens (single-currency stablecoins like USDC), and "other" crypto-assets including utility tokens. Bitcoin and Ether are caught by the trading and service-provider rules but not the issuer rules, since they have no identifiable issuer.

The regulation creates a passporting regime: a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorised in one Member State can operate across all 27. It imposes capital requirements, governance rules, custody obligations, market abuse prohibitions, and white-paper disclosure requirements modelled loosely on the prospectus regime. Stablecoin issuers face the heaviest burdens — full reserve backing, daily redemption rights, and, for "significant" stablecoins, direct supervision by the European Banking Authority.

MiCA does not cover NFTs (unless issued in fungible series), decentralised finance protocols without an identifiable issuer, or central bank digital currencies. Those gaps are being studied for a possible MiCA II.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was Facebook's Libra. When Mark Zuckerberg unveiled the project in June 2019 — a global stablecoin backed by a basket of currencies, distributed to 2.7 billion Facebook users — French Finance Minister Bruno Le Maire declared within weeks that Libra "cannot and must not happen" on European soil. At the September 2019 Eurogroup meeting in Helsinki, Le Maire and German Finance Minister Olaf Scholz issued a joint statement calling for an outright ban, warning that a private currency from Silicon Valley threatened European monetary sovereignty. ECB board member Benoît Cœuré, then chairing the G7 stablecoin working group, called Libra a "wake-up call."

The Commission had been studying crypto since the 2018 FinTech Action Plan, but Libra accelerated everything. Valdis Dombrovskis, then Executive Vice-President for Financial Stability, announced the Digital Finance Package on 24 September 2020, with MiCA as its centrepiece. The political logic was twofold: prevent another Libra (the project, renamed Diem, was abandoned in January 2022 after sustained regulatory hostility), and give legitimate crypto firms a single set of rules instead of 27 national approaches that ranged from Malta's permissive licensing to outright hostility in some Eastern European states.

The collapse of TerraUSD in May 2022 — a $40 billion algorithmic stablecoin that imploded in days — and the bankruptcy of FTX in November 2022, which left 1.2 million creditors including European retail investors out of pocket, hardened the political mood during the trilogues. Negotiators led by rapporteur Stefan Berger (EPP, Germany) tightened the stablecoin rules, capped large non-euro stablecoin transactions at 1 million per day to protect the euro's role, and added environmental disclosure requirements after a Greens push to ban proof-of-work failed by a narrow margin in March 2022.

- **30 June 2024** — Stablecoin (ART/EMT) provisions apply
- **30 December 2024** — Full application; CASP authorisation regime begins
- **2025** — National transitional periods (up to 18 months) allow existing firms to continue operating while seeking authorisation

## Impact and consequences

The first casualty was Binance's BUSD partner Paxos, which had already been forced out by US regulators; Binance itself delisted non-compliant stablecoins for European users in March 2024 ahead of the June deadline. Tether's USDT — the world's largest stablecoin at over $140 billion — was delisted by Coinbase, Kraken, and Crypto.com for EU users, since Tether declined to seek MiCA authorisation. Circle, issuer of USDC, secured an EMI licence in France in July 2024 and became the first major stablecoin issuer to operate fully under MiCA.

By mid-2025, national regulators had issued fewer than 30 full CASP authorisations, with hundreds of applications in pipelines at AMF (France), BaFin (Germany), and CSSF (Luxembourg). Smaller exchanges complained that capital requirements (€50,000 to €150,000 depending on services) and governance demands favoured incumbents. Several firms relocated headquarters within the EU to jurisdictions with faster authorisation timelines — Malta and France emerged as preferred bases, while Estonia, once a crypto hub, saw an exodus after tightening its pre-MiCA rules.

The regulation is being copied: the UK, Hong Kong, Singapore, and Brazil have all drawn on MiCA's stablecoin reserve rules in their own frameworks. The Trump administration's GENIUS Act of 2025, governing US stablecoins, mirrors MiCA's full-reserve approach despite political differences.

## Key questions answered

### Why does MiCA cap large non-euro stablecoins at 1 million transactions per day?

The cap, in Article 23, is explicitly designed to protect monetary sovereignty. ECB officials including Fabio Panetta argued during the trilogues that widespread use of dollar-denominated stablecoins for everyday EU payments could undermine the transmission of monetary policy and the euro's role. The threshold is a soft brake: issuers exceeding it must stop issuing until usage falls back.

### Why are NFTs largely excluded from MiCA?

The Commission's original draft had no clear NFT carve-out, but lobbying from digital art platforms and the gaming industry produced Recital 10, which excludes "unique and non-fungible" tokens. The exclusion is narrow — fractionalised NFTs and large series issued as collections are caught — and ESMA has signalled it will look through labels to economic substance. A review clause requires the Commission to reassess NFT treatment by 2025.

### Why did the proof-of-work ban fail in MiCA?

A draft amendment in early 2022 would have effectively banned Bitcoin from EU exchanges by prohibiting crypto-assets using "environmentally unsustainable consensus mechanisms." Industry lobbying — particularly from Blockchain for Europe and Bitcoin advocacy groups — combined with warnings from EPP and Renew MEPs that the ban was technologically illiterate, defeated the amendment 32-24 in ECON. Instead, MiCA requires CASPs to disclose the energy consumption and climate impact of the crypto-assets they list.

### Why didn't MiCA cover DeFi?

The Commission concluded that "fully decentralised" protocols without an identifiable issuer or service provider could not be regulated using a traditional licensing model — there is no entity to license. Article 142 mandates a Commission report on DeFi by 30 December 2024, which is expected to inform a potential MiCA II covering decentralised lending, DEXs, and staking services.
