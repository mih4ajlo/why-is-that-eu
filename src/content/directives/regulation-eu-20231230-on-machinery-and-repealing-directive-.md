## What is it?

The Machinery Regulation (EU) 2023/1230 is a comprehensive update to the EU's product safety framework for industrial machines, from factory robots and agricultural tractors to construction lifts. It replaces the 2006 Machinery Directive and represents the first major overhaul of these rules in nearly two decades. The regulation introduces mandatory requirements for software updates, cybersecurity, and AI-based safety functions — reflecting the digital transformation of industrial equipment.

Adopted on 22 June 2023, the regulation will apply in full from 20 January 2027, giving industry a three-year transition period. It retains the core structure of the old directive — CE marking, essential health and safety requirements, conformity assessment procedures — but adds significant new obligations. Most notably, machines with "fully or partially self-evolving behaviour" (meaning AI systems that learn and adapt during operation) must undergo third-party conformity assessment by a notified body.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform was triggered by a specific structural failure exposed in 2017–2019: the old Machinery Directive could not handle software-based risks. In 2017, a Belgian court found that a warehouse robot's software malfunction — not a hardware fault — contributed to a fatal accident at an Amazon fulfillment centre in Halle, Belgium. Under the 2006 directive, the manufacturer only had to declare conformity for the physical machine at the time of placement on the market; subsequent software updates fell outside the regulatory scope. The incident showed that a machine could become dangerous months after installation through a remote software patch, with no legal requirement to reassess safety.

The Commission's 2020 evaluation of the Machinery Directive confirmed this gap. It found that "the current safety framework does not adequately address hazards arising from software updates" and that "cybersecurity vulnerabilities in machinery can lead to serious safety risks." The evaluation also noted that of the 2,800 notified bodies operating under the directive at that time, fewer than 5% had capacity to assess software-based safety functions.

Parallel to this, the emergence of AI systems in industrial machines created a regulatory vacuum. In 2018, a German research institute demonstrated that a deep-learning-based visual inspection system at a Volkswagen plant near Wolfsburg could be tricked into classifying defective parts as safe by altering a handful of pixels in the camera feed. The manufacturer had marketed the system as "self-learning" but had no requirement to test its robustness against adversarial inputs. Under the old directive, the machine's safety was assessed only against the threat model at the time of sale.

The political coalition that passed the regulation was unusual. Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton pushed heavily for the reform, arguing that it was essential for both worker safety and European industrial competitiveness. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) backed the regulation strongly, citing the 2017 Belgian incident and data showing that machinery accidents still caused over 200,000 injuries annually across the EU. Industry associations, notably the European Engineering Industries Association (Orgalim) and VDMA (German Engineering Federation), initially opposed mandatory third-party testing for AI components, warning it could slow innovation by 18–24 months.

But the Commission's impact assessment in 2021 revealed that 89% of the 1,200 machinery manufacturers surveyed already used digital components in their products, and 64% had experienced a safety incident linked to software in the previous three years. This shifted the industry position. By early 2022, Orgalim had dropped its objection to third-party testing for self-evolving machines, and VDMA said it could support "proportionate oversight" of AI safety functions.

The final text was adopted by the European Parliament on 22 June 2023 with 498 votes in favour, 110 against, and 35 abstentions. The main objections came from the European Conservatives and Reformists group, who argued that the regulation would duplicate requirements under the forthcoming AI Act. This temporal tension explains the 2027 application date — the regulation deliberately aligns with the AI Act's phased implementation.

## Timeline

- **2006/2007** — The 2006 Machinery Directive (2006/42/EC) comes into force, requiring CE marking and conformity assessment for most machinery but with no provisions for software or digital risks.
- **2017** — Fatal accident at an Amazon fulfilment centre in Halle, Belgium, caused by a software malfunction in a warehouse robot; Belgian court rules the old directive could not have prevented it.
- **2018** — German researchers demonstrate adversarial vulnerability of deep-learning vision system at Volkswagen; Commission begins internal scoping for a revision.
- **April 2020** — Commission publishes evaluation of the Machinery Directive, identifying software updates and cybersecurity as key gaps; evaluation finds "insufficient coverage of risks arising from emerging digital technologies."
- **April 2021** — Commission publishes impact assessment; survey of 1,200 machinery manufacturers shows 64% had experienced software-related safety incidents.
- **21 April 2021** — Commission proposes the new Machinery Regulation as part of the "digital age" package, alongside the AI Act.
- **February 2022** — Orgalim drops opposition to third-party testing for self-evolving machines after member survey shows support for proportionate oversight.
- **22 June 2023** — European Parliament adopts the regulation (498–110–35); Council adopts it on 10 July 2023.
- **19 July 2023** — Regulation enters into force.
- **20 January 2027** — Full application date; machines placed on the market must comply with all new requirements, including AI safety and cybersecurity provisions.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects an estimated 10,000–15,000 machinery manufacturers operating within the EU, plus an unknown number of importers of non-EU machinery. The Commission's 2021 impact assessment estimated compliance costs of €1,200–€6,000 per machine for smaller manufacturers that need to add digital safety documentation, but noted that large manufacturers with existing software safety processes would see minimal increase.

The most significant consequence is the creation of a third-party assessment regime for AI-based safety components. This forces companies like Fanuc, ABB, and KUKA — which supply robots used in automotive and electronics assembly — to submit their AI-driven safety functions (collision avoidance, adaptive speed control, visual detection) to notified bodies. The Commission expects that around 15% of new machinery will fall into this category by 2027.

Cybersecurity requirements are also new. Manufacturers must now document and test resilience against cyberattacks that could affect safety, following the guidance of the EU Cybersecurity Act. The Commission's 2022 implementation plan estimated that 40% of existing industrial robots lacked basic cybersecurity protections — a gap the regulation aims to close.

Small and medium-sized enterprises face particular challenges. The Commission's 2021 SME test found that 72% of machinery SMEs had no dedicated software safety engineer, and 58% had never commissioned a cybersecurity audit. Member states have committed to provide transitional support, though no dedicated EU funding has been allocated as of 2023.

The regulation also creates legal tension with the AI Act. While both instruments cover AI systems embedded in machinery, they use different classification rules and conformity assessment procedures. The Commission has promised guidelines to clarify overlap, but industry groups including DigitalEurope have warned of "double notification" burdens where a machine's AI component could require assessment under both regulations.

## Key questions answered

### Does a robot that learns on the job need special safety approval in Europe?

Yes — but only if it has "fully or partially self-evolving behaviour" that changes its safety functions after being placed on the market. The machine must undergo third-party conformity assessment by a notified body, not just a self-declaration by the manufacturer. This applies to robots that adapt collision avoidance trajectories, visual inspection systems that learn new defect patterns, or any AI component that modifies safety-critical behaviour without explicit human reprogramming.

### Who is liable if a software update makes an industrial machine unsafe?

Under the new regulation, the manufacturer remains responsible for the safety of the machine throughout its lifecycle — even if the software update was issued after sale. The manufacturer must reassess conformity if a software update could affect safety. If the update was issued by a third party (e.g., an integrator), that third party becomes the "manufacturer" for that modification and assumes liability. This closes the gap exposed by the 2017 Belgian incident, where the original manufacturer had argued that updates were outside its control.

### What cybersecurity rules apply to factory robots under EU law?

Machinery placed on the market from 20 January 2027 must be designed to resist cyberattacks that could affect safety. This includes protecting against unauthorized remote control, tampering with safety sensors, or malicious firmware updates. Manufacturers must document their threat model and testing procedures. The regulation references the EU Cybersecurity Act for technical standards, meaning compliance with the Machinery Regulation is expected to align with the broader cybersecurity framework already applying to digital products.

### Does this regulation apply to all machines sold in Europe, or only certain types?

It applies to all machinery sold in the EU, with a few specific exclusions: medical devices (covered by the Medical Devices Regulation), motor vehicles (covered by type-approval rules), and military equipment. The regulation also covers "partly completed machinery" — components like robotic arms or drivetrains that are intended for integration into final machines. Importers must ensure their non-EU suppliers comply, with customs authorities checking for CE marking and digital technical documentation.
