## What is it?

The Postal Services Directive created the legal framework for a single European market in postal services. It required EU member states to gradually dismantle the existing monopolies held by national postal operators — the "reserved areas" that gave incumbents like Deutsche Post, La Poste, and Royal Mail exclusive rights to deliver letters under a certain weight. At the same time, the directive mandated that every citizen, regardless of where they lived, must have access to a minimum set of postal services at affordable prices — the Universal Service Obligation (USO).

The directive was implemented in three stages. Full market opening for letters under 50 grams was required by 2011, with later extensions for new member states and small countries. The USO covers at least one collection and delivery per working day, five days a week, for all letters up to 2 kilograms and parcels up to 20 kilograms. National regulators were established in each member state to oversee the transition and ensure fair competition.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a political crisis of legitimacy. By the early 1990s, European postal monopolies were visibly failing. In Italy, mail took over a week to travel 100 kilometres. In Greece, letters regularly vanished. A 1992 Commission survey found that 40% of cross-border mail within the EU took longer than three days to arrive. The system was not a public service — it was a tolerated failure propped up by state protection.

The structural tension was straightforward: national postal monopolies had been designed for a world of paper letters and physical mail, but they were being asked to operate in an economy where fax machines were ubiquitous, email was emerging, and private couriers like DHL, UPS, and TNT were taking the profitable urban business. The existing system meant that consumers paid high prices for slow service, while the incumbents used their monopoly profits to cross-subsidise inefficient operations rather than modernise.

The coalition that pushed the directive through was unusual. The European Commission's Internal Market Directorate-General (DG XV), then led by Commissioner Mario Monti, formed an alliance with the European Parliament's socialist group and the European Consumers' Organisation (BEUC). Monti argued that postal services were the "last great monopoly" in the single market and that liberalisation was the only way to force incumbents to innovate. The Parliament's rapporteurs, notably Welsh Labour MEP Ken Stewart, insisted that liberalisation be paired with a legally binding universal service guarantee — a trade-off that secured left-wing support.

The opposition was fierce and came from two directions. The largest national postal operators — Deutsche Post, La Poste, and Royal Mail — lobbied aggressively through the European Postal Union (PostEurop) and their national governments. Germany's Post Minister Wolfgang Bötsch argued that the directive would destroy 100,000 jobs. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) orchestrated strikes across six member states in 1995, with 300,000 postal workers walking out on a single day in France alone. The French government, under President Jacques Chirac, threatened to veto the directive in the Council.

What changed was the combination of a Commission President determined to push through liberalisation and a series of scandals that undermined the incumbents' credibility. Jacques Santer, Commission President from 1995, made postal liberalisation a flagship of his single-market agenda. In 1996, Deutsche Post was fined 24 million Deutschmarks by the German cartel office for predatory pricing in the parcel market, revealing that the German monopoly was using letter revenues to undercut private competitors. That same year, a Commission investigation found that La Poste was cross-subsidising its courier services using funds intended for rural post offices.

The breakthrough came in the Council of Ministers in December 1996, when the German and French governments — facing their own domestic pressures — agreed to a compromise. The full opening would be phased over 12 years, and incumbents could keep exclusive rights for letters under 350 grams until 2003. Small member states like Luxembourg got an extension until 2009. The European Parliament approved the final text on 15 December 1997 by 403 votes to 101, with 25 abstentions.

## Timeline

- **1988** — The Commission's "Green Paper on the Development of the Single Market for Postal Services" identifies national monopolies as barriers to cross-border trade
- **1992** — The Maastricht Treaty creates the legal basis for EU action on services of general economic interest
- **10 December 1996** — Council of Ministers reaches political agreement on the directive after a year of deadlock
- **15 December 1997** — European Parliament adopts the final text by 403 votes to 101
- **15 December 1998** — Directive enters into force; member states have 12 months to transpose into national law
- **1 January 2003** — First phase of liberalisation: reserved area reduced to letters under 100 grams
- **1 January 2006** — Second phase: reserved area reduced to letters under 50 grams
- **1 January 2011** — Full market opening for all member states except new entrants (deadline extended to 2013 for Cyprus, Malta, and other small states)
- **2013** — The final extension expires; all EU postal markets are legally open to competition
- **2018** — Commission evaluation report finds that liberalisation has reduced prices by 20-30% for business mail but has not extended universal service to rural areas as anticipated

## Impact and consequences

The directive's impact was uneven and often contradictory. In Germany and the Netherlands, full liberalisation led to rapid innovation — Deutsche Post bought DHL in 2002 and became a global logistics giant. In the UK, Royal Mail was privatised in 2013 but struggled to maintain rural services, with the number of post offices falling from 14,300 in 2000 to 11,500 by 2020, according to the UK's National Audit Office.

The USO itself became a battleground. Incumbents in France and Italy argued that mandatory five-day delivery was impossible in the age of email, while consumer groups pointed to the Commission's 2018 evaluation, which found that only 6% of EU households still relied on postal services for essential communication — down from 38% in 2000. The Commission's own 2013 report acknowledged that the number of letters delivered across the EU had fallen by 20% since 2008, while parcel volumes had risen by 60% due to e-commerce.

Countries that struggled most to comply were those with difficult geography and low population density. Greece was fined €10 million by the European Court of Justice in 2014 for failing to guarantee five-day delivery in all regions, according to the Commission's infringement database. Portugal's CTT was forced to maintain 2,100 rural post offices despite losing €50 million annually on them, according to Anacom, the national regulator.

The directive also created an entirely new regulatory architecture. By 2020, all member states had established independent national regulatory authorities for postal services, costing an estimated €120 million annually to operate, according to the Commission's 2018 evaluation. The European Regulators Group for Postal Services (ERGP), established in 2010, became a forum for coordinating cross-border enforcement.

## Key questions answered

### Can a private company deliver my mail in the EU?

Yes, in every EU member state since 2011 for letters over 50 grams, and for all letters since 2013. Private operators like Sandd in the Netherlands and UK Mail in Britain compete with former monopolies. However, the incumbent operator in most countries still retains the universal service obligation and must deliver to every address five days a week, while private operators can choose profitable routes.

### Does every EU country have to guarantee mail delivery five days a week?

Yes. The directive requires at least one collection and one delivery per working day, five days a week (Monday to Friday), for all EU citizens and businesses. However, some member states have been granted temporary exemptions for remote islands or mountain regions. The Commission has threatened infringement proceedings against countries that fail to meet this standard, successfully fining Greece in 2014.

### Why did postal liberalisation take 15 years to implement?

The phased approach was a political compromise. German and French governments, representing the two largest postal incumbents, insisted on a long transition to protect jobs and allow state-owned operators to restructure. Small member states like Luxembourg and Malta worried that liberalisation would make universal service uneconomical in their territories and received extensions to 2013. The 2008 Postal Services Directive (2008/06/EC) set the final deadline.

### Who benefited more from postal liberalisation — businesses or consumers?

Business mailers benefited most. According to the Commission's 2018 evaluation, bulk business mail prices fell by 20-30% across the EU after liberalisation. Consumer letter prices, however, were more stable, as incumbents were allowed to increase single-item prices to compensate for lost revenue from business mail. The biggest consumer impact was in parcel delivery, where competition from private operators like DHL, Hermes, and DPD drove prices down and delivery speeds up.
