## What is it?

Regulation (EC) 1333/2008 creates a single, legally binding list of all food additives authorised for use across the European Union. It replaces a patchwork of national and pre-existing EU directives dating back to the 1960s. The regulation classifies additives by function (e.g., preservatives, colours, sweeteners, emulsifiers) and sets maximum permitted levels per food category.

The regulation also mandates that additives must be labelled by their functional category and either name or E-number on food packaging. It introduced a "sunset clause" requiring the European Commission to review all previously authorised additives by 2020, ensuring ongoing safety reassessment.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was a 2005 ruling by the European Court of Justice (Case C-217/04, *United Kingdom v European Parliament and Council*) that struck down the legal basis for the existing additives directive (94/35/EC) because it had been adopted under the wrong Treaty article. This created an urgent legal gap: member states' national authorities could no longer rely on the old framework to enforce bans or authorisations.

But the deeper structural tension was older. Since the 1970s, food additives had been governed by separate, fragmented directives — one for colours (94/36/EC), one for sweeteners (94/35/EC), and a general framework (89/107/EEC). Each had been amended repeatedly, creating compliance costs for industry and confusion for enforcement agencies. The 2004 enlargement to 10 new member states, many with inconsistent national lists, made the situation untenable. The Commission's 2006 impact assessment estimated that the fragmentation cost food manufacturers an additional €500 million per year in duplicate testing and legal fees.

The coalition for reform was unusual. Consumer groups like BEUC (the European Consumer Organisation) and food safety advocates, led by European Parliament rapporteur Ása Westlund (a Swedish Social Democrat), pushed for stricter safety standards and mandatory labelling. Large multinational food companies like Nestlé, Unilever, and Danone quietly supported harmonisation because it reduced their regulatory burden across 27 markets. Opposition came from small and medium-sized manufacturers, particularly in southern Europe, who feared losing traditional products containing locally authorised additives.

The political breakthrough came in 2007, when Commissioner Markos Kyprianou (DG SANCO, 2004-2008) made food additives reform part of his broader "Better Regulation" agenda, arguing that the existing system was both unsafe and anti-competitive. The European Parliament's Environment Committee voted 54-2 in favour of the consolidated list approach. The final regulation passed with 98% of MEPs voting in favour.

What changed was the combination of a legal shock (the 2005 ECJ ruling) and a regulatory window: the Commission was already drafting the General Food Law Regulation (EC 178/2002) and the new additives regulation was positioned as its logical extension. The 2006 "Southampton study" on artificial colours and hyperactivity provided a further scientific justification for tightening rules, though the regulation itself does not specifically ban the six colours later subject to mandatory warning labels.

## Timeline

- **2005** — European Court of Justice strikes down Directive 94/35/EC for wrong legal basis.
- **November 2006** — Commission publishes impact assessment on food additives consolidation.
- **July 2007** — Commission proposal for Regulation (EC) 1333/2008 published.
- **January 2008** — European Parliament approves with 624 votes in favour, 16 against.
- **November 2008** — Council adopts regulation.
- **20 January 2010** — Regulation enters into force.
- **1 June 2013** — Mandatory application date for new labelling rules.
- **2018-2020** — Commission completes review of all permitted additives; several, like titanium dioxide (E171), are withdrawn or subject to new restrictions.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation reduced the number of permitted food additives from over 1,500 existing on national lists to a single EU list of approximately 320 substances. Companies had to reformulate products or seek re-authorisation for hundreds of additives. The 2010-2013 transition period cost the food industry an estimated €300-400 million in compliance, according to the Commission's 2009 implementation report.

The most visible consequence was the 2013 requirement to label artificial colourings (like sunset yellow FCF and tartrazine) with the warning "may have an adverse effect on activity and attention in children." This directly followed from the 2007 Southampton study published in *The Lancet*, which the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) later assessed but did not endorse for a full ban. Several major manufacturers, including Kellogg's and Kraft, voluntarily removed artificial colours from their European products rather than carry the label.

EFSA's role expanded significantly: it became the sole scientific authority for evaluating additive safety, a function previously shared between member states. By 2020, EFSA had reviewed 324 substances under the sunset clause, recommending the withdrawal of 15 (including titanium dioxide in 2021).

## Key questions answered

### Why are additives listed with E-numbers?

E-numbers originated from the 1962 EU colour directive and were formalised in Regulation 1333/2008 to provide a uniform identification system. "E" stands for "Europe" — the number is the additive's unique identifier across all EU languages. This eliminates confusion from different brand names or chemical terms and makes enforcement by national food safety authorities straightforward.

### Why were six artificial colours subject to a warning label instead of banned?

The 2007 Southampton study found a small but statistically significant link between mixtures of six artificial colours (including sunset yellow, quinoline yellow, and allura red) and hyperactivity in children. However, EFSA's 2008 opinion concluded the study's methodology was insufficient to justify a ban on individual additives. Commissioner Kyprianou brokered a compromise: mandatory warning labels from 2010, with a review clause if new evidence emerged. This satisfied both industry (which avoided a ban) and consumer groups (which got a visible warning).

### Why did the regulation allow titanium dioxide until 2022?

Titanium dioxide (E171) was originally included in the permitted list because EFSA's 2004 assessment considered it safe at authorised levels. In 2016, France raised new concerns about its nanoparticle form. The Commission asked EFSA to re-evaluate; its 2021 opinion concluded that genotoxicity could not be ruled out. Under Regulation 1333/2008's review clause, the Commission then banned E171 from February 2022. The delay illustrates the regulation's central tension: it requires conclusive scientific evidence before banning an additive, which can take years.
