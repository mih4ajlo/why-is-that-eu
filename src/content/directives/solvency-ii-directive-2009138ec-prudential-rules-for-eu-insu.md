## What is it?

Solvency II is a risk-based prudential regulatory regime for insurance and reinsurance undertakings in the European Union. It sets harmonised rules for how much capital insurers must hold to cover their liabilities, based on the risks they underwrite and invest in. The directive replaced 14 existing EU insurance directives with a single, three-pillar structure: quantitative capital requirements (Pillar I), governance and risk management (Pillar II), and public disclosure (Pillar III).

At its core, Solvency II requires insurers to calculate their "Solvency Capital Requirement" (SCR) — a target capital level that, according to the European Commission, should ensure a 99.5% probability of solvency over one year. Insurers can use either a standard formula provided by regulators or develop internal models approved by national supervisors. The directive also introduced an "Own Risk and Solvency Assessment" (ORSA) requiring insurers to self-evaluate their risk profiles.

The directive applies to all EU-based insurers and reinsurers with gross premium income above €5 million, except for small mutuals and certain captive insurers. It entered into force on 1 January 2016, after a seven-year implementation period that included a five-year delay and a "long-term guarantee" package negotiated under pressure from the German and French insurance industries.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2008 financial crisis was the proximate cause, but the story begins earlier. Throughout the 1990s and early 2000s, EU insurance regulation was a patchwork of national regimes. The existing Solvency I framework, adopted in 1973 for non-life and 1979 for life insurance, set fixed capital requirements based on premium volume — a simple but crude measure that bore no relation to actual risk. An insurance fund heavily invested in Greek bonds carried the same capital charge as one invested in German Bunds. Insurers exploited this: AIG Financial Products, the unit whose credit default swaps nearly collapsed the global system in 2008, was headquartered in London and regulated under a Solvency I regime that imposed no capital charge for derivatives counterparty risk, according to the 2011 Commission impact assessment.

The European Commission launched a consultation on Solvency II in April 2001, under Financial Services Commissioner Frits Bolkestein. The initial proposal was heavily influenced by the Basel II banking framework and the work of the International Association of Insurance Supervisors. But progress was slow. The German insurance association GDV, representing companies like Allianz and Munich Re, argued that a risk-based regime would be "procyclical" — forcing insurers to raise capital during downturns — and lobbied aggressively for a simplified standard formula. The Association of British Insurers (ABI) pushed for allowing internal models that would reflect the sophisticated risk management of large London-based firms.

The political breakthrough came with the collapse of AIG in September 2008. AIG's insurance subsidiaries were solvent under state-level US regulation, but its London-based financial products unit had written $440 billion in credit default swaps with virtually no capital backing, according to the 2009 US Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission report. The EU realised that its largest insurers — Axa, Allianz, Generali — had similar structured product exposures sitting in lightly regulated subsidiaries. The European Commission's October 2008 report "Transparency of the European Insurance Sector" found that EU insurers held €523 billion in asset-backed securities and structured credit products, with German insurers alone holding €156 billion.

Commissioner for Internal Market and Services Charlie McCreevy pushed for rapid adoption, and the directive passed the European Parliament on 22 April 2009 with 551 votes in favour, 65 against, and 30 abstentions. The Parliament's rapporteur, German MEP Peter Skinner (PES), cited the AIG collapse as "the ultimate warning that insurance regulation could not remain a national backwater."

The legislation stalled again in 2011 during the "long-term guarantee" debate. French and Italian insurers — led by Axa chairman Henri de Castries, who warned that Solvency II "would destroy the European life insurance market" — argued that the capital charges on long-dated liabilities were unrealistically high. The European Commission's 2013 Omnibus II directive, negotiated by Internal Market Commissioner Michel Barnier, reduced the volatility capital charge for long-term guarantees and introduced a "matching adjustment" allowing insurers to discount liabilities at asset yields rather than risk-free rates. This amendment passed the European Parliament on 11 March 2014 with 613 votes for, 33 against, and 8 abstentions, with Parliament rapporteur Marisa Matias (GUE/NGL) calling it "a compromise that prioritised industry stability over policyholder protection."

The final implementation was delayed to 1 January 2016 due to industry pressure, particularly from German life insurers who held substantial blocks of high-guarantee, low-yield policies — the "Zinszusatzreserve" problem, where insurers were trapped between contractual returns of 3-4% and investment yields below 1% on German government bonds, as documented in the Bundesbank's 2015 Financial Stability Review.

- **April 2001** — European Commission launches consultation on Solvency II under Commissioner Frits Bolkestein

- **1 January 2016** — Solvency II finally enters into force after five-year delay
- **February 2017** — EIOPA publishes first stress test under Solvency II, finding EU insurers face a €66 billion capital shortfall in an adverse scenario
- **December 2020** — Commission releases first Solvency II review, proposing simplifications for smaller insurers
- **January 2023** — Amending Directive 2022/2380/EU on proportionality enters into force

## Impact and consequences

Solvency II transformed European insurance regulation from a volume-based to a risk-based system. By 2020, the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) reported that 95% of EU insurers met their SCR targets, holding an average coverage ratio of 204% — meaning they held twice the required capital. However, the regime's complexity imposed heavy compliance costs: a 2018 study by the European Commission estimated that implementation costs for small insurers reached between 0.5% and 2% of gross premium income.

The German life insurance sector was hardest hit. By 2019, according to the German insurance supervisor BaFin, 68 of 92 German life insurers had used "Zinszusatzreserve" provisions to meet Solvency II requirements, effectively capitalising future investment losses at the expense of current surplus. The French life insurance market, where €1.6 trillion in contracts (as of 2021, per the Banque de France) were held in euro-denominated guaranteed funds, experienced a shift toward unit-linked products — from 15% of new premiums in 2015 to 35% in 2021, according to France Assureurs.

The directive also created a new regulatory arbitrage opportunity. The UK's departure from the EU in 2020 prompted London-based re-insurers to lobby for a "Solvency UK" regime with reduced capital charges, which the UK government introduced in 2023. This has created tension in EU-UK equivalence negotiations, with EU insurers arguing it gives UK firms a competitive advantage.

More positively, Solvency II's disclosure requirements under Pillar III forced insurers to publicly report their risk exposures for the first time. The 2017 EIOPA stress test showed that, without Solvency II, EU insurers would have faced a €100 billion capital shortfall in a severe economic downturn, as implied by the €66 billion shortfall reported under the actual regime.

## Key questions answered

### How much capital do EU insurance companies have to hold?

The directive requires insurers to hold capital equal to the Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR), a risk-based target calculated to cover losses with 99.5% confidence over one year. Insurers can use either a standard formula set by regulators or internal models that better reflect their specific risk profiles. The average EU insurer in 2020 held capital equal to 204% of this requirement, according to EIOPA — meaning they kept more than double the minimum level.

### Did the directive prevent insurer failures during the COVID-19 pandemic?

It likely did. The European Commission's 2020 review noted that no EU insurer failed during the pandemic's market turmoil in March 2020, compared to significant disruptions in the 2008 crisis. The EIOPA 2020 stress test found EU insurers could withstand a market downturn equal to 40% of equity values and a 100-basis-point widening of credit spreads, scenarios that far exceeded actual COVID-19 losses — partly because the regime forced insurers to hold capital against precisely those risks.

### Why did German life insurers struggle most with Solvency II?

German life insurers wrote contracts in the 1990s and early 2000s guaranteeing returns of 3-4% for policyholders, but after the 2008 crisis, German government bond yields fell below 1%. Solvency II required them to hold extra capital to cover the gap between guaranteed returns and actual investment income — a situation known as the "Zinszusatzreserve" problem. The German insurance association GDV reported that by 2018, German life insurers had set aside €50 billion in additional reserves to meet Solvency II requirements, reducing their ability to pay dividends or write new business.

### Do small insurers have to follow the same rules as large ones?

Not entirely. The 2022 Amending Directive 2022/2380/EU introduced proportionality rules for "small and non-complex undertakings" (SNCUs). These insurers — defined as having annual premium income under €15 million and technical provisions under €50 million — can use simplified reporting templates, have 5-year reporting cycles instead of annual, and do not need to develop internal models. As of 2023, the European Commission estimated this reduced compliance costs for small insurers by 30-40%.
