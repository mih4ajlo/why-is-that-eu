## What is it?

The Geo-blocking Regulation (2018/302/EU) is a piece of EU consumer and internal market law that makes it illegal for online traders to block or limit access to their websites, apps, or goods and services based on a customer’s nationality, place of residence, or place of establishment. It came into force on December 3, 2018, and became fully applicable across all EU member states on December 3, 2020, after a two-year transition period for certain sectors.

In practice, the regulation targets three specific forms of discrimination: preventing a customer from accessing a website or redirecting them to a different version (e.g., a German user forced onto a French version of a site); applying different general terms and conditions (including prices) to customers based on their location; and refusing to sell or deliver goods to a customer in another member state. It does not, however, force traders to deliver physical goods across borders, nor does it harmonise national pricing or VAT rules. The regulation is part of the European Commission’s broader Digital Single Market strategy, first outlined by President Jean-Claude Juncker in 2015.

## Why was it introduced?

The geo-blocking regulation was driven by a long-recognised structural tension in the EU’s internal market: the promise of a borderless digital economy was being systematically undermined by commercial practices that carved up the single market along national lines. As the Commission’s 2015 Digital Single Market strategy noted, only 15% of consumers bought online from another EU country, and 63% of cross-border purchase attempts by consumers were blocked or redirected. This was not a technical problem — it was a deliberate business strategy.

The political coalition that pushed the regulation through was unusual and aggressive. The key driver was the European Parliament’s Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO), led by rapporteur Róża Thun (EPP, Poland), who treated geo-blocking as a fundamental internal market failure. She received strong backing from then-Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker, who made the Digital Single Market his signature project, and from Competition Commissioner Margrethe Vestager, who publicly denounced “digital borders” as incompatible with the EU’s founding treaties. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) provided relentless evidence: their 2015 investigation found that online retailers blocked cross-border purchases in 68% of cases for electronics, 55% for clothing, and 77% for video games.

Opposition came from two powerful quarters. First, Continental European retailers — particularly in France and Germany — argued that geo-blocking was necessary to manage different national tax regimes, warranty obligations, and return logistics. The German Retail Federation (HDE) warned that forcing cross-border sales would “destroy the business model of small retailers.” Second, content-rights holders and broadcasters — led by the football leagues and film studios — warned that the regulation would unravel territorial licensing, which is how sports and film rights are sold in Europe. They were excluded from the main regulation, however; a separate 2018 directive on cross-border portability of online content services (2017/1128/EU) addressed some of these concerns.

Timing was crucial. The regulation succeeded under the Juncker Commission (2014-2019) because of a confluence of factors: the 2014-2015 refugee crisis had created deep political divisions, but the Digital Single Market was seen as a rare area of consensus; the 2015 e-commerce sector inquiry by the Commission’s competition directorate had gathered damning data on geo-blocking; and the 2016 Brexit referendum — while not directly related — pushed remaining member states to prove the EU could deliver concrete benefits to citizens. The Commission’s 2016 impact assessment estimated that eliminating geo-blocking could boost cross-border e-commerce by €4 billion annually.

What changed was the Commission’s strategy. Earlier attempts in the 2000s and early 2010s to address geo-blocking through competition law (fining companies like Nintendo and Guess for territorial restrictions) had proved slow and piecemeal. The Juncker Commission switched to a targeted regulation — narrow enough to survive industry lobbying but broad enough to have real impact. The vote in the European Parliament on March 1, 2018, was 577 in favour, 32 against, and 100 abstentions — a supermajority that was virtually unprecedented for a controversial business regulation. Only the far-left GUE/NGL group voted overwhelmingly against, arguing the regulation did not go far enough in forcing actual cross-border deliveries.

## Timeline

- **2014** — Juncker announces Digital Single Market as a priority in his political guidelines for the Commission.
- **2015, May** — Commission publishes Digital Single Market strategy, identifying geo-blocking as a key barrier.
- **2015, December** — BEUC publishes "The Cross-Border Online Shopping Obstacle Course," showing 63% of cross-border attempts blocked.
- **2016, May** — Commission publishes e-commerce sector inquiry final report, documenting widespread geo-blocking.
- **2016, May** — Commission tables legislative proposal for the Geo-blocking Regulation.
- **2017, February** — European Parliament’s IMCO committee adopts its report, led by MEP Róża Thun.
- **2018, February** — Council of the EU (member states) adopts its position, with 27 member states in favour.
- **2018, February 27** — Informal trilogue agreement reached between Parliament, Council, and Commission.
- **2018, March 1** — European Parliament votes 577-32-100 in favour of the final text.
- **2018, February 28** — Regulation (EU) 2018/302 is officially published in the Official Journal.
- **2018, December 3** — Regulation enters into force, with a two-year transition period for certain sectors (e.g., booking of transport services).
- **2019, June** — Commission publishes first guidance to help businesses comply.
- **2020, December 3** — Regulation becomes fully applicable for all sectors.
- **2020, December** — Commission adopts delegated regulation (2020/1669) exempting certain copyright-protected content.
- **2021, November** — Commission publishes first evaluation report, finding broad compliance but noting gaps in enforcement and lack of cross-border delivery obligations.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation’s impact has been significant but uneven. According to the Commission’s 2021 evaluation, cross-border e-commerce as a share of total EU e-commerce rose from 15% in 2015 to 24% in 2020, though this was also driven by the COVID-19 pandemic. The number of consumers reporting that they had attempted cross-border online purchases rose from 27% in 2015 to 38% in 2020 (Eurostat data, 2020). However, the Commission acknowledged that “unjustified geo-blocking remains an issue for certain categories of goods and services.”

The most tangible effect has been in the clothing and electronics sectors, where major retailers like Zara (Inditex), H&M, and MediaMarkt eliminated website redirects for EU customers. In the video game sector, Steam, the dominant digital platform, ended regional pricing and geo-blocking for EU customers in 2019 after the regulation came into force, though it maintained separate pricing for non-EU regions.

Enforcement has been weak. As of the Commission’s 2021 evaluation, only 12 member states had designated competent authorities for enforcing the regulation, and only two — Germany and Austria — had issued fines. The largest fine was €250,000 imposed on an unnamed Austrian retailer by the Austrian competition authority (BWB) in 2020 for refusing to sell to German customers. Consumer organisations in several countries — notably Italy and Spain — filed complaints against small retailers who continued geo-blocking after the deadline.

The regulation’s most important limitation — that it does not force traders to deliver physical goods across borders — has been widely criticised. The Commission’s 2021 evaluation found that 42% of cross-border transaction failures were due to traders’ refusal to deliver to the customer’s country, which is not covered by the regulation. BEUC and consumer groups have called for the regulation to be extended to include a delivery obligation. The Commission announced in 2022 that it would launch a review of the regulation in 2023, with possible amendments.

## Key questions answered

### Can a European website refuse to sell to me because I live in another EU country?

Generally, no. The Geo-blocking Regulation prohibits online traders from blocking or restricting access to their website or from applying different general terms and conditions — including prices — based on your nationality, place of residence, or place of establishment. However, a trader can still refuse to physically deliver goods to your country, and the regulation does not apply to services where the customer must be physically present (like hotel stays, concerts, or car rentals).

### Why can’t I always buy something from a website in another EU country even though geo-blocking is banned?

The regulation targets only “unjustified” geo-blocking — meaning barriers based on nationality or residence. It does not force a trader to ship physical goods across borders, nor does it harmonise national VAT rates, tax laws, or consumer protection rules. A trader can legitimately refuse delivery if a product cannot be shipped easily (e.g., bulky furniture) or if it is subject to copyright territorial licensing (e.g., e-books, video games, streaming content). The regulation covers access, not delivery.

### Does the Geo-blocking Regulation apply to streaming services like Netflix or Spotify?

Yes, but only partially. The regulation prohibits a streaming service from blocking access to its website or app based on your location, and from charging different subscription prices based on your place of residence. However, it does not force a service to offer the same content catalogue across all EU countries, because audiovisual content is still licensed on a territorial basis. A separate piece of EU law — the Portability Regulation (2017/1128/EU) — allows you to access your home-country subscription when travelling in another EU country, but does not give you access to another country’s catalogue.

### What should I do if an online retailer blocks me or redirects me to a different country's website?

You can file a complaint with the national consumer protection authority or competition authority in the country where the trader is based. The European Consumer Centres Network (ECC-Net) can help you identify the correct authority and file a complaint. If the geo-blocking involves a large platform (like Amazon, eBay, or Steam), you can also report it to the European Commission’s Internal Market Directorate-General. Fines for non-compliance vary by member state, but can reach up to 4% of the trader’s annual turnover in some countries.
