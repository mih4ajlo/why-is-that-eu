## What is it?

Regulation 1049/2001 gives any EU citizen — and any natural or legal person residing in a Member State — the right to request and receive documents held by the European Parliament, the Council, and the European Commission. Requests must be answered within 15 working days, refusals must be reasoned, and applicants can challenge a refusal before the EU Ombudsman or the Court of Justice. The institutions also have to maintain public registers of their documents.

The regulation enumerates a narrow set of exceptions: public security, defence and military matters, international relations, financial and economic policy, privacy, commercial interests, court proceedings, inspections, and the protection of an institution's internal decision-making process. Some of these are absolute; others can be overridden by a "public interest" test. Critically, the law covers documents "drawn up or received" by an institution, including documents from Member States — a scope that proved controversial.

Although it was a single regulation rather than a constitutional reform, 1049/2001 is the operational backbone of EU transparency. Subsequent rules — on lobbying registers, comitology, trilogue documents, environmental information under the Aarhus Convention — all sit on top of it.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a referendum. On 2 June 1992, Danish voters rejected the Maastricht Treaty by 50.7% to 49.3%, a margin of about 48,000 votes that froze the entire ratification process and sent EU leaders into emergency mode. French voters then ratified the same treaty in September 1992 by an equally narrow "petit oui". Polling and post-referendum analyses — including those commissioned by the Danish government and the European Parliament — pointed to the same diagnosis: voters felt the Community was a remote, opaque machine in which decisions were taken by ministers behind closed doors in Brussels and Luxembourg.

The Edinburgh European Council of December 1992, presided over by John Major, responded with the "Birmingham and Edinburgh declarations" on openness and subsidiarity, designed to coax Denmark back to the polls. The Council and Commission adopted a joint Code of Conduct on access to documents on 6 December 1993, followed by separate decisions (93/731/EC for the Council, 94/90/ECSC for the Commission). It was a voluntary, institution-by-institution patchwork — but it was the first time the institutions formally accepted that their documents belonged, in principle, to the public.

The Code's limits became obvious quickly. The Court of First Instance ruled against the Council in *Carvel and Guardian Newspapers v Council* (Case T-194/94, 1995), finding that the Council had refused access without genuinely weighing the applicant's interest. The Swedish journalist Tony Bunyan and his Statewatch network filed dozens of requests and complaints, exposing the Council's habit of classifying Justice and Home Affairs documents wholesale. Sweden and Finland, both of which joined in 1995 carrying centuries-old freedom-of-information traditions (Sweden's dates from 1766), pushed hard from the inside for binding rules. The European Ombudsman, Jacob Söderman — himself a Finn — launched an own-initiative inquiry in 1996 into how every EU body handled access requests and named-and-shamed those without proper rules.

The Treaty of Amsterdam, signed in 1997 and in force from 1 May 1999, settled the legal question. New Article 255 TEC gave every citizen a Treaty-based right of access to Parliament, Council and Commission documents and obliged the institutions to adopt implementing legislation within two years. That deadline — 1 May 2001 — is what forced the file through.

Negotiation was bruising. The Commission's draft, presented by Secretary-General David O'Sullivan and Commissioner Loyola de Palacio in January 2000, was widely criticised by MEPs, NGOs and the Nordic governments for excluding too many document categories and giving Member States a veto over the release of their own documents. The so-called "Solana decision" of August 2000 — an internal Council reclassification of security and defence documents adopted under High Representative Javier Solana — was seen by the Parliament as a pre-emptive strike to keep CFSP papers out of reach, and triggered a Court challenge by the Netherlands. The European Parliament's rapporteur, British Labour MEP Michael Cashman, leveraged the resulting outrage to extract concessions: a wider definition of "document", coverage of all policy areas including the second and third pillars, and a mandatory public register. The final text was agreed in conciliation and adopted on 30 May 2001, just in time for the Treaty deadline.

## Timeline

- **1992** — Danish voters reject Maastricht (2 June); Edinburgh Council declarations commit to greater openness
- **1993** — Council and Commission adopt joint Code of Conduct on access to documents (6 December)
- **1995** — Sweden and Finland join, bringing strong FOI traditions; *Carvel* judgment finds Council's refusal practice unlawful
- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam signed, creating Article 255 TEC right of access
- **1999** — Amsterdam enters into force (1 May), starting a two-year clock for implementing legislation
- **2000** — Commission proposal published in January; "Solana decision" on security documents provokes Parliament backlash
- **2001** — Regulation 1049/2001 adopted on 30 May; enters into application on 3 December
- **2007** — Commission opens review consultation; recast proposal tabled in 2008
- **2008–2014** — Recast negotiations stall in inter-institutional deadlock over scope, Member State veto, and trilogue documents
- **2018** — *Access Info Europe* and *De Capitani* judgments require greater access to legislative trilogue documents
- **present** — recast effort never concluded; 1049/2001 remains in force in its original form

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed EU document access from a discretionary favour into an enforceable right. The Commission, Council and Parliament each maintain public registers running into the millions of records, and process tens of thousands of requests a year — the Commission's annual reports on the application of Regulation 1049/2001 publish the running figures.

Litigation has driven much of the actual change. In *Turco* (Joined Cases C-39/05 P and C-52/05 P, 2008), the Court of Justice ordered the Council to release legal-service opinions on legislation, rejecting the blanket secrecy the Council had defended. In *Access Info Europe v Council* (Case T-233/09, 2011, upheld on appeal in 2013), the Court forced the Council to disclose which Member States held which positions during legislative negotiations — ending the practice of redacting national names. *De Capitani v Parliament* (Case T-540/15, 2018) extended transparency obligations to trilogue four-column documents, the previously opaque core of EU law-making. NGOs including ClientEarth, Statewatch, Corporate Europe Observatory and Access Info Europe have been the most persistent litigants, frequently winning.

The regulation also reshaped journalism and lobbying. Investigations into Glyphosate authorisation, vaccine procurement contracts during the COVID-19 pandemic (where the Commission's heavily redacted Pfizer disclosures became a political flashpoint and a focus of Ombudsman Emily O'Reilly's inquiries), and the "Qatargate" follow-up all relied on document requests. Member States that resisted disclosure of their national positions in Council — Germany and France most often — adapted to a world in which their delegations' written interventions could end up in newspapers.

The unfinished business is the recast. A 2008 Commission proposal to update the regulation has been blocked for more than a decade by disagreement between an institutionalist Council, a transparency-maximalist Parliament, and a Commission caught in the middle. The Ombudsman has repeatedly criticised the Commission and Council for slow responses and over-broad use of exceptions, particularly for text-message communications — most famously the unrecovered messages between Commission President Ursula von der Leyen and Pfizer CEO Albert Bourla.

## Key questions answered

### Can ordinary people request documents from the EU?

Yes. Any EU citizen and any person living in the EU can ask the Parliament, Council or Commission for any document they hold, in any language, without giving a reason. The institution has 15 working days to reply and must justify any refusal with reference to a specific listed exception. Requests are typically free; only large reproductions can be charged.

### Why are EU institutions seen as so secretive despite transparency rules?

Because the rules contain wide exceptions and because much of EU decision-making happens in informal settings the rules barely cover: trilogue meetings between Parliament and Council negotiators, expert groups, comitology committees, and increasingly text messages and instant messaging. The Court of Justice has expanded access through case law — notably the 2018 *De Capitani* ruling on trilogues — but the Council still routinely withholds Member States' negotiating positions until after a file is closed, and the Commission's record on informal communications remains contested by the European Ombudsman.

### What did the Danish 'No' vote actually change about how Brussels works?

The 1992 Danish referendum was the moment EU institutions accepted that their old habits of confidentiality were politically unsustainable. It produced the Edinburgh declarations on openness, the 1993 Code of Conduct, the Amsterdam Treaty's right of access, and ultimately Regulation 1049/2001. It also embedded a permanent Nordic-led transparency caucus inside the Council that has shaped every subsequent reform debate — including ongoing battles over lobbying registers and conflict-of-interest rules.

### Can you get hold of contracts the EU signs with private companies?

In principle yes, but commercial confidentiality is one of the listed exceptions and is invoked aggressively. The Commission's COVID-19 vaccine contracts were released only in heavily redacted form, and the European Ombudsman found maladministration in how the Commission handled requests for text messages between Commission President von der Leyen and Pfizer's CEO. Several cases on contract transparency are pending or have produced partial disclosures after litigation by MEPs, journalists and NGOs.
