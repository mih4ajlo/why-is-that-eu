## What is it?

The Open Data Directive (Directive 2019/1024/EU) is a recast of the 2003 Public Sector Information (PSI) Directive. It sets rules for the re-use of public sector information held by EU member states' public bodies — data ranging from geospatial and meteorological information to legal statistics and business registries. The directive introduces a default of "open by design and by default," meaning public bodies must make data available in accessible, machine-readable formats and via application programming interfaces (APIs) wherever possible.

Key provisions include:
- A list of six categories of "high-value datasets" (geospatial, earth observation and environment, meteorological, statistics, companies and company ownership, and mobility) that must be free of charge, provided in machine-readable formats, and accessible via APIs.
- A requirement that data formats and APIs be based on open, commonly used standards.
- A strengthened "re-use" principle: if public bodies charge for data, fees are capped at the marginal cost of reproduction, dissemination, and anonymisation — exceptions exist for public undertakings and libraries, but are tightly scoped.
- Coverage expansion to include public undertakings in transport, energy, and utilities sectors, previously excluded.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origins lie in a political and technological failure: by the late 2010s, the original 2003 PSI Directive and its 2013 revision were widely seen as having failed to unlock the economic and social value of public sector data. The European Commission's 2017 mid-term evaluation of the PSI Directive found that data re-use in the EU generated an estimated €52 billion in direct economic value per year — but that this was far below the potential, which the Commission’s 2018 impact assessment estimated at up to €334 billion under an "optimised, full open data scenario." The gap was structural.

**Structural tension** — The core failure was that the existing directive allowed public bodies to charge above-marginal cost for data, which stifled re-use. A 2017 study by the European Data Portal found that 60% of public sector datasets in the EU were still not available for free. In countries like Germany and France, national statistical offices and mapping agencies charged hundreds to thousands of euros per dataset, effectively excluding startups and small companies. At the same time, private-sector tech giants such as Google, Microsoft, and HERE Technologies had built multi-billion-dollar mapping and navigation services partly on the back of cheap or free US public data (the US Landsat programme, NOAA weather data, US Census geospatial data), giving them a structural advantage over European competitors who paid high fees for equivalent EU data.

**Coalition dynamics** — The Commission’s proposal, tabled in April 2018 by Vice-President Andrus Ansip (responsible for the Digital Single Market) and Digital Commissioner Mariya Gabriel, built a broad coalition. The European Parliament's rapporteur, Neoklis Sylikiotis (GUE/NGL, Cyprus), shepherded the file through. The Parliament’s ITRE committee voted a strong position in February 2019 that pushed for the high-value datasets list to be mandatory and free. The Council of the EU, under the Romanian and Finnish presidencies, backed a requirement for "dynamic data" (frequently updated datasets) to be provided via APIs. The key opposition came from national statistical offices (especially Destatis in Germany and INSEE in France), which argued that free data would slash their budgets. The European Commission’s JRC modelling estimated that a transition to free data would reduce statistical offices’ revenues by roughly 30–40% on average across the EU.

**Timing** — The recast succeeded because it rode a wave of political consensus around the "data economy" as a strategic priority. In 2017, the Commission published a "Building a European Data Economy" communication that framed data re-use as essential for artificial intelligence, IoT, and competitiveness against the US and China. The 2018–2019 legislative cycle saw simultaneous progress on the Open Data Directive, the GDPR enforcement ramp-up, and the early seeds of the European Data Strategy (published February 2020). The catalyst was a specific Commission study published in 2017: "The Economic Impact of Open Data: Opportunities for Value Creation," which estimated that under current rules, the EU was missing out on up to €282 billion per year in potential economic value — a figure repeated by Commissioner Gabriel in her presentation to the Council.

**What changed** — Earlier attempts to push for mandatory free data, such as in the 2011–2013 revision, had failed because of resistance from large member states' statistical offices and ministries of finance. The 2017–2019 window broke the deadlock because of two factors: first, the Commission's impact assessment demonstrated convincingly that the revenue losses to public bodies were small relative to the potential economic gains (the JRC estimated that total revenue from data sales across all EU public bodies was less than €500 million per year); second, the political narrative around "digital sovereignty" and "European tech champions" made free, machine-readable data seem essential for AI and startup ecosystems. The final trilogue in February 2019 settled on a compromise: high-value datasets must be free, but member states could still charge for other datasets at a "reasonable return on investment" rate for specific public undertakings — a carve-out pushed by Germany and France.

The directive was formally adopted by the European Parliament on 4 April 2019, with 587 votes in favour, 33 against, and 17 abstentions. The Council adopted it on 6 June 2019, with the UK (still a member state) abstaining.

## Timeline

- **2003** — Original Public Sector Information Directive (2003/98/EC) adopted, establishing minimum rules for re-use of public sector data across the EU.
- **2011** — Commission proposal for revision tabled. The revision aimed to extend the directive to libraries, museums, and archives, and to cap fees at marginal cost.
- **2013** — Revised PSI Directive (2013/37/EU) adopted, setting marginal cost as the default but allowing exceptions for "self-financing" public bodies.
- **2017** — Commission publishes mid-term evaluation of the PSI Directive, concluding that data re-use is "significantly below potential" and that the 2013 revision had failed to achieve its objectives.
- **25 April 2018** — Commission proposes the Open Data Directive (COM/2018/234 final), alongside a broader European Data Strategy package.
- **February 2019** — European Parliament adopts its position in ITRE committee, adding measures on dynamic data and API access.
- **4 April 2019** — European Parliament adopts the directive on first reading (587 votes in favour, 33 against, 17 abstentions).
- **6 June 2019** — Council adopts the directive. UK abstains.
- **20 June 2019** — Directive published in the Official Journal of the EU.
- **17 July 2021** — Deadline for member states to transpose the directive into national law.
- **22 December 2021** — First Commission implementing regulation on high-value datasets (2023/138) adopted, specifying the six categories and technical requirements.
- **9 February 2024** — Deadline for member states to make high-value datasets available in machine-readable via APIs.

## Impact and consequences

The directive’s most tangible impact has been on the high-value datasets mandate. The six categories — geospatial, earth observation and environment, meteorological, statistics, companies and company ownership, and mobility — cover some of the most commercially valuable public data. The 2021 implementing regulation (2023/138) specified specific datasets within each category: for example, the geospatial category includes the EU's administrative boundaries, address data, and cadastral parcels; the mobility category includes public transport timetables and real-time traffic information. A European Commission evaluation published in 2023 found that, by the end of the transposition deadline, 23 of the 27 member states had fully transposed the directive into national law; the four laggards (Bulgaria, Greece, Cyprus, Malta) were sent letters of formal notice in June 2023.

Industry response was initially cautious. The European Federation of Statistical Offices (ESGAB) lobbied hard for the "reasonable return on investment" exception, but in practice few member states use it for core statistical datasets. Germany's Statistisches Bundesamt, which previously charged up to €3,000 per dataset for detailed regional data, moved to free provision in early 2022, citing the directive. The French Institut National de la Statistique et des Études Économiques (INSEE) followed in 2023.

For startups and SMEs, the impact has been significant but uneven. The European Data Portal’s 2022 survey found that 45% of European businesses that already used public sector data reported that the new directive had reduced their costs or improved product offerings. However, the same survey found that only 12% of SMEs were aware of the directive at all — suggesting that the awareness gap remains a barrier.

Enforcement has been minimal to date. No fines have been issued under the directive, as it is a directive rather than a regulation and thus relies on national transposition and enforcement. The Commission has, however, launched infringement procedures against Belgium, Poland, and Slovakia for non-transposition.

## Key questions answered

### Can I get European weather data for free now?

Yes, under the Open Data Directive, meteorological datasets are one of the six categories of high-value data that must be provided free of charge. This means national weather services like the UK Met Office, Germany's DWD, and France's Météo-France must make their core data — temperature, precipitation, wind speed, forecasts — available in machine-readable formats via API, without charging licensing fees. Some historical or high-resolution data may still be charged for under the "reasonable return on investment" exception, but basic datasets are now free.

### What data counts as "high-value"?

The directive defines six categories: geospatial data (maps, addresses, cadastral parcels), earth observation and environment (land cover, water quality, air pollution), meteorological (temperature, precipitation, forecasts), statistics (GDP, population, employment by region), companies and company ownership (registration numbers, ownership structures, financial data), and mobility (public transport timetables, real-time traffic, road circulation). The 2021 implementing regulation further specifies which specific datasets within each category are mandatory. Member states can add more, but cannot remove any.

### Why was it so hard to get free public data before?

The key reason was that many national public bodies — especially statistical offices, mapping agencies, and weather services — had business models built around selling data. For example, the German Federal Agency for Cartography and Geodesy earned millions of euros annually from licensing geospatial data. These bodies argued that revenue from data sales funded their operations and that free provision would require taxpayers to subsidise commercial re-users. The Commission’s impact assessment showed that these revenue streams were actually small relative to the potential economic benefits of open data, but the political fight was fierce because it threatened the institutional autonomy of these agencies.

### Does this directive apply to private companies?

Only indirectly. The directive applies to "public sector bodies" — government agencies, ministries, state-owned enterprises, and public undertakings in transport, energy, and utilities. Private companies are not required to share their data under this directive. However, if a private company holds data that originates from a public sector body (e.g., a licensed version of a public map), that data may be subject to re-use rules if the public body originally collected it. The directive also encourages "data altruism" — voluntary sharing by private entities — but does not mandate it.
