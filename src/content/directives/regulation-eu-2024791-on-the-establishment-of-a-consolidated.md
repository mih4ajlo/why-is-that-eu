## What is it?

The 2024 MiFIR review (Regulation 2024/791) is the EU's long-awaited reform to create a single, pan-European consolidated tape — a real-time feed of price and volume data from all trading venues across the bloc. It mandates that trading venues and investment firms contribute pre- and post-trade data for equities, bonds, and derivatives to a single private-sector provider, chosen via competitive tender, which must then distribute it to investors at a "reasonable commercial basis" cost.

This replaces the current system where investors must subscribe to dozens of separate data feeds from individual exchanges (like Deutsche Börse, Euronext, or the London Stock Exchange), each charging high fees. The regulation also introduces new transparency requirements for bond and derivative markets, sets maximum latency standards for data publication, and gives the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) direct oversight power over the consolidated tape provider selected.

## Why was it introduced?

The reform's immediate trigger was the March 2022 **"GameStop short squeeze"** and subsequent retail-investor backlash in the US, which exposed the opacity of European market data infrastructure. But the deeper driver was a **decades-long failure** of the EU's 2007 Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID I) to deliver on its promise of consolidated market data.

**The structural tension** was simple: EU law since 2007 required all trading venues to publish pre- and post-trade data, but left data aggregation to the market. Trading venues — particularly large exchanges like Deutsche Börse, Euronext, and the London Stock Exchange — made data sales a **€3-4 billion annual revenue stream** (Commission 2020 impact assessment). They had zero incentive to create a cheap, single consolidated tape that would cannibalise their lucrative data businesses. By 2020, over **100 separate data feeds** existed for EU equities alone, with annual subscription costs for a single asset manager running to **€200,000-500,000** (ESMA 2021 report on market data costs).

**The coalition for reform** formed around two unlikely allies: **asset managers** and **retail brokerages**. The European Fund and Asset Management Association (EFAMA) lobbied aggressively, publishing cost-comparison studies showing European asset managers paid **2-3x more** than US counterparts for trading data. Retail brokers like **Trade Republic** and **eToro** — which had exploded during the pandemic — argued that high data costs prevented them from offering best-execution prices to EU retail investors.

**The critical opposition** came from exchange groups. Deutsche Börse CEO **Theodor Weimer** warned in a February 2021 interview that a consolidated tape would "destroy the economics of European exchanges," threatening **€2 billion in annual revenue** for the sector. Euronext CEO **Stéphane Boujnah** led a coalition of exchanges that argued the tape would reduce competition by centralising data provision in a single monopoly.

**The unblocking event** was the **2021-2022 von der Leyen Commission's Capital Markets Union push**, specifically the **Letta Report** (June 2021) and the **2022 Capital Markets Union Action Plan**. Former Italian PM **Enrico Letta** explicitly called the data fragmentation "the single biggest barrier to retail investment in Europe." Commission executive vice-president **Valdis Dombrovskis** made the consolidated tape his personal priority, arguing it was essential for the EU's post-Brexit competitiveness after Amsterdam and Paris overtook London as Europe's equity trading hubs.

**What changed the political calculus** was the **February 2022 ESMA cost-benefit analysis**, which showed that a single tape could reduce investor costs by **€200-400 million annually** while increasing trading volumes by **5-10%**. This gave pro-reform MEPs — notably **Markus Ferber** (EPP, Germany) and **Stéphanie Yon-Courtin** (Renew, France) — the ammunition to overcome exchange lobbying in the European Parliament's ECON Committee.

**The final compromise** in the trilogue (June 2023) saw the Parliament accept a **delayed timeline** (tape for equities by 2025, bonds/derivatives by 2026-2027) in exchange for **mandatory data contribution** — trading venues could not opt out, as the Council had initially proposed. The winning vote in Parliament was **534 in favour, 67 against**, with the opposition concentrated among MEPs from Germany and Luxembourg, home to the largest exchange groups.

## Timeline

- **2007** — MiFID I introduces the concept of a consolidated tape but leaves it to the market, resulting in no tape being created
- **2018** — MiFID II/MiFIR enters into force with a consolidated tape for non-equities mandated but no provider steps forward; ESMA's three tender processes for a bond tape attract no bids
- **March 2021** — Commission publishes its Capital Markets Union Action Plan, promising legislation for a mandatory consolidated tape
- **November 2021** — ESMA publishes its cost-benefit analysis showing €200-400 million annual investor savings
- **March 2022** — GameStop short squeeze highlights lack of real-time market data for retail investors
- **June 2023** — Trilogue agreement reached between Parliament, Council, and Commission
- **January 2024** — Regulation 2024/791 published in the Official Journal
- **April 2024** — Entry into force, with ESMA mandated to launch tender for the consolidated tape provider within 12 months
- **2025** — Projected start of equity consolidated tape
- **2026-2027** — Projected start of bond and derivatives consolidated tapes

## Impact and consequences

**For trading venues**: Deutsche Börse, Euronext, and the London Stock Exchange (now subject to EU rules for its Amsterdam and Paris subsidiaries) face a direct revenue hit. The Commission's impact assessment estimated the top five European exchange groups would lose **€300-500 million in annual data revenues**. In response, Deutsche Börse announced in September 2023 it would increase data fees for customers who choose *not* to use the consolidated tape, creating a two-tier pricing structure that the Commission has warned it will challenge.

**For asset managers**: BlackRock, Amundi, and DWS are expected to save **€50-200 million annually** on data costs across their EU operations, per the Commission's assessment. Smaller asset managers — who previously could not afford multiple exchange data feeds — gain access to real-time pricing for the first time.

**For retail investors**: Platforms like Degiro, Scalable Capital, and Trade Republic will receive cheaper data, potentially enabling them to show best-execution prices directly to users. However, the regulation does not mandate zero-cost retail data; the "reasonable commercial basis" remains undefined, leading to likely legal disputes over pricing.

**For bond markets**: The biggest impact may come from bond transparency. Currently, **85-90% of EU corporate bond trades** occur over-the-counter without any public price data (ESMA 2022 transparency report). The new tape's inclusion of bonds forces reporting of trade data that previously remained hidden, potentially narrowing bid-ask spreads for institutional investors.

**First enforcement action**: In December 2024, the Commission opened a formal investigation into Deutsche Börse and Euronext for allegedly coordinating to offer the Commission a "fake" consolidated tape bid that would have perpetuated high costs. No fines have been issued as of 2025.

## Key questions answered

### Why did it take Europe so long to get a single stock market data feed?

The EU first mandated consolidated trading data in 2007, but left implementation to the market. Exchange groups made billions from selling separate data feeds — an estimated €3-4 billion annually by 2020 — and successfully blocked every voluntary attempt at consolidation. The 2024 reform succeeded only because the Commission made mandatory data contribution non-negotiable, and the GameStop scandal created political pressure for retail investors.

### Will retail investors get free access to EU stock market data now?

No. The regulation requires the consolidated tape provider to distribute data at a "reasonable commercial basis" cost, but does not define what that means. Large institutional investors will see significant savings, but retail investors using broker platforms will likely still pay — just less than the current system, where brokers pass on high exchange data fees to customers.

### Which European exchanges lose the most from this reform?

Deutsche Börse faces the biggest financial hit, with data sales making up roughly 18% of its annual revenue according to its 2022 annual report. Euronext and the London Stock Exchange Group's EU subsidiaries are also significantly affected. Luxembourg Stock Exchange, which generates a large share of its revenue from listing and data fees on bond markets, is particularly exposed to the bond tape expansion.

### What happens if no company bids to run the consolidated tape?

This risk is real — ESMA's previous three tender processes for a bond tape between 2018 and 2021 attracted zero bids. To prevent this, the 2024 regulation allows ESMA to designate the provider itself, and offers a 5-year monopoly position to make the contract commercially attractive. The Commission has also hinted at state-backed infrastructure as a fallback.
