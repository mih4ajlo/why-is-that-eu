## What is it?

The Digital Europe Programme (DIGITAL) is an EU funding programme that funnels €7.5 billion (at current prices from the 2021-2027 Multiannual Financial Framework) into strategic digital investments that the European Commission judged were not being adequately addressed by national governments or private markets. It is structured around five pillars: high-performance computing (€2.2 billion), artificial intelligence (€2.1 billion), cybersecurity (€1.6 billion), advanced digital skills (€580 million), and the deployment and best use of digital capacities (€1.1 billion).

Unlike Horizon Europe, which funds research, DIGITAL focuses on rolling out existing technologies at scale — building Europe's first exascale supercomputers, creating "testing and experimentation facilities" for AI in manufacturing and healthcare, and training cybersecurity specialists. The programme is managed directly by the European Commission's Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology (DG CONNECT), with co-financing requirements for member states.

## Why was it introduced?

The programme’s origins lie in a stark realisation that emerged around 2016–2017: Europe was losing the battle for digital infrastructure. The European Commission’s 2016 "Digitising European Industry" communication noted that, while the EU accounted for 24% of global GDP, it hosted only 4% of the world’s top 500 supercomputers. By 2017, the Joint Research Centre found that the US and China controlled over 70% of global supercomputing capacity. The trigger for legislative action, however, was not a single scandal but a slow-burning structural tension: the EU had funded digital research through Horizon 2020 but lacked a dedicated instrument to deploy digital infrastructure.

The coalition that drove the programme forward was built around three forces: the European Commission’s digital agenda under Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society Mariya Gabriel (2017-2019) and later Commissioner for Internal Market Thierry Breton (2019-present); a group of member states, led by France and Germany, that had already launched the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking in 2018 as a pilot; and the European Parliament’s ITRE committee, where rapporteur Henna Virkkunen (EPP, Finland) pushed for increased funding. The European Parliament’s initial position in early 2019 called for €9.2 billion, while the Council of the EU proposed €6.8 billion; the final compromise of €7.5 billion was reached during the July 2020 budget negotiations.

Opposition came from two directions. The group of member states that were net contributors to the EU budget — including the Netherlands, Sweden, Austria, and Finland — pushed for a smaller programme, arguing that digital investments were primarily a national competence. A coordinated letter from the finance ministers of these four countries to the Commission in early 2020 warned that the programme risked duplicating existing national initiatives. Meanwhile, some MEPs from the Greens/EFA group raised concerns about the environmental impact of building large data centres and supercomputers, which consume significant energy and water.

The timing was decisive. The COVID-19 pandemic, which hit Europe in March 2020, served as the unblocking event. The crisis demonstrated that Europe lacked the digital sovereignty to process health data, run pandemic models, or manage remote work at scale — many EU institutions and member states relied on US cloud providers. In May 2020, the Commission’s recovery plan (NextGenerationEU) proposed a stronger digital budget, and DIGITAL was reframed from a niche infrastructure programme into a strategic sovereignty tool. The 2019 election of Ursula von der Leyen as Commission President, who made "A Europe fit for the Digital Age" one of her six political priorities, sealed the political will.

What changed compared to earlier proposals was the explicit focus on sovereignty. Earlier drafts of the programme, dating back to the 2017 Mid-Term Review of Horizon 2020, had framed DIGITAL as a competitiveness programme. By the time the final text was adopted in April 2021, the language had shifted to "digital sovereignty" — particularly in supercomputing and AI, where the Commission cited the need to reduce dependency on non-European data-processing infrastructure.

## Timeline

- **2017** — European Commission publishes "Digitising European Industry" communication, identifying gap in digital infrastructure funding
- **2018** — EuroHPC Joint Undertaking created as a pilot for supercomputing cooperation
- **6 June 2018** — Commission publishes first legislative proposal for Digital Europe Programme (€9.2 billion)
- **2019** — European Parliament's ITRE committee adopts its position, supporting €9.2 billion
- **December 2019** — von der Leyen Commission takes office, prioritising "A Europe fit for the Digital Age"
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 pandemic begins, accelerating recognition of digital sovereignty needs
- **July 2020** — European Council reaches agreement on €7.5 billion for DIGITAL as part of MFF 2021-2027
- **29 April 2021** — Regulation (2021/694/EU) adopted by European Parliament and Council
- **11 May 2021** — Regulation enters into force
- **1 January 2021** — Programme applied retroactively (with funding available from start of MFF period)
- **2023** — First EU exascale supercomputer (Jupiter) announced, funded partly by DIGITAL
- **2024** — Commission reports that DIGITAL has funded 30+ AI testing facilities and trained 50,000+ cybersecurity specialists

## Impact and consequences

The programme’s most tangible impact has been in supercomputing. By 2024, DIGITAL funding had enabled the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking to deploy three of the world’s top 10 most energy-efficient supercomputers — LUMI in Finland, Leonardo in Italy, and MareNostrum 5 in Spain. The Commission’s 2023 progress report found that DIGITAL had reduced the EU’s share of global supercomputing capacity from 4% in 2017 to approximately 20% by 2023. However, the AI pillar has progressed more slowly: the European Court of Auditors' 2024 report noted that only 30 of the planned 140 AI testing facilities were operational by mid-2024.

The digital skills pillar has faced criticism. The European Parliament's 2023 evaluation found that the €580 million budget was insufficient to address the EU’s talent shortage — Eurostat data from 2022 showed that only 55% of EU adults had basic digital skills. Member states have struggled to absorb funding, particularly in Central and Eastern Europe, where administrative capacity for digital projects is often lower.

Industry response has been mixed. Large technology companies like SAP, Siemens, and ASML have praised the programme's focus on real-world deployment, while smaller startups have complained about burdensome application processes — the Commission's own 2022 stakeholder survey found that 43% of SME applicants found the administrative requirements "very high". The programme’s most politically contested element has been the cybersecurity pillar, where the Commission originally proposed that DIGITAL would fund the deployment of "security operations centres" across member states; several national intelligence agencies, including those of France and Poland, raised concerns about data-sharing arrangements.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU invest billions in its own supercomputers?

The EU realised that by 2017 it owned only 4% of the world's top supercomputers, despite accounting for 24% of global GDP, while the US and China controlled over 70%. This meant European scientists, drug developers and climate modellers depended on foreign infrastructure for cutting-edge research. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the problem when European health authorities struggled to run epidemiological models without US cloud capacity.

### Does the Digital Europe Programme fund AI research or AI deployment?

It primarily funds deployment, not research. Unlike Horizon Europe, which funds basic AI research, DIGITAL builds "testing and experimentation facilities" where companies can test AI in real industrial settings — for example, a factory in Italy testing AI-powered quality control systems. The Commission's 2024 report found 30 such facilities had been funded.

### Who was opposed to the programme and why?

Net contributor countries like the Netherlands, Sweden, Austria and Finland opposed the spending increase, arguing digital investments were national competences. The Greens/EFA group raised environmental concerns about energy-intensive supercomputers. Several member states with advanced digital sectors worried the programme would duplicate existing national initiatives.

### Does the programme actually address Europe's digital skills shortage?

Only partially. The €580 million allocated for digital skills is the smallest pillar and critics, including the European Parliament's 2023 evaluation, argue it is insufficient given that only 55% of EU adults had basic digital skills in 2022. The programme funds specialist training for cybersecurity experts and AI engineers, but does not meet the scale of the gap.
