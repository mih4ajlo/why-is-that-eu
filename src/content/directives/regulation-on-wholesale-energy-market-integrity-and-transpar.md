## What is it?

The Regulation on wholesale energy market integrity and transparency — known as REMIT — is the EU’s primary weapon against manipulation, insider dealing, and fraudulent behaviour in wholesale electricity and natural gas markets. It covers all trading of energy products across the EU, including physical delivery contracts, derivatives, and capacity trading. The regulation creates a legal framework equivalent to the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) that governs financial markets, but tailored to the unique structure of energy trading.

Under REMIT, market participants must report every wholesale energy transaction to the Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER), which maintains a Europe-wide database known as the Transaction Reporting User System (TRUM). ACER analyses the data for suspicious patterns and coordinates enforcement with national regulators. The regulation also gives ACER and National Regulatory Authorities (NRAs) powers to investigate, impose fines, and refer cases for criminal prosecution. REMIT applies to any person trading in EU wholesale energy markets, regardless of where they are based.

## Why was it introduced?

REMIT was a direct response to a regulatory blind spot exposed by the liberalisation of European energy markets. When the EU adopted the Third Energy Package (2009), it broke up national monopolies and created open markets for electricity and gas across borders. But the same package left a glaring hole: no single rulebook prohibited market abuse in these newly competitive wholesale markets. A trader could, in theory, manipulate price benchmarks or trade on confidential information about a power plant outage with no clear EU-level sanction.

The trigger for action came from two parallel developments. First, during the 2008-2009 gas pricing disputes, several Member States and the European Commission suspected that companies were deliberately withholding information about gas storage levels to inflate spot prices. The Commission’s 2009 sector inquiry into energy markets found "widespread suspicion of anti-competitive practices" but lacked a specific legal instrument to investigate or punish manipulation of wholesale energy prices. Second, the 2010 “flash crash” in US financial markets and subsequent concern about cross-asset manipulation made regulators alert to the risks of unregulated energy derivatives trading.

Energy Commissioner Günther Oettinger (2010-2014) made market transparency a priority. In December 2010, the Commission proposed REMIT, explicitly linking it to the already-existing Market Abuse Directive for financial instruments. The proposal argued that energy markets were just as susceptible to insider trading and price manipulation as stock markets — perhaps more so, given the opacity of physical trading contracts and the influence of grid data.

The legislation passed relatively quickly. The European Parliament’s rapporteur, German MEP Jürgen Creutzmann (ALDE), pushed for a strong enforcement role for ACER and for mandatory reporting of transactions. The main opposition came from some energy companies and national exchanges, who argued that reporting every trade would be costly and that existing contract law was sufficient. But the political momentum after the Third Energy Package and the financial crisis made inaction untenable. The regulation was adopted in October 2011 with broad cross-party support and entered into force in December 2011.

## Timeline

- **2009** — Third Energy Package adopted, liberalising EU electricity and gas markets; Commission sector inquiry flags lack of market abuse rules.
- **December 2010** — European Commission publishes proposal for REMIT.
- **October 2011** — European Parliament and Council adopt Regulation (EU) No 1227/2011.
- **December 2011** — REMIT enters into force.
- **2013** — Commission adopts Implementing Regulation (EU) No 1348/2013 on transaction reporting standards.
- **2015** — Mandatory reporting of wholesale energy transactions begins; ACER starts receiving trade data.
- **2018** — First significant enforcement action: Italian energy authority fines a trading company €2 million for manipulation of the gas day-ahead market (source: Italian Regulatory Authority for Energy, Networks and Environment).
- **2021** — European Commission publishes evaluation report noting “overall success” but identifying need for improved data quality and faster national enforcement.
- **2022** — ACER reports that it processed over 400 million transaction records in 2021, and that national regulators opened 150 new cases (source: ACER 2022 Annual Report).
- **2024** — Commission proposes REMIT II (Regulation 2024/1108) to expand scope to include trading platforms for LNG and hydrogen, and to strengthen ACER’s enforcement powers.

## Impact and consequences

REMIT transformed the oversight of European energy trading. Before the regulation, market surveillance was fragmented across national authorities with different standards and no central database. After 2015, ACER receives detailed transaction reports in a standardised format, enabling cross-border pattern detection. The Commission’s 2021 evaluation found that the regulation had “significantly increased transparency” and that most market participants believed REMIT had reduced the risk of manipulation.

The burden of compliance fell hardest on smaller traders who lacked sophisticated reporting systems. Industry group EFET (European Federation of Energy Traders) argued in 2014 that the cost of reporting could force some firms out of the market. In response, ACER introduced a “minimum lot size” threshold and allowed for delegated reporting by third parties.

Enforcement has been uneven. By 2020, countries like Italy, France, and Germany had imposed significant fines, while others had not brought a single case. ACER’s 2022 report noted that NRAs struggled with data analysis capacity and that the number of cases opened (150 in 2021) was “low relative to the volume of trading”. Still, REMIT has become the backbone of EU energy wholesale supervision and is widely cited as a model by the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO).

## Key questions answered

### How does the EU stop companies from inflating electricity or gas prices?

EU law bans any trading activity that gives a false or misleading impression of supply, demand, or price. This includes publishing fake plant outage reports, closing large physical positions to create artificial scarcity, or colluding on bids. Companies must report every trade to the EU energy regulator ACER, which uses software to flag suspicious price spikes or abnormal trading volumes. National regulators investigate and can fine companies up to 10% of annual turnover under rules set by each Member State.

### Can someone be punished for trading on inside information about a power plant?

Yes, exactly like insider trading in stocks. REMIT prohibits anyone who possesses confidential information — such as an unplanned generator outage, a pipeline leak, or a grid constraint — from trading wholesale energy products before that information is made public. Market participants must also disclose inside information about facilities they own or control, so that the market is not trading blind. The UK’s energy regulator Ofgem fined a company €1.2 million in 2018 for insider trading related to a power plant outage (source: Ofgem).

### What happens if a trader manipulates a cross-border energy price benchmark?

If a manipulation involves contracts traded in multiple Member States, ACER can coordinate a joint investigation. For example, if a trader manipulates the Dutch TTF gas benchmark by reporting false delivery data, ACER can share the analysis with the Dutch regulator ACM. The trader can be fined in the country where the effect is felt, or the EU can issue a binding decision under REMIT’s cooperation mechanism. In 2021, ACER published a list of “red flags” for benchmark manipulation, including submitting trades that are known to be used for index calculations without an underlying market need.

### Does every energy trade in Europe have to be reported?

Nearly all wholesale energy transactions above a small size threshold must be reported. This includes physical power and gas schedules, derivatives, and capacity auctions. Exceptions exist for transactions below 100 MWh of gas or 10 MWh of electricity per contract (the “minimum lot size”), and for retail trades with end customers. However, the reporting obligation covers trades made on exchanges, through brokers, or directly between two companies (OTC). ACER received over 400 million reports in 2021, making it one of the world’s largest energy trading databases.
