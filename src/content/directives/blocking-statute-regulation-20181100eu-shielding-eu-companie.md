## What is it?

The EU Blocking Statute Regulation (2018/1100/EU) is a legal tool designed to shield European companies and individuals from the effects of US extraterritorial sanctions. It prohibits EU persons from complying with specified US sanctions laws and court orders, and allows them to recover damages caused by such measures through EU courts.

The regulation essentially tells EU companies: you must not follow US sanctions on third countries like Iran, and if a US company tries to enforce such sanctions against you, you can sue them in Europe. It also establishes EU authorisation procedures for companies that face severe economic hardship from compliance disputes.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the Trump administration’s 2018 withdrawal from the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) — the Iran nuclear deal — and the subsequent reimposition of US secondary sanctions targeting non-US companies doing business with Iran. European Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker, speaking on 17 May 2018, called the US move "unacceptable" and committed to protecting European firms.

The structural tension dated back decades: US secondary sanctions assert jurisdiction over non-US companies for conduct that takes place entirely outside US territory. The EU had passed a weak blocking statute in 1996 (Regulation 2271/96) in response to the Helms-Burton Act on Cuba, but it was never enforced. By 2018, major European companies — including Total, Siemens, and Deutsche Bank — had billions in contracts at stake in Iran. The Commission’s 2018 impact assessment noted that EU-Iran trade was worth €20 billion annually, with significant exposure in energy, automotive, and financial sectors.

The coalition that pushed the 2018 update included the European Parliament (which voted 421-62 in favour on 7 June 2018) and EU member states like Germany and France, whose companies were most exposed. The opposition came primarily from US-aligned EU business interests and the European banking sector, which warned that complying with EU law would trigger US sanctions and potentially cut them off from dollar clearing—a threat the European Banking Federation explicitly raised in a letter to the Commission.

Timing was critical: the EU needed to respond before US sanctions began biting in August and November 2018. The updated regulation was adopted via an urgent procedure in just six weeks—from Commission proposal on 6 June to Council adoption on 6 August 2018. This speed was possible because the existing 1996 regulation already had a legal base, which allowed for expedited updates.

What changed from the failed 1996 version was enforcement: the 2018 regulation created Annex II listing specific US sanctions that EU companies must not follow, and added a notification requirement for EU companies that still chose to comply due to "overwhelming hardship." This was a direct response to the Iran crisis—the 1996 statute had remained largely theoretical because the Cuba sanctions it addressed were narrow and rarely enforced against European companies.

## Timeline

- **1996** — EU adopts Regulation 2271/96 in response to US Helms-Burton Act targeting Cuba; never enforced against any company
- **2015** — JCPOA (Iran nuclear deal) signed, leading to suspension of most EU-Iran sanctions-related measures
- **8 May 2018** — President Trump announces US withdrawal from JCPOA, promises to reimpose secondary sanctions
- **17 May 2018** — Commission President Juncker announces plan to update the Blocking Statute
- **6 June 2018** — Commission proposes updated regulation (2018/1100/EU)
- **7 June 2018** — European Parliament votes 421-62 in favour
- **6 August 2018** — Council adopts regulation; enters into force the following day
- **7 August 2018** — First round of US sanctions reimposed (financial, metals, automotive)
- **5 November 2018** — Second round of US sanctions reimposed (energy, shipping, banking)
- **2019-2022** — INSTEX mechanism created to facilitate Iran trade, but remains largely unused
- **2023** — No EU court has yet issued a damages award under the statute; several cases brought by EU companies against US sanctions decisions remain unresolved

## Impact and consequences

The Blocking Statute has had limited practical effect. Major European companies—including Total (withdrew from Iran’s South Pars gas field in August 2018), Siemens (cancelled projects in Iran), and major EU banks (terminated Iran-related accounts)—chose to exit Iran rather than risk US sanctions exposure. The Commission’s 2020 report on the regulation found that 80% of EU companies with Iran operations withdrew or suspended activities within months of the US reimposition.

The reason: the regulation cannot solve the fundamental asymmetry of power. US dollar clearing and access to the US financial system are worth more to most European companies than their Iran business. As one Commission official noted privately in 2019, "the statute is a political statement more than an operational tool."

The regulation has created legal uncertainty for EU companies caught between two contradictory legal regimes. In 2019, the German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) explicitly advised German banks to disregard the Blocking Statute if compliance would threaten their US market access. Several EU companies have applied for "authorisation to comply" under the regulation's hardship provisions, but the Commission has not publicly disclosed how many were granted.

Diplomatically, the regulation served as leverage: it allowed the EU to argue it had taken concrete legal steps to protect the JCPOA, strengthening Europe's negotiating position in subsequent talks with the US. It also provided a legal basis for the EU to argue at the WTO that US secondary sanctions violate international trade rules.

## Key questions answered

### Can European companies ignore US sanctions on Iran?

No — not in practice. While the EU Blocking Statute legally prohibits European companies from complying with US secondary sanctions, nearly all major European firms have chosen to withdraw from Iran rather than risk losing access to the US financial system. The statute has no mechanism to prevent the US from cutting off a company from dollar clearing, which is critical for international trade.

### Why did the EU's law meant to protect companies from US sanctions fail to stop them from leaving Iran?

Because the US controls the global dollar-clearing system and the world's largest financial market. The Blocking Statute can only prohibit compliance within EU territory, but US sanctions threaten access to the entire US financial system. European companies calculated that losing access to the US market was far more damaging than exiting Iran. As the Commission acknowledged in its 2020 review, the "fundamental asymmetry" between US and EU sanctions enforcement power makes the statute largely symbolic.

### What happens if a US company tries to enforce sanctions against a European company in court?

Under the Blocking Statute, the European company can sue the US company in an EU court for damages and have any US court judgment declared unenforceable in the EU. However, no successful case has yet been recorded. The practical barrier is that US companies rarely need to sue in European courts—they can pressure European firms through denial of US market access or dollar clearing, which the EU statute cannot prevent.
