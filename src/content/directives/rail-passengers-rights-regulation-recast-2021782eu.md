## What is it?

The Regulation on Rail Passengers’ Rights and Obligations (2021/782/EU) is an EU law that consolidates and strengthens passenger protections for all rail services across the European Union. It replaces the original 2007 regulation (1371/2007/EC) and applies to both domestic and international rail travel on all licensed operators, not just those running cross-border services.

Key provisions include: a right to compensation of 25–50% of the ticket price for delays of 60–119 minutes and 50% for delays of 120+ minutes; mandatory through-ticketing for journeys involving multiple operators; strengthened assistance for persons with disabilities; clearer rules on rerouting and reimbursement for missed connections; and a requirement that operators provide clear, accessible information about passenger rights. The recast entered into force on 7 June 2023.

## Why was it introduced?

The recast was driven by a structural tension that became impossible to ignore after a series of well-publicised incidents. In December 2017, the European Court of Justice ruled in *ÖBB-Personenverkehr AG v. Getreide-Import GmbH* (Case C-290/16) that the original 2007 regulation’s exemption for domestic services—which allowed 24 out of 27 member states to waive key passenger rights—was fundamentally at odds with the EU’s transport policy objectives. The court found that Austria’s blanket opt-out for domestic rail meant passengers on long-distance domestic trains had fewer rights than those on international ones, creating a legal absurdity where rights depended on crossing a border rather than the quality of service.

But the political momentum came from a different crisis: the series of high-profile train cancellations and mass passenger stranding events across Europe in 2018–2019. In July 2018, a major heatwave caused widespread rail disruption in Germany, leaving thousands of passengers stranded with no clear rules on compensation from Deutsche Bahn. In November 2019, a violent storm in northern Italy forced Trenitalia to cancel hundreds of services, triggering chaotic scenes at Milan Central station. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) documented in a 2019 report that only 7% of passengers who experienced delays of over an hour actually received compensation under the 2007 rules, largely because operators made the process deliberately obscure.

The Commission’s 2020 impact assessment accompanying the recast proposal found that only three member states—the Netherlands, France, and Austria—had opted into full application of the original regulation for domestic services. The other 24 had used the opt-out to varying degrees, meaning the vast majority of European rail passengers had weaker protections than airline or bus passengers. The assessment specifically cited Eurobarometer data from 2018 showing that 68% of EU citizens believed their rail passenger rights were insufficient.

Coalition dynamics were unusually straightforward. The European Parliament’s TRAN (Transport and Tourism) Committee was led by Rapporteur Bogusław Liberadzki (S&D, Poland), who pushed for comprehensive reform. The Parliament voted overwhelmingly in favour on 29 April 2021 with 626 votes in favour, 26 against, and 43 abstentions. Member states in the Council, particularly those with dominant national rail operators like Germany and Italy, initially resisted mandatory through-ticketing requirements, arguing they would impose disproportionate costs on smaller operators. The German Railway Association (Allianz pro Schiene) warned in a 2020 position paper that through-ticketing could add up to €200 million annually in IT integration costs. The compromise, brokered by the Portuguese Presidency in early 2021, allowed a transition period until July 2025 for operators to implement through-ticketing systems, with an exemption for journeys involving fewer than three services.

The timing was decisive: the recast was finalised during the COVID-19 pandemic, which had devastated rail passenger numbers (down 70-90% across Europe in April 2020 according to the Community of European Railway and Infrastructure Companies (CER)), but also exposed the fragility of the existing rights framework. When member states unilaterally imposed border closures and quarantine requirements in March 2020, passengers holding advance-booking tickets had no EU-wide right to refunds for cancelled domestic services—only international ones. The Commission’s March 2021 enforcement report noted that over 12,000 passenger complaints had been filed during the pandemic relating to domestic rail cancellations that fell outside the 2007 regulation’s scope, a figure cited repeatedly by MEPs during the recast debates.

What changed the industry’s position? The threat of the European Court of Justice’s *ÖBB* ruling being applied retroactively by national courts. In June 2019, the German Federal Court of Justice (BGH) cited the ECJ’s decision in a case against Deutsche Bahn, awarding €300 in compensation to a passenger for a delayed domestic journey—the first time a national court had applied the 2007 regulation to domestic services against an operator’s opt-out. The ruling sent a shockwave through national rail operators who realised they could face cascading litigation. The recast effectively codified the *ÖBB* ruling’s logic, eliminating the opt-out and forcing all operators to comply.

## Timeline

- **2007** — Original Regulation (1371/2007/EC) adopted, allowing member states to exempt domestic services from key passenger rights provisions
- **2017** — European Court of Justice ruling in *ÖBB-Personenverkehr AG* undermines legality of domestic exemptions
- **2018** — Eurobarometer survey shows 68% of EU citizens believe rail passenger rights insufficient
- **2019** — BEUC report finds only 7% of eligible passengers receive compensation; German BGH awards retroactive compensation against Deutsche Bahn
- **2019** — Commission launches public consultation on the recast, receiving over 6,000 responses
- **December 2020** — Commission publishes formal proposal (COM/2020/849) as part of the Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy
- **April 2021** — European Parliament adopts final text with 626 votes in favour
- **May 2021** — Council formally adopts the regulation
- **7 June 2021** — Regulation enters into force
- **7 June 2023** — Regulation applies in all member states (two-year transition period)
- **July 2025** — Deadline for operators to implement through-ticketing systems for journeys involving up to two connecting services

## Impact and consequences

The immediate effect was to harmonise rights across domestic and international rail travel for the first time. By mid-2024, the European Commission had received 47 formal complaints about non-compliance, primarily from German and Italian operators regarding delay compensation procedures. Deutsche Bahn alone reported processing over 340,000 compensation claims in 2023, a 19-fold increase from 2020, according to the company’s 2023 annual report.

The regulation’s most controversial provision—mandatory through-ticketing—has been slow to implement. As of April 2025, rail.eu reported that only 38% of eligible cross-border journeys in the EU offered seamless through-ticketing, with smaller operators in Eastern Europe citing IT integration costs of up to €12 million per operator. The European Rail Agency (ERA) acknowledged in its 2024 progress report that “the transition from separate booking systems remains the single greatest operational challenge.”

Accessibility provisions have had measurable impact. The European Disability Forum reported in 2024 that complaints from passengers with reduced mobility had dropped by 23% compared to 2021, though they noted that only 11 of 27 member states had fully implemented the requirement for real-time accessible information at stations by the deadline.

Enforcement remains uneven. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against Poland and Romania in October 2024 for failing to transpose the regulation into national law, and against Belgium for failure to enforce compensation rules on domestic services operated by SNCB/NMBS. Fines have not yet been imposed, but the Commission has warned that sanction amounts will follow the standard scale of up to 0.5% of the operator’s annual turnover for non-compliance from 2025 onward.

## Key questions answered

### What compensation can I get if my train in Europe is delayed?

Under EU law, you are entitled to 25% of the ticket price for delays of 60 to 119 minutes, and 50% for delays of 120 minutes or more. This applies to both domestic and international services on all licensed operators. The compensation must be paid within one month of your claim. The regulation also requires operators to offer rerouting at the earliest opportunity or a full refund for delays over 100 minutes.

### Do European train companies have to sell you a single ticket for a trip with multiple trains?

Yes, but the requirement is being phased in. Operators must offer through-ticketing for journeys involving up to two connecting services by July 2025. This means you can buy a single ticket for a multi-operator journey and have your rights protected for the entire trip—including compensation if a missed connection causes a delay. As of early 2025, about 38% of eligible cross-border journeys have implemented this.

### What help and support must European trains provide for passengers with disabilities?

Rail operators must provide free assistance at stations and on trains for persons with disabilities, must accept passengers’ own mobility equipment without extra charge, and must publish accessibility information for all stations and trains. If an operator refuses to carry a passenger because of a disability, they must provide a written justification. The regulation also sets minimum standards for information accessibility, including audio announcements and visual displays.

### How do train passenger rights in Europe compare to airline passenger rights?

Rail rights are generally stronger for delay compensation: airlines only pay compensation for delays over 3 hours (€250–600 depending on distance), while rail compensates from 60 minutes. However, air passengers have a clear right to care (meals, accommodation) from the start of the delay, while rail rights to care only kick in after 100 minutes for domestic and 60 minutes for international services. Rail also offers a rerouting right that airlines do not: the operator must find you an alternative route by any mode of transport if the delay exceeds 100 minutes.
