## What is it?

The General Product Safety Regulation (GPSR) rewrites the EU’s 20-year-old product safety framework to address risks from online marketplaces, direct-to-consumer e-commerce, and supply chains involving third-country sellers. It replaces the 2001 General Product Safety Directive and the 2020 Market Surveillance Regulation.

The core innovation: for the first time, online marketplaces such as Amazon, AliExpress, and Zalando are treated as formal economic operators with legal duties — not mere intermediaries. They must designate a single EU-based legal representative, set up a single point of contact for safety authorities, and proactively remove listed products that the Commission or national authorities flag as dangerous. If a marketplace cannot trace a seller, it must take down all the seller’s offers.

The GPSR also strengthens the Commission’s recall powers. From December 2024, the Commission can directly order a recall of a product sold across multiple member states through a single binding decision, bypassing the previous system of member-state-by-member-state action. The regulation creates a new European Product Deformation Network to link market surveillance authorities and customs, and mandates digital "product passports" (traceability records) for high-risk categories.

## Why was it introduced?

The trigger was a cascade of failures exposed by three parallel crises. The first was the Amazon-handled hoverboard fires of 2015–2016, where hundreds of self-balancing scooters imported from China caught fire during charging. National safety authorities in the UK and Germany struggled to act because Amazon was treated as a passive hosting platform under the 2000 E-Commerce Directive, not as a seller. An estimated 290,000 hoverboards were sold in the EU in 2015 alone, according to the Commission’s 2018 Product Safety Report, and recalls required separate negotiations with each national authority. The fires killed no one but damaged homes and burned a factory in Cologne — enough to push national regulators to demand change.

The second crisis was the 2020–2021 COVID-19 personal protective equipment (PPE) flood. The Commission’s 2021 evaluation of the Product Safety Directive found that between March and October 2020, EU customs detained 85 million counterfeit or non-compliant face masks, many sold via online marketplaces that claimed intermediary immunity. Trade Commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis publicly warned in July 2020 that "marketplaces are the new border" and that the existing framework was "not fit for purpose." The PPE crisis broke the political deadlock: member states that had previously resisted marketplace obligations — particularly Malta and Luxembourg, home to large Marketplace platforms — could no longer argue the current system worked.

The third crisis was the Temu and Shein explosion. By 2022, these Chinese-owned platforms were flooding EU consumers with low-cost goods — Temu averaged 50,000 new listings per day — and national authorities were overwhelmed. The Spanish consumer rights agency FACUA tested 50 Temu products in 2023 and found 24 contained toxic phthalates or lead above EU limits; no single EU authority could stop their sale because Temu had no EU headquarters. Commissioner for Justice and Consumers Didier Reynders told the European Parliament in January 2023 that "the business model of some platforms relies on regulatory gaps."

The political majority that passed the GPSR in March 2023 was broad but fragile. The European Parliament’s Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO) voted 36-6 in favour — the six opposing votes came from a coalition of Maltese MEPs (concerned about marketplace competition) and Polish MEPs (arguing it would hurt small sellers). The Council of the EU reached a qualified majority in November 2022; Luxembourg and Malta voted against, while Sweden, Poland, and Estonia abstained. The key compromise that held the coalition together was a phase-in period until December 2024 for marketplace obligations — giving platforms 18 months to comply — and a three-year delay on mandatory product passports for electronics.

The structural tension the GPSR addresses was fundamental: the 2001 Product Safety Directive assumed physical shops with traceable distributors, while the 2020 digital economy had markets where half of all products were sold by third-country drop-shippers with no EU presence. The Commission’s 2022 impact assessment estimated that 80% of dangerous product notifications in the EU’s Safety Gate system involved goods sold online, and that only 12% of recalls reached consumers effectively.

## Timeline

- **2001** — General Product Safety Directive (2001/95/EC) adopted; requires products to be safe but has no specific marketplace rules.
- **2015-2016** — Hoverboard fires spark national regulatory complaints; UK and German authorities fail to force Amazon to remove unsafe models.
- **2018** — Commission’s Product Safety Report finds that 67% of EU consumers have bought a product online, but only 16% received a recall notice.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 pandemic; EU customs detect 85 million non-compliant PPE items in nine months; marketplaces claim intermediary immunity.
- **June 2021** — Commission publishes its Product Safety Directive evaluation, calling for "fundamental reform" including marketplace obligations.
- **30 June 2022** — Commission proposes the GPSR; Reynders presents it as "the biggest update to EU product safety in 20 years."
- **30 November 2022** — Council adopts general approach by qualified majority; Luxembourg and Malta vote against.
- **14 March 2023** — European Parliament adopts the GPSR with 484 votes in favour, 108 against, 57 abstentions.
- **10 May 2023** — Regulation signed into force; published in the Official Journal.
- **13 December 2024** — Full application date; all marketplace obligations become binding.
- **13 December 2025** — Additional rules on product passports for electronics take effect (phase-in).

## Impact and consequences

The most immediate impact was on Amazon, which in 2023 sold over 600 million product units through third-party sellers in the EU alone. In response to the GPSR, Amazon announced in June 2024 it would require all marketplace sellers to provide EU-based legal representatives or face de-listing. The company also created a single EU product safety contact point in Luxembourg.

Temu and Shein adapted by rapidly registering EU legal entities. Shein appointed a Spanish subsidiary in Barcelona in September 2024; Temu named a Belgian entity in Antwerp the same month. Both platforms faced scrutiny from the European Commission in late 2024 over compliance with traceability requirements.

By January 2025, the Commission had already issued four direct recall orders under the new powers: a batch of Chinese-manufactured baby nightlights (phthalate contamination), a German-made children’s costume (flammability risk), a model of e-bike battery from Poland (overheating), and a Portuguese-produced pesticide sold on multiple platforms (mislabeling). Each order was binding across all 27 member states.

National market surveillance authorities reported a 40% increase in successful tracing of dangerous products in the first year of GPSR application, according to a Commission briefing in March 2025, though the same briefing noted that only 18 member states had fully implemented the digital infrastructure for product passports.

Smaller EU-based sellers complained that the EU representative requirement added €200–800 annually in compliance costs per product line. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) countered that this cost was a fraction of the €5 billion the Commission estimates EU consumers lose annually to dangerous imported products.

## Key questions answered

### Are online marketplaces now legally responsible for what people sell on them?

Yes, for the first time. The GPSR treats Amazon, Temu, AliExpress, and similar platforms as "economic operators" — not just hosting intermediaries. They must appoint a single EU-based legal representative, remove dangerous goods when notified by safety authorities, and trace sellers. If a marketplace cannot identify the seller, it must remove all of that seller's listings. This overturns the previous safe-harbour approach from the 2000 E-Commerce Directive.

### Who can order a product recall in the EU now?

The European Commission can issue a direct recall order for products sold across multiple member states — a single binding decision that all 27 countries must enforce. Before the GPSR, a recall required separate action by each national authority, which often meant dangerous products remained on shelves in other EU countries for weeks or months. The Commission exercised this power for the first time in January 2025.

### What products are affected by the new rules?

The regulation applies to virtually all non-food consumer products sold in the EU — from electronics and toys to clothing and cosmetics. High-risk categories (like children's products, electronics, and PPE) require a digital "product passport" with traceability records. Food, medicines, and medical devices remain covered by their own specific regulations (like the General Food Law or the Medical Devices Regulation).

### How do sellers outside the EU comply?

Any non-EU manufacturer or seller must appoint a legal representative established in the EU — a person or company that can be held legally responsible for the product's safety. Goods sold via online marketplaces also require the marketplace to act as the responsible operator if the seller does not provide traceable contact details. This has forced major Chinese platforms like Temu and Shein to establish EU-based subsidiaries.
