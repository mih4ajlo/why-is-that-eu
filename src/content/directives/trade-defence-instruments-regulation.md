## What is it?

The EU’s Trade Defence Instruments (TDI) Regulation is the legal framework for imposing anti-dumping and anti-subsidy measures on imports that harm EU industry. It replaced earlier legislation dating to 1995, updating the rules to align with modern trade realities — particularly the rise of China and other state-capitalist economies.

The regulation sets out how the European Commission investigates complaints, calculates dumping margins, assesses injury to EU producers, and imposes duties. It also introduced a new methodology for calculating normal value when imports come from countries with "significant market distortions" — a change explicitly aimed at China.

## Why was it introduced?

The catalyst for the 2016 TDI Regulation was the bitter, decade-long battle over China’s "Market Economy Status" (MES). Under China’s 2001 WTO accession protocol, EU anti-dumping rules could treat China as a non-market economy until 2016. This allowed the Commission to use "surrogate" countries (e.g. the US or Brazil) to calculate normal values — producing higher dumping margins and higher duties.

As the 2016 deadline approached, European industry panicked. The steel sector — already reeling from global overcapacity — warned that granting China blanket MES would unleash a flood of cheap imports. In December 2015, the European Steel Association (Eurofer) published a study claiming that an MES concession could increase Chinese steel exports to the EU by 35-50%, threatening 130,000 jobs. Trade Commissioner Cecilia Malmström faced intense pressure from the "Steel Caucus" — MEPs from steel-reliant regions in Germany, Italy, Belgium, and Poland.

Yet the Commission could not simply deny China MES without violating WTO rules. The solution, crafted by Malmström’s team in 2015-2016, was a legislative workaround: new anti-dumping rules that neither granted nor denied MES but instead created a third category — "significant market distortions." Under the new methodology, if the Commission found evidence of state intervention (price controls, state-owned banks, industrial policy), it could disregard domestic prices and costs in calculations, using international benchmarks instead.

The political coalition that got this passed was fragile but effective. The European Parliament voted 598 to 60 in favour in February 2016, with broad support from the EPP, S&D, and ALDE groups. The Greens and some Nordic countries opposed it, arguing it was a protectionist tool that would raise consumer prices. China's then-ambassador to the EU, Yang Yanyi, warned it would "seriously damage EU-China trade relations." But the steel crisis — with US tariffs looming and global overcapacity peaking at 700 million tonnes in 2015 — gave the legislation urgency.

The timing was also shaped by the 2015 EU-China summit, where President Xi Jinping demanded MES as a diplomatic prize. The Commission delayed a decision until after the summit, then used the new regulation to effectively sidestep the question. Malmström later told the European Parliament that the new rules were "not about targeting China specifically" — a statement widely seen as diplomatic cover.

What changed decisively was the 2015 "surrogate country" ruling by the European Court of Justice in *C & J Clark International* (joined cases C-659/13 and C-34/14). The Court annulled anti-dumping duties on Chinese shoes because the Commission failed to justify its choice of Brazil as a surrogate country. This legal vulnerability, combined with the 2016 deadline, forced the Commission to act.

## Timeline

- **2001** — China joins WTO; EU anti-dumping rules treat it as non-market economy
- **2012** — European steel industry begins lobbying for MES exclusion
- **2014** — ECJ annuls shoe duties in *C & J Clark* (C-659/13 and C-34/14)
- **2015** — Commission proposes revised TDI Regulation on 9 April
- **2015** — European Parliament's INTA committee approves text in November
- **2016** — Full Parliament votes 598-60 on 4 February; Council adopts in March
- **2016** — Regulation published as 2016/1037/EU on 8 July; enters force 20 days later
- **2017** — Commission applies new methodology in steel anti-dumping cases
- **2018** — First use of "significant market distortions" in aluminium foil case from China

## Impact and consequences

The TDI Regulation gave the EU new legal tools but did not reverse the fundamental trend of import penetration. Between 2016 and 2021, the Commission opened 53 new anti-dumping investigations under the new rules, roughly on par with the previous five years. However, the duties imposed were higher — an average of 35% versus 25% under the old methodology, according to a 2022 study by the Global Trade Alert project.

The biggest impact was political. Beijing formally challenged the new methodology at the WTO in December 2016, arguing it violated anti-dumping rules (DS516). The dispute remains unresolved, as the WTO Appellate Body is non-functional. This effectively granted the EU legal cover — no one could overturn the rules.

Smaller EU member states with steel and ceramics industries — Poland, Slovakia, Belgium — reported fewer anti-dumping complaints being dismissed at the preliminary stage. The Commission’s 2019 report on TDI noted that the "market distortion" clause was used in 12 investigations by 2020, all against Chinese products.

Critics — particularly the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) — argued the regulation raised prices for downstream industries. A 2018 study by the University of St. Gallen estimated that steel anti-dumping measures cost EU automotive and construction firms €2.5 billion annually in higher input prices. The Commission itself acknowledged this tension in its 2019 evaluation report.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU need new rules for handling unfair trade instead of just deciding whether to grant market economy status to countries?

Granting full MES to China would have lowered anti-dumping duties, while denying it outright violated WTO rules after the 2016 expiry. The "significant market distortions" methodology provided a WTO-consistent middle ground that maintained effective protection without triggering direct retaliation. It also aimed to future-proof the rules against other state-influenced economies like Vietnam or Russia.

### Why did China strongly oppose the EU's updated rules on handling cheap imports and unfair trade practices?

China saw the regulation as a permanent stain on its market economy status. The term "significant market distortions" was viewed as discriminatory, singling out China by name in Commission explanatory notes. Beijing also feared the methodology would spread — and indeed, the UK and EU later used similar language in forced labour import bans.

### Did the EU's updated anti-dumping and anti-subsidy rules actually help European steelmakers?

Partially. EU steel production stabilised after 2016, with output remaining near 160 million tonnes annually according to Eurofer. But imports from China fell only modestly — from 7.6 million tonnes in 2015 to 6.2 million in 2019 per Eurostat. The biggest impact was on Chinese exporters themselves: many pivoted to Vietnam or Turkey, which then faced their own EU investigations. The regulation was a shield, not a solution. The deeper problem — global overcapacity — required the EU-US-Japan trilateral initiatives of 2018-2020 to address.
