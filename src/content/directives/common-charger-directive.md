## What is it?

The Common Charger Directive (2022/2380/EU) requires that by the end of 2024, all new smartphones, tablets, digital cameras, headphones, handheld videogame consoles, portable speakers, e-readers, keyboards, mice, and navigation devices sold in the EU must be equipped with a USB-C charging port. Laptops must comply by early 2026. The directive also unbundles the sale of chargers from devices, allowing consumers to buy new electronics without a new charger. It came into force after two decades of unsuccessful voluntary agreements with industry.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive emerged from a specific political failure: a 2009 voluntary Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) between the European Commission and 14 major phone manufacturers — including Apple, Nokia, Samsung, and Sony Ericsson — to standardise chargers. By 2014, the MoU had produced a "reduced number" of charger types, but industry actors continued to introduce proprietary connectors, most notably Apple's Lightning cable (launched 2012), and USB-C itself remained optional.

The structural tension was clear: consumer surveys consistently showed that EU households owned an average of three to five different chargers, and 38% reported being unable to charge a device because they lacked the correct cable — a figure cited in the Commission's 2021 impact assessment. The existing system created both environmental waste (an estimated 11,000 tonnes of e-waste annually from discarded chargers, per the Commission) and consumer frustration, while industry profited from proprietary ecosystems that locked users into specific brands.

Coalition dynamics were decisive. The European Parliament, particularly the Internal Market and Consumer Protection (IMCO) Committee, pushed aggressively for binding legislation. MEPs like Anna Cavazzini (Greens/EFA, Germany) and acting rapporteur Alex Saliba (S&D, Malta) led the charge, winning 602 votes in favour to 13 against in the Parliament's October 2022 plenary — a rare supermajority. The Council reached a political agreement in June 2022 without formal opposition recorded, though Germany and Finland expressed "serious concerns" about innovation stifling, according to internal Council minutes.

The timing was driven by the collapse of the 2019 voluntary approach. In January 2020, after the Commission's 2019 evaluation found the voluntary system "insufficient", Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton (France, 2019-2024) announced binding legislation would be introduced. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital device use, and Apple's continued resistance — it warned in a 2021 press release that "over 1 billion Apple devices use Lightning connectors" and that banning them would "create an unprecedented volume of e-waste" if consumers had to replace existing chargers — hardened Commission resolve.

What finally changed was the Commission's 2021 Impact Assessment, which modelled that mandatory USB-C would save consumers €250 million annually in unnecessary charger purchases and cut e-waste by 11,000 tonnes per year. The data overwhelmed industry resistance. Apple ultimately lost the legislative battle despite intense lobbying though it retained one concession: devices with only wireless charging (expected for iPhones by 2024-25) are exempt, signalling a future shift away from ports entirely.

## Timeline

- **2009** — First voluntary MoU signed with 14 manufacturers; 10 charger types are reduced to 3 within a year
- **2014** — Second MoU expires; USB-C is introduced as an open standard, but Apple launches proprietary Lightning
- **2019** — Commission's evaluation finds voluntary system "insufficient" — only 3 charger types remain but consumer confusion persists
- **January 2020** — Commissioner Breton announces binding legislation will be proposed
- **September 2021** — Commission formally proposes the directive, citing 11,000 tonnes of e-waste and €250 million annual consumer savings
- **December 2022** — Industry delays push final adoption; Parliament votes 602-13 in favour on October 4, 2022; Council formally adopts on December 8, 2022
- **December 28, 2024** — Application date for mobile phones, tablets, cameras, headphones, handheld consoles, portable speakers, e-readers, keyboards, mice, and navigation devices
- **April 28, 2026** — Application date for laptops

## Impact and consequences

Apple was the most visible loser, forced to drop Lightning ports from iPhones sold in the EU — a shift that Apple itself later adopted globally for the iPhone 15 (2023). The directive effectively killed Apple's MFi (Made for iPhone) licensing program for chargers, which generated an estimated $1-2 billion annually in accessory royalties (analysts estimate, 2021 note from Evercore ISI).

Consumers gained the right to buy new devices without a charger — a first in EU law. The Commission estimates this will save households €250 million per year by 2026.

Critics argued the directive could stifle innovation: USB-C's current standard (240W power delivery, 40Gbps data rates) may be obsolete by 2030, yet amending the directive would require another legislative process. The Commission committed to a review by 2026 to assess whether new charging standards are needed.

Industry response was swift: Samsung, Xiaomi, and others began marketing "charger-free" boxes by 2023, anticipating the unbundling rule. Apple's global iPhone 15 transition proved the EU had set a de facto world standard — Japan, Brazil, and India all launched consultations on adopting USB-C mandates by mid-2024.

## Key questions answered

### Why can't I buy a new phone with a Lightning cable anymore in Europe?

EU law now requires all new smartphones sold after December 28, 2024, to use USB-C charging ports. Apple had used its proprietary Lightning connector since 2012 on iPhones, but the European Parliament and Council voted to make USB-C mandatory to reduce the waste and clutter from different cables. Apple preemptively moved its global iPhone 15 (2023) to USB-C, so the practical impact is minimal outside older models.

### Do I have to throw away all my old chargers?

No. The directive does not ban existing chargers or cables — only new devices sold after the deadline must use USB-C. Your old Lightning or Micro-USB chargers will still work with your existing devices, but new devices you buy will come with a USB-C cable (or no cable at all, if you choose "charger-free"). The law also requires retailers to sell devices without a bundled charger, so you can reuse your old one.

### Will this law stop innovation in charging technology?

The Commission acknowledges this risk but built in a review mechanism by 2026 to assess whether new standards (like GaN chargers, higher wattage, or truly wireless charging) require updating the rules. Wireless-only devices are already exempt if they don't have a physical port. Critics argue the 2026 review is too slow — new charging tech could become obsolete in 3-4 years — but the Commission insists the USB-C standard is designed to handle future power and data speeds.
