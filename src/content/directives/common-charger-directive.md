## What is it?

The Common Charger Directive (formally Directive 2022/2380/EU) amends the Radio Equipment Directive to require that most consumer electronic devices sold in the EU use USB Type-C as their charging port. It covers smartphones, tablets, cameras, headphones, portable speakers, e-readers, keyboards, mice, and earbuds.

The directive also requires that chargers be sold separately from devices — manufacturers can no longer bundle a charging brick in every box. This applies from 28 December 2024 for small and medium devices, and from 28 April 2026 for laptops.

## Why was it introduced?

The short answer: Apple refused to adopt USB-C for over a decade, and the EU finally ran out of patience.

The longer answer involves three converging pressures that built over fifteen years.

**E-waste.** By the late 2010s, the EU estimated that discarded and unused chargers generated around 11,000 tonnes of e-waste per year in Europe alone. Every new device often came with a proprietary charger that replaced the previous one — and the old one went in a drawer, and eventually in landfill.

**Consumer frustration.** Surveys consistently showed consumers found incompatible chargers annoying and wasteful. Carrying multiple cables for multiple devices, or arriving somewhere without the right charger, was a universal irritation.

**Market fragmentation.** In the early 2000s, the EU counted over 30 different charger types. By 2009, the Commission had already tried a voluntary approach.

The **voluntary industry agreement of 2009** is critical to understanding why the directive exists. Facing the threat of regulation, most major manufacturers — Samsung, Nokia, LG, and others — signed a Memorandum of Understanding committing to converge on Micro-USB. This worked: by 2011, most Android manufacturers had standardised on Micro-USB. But Apple signed too, and then immediately offered a Micro-USB adapter as an accessory instead of actually changing its Lightning connector. They complied with the letter of the agreement, not the spirit.

When USB-C emerged in 2014–2015, most Android manufacturers migrated over several years. Apple introduced USB-C on the iPad Pro in 2018, on MacBooks from 2016 — but kept Lightning on iPhones until 2023.

By 2019, the Commission had concluded that voluntary agreements had failed. It launched a formal impact assessment, and the legislative process began. Apple lobbied heavily against it, arguing (variously) that it would stifle innovation, create more e-waste from obsolete Lightning accessories, and harm consumers. The European Parliament was unpersuaded — the "innovation" argument was particularly dismissed, since USB-C had been the technical standard since 2015.

The directive was adopted in October 2022 with near-unanimous support in Parliament (602 votes in favour, 13 against).

## Timeline

- **2003** — European Commission begins studying charger fragmentation; 30+ incompatible chargers in the market
- **2009** — Voluntary Memorandum of Understanding signed by major manufacturers including Apple; commits to common charger convergence on Micro-USB
- **2011** — Most Android manufacturers standardise on Micro-USB; Apple offers a Micro-USB adapter accessory but keeps its proprietary 30-pin connector
- **2012** — Apple introduces the Lightning connector, replacing the 30-pin connector; does not adopt Micro-USB
- **2014** — USB Type-C specification finalised by USB Implementers Forum
- **2015–2018** — Android manufacturers progressively migrate from Micro-USB to USB-C
- **2018** — Apple introduces USB-C on iPad Pro; MacBooks have used USB-C since 2016
- **2019** — European Commission concludes voluntary approach has failed; launches formal review and impact assessment
- **2020** — Commission publishes feasibility study; USB-C identified as preferred common standard
- **2021** — European Parliament passes resolution demanding binding legislation by 2022
- **September 2021** — Commission publishes formal legislative proposal
- **October 2022** — Directive 2022/2380/EU adopted (602–13 in Parliament)
- **December 2022** — Directive enters into force
- **September 2023** — Apple releases iPhone 15 with USB-C, dropping Lightning after 11 years
- **28 December 2024** — Compliance deadline for small and medium devices (smartphones, tablets, earbuds, etc.)
- **28 April 2026** — Compliance deadline for laptops

## Impact and consequences

The most visible consequence was Apple's switch to USB-C on the iPhone 15 in September 2023 — over a year before the compliance deadline. Apple had resisted for over a decade; the directive's passage made the transition inevitable and commercially pointless to delay further.

The bundled-charger ban has more complex effects. The EU estimates it will reduce manufacturing of new chargers substantially. But it also means consumers who buy a new phone without a charger must either already own a compatible one or buy one separately. Critics argued this is a hidden price increase; supporters noted most consumers already have USB-C chargers and argued that unbundling makes the true cost visible.

For accessory makers with Lightning-based products (cables, cases, speaker docks, car chargers), the transition has required product refreshes. Apple's MFi licensing programme for Lightning accessories — a significant revenue stream — has been disrupted.

## Key questions answered

### Why USB-C specifically, and not a different standard for the Common Charger Directive?

USB-C was the only cable and connector type that met all the technical requirements: it supports both charging and data transfer, can deliver sufficient power for everything from earbuds to laptops, has broad industry support, and was already dominant on most non-Apple devices. The alternative — defining a new EU-specific standard — would have been counterproductive and taken years longer.

### Why did Apple fight the Common Charger Directive so hard?

Lightning was a significant source of revenue. Apple licensed the MFi (Made for iPhone) programme to accessory manufacturers, earning royalties on every certified Lightning cable and accessory sold. USB-C accessories are not subject to Apple licensing fees. The transition also meant consumers' existing Lightning accessories became less useful.

### Why wasn't the Common Charger Directive done sooner?

The Commission genuinely believed the 2009 voluntary agreement would work. It did reduce fragmentation — from 30+ types down to essentially two (Micro-USB and Lightning). But the failure to achieve full convergence, combined with the environmental cost, eventually justified binding legislation. The political will in Parliament had been building since at least 2014.

### Why do chargers need to be sold separately under the Common Charger Directive?

Bundling chargers with devices drives unnecessary manufacturing. If a consumer already has a compatible charger (increasingly likely as USB-C proliferates), the bundled charger is waste from the moment the box is opened. Unbundling forces the cost to be visible and lets consumers who already have chargers skip the purchase.
