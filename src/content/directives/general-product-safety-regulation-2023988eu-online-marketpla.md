## What is it?

The General Product Safety Regulation (GPSR) is a European law that replaces the 2001 General Product Safety Directive, updating the EU's framework for ensuring consumer goods sold on the European market are safe. Its central innovation is bringing online marketplaces — from Amazon to AliExpress — directly into the regulatory net for the first time.

Under GPSR, marketplaces must appoint a single point of contact for national authorities, remove listings for dangerous products within two business days of notification, and proactively check products at scale for safety risks. The regulation also gives market surveillance authorities — the national bodies that inspect and test products — the power to order a marketplace to take down fraudulent listings, even without first contacting the seller. For recalls, the GPSR strengthens requirements: consumers must be contacted directly when a dangerous product is recalled, and recall notices must be easier to understand and more prominent than before.

The regulation entered into force on December 12, 2023, and will apply from December 13, 2024.

## Why was it introduced?

The driving force behind GPSR was the explosion of online marketplaces selling directly to European consumers — and a series of deadly product failures that exposed the old system as completely unfit for e-commerce.

The 2001 General Product Safety Directive was written for a world of physical shops and domestic supply chains. It assumed a manufacturer or importer was always identifiable and within the EU. By 2018, that assumption was collapsing. The Commission's own impact assessment found that in 2017, 66% of European consumers bought from online marketplaces; among 16–24 year olds, the figure was 84%. At the same time, the EU's Rapid Alert System (RAPEX) for dangerous products was catching a flood of non-compliant goods — nearly 2,200 alerts in 2017 alone, with toys, motor vehicles, and electrical appliances topping the list.

The specific incident that crystallised the need for reform was the series of Samsung Galaxy Note 7 battery fires in 2016. The phone was recalled globally, but EU safety authorities had no direct power to force marketplaces to remove listings for dangerous third-party products — and the existing voluntary cooperation was slow and inconsistent. Then in 2017, hoverboards — self-balancing scooters sold almost entirely online — began catching fire across Europe. A Commission investigation found that of 67 models tested, 85% failed electrical safety standards. The existing directive could only hold the importer liable, but for direct-from-China sales on marketplaces, there was no identifiable EU importer.

Inside the Commission, the GPSR was led by Justice Commissioner Didier Reynders and Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton, both of whom made product safety an element of the broader digital sovereignty agenda. The political coalition that secured passage was unusual: consumer protection groups like BEUC, which had long pushed for marketplace liability, aligned with large European retailers — companies like Carrefour and MediaMarkt — who complained that online platforms were undercutting them with unsafe, untested goods. The European Parliament's Internal Market Committee (IMCO) under rapporteur Marion Walsmann (EPP, Germany) drove the file, and the Parliament adopted its negotiating position in June 2022 with a large majority — 545 votes in favour, 44 against.

The opposition came from online marketplaces themselves — Amazon, eBay, and Wish — and from some free-trade-oriented member states led by the Netherlands and Czechia. Amazon argued that making marketplaces proactive safety enforcers would kill the business model of small third-party sellers. The platform industry association EDiMA (European Digital Media Association) warned of "disproportionate burdens" and called for a narrower approach based on notice-and-takedown.

The timing was crucial. In 2019, the European Court of Justice's *Coty Germany* ruling (C-230/16) had already eroded the "safe harbour" defence for online platforms in trademark cases. Then in 2020, the European Parliament's special committee on foreign interference (INGE) identified counterfeit and unsafe goods sold via marketplaces as a vector for organised crime and sanctions evasion. And critically, the 2022 EU Digital Services Act (DSA) created a new legal framework for platform accountability — GPSR was explicitly designed to complement the DSA, regulating product safety specifically while the DSA covered illegal content generally.

What changed the deadlock was the 2021 von der Leyen Commission's push for "Europe fit for the digital age." The Commission published its GPSR proposal on June 30, 2021 — wedged between the DSA (December 2020) and the Digital Markets Act (March 2022) — as part of a coordinated offensive to regulate digital platforms. The legislative process took only 18 months, unusually fast for a major consumer regulation.

- **May 2023** — Regulation formally adopted by Council.
- **December 12, 2023** — GPSR enters into force.
- **December 13, 2024** — GPSR applies (full enforcement start date).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation directly affects over 10,000 online marketplace operators active in the EU, though enforcement will initially focus on the largest platforms — Amazon, AliExpress, eBay, Wish, and Temu. The Commission estimated in its 2021 impact assessment that compliance costs for marketplaces would range from €100 million to €250 million across the EU over five years, but that consumer harm avoided — from injuries, fires, and toxic exposure — would exceed €1 billion in the same period.

The most contested provision is the "two-business-day takedown" rule. During negotiations, marketplaces argued that a three-day window was needed to verify safety claims; consumer groups wanted 24 hours. The compromise of two business days — which can extend to five for complex cases — is now the benchmark. The first enforcement test will be whether smaller marketplaces can actually comply; the Commission has promised guidance by mid-2024.

For recalls, the GPSR requires that consumers be contacted "directly, where possible" via email, text, or app notification — not just through a public notice on the company website. This addresses the widespread problem of recall notices on product pages being buried below promotional content. The European Consumer Centre Network reported in 2022 that only 12% of consumers who bought a recalled product online were ever informed.

Member states are obliged to strengthen their market surveillance authorities. According to the Commission's 2020 Fitness Check on product safety, the EU's 27 member states collectively employed just 573 market surveillance officers — meaning each officer was responsible for monitoring approximately €18.5 billion of consumer goods sold annually. The GPSR requires member states to ensure "adequate resources" but sets no minimum staffing level, which consumer groups like BEUC have criticised as a weakness.

## Key questions answered

### Why did Europe rewrite its product safety rules for the internet age?

The old 2001 directive assumed products were sold in physical shops by identifiable EU companies. But by 2017, two-thirds of Europeans shopped online, and dangerous goods — from exploding hoverboards to toxic toys — were flooding in from third-country sellers with no EU presence. The 2001 law couldn't hold marketplaces accountable for products they effectively sold. After a series of fires and recall failures, the EU decided marketplaces needed the same safety obligations as physical retailers.

### How can an online marketplace be forced to remove a dangerous product?

National market surveillance authorities can now issue a direct takedown order to a marketplace — no court order needed. The marketplace must remove or disable access to the listing within two business days. If the marketplace believes the order is wrong, it can appeal, but must remove the listing first. This "notice-and-takedown" system is modelled on the Digital Services Act, but for products instead of content.

### Why can't consumers be reached directly when a product they bought is dangerous?

That's exactly what the GPSR fixes. Previously, companies could fulfil their recall obligations by publishing a notice on a website or in a newspaper. The GPSR requires companies to contact customers directly — by email, text, or app notification — if they have the contact information. For products bought as guests without accounts, marketplaces must ask customers to register for recall alerts. In its impact assessment, the Commission found that fewer than 1 in 10 consumers received a direct recall notice for online purchases.

### What happens if a marketplace disobey a takedown order?

Member states set their own penalties, but the regulation requires fines to be "effective, proportionate, and dissuasive" — meaning they must hurt enough to change behaviour. The maximum penalty for a serious breach can be up to 4% of annual turnover in the member state concerned. For a platform like Amazon, which generated €50 billion in European revenue in 2022, that could mean a fine of up to €2 billion for a single violation.
