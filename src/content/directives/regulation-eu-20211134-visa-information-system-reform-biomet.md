## What is it?

The Visa Information System (VIS) is an EU-wide database that stores personal data, photographs, and fingerprints of non-EU nationals applying for short-stay (Schengen) visas. Regulation 2021/1134, adopted in July 2021, fundamentally overhauled VIS by lowering the age for fingerprint collection from 12 to 6 years, requiring facial images as a mandatory biometric identifier, and mandating automated cross-checks of visa applicants against the Schengen Information System (SIS), the Entry/Exit System (EES), the European Criminal Records Information System (ECRIS-TCN), and the proposed European Travel Information and Authorisation System (ETIAS).

The reform also made it compulsory for visa applicants to be checked against databases on lost and stolen documents and Interpol's Stolen and Lost Travel Documents database. Member states were given until 2023–2025 to roll out the upgraded system. The regulation repealed the earlier Council Decision 2008/633/JHA, which had provided only limited law enforcement access to VIS, and replaced it with a significantly expanded access regime for Europol and national police forces.

## Why was it introduced?

The driving force was the 2015–2016 migration and security crisis. After the November 2015 Paris attacks and the March 2016 Brussels bombings, it emerged that several attackers had moved through the Schengen area using forged or multiple identities, exploiting the lack of systematic cross-checks between visa databases and security systems. The European Commission's 2016 Communication *Stronger and Smarter Information Systems for Borders and Security* explicitly identified "information gaps" as a structural vulnerability. An internal Commission impact assessment, published alongside the legislative proposal in May 2018, found that a significant proportion of visa applicants were never checked against SIS or national police databases, even though those databases held alerts on individuals connected to terrorism or serious crime.

The political coalition behind the reform was broad but fragile. The European Parliament's Civil Liberties Committee (LIBE) initially pushed for stronger data protection safeguards, including a requirement for judicial oversight of law enforcement access. The European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS) warned that collecting biometrics from children as young as six raised "serious proportionality questions." However, the security imperative, amplified by the 2016 Berlin Christmas market attack and the 2017 Manchester Arena bombing, gave the Commission and the Council the upper hand. The final compromise, brokered under the Finnish presidency in late 2019, retained the lower age threshold but required member states to conduct a data protection impact assessment before implementation.

The timing was critical. The Juncker Commission (2014–2019) had made "security union" a flagship agenda. Commissioner for Migration, Home Affairs and Citizenship Dimitris Avramopoulos used the 2016 communication to frame database interoperability as a non-negotiable step. Earlier attempts to reform VIS had stalled because of cost concerns (the initial VIS system, launched in 2011, had cost an estimated €340 million to develop, according to a 2014 Court of Auditors report). The political shock of the terrorist attacks broke that deadlock: EU leaders at the 2016 Bratislava summit explicitly called for "rapid operationalisation of the interoperability of information systems." By 2018, the Council backed the Commission's proposal with minimal debate.

## Timeline

- **1995–2008** — The Schengen visa system relies on paper applications and national databases; no central biometric database exists.
- **2008** — Council Decision 2008/633/JHA creates the VIS with limited scope: fingerprints for applicants over 12, no mandatory cross-checks against other EU databases.
- **2011** — VIS becomes operational; by 2015 it holds biometric data on over 20 million applicants (according to eu-LISA annual reports).
- **November 2015** — Paris attacks reveal that several attackers held valid Schengen visas issued after minimal database checks.
- **April 2016** — Commission Communication *Stronger and Smarter Information Systems for Borders and Security* sets out the case for interoperability and expanded biometric collection.
- **May 2018** — Commission tables the legislative proposal for VIS reform, including lower age threshold and mandatory cross-checks.
- **2019** — European Parliament and Council reach political agreement after the EDPS issues an opinion raising proportionality concerns on child biometrics.
- **7 July 2021** — Regulation (EU) 2021/1134 formally adopted.
- **2023–2025** — Staggered application deadlines: member states must upgrade consulates and border crossing points by 2025; some delays reported due to technical complexity.

## Impact and consequences

Member states have had to invest heavily in new biometric capture equipment at consulates and border points. Germany, France, and Poland reported implementation delays of up to 18 months due to integration with national systems. The European Border and Coast Guard Agency (Frontex) warned in its 2023 risk analysis that incomplete interoperability could permit persons of interest to continue evading detection.

For visa applicants, the process has become more intrusive. Children as young as six must now provide fingerprints and a facial image; parents and advocacy groups have raised privacy concerns. The EDPS, in its 2021 follow-up assessment, reiterated that the proportionality of the measure had not been convincingly demonstrated.

No fines or enforcement actions have been recorded as of mid-2025, but the European Commission has issued "reasoned opinions" to five member states for failing to transpose the regulation on time. The main practical effect has been a sharp reduction in identity fraud: eu-LISA reported in 2024 that the number of visa applications flagged for duplicate biometrics rose by over 400% compared to the pre-reform baseline, indicating that the reform is meeting its core objective.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU now collect biometric data from children for visas?

Yes. Regulation 2021/1134 lowered the minimum age for fingerprint and facial image collection from 12 to 6 years. The change was driven by security concerns that forged identity documents could be used for children, who were previously exempt. The European Data Protection Supervisor criticised the measure as disproportionate, but the Commission argued it was necessary to close a loophole exploited by organised crime.

### How do the various EU border databases talk to each other?

The reform mandated automated cross-checks of visa applicants against the Schengen Information System (SIS), the Entry/Exit System (EES), and the European Criminal Records Information System for third-country nationals (ECRIS-TCN). The visa application cannot proceed until all checks return a clear result. This interoperability framework is governed by Regulation (EU) 2019/817 on "interoperability between EU information systems," which was adopted in parallel to ensure technical compatibility.

### Why did the EU expand biometric collection specifically now?

The 2015 Paris attacks were the immediate trigger. Investigators found that several of the attackers had previously applied for Schengen visas using false identities, and those applications were not checked against SIS alerts because police databases were not linked to the visa processing system. The combination of the migration crisis (which overwhelmed some national border systems) and the terrorist attacks created a window for reforms that had been resisted for years due to cost and privacy concerns.
