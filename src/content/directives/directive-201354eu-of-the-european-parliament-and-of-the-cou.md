## What is it?

Directive 2013/54/EU translates the International Labour Organization’s Maritime Labour Convention (MLC, 2006) into binding EU law. It requires all ships calling at EU ports — whether they fly an EU flag or a third-country flag — to comply with the MLC’s core standards on seafarers' employment conditions, accommodation, food, health protection, medical care, and welfare. The directive empowers port state control officers to detain non-compliant vessels and links enforcement to the existing port state control regime (Directive 2009/16/EC).

The directive also sets up a certification system: an EU‑flagged ship must carry a Maritime Labour Certificate and a Declaration of Maritime Labour Compliance. Ships from non‑EU countries are inspected periodically, and if found seriously deficient, can be banned from EU waters.

## Why was it introduced?

The MLC, adopted by the ILO in February 2006, was meant to create a single, global “bill of rights” for the world’s 1.5 million seafarers. But the Convention only enters into force for a state once that state ratifies it — and until 2011, fewer than 20 countries had done so. The EU, which collectively controls over 40% of world shipping tonnage at the time, saw an opportunity to accelerate enforcement and prevent a race to the bottom.

The immediate trigger was the looming entry into force of the MLC in August 2013. In 2010, the European Commission began preparing a directive to ensure that all EU member states would implement the Convention simultaneously, avoiding a patchwork of national laws that would allow unscrupulous shipowners to shop for the most lenient flag. The Commission’s then‑Transport Commissioner, Siim Kallas, argued that without EU action, “decent working conditions for seafarers would remain a paper promise.”

Two earlier attempts to harmonise maritime labour enforcement had stalled — a 2005 proposal on minimum standards for ship accommodation and a 2007 push to update the 1994 Working Time Directive for seafarers. Both failed because they tried to create entirely new EU requirements, which provoked fierce resistance from shipowner associations (e.g., the European Community Shipowners’ Associations, ECSA) who feared losing competitiveness to flags of convenience. The MLC framework broke that deadlock: it was an already‑negotiated global compromise, acceptable to both unions (the European Transport Workers’ Federation, ETF) and employers. The directive merely made it enforceable.

The European Parliament’s rapporteur, German MEP Thomas Mann (EPP), pushed for stronger inspection powers and faster phasing‑in, but the biggest battle was over whether to extend the directive to fishing vessels. The Parliament wanted inclusion; the Council, pressed by Spain, France, and Portugal who feared high costs for small fishing fleets, refused. Fishing vessels were left out — a gap later partially addressed by the 2017 Work in Fishing Convention (ILO 188) and a separate EU directive in 2019.

The vote in the Parliament on 12 September 2013 was 623 in favour, 32 against, with 19 abstentions — a clear consensus. The directive entered into force on 20 November 2013, just a few months after the MLC itself came into effect globally on 20 August 2013.

## Timeline

- **23 February 2006** — ILO adopts the Maritime Labour Convention (MLC, 2006) in Geneva.
- **20 August 2011** — MLC receives the required 30 ratifications; the two‑year countdown to entry into force begins.
- **23 March 2012** — European Commission publishes its proposal for a directive enforcing the MLC (COM(2012) 134).
- **12 September 2013** — European Parliament adopts the directive at first reading (623‑32‑19).
- **10 October 2013** — Council formally adopts the directive.
- **20 November 2013** — Directive 2013/54/EU enters into force.
- **20 May 2014** — EU member states must transpose the directive into national law.
- **28 December 2018** — Amending Directive 2018/739/EU aligns the text with the 2014 and 2016 amendments to the MLC.
- **2019–2020** — First round of port state control inspections under the directive; the European Maritime Safety Agency (EMSA) reports “broadly satisfactory compliance” but notes persistent problems with seafarers’ employment agreements on non‑EU ships.

## Impact and consequences

The directive created Europe’s first binding, uniform enforcement of international seafarers’ rights. By 2016, all 28 EU member states (plus Iceland and Norway) had ratified the MLC, partly because the directive required them to. The compliance rate among EU‑flagged ships reached 98% within four years, according to EMSA’s 2017 port state control report. For non‑EU ships, the detention rate due to MLC‑related deficiencies hovered around 4% — lower than for structural safety issues but still resulting in dozens of ships being banned from EU ports.

The biggest practical change was the introduction of the Maritime Labour Certificate. Shipowners reported an average cost increase of 3–5% for compliance (mainly for crew accommodation upgrades and medical insurance), but the European Community Shipowners’ Associations (ECSA) accepted it as a price for market access.

Critics — primarily from flag‑of‑convenience registries such as Panama, Liberia, and the Marshall Islands — argued that the directive imposed a “second layer” of regulation that undercut the MLC’s global uniformity. The EU responded that the directive simply mirrors the MLC and that inspections merely check existing certification. A 2018 Commission evaluation (SWD(2018) 277) found no evidence of trade distortion.

One unintended consequence: some non‑EU ship operators began rerouting vessels away from EU ports to avoid inspections, particularly on low‑margin routes in the North Sea. The Commission estimated this affected fewer than 200 journeys per year.

## Key questions answered

### What basic rights must every ship trading with Europe guarantee for its crew?

Every ship calling at an EU port must provide minimum working conditions set by the International Maritime Labour Convention: a written employment agreement, maximum work hours (14 hours in any 24‑hour period, 72 in any 7 days), adequate food and drinking water, clean accommodation with sanitary facilities, free medical care while on board, and repatriation at the shipowner’s expense. Ships that fail either face detention or a ban from EU waters.

### How does the EU enforce these rules on ships from non‑European countries?

Port state control officers inspect foreign‑flagged ships based on a risk‑rating system. Ships from countries that have not ratified the MLC are inspected more frequently (every 6–12 months). If inspectors find serious deficiencies — e.g., no crew contract or unsafe living quarters — the ship can be detained until fixed. Repeated failures lead to an EU‑wide ban. The European Maritime Safety Agency coordinates and publishes the list of banned ships.

### Why did the EU need a separate directive when the International Labour Organization already had a global convention?

The MLC is a treaty: it only binds countries that ratify it, and enforcement relies on each flag state inspecting its own ships. By 2013, about 20 major flag states had not ratified, including several open registries. A directive allowed the EU to enforce the same rules for all ships in its ports — regardless of flag — and to harmonise inspection procedures across member states, closing loopholes that could be exploited by unscrupulous operators.

### Does this directive apply to fishing vessels?

No. The directive explicitly excludes fishing vessels (and ships of less than 200 gross tonnage that do not go on international voyages). The Parliament pushed to include them during negotiations, but the Council, led by coastal states like Spain and France, argued that the MLC itself sets a lower threshold for compliance and that small fishing boats could not afford the required accommodation upgrades. The issue was revisited in 2019 with Directive (EU) 2019/1151 on the Work in Fishing Convention, which introduced separate but weaker rules for fishing vessels.
