## What is it?

The Open Data Directive (2019/1024/EU) is a recast of the 2003 Public Sector Information (PSI) Directive. It establishes a common legal framework for reusing data held by public sector bodies across the EU. The directive introduces the principle that public sector information should be open by default and machine-readable by design, marking a shift from permission-based re-use to proactive publication.

Core provisions include: a ban on exclusive agreements for public data; a requirement that public bodies make data available in open, machine-readable formats via application programming interfaces (APIs); and the identification of a specific list of "high-value datasets" — such as geospatial, earth observation, meteorological, statistical, and company data — that must be free of charge and accessible in real time. The directive also extends the scope to include public undertakings in the transport and utilities sectors, and public service broadcasters in their commercial activities.

## Why was it introduced?

The recast was driven by a fundamental structural tension: the original 2003 PSI Directive had opened the door to re-use, but the European data economy was leaving money on the table because public bodies charged fees, used proprietary formats, and signed exclusive deals that locked out startups and small businesses. A 2017 European Data Market study commissioned by the European Commission estimated that the value of data-driven products and services based on public sector information could reach €194 billion by 2020 — but that this required a shift from fee-based to free, machine-readable access.

The political coalition that drove the recast was an unusual alliance: the Commission's digital agenda under Vice-President Andrus Ansip (2014–2019), the Open Data Institute (founded by Tim Berners-Lee), and a coalition of European data-journalism startups like OCCRP and Correctiv who had documented how high fees blocked investigative reporting. Industry opposition came from public sector bodies that relied on fee revenue — particularly national mapping agencies (e.g., France's IGN and Germany's Bundesamt für Kartographie) and meteorological offices, which argued that free data would destroy their business models. The European Federation of Geologists and the International Association of Assessing Officers lobbied MEPs to preserve fee models.

The timing was critical: the 2017 German Bundestag election campaign had included a cross-party push for open data as a transparency tool, and the 2018 GDPR enforcement brought public attention to data rights. The Commission's 2018 Digital Economy and Society Index showed the EU lagging behind the United States in data-driven innovation — only 18% of European companies used open data for business purposes, compared to 31% in the US. This competitiveness gap broke the deadlock: national governments that had previously defended their map agencies' fees now saw open data as essential for AI training and economic growth.

What changed was a specific incident: in 2016, the German Federal Ministry of Transport published a cost-benefit analysis showing that making real-time traffic data free would generate €3 billion in economic benefits over 10 years through reduced congestion and new mobility apps, while the cost of freeing the data was only €50 million. This calculation — from a traditionally conservative ministry — was cited repeatedly in Council negotiations and convinced finance ministries that free data was cheaper than fee revenue lost.

## Timeline

- **2003** — First PSI Directive (2003/98/EC) adopted, establishing minimum rules for re-use of public sector information
- **2013** — PSI Directive amended (2013/37/EU) to expand scope to libraries, museums, and archives; charging limited to marginal costs
- **2016** — German Transport Ministry study shows €3bn economic benefit from free real-time traffic data
- **March 2017** — European Commission launches public consultation on PSI Directive reform
- **April 2018** — Commission proposal for a recast directive published, including high-value datasets and API requirements
- **June 2018** — European Parliament rapporteur Neoklis Sylikiotis (GUE/NGL) tables amendments on transparency
- **February 2019** — Council adopts its position; trilogue negotiations begin
- **June 2019** — Political agreement reached between Parliament and Council
- **July 2019** — Directive formally adopted by the European Parliament and Council
- **January 2021** — Member states must transpose into national law (extended to 18 months due to COVID-19)
- **June 2022** — European Commission adopts Implementing Regulation (2022/138) on high-value datasets — list includes geospatial, earth observation, meteorological, statistical, company ownership, and transport data
- **June 2024** — Deadline for member states to make high-value datasets available via APIs free of charge

## Impact and consequences

The directive fundamentally changed how European public bodies think about data. By mandating API-first publication for high-value datasets, it forced national mapping agencies, weather services, and statistical offices to shift from selling data-as-product to providing data-as-infrastructure. The European Commission's 2023 Open Data Maturity Report found that 28 of 33 countries surveyed had adopted open-by-default legislation, up from 19 in 2018.

Industry response was mixed. Startups and SMEs benefited immediately — the Dutch company CyclusMap, which provides real-time traffic prediction APIs, told the Commission in a 2022 consultation that its data costs dropped 80% after the Dutch government implemented free API access. But traditional data providers like the UK's Ordnance Survey and France's IGN saw revenue drops of 15–30% in their commercial data sales, leading to staff cuts and diversification into value-added services.

The most visible conflict came in the transport sector. Berlin's BVG public transport authority initially refused to release real-time arrival data, arguing it was commercially sensitive; the German Federal Cartel Office fined them €100,000 in 2021 for violating the directive's prohibition on exclusive agreements. By 2024, all major German public transport operators had open APIs, enabling third-party apps like VBB Fahrinfo and Moovel.

Enforcement proved uneven. The Commission's 2024 implementation report noted that only 12 of 27 member states had fully transposed the high-value datasets provisions by the deadline. Poland and Cyprus were the worst performers, with no open geospatial APIs operational by June 2024. The Commission launched infringement proceedings against both countries in September 2024.

## Key questions answered

### Can I use any government data for free in the EU?

Not all data, but high-value datasets in six categories (geospatial, earth observation, meteorological, statistical, company ownership, and transport) must be free and available via API by June 2024. Other public sector data can still have fees, but only at marginal cost of reproduction — usually no more than a few euros per dataset. Exclusive deals where one company gets special access are banned.

### Why did EU rules on public data take so long to update?

The original 2003 directive let public bodies charge fees that covered collection and dissemination costs — which national mapping agencies and weather services used to fund their operations. The change happened because independent studies showed the economic value of free data far exceeded the fee revenue lost, and because data-driven startups convinced national finance ministries that open data was cheaper than trying to build an AI industry with locked-up public information.

### What counts as a high-value dataset under this directive?

The European Commission defined six categories: geospatial data (addresses, buildings, land parcels), earth observation data (Copernicus satellite data), meteorological data (temperature, precipitation, wind), statistical data (GDP, population, employment), company and company ownership data (business registers), and transport data (traffic flow, public transport schedules, road works). These must be free of charge, machine-readable, and available through APIs that allow real-time access.

### Can a city government charge for its open data if it needs the money?

No — for high-value datasets, charging is banned. For other public sector information, public bodies can charge at marginal cost (the cost of reproduction and dissemination), but not more. If a public body needs to recover full costs, it must apply for a specific derogation from the Commission — which has only been granted twice, both for specialised research data collections from the European Library.
