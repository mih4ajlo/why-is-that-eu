## What is it?

The Digital Markets Act (DMA) is an EU regulation that targets the largest online platforms — companies like Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, ByteDance, and Booking — by designating them as "gatekeepers" and imposing a list of dos and don'ts on how they operate their core platform services. These services include search engines, app stores, operating systems, messaging apps, social networks, video-sharing platforms, browsers, and online marketplaces.

Unlike traditional competition law, which kicks in only after harm has been proven in lengthy investigations, the DMA establishes ex-ante rules: prohibitions and obligations that apply automatically once a company crosses defined thresholds (roughly €7.5 billion EU turnover or €75 billion market cap, plus 45 million monthly EU users). Gatekeepers must, for example, allow users to uninstall pre-installed apps, permit alternative app stores and sideloading, ensure interoperability of messaging services, and refrain from self-preferencing their own products in rankings.

Fines can reach 10% of worldwide annual turnover, doubling to 20% for repeat offenders. In extreme cases, the Commission can force structural remedies, including divestitures.

## Why was it introduced?

By the late 2010s, EU policymakers had grown deeply frustrated with traditional competition enforcement against US tech giants. Despite landmark cases — Google Shopping (€2.4 billion fine in 2017), Google Android (€4.3 billion in 2018), Google AdSense (€1.5 billion in 2019) — Margrethe Vestager, the EU's competition commissioner, observed that markets remained no more competitive afterwards. The Google Shopping case alone took seven years to investigate, by which time the targeted comparison-shopping market had largely consolidated. Antitrust was simply too slow for digital markets, where network effects entrench dominance within a few years.

A second driver was sovereignty. Europe had failed to produce a domestic equivalent of Google, Amazon, or Facebook, and a handful of US (and increasingly Chinese) firms acted as private regulators of European commerce, speech, and innovation. Apple's 30% App Store commission, Amazon's use of third-party seller data to launch competing private-label products, Google's privileging of its own services in search results, and Facebook's acquisition of WhatsApp and Instagram had all generated political backlash. The 2019 Cremer-Montjoye-Schweitzer report commissioned by Vestager and the German "Commission Competition Law 4.0" report both concluded that competition law needed structural supplementation.

The political trigger came with the new von der Leyen Commission in 2019, which made digital sovereignty a flagship priority alongside the Green Deal. Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton and Vestager jointly proposed the DMA in December 2020, deliberately paired with the Digital Services Act. Heavy lobbying followed: Big Tech firms spent record sums in Brussels (Google alone disclosed over €5.5 million annually), while European publishers, telecoms (Deutsche Telekom, Orange), app developers (Spotify, Epic Games), and hotel platforms (which had long complained about Booking.com's parity clauses) pushed for stronger rules. The final text, adopted in remarkably swift legislative time, was tougher than the original proposal — adding interoperability obligations and lowering thresholds.

## Timeline

- **2017** — Google Shopping decision; €2.42 billion fine after a seven-year investigation, illustrating slowness of antitrust.
- **2018** — Google Android fine (€4.34 billion); Spotify files complaint against Apple's App Store practices.
- **2019** — Cremer/Montjoye/Schweitzer report "Competition Policy for the Digital Era" published for the European Commission.
- **2019** — Ursula von der Leyen takes office; "A Europe Fit for the Digital Age" set as Commission priority.
- **June 2020** — Public consultation on a "New Competition Tool" and ex-ante platform regulation launched.
- **December 2020** — Commission publishes DMA proposal alongside DSA.
- **March 2022** — Political agreement reached between Council and Parliament.
- **September 2022** — DMA formally adopted; published in Official Journal in October.
- **November 2022** — DMA enters into force.
- **May 2023** — DMA becomes applicable; companies must self-notify if they meet thresholds.
- **September 2023** — Commission designates first six gatekeepers: Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, ByteDance, Meta, Microsoft (covering 22 core platform services).
- **March 2024** — Compliance deadline; Apple opens iOS to third-party app stores in the EU; Commission opens first non-compliance investigations against Apple, Alphabet, and Meta.
- **April 2024** — Booking.com designated as gatekeeper.
- **2024–2025** — First non-compliance findings; Apple fined €1.8 billion in a parallel antitrust case (Spotify complaint), and additional DMA fines issued.

## Impact and consequences

For users in the EU, visible changes appeared from March 2024: Apple permitted alternative app stores and sideloading on iOS for the first time globally; Google search results showed comparison sites instead of Google's own boxes for hotels and flights; WhatsApp announced interoperability with third-party messengers; consent screens proliferated as Meta had to obtain explicit consent before combining data across Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp.

Industry response has been mixed and litigious. Apple introduced controversial "Core Technology Fees" that critics — including Spotify, Epic, and the Commission — argue undermine the DMA's intent. Meta launched a paid ad-free tier in response to consent rules, which the Commission preliminarily found non-compliant. ByteDance and Meta have challenged their gatekeeper designations in EU courts. Smaller competitors and European startups have generally welcomed the DMA as opening new opportunities, while some critics warn of unintended consequences such as fragmented user experiences and reduced security.

Globally, the DMA has become a regulatory template: the UK's Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024, Japan's Mobile Software Competition Act, and proposals in India, Brazil, and Australia draw heavily on it. In the US, where federal antitrust legislation stalled, the DMA effectively sets de facto rules for global platform behavior — an example of the "Brussels Effect."

## Key questions answered

### Why didn't the EU just rely on existing antitrust law?

Because Articles 101 and 102 TFEU require case-by-case proof of dominance and abuse, with investigations typically lasting 5–10 years. By the time a remedy is imposed, network effects have entrenched the dominant position. The DMA flips this: rules apply automatically to designated gatekeepers, without needing to prove harm in each instance.

### Why these specific thresholds and companies?

The €7.5 billion turnover / €75 billion market cap thresholds plus 45 million monthly EU users were calibrated to capture exactly the handful of firms with structural gatekeeping power — primarily large US tech firms plus ByteDance and Booking — without ensnaring smaller European platforms. Gatekeeper status is also linked to controlling an "important gateway" between businesses and consumers.

### Why include interoperability for messaging?

Because messaging exhibits extreme network effects: WhatsApp's value comes from everyone being on it. Forcing iMessage and WhatsApp to open up to competitors lowers switching costs and allows new entrants to compete, addressing the "lock-in" that traditional antitrust struggles to undo retroactively.

### Why was the DMA passed so quickly compared to other EU laws?

A rare political alignment: Parliament, Council, and Commission all wanted to be seen acting against Big Tech; the COVID-19 pandemic had highlighted dependence on digital platforms; and frustration with US enforcement gridlock created urgency. The proposal moved from publication to adoption in under 22 months, exceptionally fast for major EU legislation.
