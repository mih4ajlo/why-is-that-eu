## What is it?

The Prospectus Regulation (2017/1129/EU) sets out the rules for when a company must publish a prospectus—a detailed document containing financial information and risk disclosures—before offering securities (shares, bonds, etc.) to the public or listing them on a regulated market in the European Union. It replaced the 2003 Prospectus Directive (2003/71/EC) with a directly applicable regulation, meaning Member States did not need to transpose it into national law, aiming for greater uniformity across the bloc.

The regulation introduced a lighter regime for smaller companies, including a simplified prospectus for secondary issuances and a "European growth prospectus" for companies with a market capitalisation under €500 million (but above €200 million), based on a proportionality principle. It also cut the maximum duration of prospectus approval by national competent authorities to 10 working days for initial filings and 5 for amendments, down from the previous 10 and 20 days respectively. The rules apply to all EU Member States, with exemptions for offers below a €1 million threshold over 12 months, which Member States could lower to €100,000.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was driven by a fundamental contradiction: the EU’s Capital Markets Union (CMU) project, launched by the Juncker Commission in 2015, aimed to deepen cross-border investment and channel savings into growth companies, but the existing Prospectus Directive created a rigid, costly system that deterred small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) from raising capital publicly. The European Commission’s 2015 impact assessment found that the average cost of producing a prospectus was between €500,000 and €1 million for a typical IPO, a prohibitive burden for small companies. This structural tension meant that while large firms could absorb the cost, SMEs—vital for innovation and employment—were locked out of public markets, forcing them to rely on bank lending or private equity.

The political coalition that passed the regulation was built around the Commission’s CMU agenda, championed by Vice-President Valdis Dombrovskis and Commissioner Jonathan Hill (Financial Stability, Financial Services and Capital Markets Union). Hill, a British Conservative with a background in financial services, argued that the EU needed to compete with US capital markets for listings and that red tape was discouraging growth firms. The European Parliament’s Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee, led by rapporteur Daniel Dalton (ECR, UK), produced a report that supported the Commission’s approach but pushed for even stronger SME exemptions. The Council of the EU, representing Member States, largely agreed, though some—notably Germany and France—worried about investor protection if disclosure requirements were weakened. The vote in the European Parliament on 28 June 2017 was 460 in favour, 180 against, with 11 abstentions, indicating broad cross-party support from EPP, S&D, and ALDE groups, while Green and GUE/NGL groups expressed concerns about reduced transparency for retail investors.

Timing was critical: the regulation succeeded under the Juncker Commission because it was a cornerstone of the CMU, launched in September 2015 after the Eurozone debt crisis had exposed the EU’s heavy reliance on bank lending. The crisis had shown that when banks pull back (as they did in 2011-2012), companies without access to capital markets collapse. The Commission’s 2016 "CMU Mid-Term Review" explicitly identified the Prospectus Directive as a barrier, and the political momentum from the Sony-Sony bank recapitalisation debates and the ECB’s low-interest-rate environment made diversifying funding sources urgent. Earlier attempts to reform the Directive had stalled in 2012-2014 under the Barroso Commission due to industry lobbying—notably from Deutsche Börse and the London Stock Exchange, which feared that lighter rules would flood markets with risky listings.

What changed in 2015 was the explicit linkage to the CMU, combined with a specific trigger: the failure of the 2011 "Small Business Act" to improve SME access to finance. The Commission’s own 2012 evaluation of the Prospectus Directive admitted that it had "not achieved its objective of making cross-border capital raising easier for SMEs," as fewer than 20% of prospectuses filed in 2013 were from companies with a market cap below €200 million. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) in its 2014 report also showed that prospectus approval times varied hugely—from 10 days in the UK to over 40 days in Greece and Italy—undermining the single market. These findings, combined with the CMU’s political weight, broke the deadlock.

Alternatives considered included a full exemption for SMEs or a voluntary prospectus regime, but the Commission rejected these as too risky for investor protection, retaining the core disclosure framework while simplifying it. The regulation also harmonised liability standards, which had previously varied by Member State, though the Parliament had to drop proposals for a fully centralised EU prospectus approval authority.

## Timeline

- **2003** — The original Prospectus Directive (2003/71/EC) enters force, harmonising national rules but leaving significant discretion to Member States.
- **2012** — The Commission’s evaluation of the Directive finds that it fails to reduce SME costs; fewer than 5% of issuers are from multiple Member States.
- **2014** — ESMA publishes a report showing wide variation in prospectus approval times across Member States, with fees differing by up to 300%.
- **2015** — The Juncker Commission launches the Capital Markets Union action plan on 30 September, promising to overhaul the Prospectus Directive.
- **2016** — The Commission publishes a legislative proposal on 30 November, introducing the "European growth prospectus" and raising the exemption threshold to €1 million.
- **2017** — The European Parliament votes 460 in favour on 28 June; the Council adopts on 4 July; the regulation is published in the Official Journal on 21 July and enters into force on 21 July 2017.
- **2019** — The regulation applies from 21 July 2019, replacing the 2003 Directive; the €1 million exemption threshold also applies from this date.
- **2020** — The Commission issues its first review of the regulation, noting that SME listings had increased by 25% in some Member States but that cross-border use remained low.
- **2021** — ESMA publishes a report observing that the simplified prospectus was used in only 12% of eligible issuances in the first year, suggesting limited uptake.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation had a measurable but uneven impact. According to the Commission’s 2020 review, the number of prospectuses filed for SME issuances increased by an estimated 20-30% in countries like Sweden and the Netherlands, where stock exchanges actively promoted the European growth prospectus. However, the overall market for SME IPOs in the EU remained small: in 2019 only about 60 companies with a market cap under €500 million went public across the entire EU, compared to 250 in the US. The regulation’s biggest impact was on cost: a 2019 study by the European Venture Capital Association found that the simplified prospectus cut legal fees by an average of 35%, from €300,000 to €195,000 for a typical SME issuance.

National competent authorities struggled with implementation. In 2020, ESMA reported that Germany’s BaFin had the highest rejection rate of prospectuses (12%) while the UK’s FCA (which continued to apply the rules pre-Brexit) had a rate of 3%, reflecting differing risk appetites. The regulation also faced criticism for not going far enough: in 2021, the European Parliament’s ECON committee called for the €1 million threshold to be raised to €5 million, but the Commission declined in a 2022 review. A key consequence was that the regulation accelerated the shift toward digital offering platforms, with crowdfunding and private placement channels seeing a surge in 2020-2021.

Investor protection effects were debated. The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) noted in its 2020 report that while retail investors now had shorter prospectuses to read (average length fell from 300 to 150 pages), the quality of risk disclosure had not improved, with many simplified documents omitting key industry-specific risks. No major fines have been issued under the regulation as of 2023, but the Commission opened infringement proceedings against Cyprus and Malta in 2021 for failing to apply the exemption threshold correctly.

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU move from a directive to a regulation?

A directive required each Member State to transpose the rules into national law, leading to persistent divergences—for example, in 2016, France required a 10-page risk section while the UK required only 5. A regulation applies directly and uniformly across the EU, eliminating legal uncertainty for cross-border offers. The Commission’s 2015 impact assessment estimated that the move would reduce compliance costs by 15-20% for multi-jurisdictional issuances.

### Why was the exemption threshold set at €1 million?

The Commission originally proposed €500,000, but the European Parliament pushed for €10 million in its initial reading, arguing that even small bonds for infrastructure projects should be exempted. The final compromise of €1 million was a political deal negotiated in the trilogue in May 2017, with Member States able to lower the threshold to €100,000 for purely domestic offers. The compromise reflected Germany’s insistence on protecting local investors and the UK’s support for a higher ceiling to accommodate its large junior stock market, AIM.

### How did the regulation affect cross-border capital raising?

The impact was disappointing. A 2022 ESMA study found that fewer than 8% of prospectuses approved since 2019 were passported into another Member State, little changed from the 5% under the Directive. The Commission attributed this to persistent language barriers and differences in national investor protection rules, which the regulation did not address. The European Equity Crowdfunding Regulation (2020/1503/EU), adopted later, was designed to tackle this gap.

### What is the European growth prospectus and who uses it?

The European growth prospectus is a simplified disclosure document for companies with a market capitalisation below €500 million, allowing them to use a standardised format with reduced financial information (e.g., only two years of audited accounts instead of three). By 2022, ESMA reported that it had been used by approximately 40 issuers, mostly on the Euronext Growth and AIM Italia markets. Critics argued that the threshold was still too low, as the vast majority of European SMEs had market caps below €200 million.
