## What is it?

The Platform-to-Business (P2B) Regulation, in force since July 2020, imposes transparency and fairness obligations on online intermediation services — such as Amazon Marketplace, Google Shopping, Apple’s App Store, and Booking.com — toward the businesses that use them to reach consumers. It requires terms of service to be written in plain language, restricts sudden changes without clear reasons, mandates explanation of ranking parameters, and establishes a mediation mechanism for disputes. The regulation does not mandate price controls or dictate specific commercial outcomes, but creates procedural rights that give business users a legal basis to contest platform decisions.

## Why was it introduced?

The P2B Regulation was born from a structural tension: the rapid consolidation of e-commerce and app distribution into a few gatekeepers left millions of small businesses with no effective recourse when platforms changed rules, delisted products without warning, or manipulated rankings.

The immediate trigger was a series of high-profile incidents. In 2015, Austrian hotel association ÖHV documented cases where Booking.com retroactively changed commission structures, leaving hotels with sudden cost increases they could not absorb. In 2017, the European Travel Tech Association — representing travel metasearch companies — complained to the Commission that Google was demoting competitor services in its search results while prioritising its own. Meanwhile, in Germany, the Bundeskartellamt investigated Amazon (2018) for terms that allowed it to suspend sellers without explanation while continuing to sell its own competing products.

Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society Mariya Gabriel, serving under the Juncker Commission, launched a public consultation in September 2016. The response was overwhelming: over 1,000 business users submitted complaints, according to the Commission's 2017 impact assessment. Nearly 70% of respondents said they had experienced problems with platform contracts, and 43% reported disputes that were never resolved. The Commission's 2018 report "Business-to-Platform Trading" found that 170,000 European businesses depended on a single platform for more than half their revenue.

The coalition that passed the regulation was unusually broad. The European Parliament's Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO), led by Anna Cavazzini (Greens/EFA, Germany) and rapporteur Christel Schaldemose (S&D, Denmark), pushed for strong transparency rules. They were backed by SME associations like UEAPME and the European Travel Commission. The main opposition came from platform companies — Amazon, Google, and Airbnb — who argued that regulation would slow innovation and that voluntary codes of conduct were sufficient. The U.S. Chamber of Commerce also lobbied against it, warning of trade friction. According to leaked documents, Amazon conducted weekly meetings with Commission officials in 2018 to dilute transparency requirements on ranking algorithms.

The timing was critical: the Juncker Commission had made the Digital Single Market a flagship project, and the 2019 European Parliament elections created a deadline for completing the file. Parliament adopted the regulation on 17 April 2019 by a vote of 569 in favour, 42 against, and 56 abstentions — a decisive margin that reflected the lack of strong partisan opposition. The Council adopted it unanimously on 13 June 2019.

What changed? An earlier 2016 attempt by the Commission to address platform dominance through competition law enforcement had stalled — the Google Shopping case (opened 2010, fined €2.42 billion in 2017) showed how slow antitrust cases were. A legislative route was faster and could set sector-wide rules. The P2B Regulation deliberately avoided prescribing economic outcomes (e.g., fixed commissions) because Member States with strong platform interests — like the Netherlands (Booking.com), Ireland (Google, Apple), and Luxembourg (Amazon) — would not have supported it. Instead, it opted for procedural transparency, which had broader appeal.

## Timeline

- **2015** — Incidents of sudden contract changes by Booking.com and Amazon delisting sellers trigger complaints from European business associations.
- **September 2016** — Commissioner Mariya Gabriel launches public consultation; over 1,000 responses received, mostly from SMEs reporting platform abuses.
- **May 2017** — European Commission publishes "Business-to-Platform Trading" report, finding 170,000 businesses dependent on single platforms.
- **26 April 2018** — Commission proposes the P2B Regulation as part of the Digital Single Market strategy.
- **17 April 2019** — European Parliament adopts regulation by 569-42 vote.
- **13 June 2019** — Council adopts regulation unanimously.
- **12 July 2019** — Regulation published in Official Journal.
- **12 July 2020** — Regulation becomes applicable across the EU.
- **2021** — Commission publishes first monitoring report, finding compliance uneven: large platforms mostly transparent, smaller intermediary services lagging.
- **2022** — The Digital Markets Act (DMA) expands on P2B concepts, adding behavioural obligations for designated gatekeepers.

## Impact and consequences

The P2B Regulation created the first EU-wide framework for platform accountability toward business users. Its most concrete impact has been on term-of-service changes — platforms must now give at least 15 days' notice (or instantly for security reasons) with a clear explanation. Amazon, Google, and Apple all revised their developer and seller terms in 2020 to comply.

The regulation's ranking transparency provisions were a key win for comparison-shopping services. Google now provides a publicly available ranking transparency page for its Google Shopping and Google Search results, though critics argue the explanation remains too general. Booking.com was forced to explain hotel ranking factors — listing distance, popularity, and price rather than "premium placement" deals.

The mediation mechanism has seen limited use: according to the Commission's 2022 report, only 146 cases were filed via certified mediators in the first two years. The main reason is that business users fear retaliation if they initiate disputes. The German Federal Cartel Office noted in a 2021 market study that "the regulation's effectiveness is weakened by the asymmetric power dynamic it attempts to correct."

Enforcement remains weak. The regulation relies on platforms self-reporting compliance and Member States designating national authorities to enforce it. As of 2023, only 12 Member States had assigned enforcement bodies with adequate resources, according to a Commission evaluation. No fines have been issued under the regulation itself, though national authorities have used other tools — in 2021, the Italian Competition Authority fined Amazon €1.13 billion for abuse of dominance, partly related to practices the P2B Regulation sought to prevent.

## Key questions answered

### Why does the P2B Regulation not set price controls or ban self-preferencing?

The Juncker Commission chose procedural transparency over economic regulation because it was politically achievable. The Netherlands, Ireland, and Luxembourg would have vetoed any measure that forced Booking.com, Google, or Amazon to give competitors equal commercial terms. The P2B Regulation passed because it was weak enough not to trigger Member State opposition, leaving stronger measures to the later Digital Markets Act (2022).

### Why do small businesses still lose disputes despite the P2B Regulation?

The regulation gives procedural rights but does not create a substantive right to a fair commercial outcome. A platform can still delist a seller for a legitimate reason — such as low customer ratings or intellectual property complaints — and the seller's only recourse is mediation, which is non-binding. The Commission's 2022 report found that mediation resolved only 24% of cases, with many businesses declining to participate due to fear of retaliation.

### How does the P2B Regulation relate to the Digital Markets Act (DMA)?

The P2B Regulation applies to all online intermediation services regardless of size, while the DMA (2022/1925/EU) imposes stricter obligations only on designated "gatekeepers" — the largest platforms. The DMA bans self-preferencing, data combing, and most-favoured-nation clauses — practices the P2B merely requires transparency about. The P2B is thus a baseline; the DMA is the enforcement upgrade.
