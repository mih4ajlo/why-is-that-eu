## What is it?

The Trade Secrets Directive (2016/943/EU) establishes a minimum set of rules for the protection of confidential know-how and business information across the European Union. It defines what qualifies as a trade secret, sets out the circumstances under which acquisition, use, or disclosure is unlawful, and prescribes civil remedies — including injunctions, damages, and destruction of infringing goods — that national courts must make available.

Crucially, the directive limits remedies to civil law; it does not require member states to criminalise trade secret theft, leaving criminal sanctions to national discretion under separate instruments like the 2013 IPR Enforcement Directive (2013/40/EU). It also includes safeguards to protect whistleblowers and journalists, and to preserve freedom of expression and the right to information.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origin lies in a structural failure: the EU's internal market was fragmented by wildly divergent national legal regimes for trade secret protection, creating a competitive disadvantage for European companies compared to the United States and Japan, which had long had federal trade secrets laws. By the late 2000s, a cross-border trade secret lawsuit in Europe meant navigating 27 different sets of procedures, definitions, and remedies — a situation that effectively penalised innovation and favoured large firms with dedicated legal teams.

The proximate trigger was the Siemens v. IMS Health case (C-306/05), decided by the European Court of Justice in 2007. The Court ruled that IMS Health's refusal to license a "brick structure" for pharmaceutical sales data — which the German courts had treated as an essential facility under competition law — did not constitute an abuse of dominance. But the case exposed the patchwork: IMS had relied on a trade secret claim in Germany that would have been treated entirely differently in, say, France or the UK. Then-Competition Commissioner Neelie Kroes noted in a 2008 speech that the case demonstrated "the need for greater clarity and harmonisation at EU level."

Parallel to this, the Commission's own 2012 "Study on Trade Secrets and Confidential Business Information in the Internal Market" — conducted by the consultancy Baker & McKenzie — found that a third of companies surveyed reported suffering at least one trade secret misappropriation incident in the previous decade, with estimated losses ranging from €20 billion to €43 billion annually across the EU. The same study showed that over 80% of firms considered trade secrets "very important" or "essential" to their business, particularly in the chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and automotive sectors.

The political coalition that pushed the directive was led by the European Commission's Directorate-General for Internal Market and Services (DG MARKT), under Commissioner Michel Barnier (2010-2014), and carried forward by his successor Elżbieta Bieńkowska (2014-2019). Industry associations BusinessEurope and the European Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO) lobbied heavily, warning that Europe was losing a "race for innovation" to US and Asian competitors. However, opposition was fierce: the European Parliament's JURI committee saw 1,200 proposed amendments, the most ever for a single directive. Civil society groups like Access Now and the European Digital Rights (EDRi) campaigned against what they called "corporate censorship," warning the text could be used to silence whistleblowers or journalists.

The key breakthrough came in 2014, after the Edward Snowden revelations about mass surveillance by US intelligence agencies. The scandal shifted the political framing: trade secret protection was now recast not just as an economic issue but as a matter of strategic autonomy against state-backed corporate espionage from the US and China. Commissioner Barnier explicitly referenced "foreign intelligence services" in a 2014 speech advocating the directive.

The legislative marathon ended in April 2016, when the European Parliament adopted the final text by 503 votes to 131, with 18 abstentions. The Council approved it unanimously in May 2016.

## Impact and consequences

The directive's most immediate impact was on litigation: between 2018 and 2023, the number of trade secret cases filed in EU member states rose by an estimated 40%, according to the 2022 EUIPO "Trade Secrets Litigation Trends" report. However, the majority of these cases remain concentrated in Germany, France, and the Netherlands — the three countries that already had strong pre-existing trade secret laws. Smaller member states like Malta and Luxembourg saw only a handful of cases.

The whistleblower safeguards proved controversial: NGOs like EDRi argued that the directive's carve-out for "disclosure in the public interest" was too narrow, and that whistleblowers — particularly in the pharmaceutical and finance sectors — still faced legal uncertainty. The Commission's 2022 evaluation acknowledged "limited judicial guidance" on the interaction between the directive and the 2019 Whistleblower Protection Directive (2019/1937/EU), but said it was "too early" to assess practical effects.

Industry response was broadly positive: BusinessEurope's 2020 survey found that 78% of firms with cross-border operations reported the directive had reduced legal uncertainty, and 62% said it had facilitated R&D collaboration with EU partners. However, small and medium enterprises (SMEs) reported limited awareness — only 34% of SMEs surveyed by EUIPO in 2021 knew the directive existed.

The first major enforcement action under the directive came in 2021, when the French commercial court in Paris ordered a former employee of the pharmaceutical company Ipsen to pay €2.5 million in damages for taking trade secrets to a Chinese competitor — a case that made headlines in France as the "Ipsen affair."

## Key questions answered

### Why did the EU decide to protect secret business information instead of focusing on patents or copyrights?

Patent and copyright protection already had EU-wide harmonisation through directives like 2004/48/EC (IPR Enforcement) and the 1996 Database Directive (96/9/EC). Trade secrets, by contrast, were left entirely to member state law, creating a clear gap. The Commission's 2012 study showed that companies often preferred trade secret protection over patents for processes, customer lists, and algorithms — categories that patents do not easily cover.

### Why were activists and public interest groups so strongly against the EU's new rules on protecting company secrets?

Critics feared the directive could be used to suppress whistleblowers and investigative journalists by allowing companies to sue for "misappropriation" of documents that exposed wrongdoing. The final text included a narrow exception for disclosure in the public interest — but civil society groups argued that the burden of proof would fall on the whistleblower to show their disclosure was lawful, creating a chilling effect.

### Why did it take so many years for the EU to agree on a law protecting confidential business information?

The file was delayed by the 2014 European Parliament elections and the change of Commission (from Barroso II to Juncker). Additionally, the 1,200 amendments tabled in the Parliament's JURI committee required three rounds of trilogue negotiations between Parliament, Council, and Commission. The Snowden revelations in 2013-14 initially caused further delay as MEPs demanded stronger whistleblower protections.
