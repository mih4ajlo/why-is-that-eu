## What is it?

The Employment Equality Directive (Directive 2000/78/EC) prohibits discrimination in employment and occupation on grounds of religion or belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation. It covers access to jobs, working conditions, promotions, dismissals, and vocational training across both public and private sectors in all EU member states. It requires employers to make "reasonable accommodation" for people with disabilities and permits limited exceptions for genuine occupational requirements or age-based differences justified by legitimate aims.

Passed in 2000 alongside the Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC), this directive created for the first time a binding EU-level floor of protection against these four forms of discrimination. Before it, only a handful of member states — most notably the Netherlands and Sweden — had comprehensive laws covering all these grounds; many had none at all for sexual orientation or age.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive's origin lies in a specific political crisis: Austria's far-right Freedom Party (FPÖ) entering government in February 2000. The other fourteen EU member states imposed bilateral diplomatic sanctions against Austria — an unprecedented act — and needed a positive, concrete demonstration that the EU stood for tolerance and fundamental rights. The Commission, led by President Romano Prodi (1999-2004), seized the moment to push forward an anti-discrimination package that had been stalled for years.

The structural tension was clear: the EU had created a single labour market with free movement of workers (Article 45 TFEU) and a strong body of gender equality law (Article 157 TFEU, the 1975 Equal Pay Directive, the 1976 Equal Treatment Directive), but there was no equivalent protection for other forms of discrimination. The 1997 Treaty of Amsterdam had introduced a new legal basis — Article 13 EC (now Article 19 TFEU) — explicitly empowering the EU to "take appropriate action to combat discrimination based on sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion or belief, disability, age or sexual orientation." Yet two years later, no legislation had been proposed.

The coalition that got the directive passed was the Social Democrat–led governments of the late 1990s. The strongest push came from the Belgian presidency of the Council (July–December 2001), which made anti-discrimination legislation a priority. The European Parliament's rapporteur was British MEP Michael Cashman, a former actor and co-founder of Stonewall, who argued for robust protections. The key opponents were the United Kingdom — which secured exceptions for genuine occupational requirements (e.g. churches refusing to hire non-believing chaplains) — and some eastern European candidate countries that worried about costs of disability accommodation.

The Commission's 1999 proposal had been preceded by a 1998 consultation paper that showed broad support for action across civil society, while business groups like UNICE (now BusinessEurope) warned of additional costs. The European Disability Forum, founded in 1996, lobbied heavily for the "reasonable accommodation" requirement modelled on the US Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.

The timing of 2000 was decisive: the Amsterdam Treaty had just entered into force (May 1999), the Austrian sanctions crisis created political urgency, and the 2000 Charter of Fundamental Rights (proclaimed December 2000) was being drafted, making an anti-discrimination package symbolically essential. The Commission proposed both directives in November 1999, and the Council adopted the Employment Equality Directive by qualified majority in November 2000 — remarkably fast by EU standards, taking just one year from proposal to adoption.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam introduces Article 13 EC, giving EU new legal competence to combat discrimination.
- **1998** — Commission publishes consultation paper "Measures to Combat Discrimination" (COM(1998) 460).
- **November 1999** — Commission proposes both the Racial Equality Directive and the Employment Equality Directive simultaneously.
- **February 2000** — The Austrian far-right FPÖ enters government; EU-14 impose bilateral sanctions.
- **June 2000** — European Parliament adopts strong amendments in first reading; Rapporteur Michael Cashman pushes to maintain broad scope.
- **November 2000** — Council adopts Directive 2000/78/EC. The Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) was adopted in June 2000.
- **December 2000** — EU Charter of Fundamental Rights proclaimed, including Article 21 prohibiting discrimination.
- **December 2003** — Deadline for member states to transpose directive into national law. Several states miss this deadline.
- **July 2008** — Commission publishes first implementation report (COM(2008) 225), finding significant transposition gaps, especially on age and disability.
- **2010** — European Court of Justice ruling in *Kücükdeveci v Swedex* (C-555/07) establishes that the principle of non-discrimination on age grounds is a general principle of EU law.
- **2016** — Commission issues a "New Start" Communication noting that despite progress, enforcement remains weak and many victims do not report discrimination.
- **2020** — Commission's 20-year review (SWD(2020) 108) finds that while the directive has transformed national laws, reporting gaps persist, and only 37% of Europeans with a disability know their rights under the directive.

## Impact and consequences

The directive forced every EU member state to adopt legislation prohibiting age discrimination in employment — a legal innovation that most countries had never attempted. Germany, for example, had no general age discrimination law before 2006, when it grudgingly passed the General Equal Treatment Act (AGG) under threat of infringement proceedings from the Commission. The UK's 2010 Equality Act consolidated nine earlier discrimination laws partly in response to this directive.

The "reasonable accommodation" for disability provision triggered significant infrastructure and policy changes across member states. According to the Commission's 2020 evaluation, the concept was implemented differently in each country — from France's mandatory workplace adaptation funds to Sweden's individualised support systems — creating a patchwork of protection levels.

For sexual orientation discrimination, the directive was revolutionary: in 2000, many EU member states, including Poland, Greece, and Italy, had no legal protection against homosexual discrimination in employment. By 2003, all had to adopt it. The European Court of Justice clarified in *P v S and Cornwall County Council* (C-13/94) and later *Accept v Consiliul National pentru Combaterea Discriminarii* (C-81/12) that discrimination against transgender people also falls within sex discrimination grounds, though the directive itself does not explicitly mention gender identity.

The directive's exceptions have been controversial. The "genuine occupational requirement" exception allowed religious organisations to discriminate in hiring — for example, the ECJ ruling in *Egenberger* (C-414/16) involving the German Protestant church's refusal to hire someone for a fixed-term role because they had no church membership. The age discrimination exceptions — particularly mandatory retirement ages — have generated numerous ECJ cases, including *Mangold v Helm* (C-144/04) and *Age Concern England* (C-388/07).

Enforcement remains uneven. The Commission's 2020 evaluation found that only 7% of reported discrimination cases in the EU actually go to court, with victims citing fear of reprisal, costs, and lack of specialised legal aid. The directive's requirement for "bodies for the promotion of equal treatment" (equality bodies) has been implemented varying widely: some, like the UK's Equality and Human Rights Commission, have strong enforcement powers; others remain weak advisory bodies.

## Key questions answered

### Can an employer in the EU refuse to hire someone because of their religion?

No, but there is a narrow exception. An employer can require a specific religion or belief only if it is a "genuine and determining occupational requirement" — typically for religious organisations hiring clergy, chaplains, or religious teachers. Even then, the requirement must be proportionate and justified by the nature of the job. The European Court of Justice has strictly limited this exception, ruling in *Egenberger* (2018) that national courts must verify that any such requirement is genuinely necessary.

### Does EU law require employers to make workplaces accessible for employees with disabilities?

Yes. The directive requires "reasonable accommodation" for disabled workers — meaning employers must take appropriate measures to enable a qualified person with a disability to do the job, unless this would impose a "disproportionate burden" on the employer. This includes modifying workstations, adjusting working hours, providing assistive technology, or assigning different duties. What qualifies as "disproportionate" is assessed case-by-case, considering the employer's resources, available public funding, and the accommodation's cost. Many member states provide subsidies to help employers cover these costs.

### Why can employers still set mandatory retirement ages under EU law?

The directive explicitly permits differences of treatment on grounds of age if they are "objectively and reasonably justified by a legitimate aim" — such as promoting youth employment, ensuring workforce planning, or maintaining intergenerational fairness. The European Court of Justice has upheld some national mandatory retirement ages (e.g., Spain's compulsory retirement at 65 for judges in *Petersen*, 2010), while striking down others that it found disproportionate (e.g., Austria's automatic termination of employment contracts at age 65 in *Österreichischer Gewerkschaftsbund*, 2018). Member states have wide discretion, but the law must be justified by a genuine policy aim.

### Does the directive cover discrimination based on sexual orientation outside of employment?

No — not directly. The Employment Equality Directive only covers employment, occupation, vocational training, and membership of professional organisations. Discrimination in access to goods and services (e.g., housing, healthcare, education) based on sexual orientation is not covered by this directive. However, the 2008 Commission proposal for a broader "Equal Treatment Directive" (COM(2008) 426) would have extended protection to all four grounds (religion, disability, age, sexual orientation) beyond employment, but it has been blocked in the Council since 2008 due to opposition from several member states, notably Germany (under Chancellor Angela Merkel) and Poland.
