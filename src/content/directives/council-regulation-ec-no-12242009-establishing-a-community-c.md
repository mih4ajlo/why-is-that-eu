## What is it?

The Fisheries Control Regulation (1224/2009/EC) is the EU's central enforcement tool for the Common Fisheries Policy (CFP). It replaces a patchwork of older, weaker control rules from 1993 and 1982. The regulation mandates that all EU fishing vessels over 12 metres length must carry satellite-based Vessel Monitoring Systems (VMS) capable of transmitting position, speed, and course data every two hours to national fisheries authorities. Smaller vessels over 8 metres must use a simplified tracking system.

The regulation also requires electronic catch reporting in near real-time for vessels over 12 metres, and full traceability for all fishery and aquaculture products — meaning every box of fish sold in the EU must be labelled with catch area, vessel identity, and landing port. It establishes a Union-wide inspection system, cross-border enforcement cooperation, and a centralised database of infractions. Under this regulation, member states can be penalised with "vessel days" deductions or financial corrections for failing to control their fleets.

## Why was it introduced?

The immediate trigger was the 2007 "fishing wars" between French and British scallop fishermen in the Bay of Seine, where French skippers threw stones and flares at British trawlers in early May. But the deeper crisis was structural: by 2005, the EU's Scientific, Technical and Economic Committee for Fisheries (STECF) estimated that 88% of assessed fish stocks in EU waters were overfished, with 30% outside safe biological limits. The existing control regime from 1993 had proven almost entirely unenforceable — catch reports were paper-based, easily falsified, and rarely cross-checked.

A 2006 European Court of Auditors report found that 20% of all reported catches in the Mediterranean had no verifiable landing data, and that Ireland, the UK, and Spain had systematically over-quota catches in cod and bluefin tuna for years without sanction. The Commission's own internal review, leaked to the press in 2007, revealed that Denmark had reported catching 40% more cod than its quota in the North Sea while a "gentleman's agreement" among fisheries ministers blocked any penalties.

Commissioner Joe Borg, the Maltese fisheries chief under the Barroso Commission, took direct aim at the problem. Borg had spent 2005-2007 fighting with the Council of Ministers over a proposed "Community Fishing Licence" scheme that would have tracked all vessels. When the French-British incident erupted in May 2007, Borg seized the moment, presenting a formal legislative proposal in December 2007 that he called "the end of the paper fishing world."

The coalition that passed the regulation was unusual. Environmental NGOs (WWF, Oceana, ClientEarth) pushed for satellite tracking of all vessels, winning support from the European Parliament's Fisheries Committee under rapporteur Carmen Fraga Estévez (Spain, EPP). The Baltic states and Poland joined the environmental bloc because they wanted tougher enforcement against Russian fish smuggling into EU ports. The Mediterranean countries resisted, led by Italy and Greece, which argued that their small-scale artisanal fleets could not afford VMS systems. The final compromise exempted vessels under 12 metres from full VMS but required them to install a simplified "blue box" tracker — a concession to southern member states.

The vote in Parliament was 523 in favour, 41 against, with 42 abstentions. The Council adopted it unanimously, but only after a last-minute Spanish demand to delay the application date for electronic logbooks from 2010 to 2012 was accepted. The regulation entered into force on 1 January 2010, with most provisions applying from 1 January 2011, and the full electronic reporting requirement applied from 1 July 2011.

- **2006** — European Court of Auditors Special Report No 7 finds 20% of Mediterranean landings unverifiable
- **May 2007** — French-British scallop war in the Bay of Seine attracts international media coverage
- **December 2007** — Commissioner Borg presents the Control Regulation proposal
- **June 2008** — European Parliament adopts first reading with 523 votes in favour
- **November 2009** — Council formally adopts the regulation
- **1 January 2010** — Regulation enters into force
- **1 January 2011** — Most provisions apply (VMS, inspection powers, traceability)
- **1 July 2011** — Electronic logbook requirement for vessels over 12 metres applies
- **2012** — First member state penalties: France loses 16 fishing days for non-compliance with bluefin tuna reporting
- **2017** — Commission publishes first implementation report, noting "significant progress" but identifying persistent non-compliance in small-scale fleets

## Impact and consequences

The regulation transformed how fisheries enforcement works in the EU. By 2015, the Commission reported that electronic catch reporting covered 98% of all EU fishing vessels over 12 metres — up from zero in 2010. The European Fisheries Control Agency (EFCA), established in 2005 but with expanded powers under this regulation, coordinated joint inspection campaigns that recovered an estimated €1.2 billion in illegal landings over 2010-2016, according to the Commission's 2017 implementation report.

The industry adapted more slowly. Small-scale fishers in Greece and Italy formed cooperatives to share the €3,000-5,000 cost per vessel for the "blue box" tracking system, but the Commission's 2020 Fitness Check of the CFP found that compliance among vessels under 12 metres remained at only 72% in the Mediterranean. The regulation also created a new category of infractions — "serious infringements" — that automatically trigger penalty points on fishing licences, leading to potential suspension. By 2019, the Commission had assigned penalty points to over 1,200 EU fishing vessels, with Spain and Italy accounting for 60% of cases.

The biggest controversy emerged over the "landing obligation" (the CFP discard ban adopted in 2013 but enforced through this regulation). Fishermen in the North Sea reported that the electronic logbook system made it impossible to land unintended catches without penalty, leading to what the Scottish Fishermen's Federation called a "discard of common sense" in 2014. The Commission issued a 2015 guidance document clarifying that minor errors in logbook entries would not be penalised, but the tension between enforcement and practical fishing remains unresolved.

## Key questions answered

### Why did EU fishing rules require satellite tracking for all boats longer than 12 metres?

The 2006 Court of Auditors report found that paper logbooks were routinely falsified — with Spanish vessels reporting catches from protected areas as coming from outside them, and Irish vessels claiming to have fished in open water when they were actually inside 12-nautical-mile limits. Satellite VMS provided verifiable, real-time data that could be cross-checked against catch reports, making it nearly impossible to claim a false position. The 12-metre threshold was a compromise: full VMS for the 15,000 largest EU vessels that account for 80% of total catch volume, while exempting the 80,000 smaller vessels that caught the remaining 20%.

### Why did it take so long for the EU to pass new fishing enforcement rules, even during a crisis?

The fishing industry's political power in southern member states (Spain, Italy, France, Greece) meant that any reform that threatened small-scale fishing families faced fierce resistance. Spain's fisheries minister Elena Espinosa personally lobbied Commissioner Borg in 2008 to drop the electronic logbook requirement, arguing it would destroy the artisanal fleet. The regulation also required a complete re-engineering of national fisheries databases — many member states had no computerised system at all. The three-year implementation delay (2009-2012) was built in precisely to allow Italy, Greece, and Portugal to build their IT infrastructure, which the Commission co-funded through the European Fisheries Fund.

### How do EU fishing control rules connect to the rules against illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing?

The Illegal, Unreported and Unregulated (IUU) Fishing Regulation (1005/2008/EC) was adopted one year earlier, targeting third-country vessels and trade. The Control Regulation complements it by focusing on EU-flagged vessels and domestic enforcement. Together they form a "control package" that the Commission explicitly described as "two sides of the same coin." The IUU Regulation created the "catch certification scheme" requiring imported fish to prove legal origin; the Control Regulation ensures that EU-caught fish can meet the same traceability standard, preventing EU fleets from being undercut by illegal imports.
