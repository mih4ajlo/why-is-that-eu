## What is it?

The Equal Treatment in Goods and Services Directive (2004/113/EC) extends the principle of equal treatment between men and women beyond employment and social security into the everyday marketplace — buying goods, renting property, subscribing to services, and taking out insurance. It makes it illegal for a seller or service provider to deny a product or offer worse terms solely because of the customer's sex.

The directive became famous — and deeply controversial — for one specific article: Article 5(2), which let insurers use gender as an actuarial factor for premium and benefit calculations, provided they could show it was a "determining factor" based on relevant and accurate data. This opt-out ignited a decade-long legal and political battle that culminated in the 2011 Court of Justice ruling in *Test-Achats ASBL v. Conseil des ministres*, which struck down the opt-out and forced the insurance industry to adopt unisex pricing.

## Why was it introduced?

The directive was born from a structural failure: the EU's 1976 Equal Treatment Directive (76/207/EEC) covered employment and occupational social security, but left a gaping hole in goods and services. By the late 1990s, gender discrimination was routine in areas such as car insurance (women paid less because they were statistically safer drivers), life insurance (men paid more because of shorter life expectancy), and private pension annuities (women received lower annual payments because they lived longer). The Commission's 1999 report "Equal Opportunities for Women and Men in the European Union" singled out insurance pricing as an area "where sex-based differentiation remains widespread and is often justified by reference to actuarial calculations."

The political driver was the Amsterdam Treaty (1997), which inserted a new Article 13 (now Article 19 TFEU) giving the EU explicit power to take action against discrimination based on sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion or belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation. The Treaty came into force on 1 May 1999. The Prodi Commission, led by Commissioner for Employment and Social Affairs Anna Diamantopoulou (a Greek socialist), made anti-discrimination legislation its flagship priority. In 2000, the Commission proposed two new directives — one on racial equality (2000/43/EC) and another on employment equality (2000/78/EC). But a third directive covering goods and services beyond employment was initially only proposed for racial discrimination.

The gender equality dimension was added later, largely driven by the European Parliament. MEPs, particularly from the Women's Rights Committee chaired by Swedish MEP Marianne Eriksson (GUE/NGL), argued that sex discrimination in goods and services was at least as pervasive as racial discrimination, and that the Commission had failed to address it. A 2001 Commission impact assessment estimated that across the EU, women paid on average 20-30% more for life insurance premiums than men, and men paid 30-50% more for car insurance. The European Women's Lobby and the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) pushed for full equal treatment.

The coalition dynamics were complex. The insurance industry, led by the European Insurance and Reinsurance Federation (CEA, now Insurance Europe), mounted a fierce lobbying campaign. They argued that unisex pricing would be actuarially unsound and would increase premiums for women by €2-5 billion per year across the EU, according to a 2003 CEA study. The German insurance association GDV warned it would "destroy the link between risk and price." Germany and Austria — countries with heavily gender-segmented insurance markets — were the most resistant in the Council. The UK, with its large motor insurance market where women routinely paid less, was also concerned.

The compromise was Article 5(2), inserted during Council negotiations in late 2003. It allowed member states to "permit proportionate differences in individuals' premiums and benefits where the use of sex is a determining factor in the assessment of risk based on relevant and accurate actuarial and statistical data." This was essentially a grandfather clause for the insurance industry. The directive was formally adopted on 13 December 2004, with a transposition deadline of 21 December 2007. The directive passed with qualified majority; the legal base was Article 13(1) TEC, requiring unanimity in Council and only consultation of Parliament. No formal vote count is available due to confidentiality of Council proceedings, but diplomatic sources indicate Austria and Germany abstained.

The directive sat relatively quietly until the Belgian consumer organisation Test-Achats brought a legal challenge. On 1 March 2011, the Court of Justice (Grand Chamber) delivered its ruling: Article 5(2) was incompatible with the principle of equal treatment and the directive's own objective. The Court gave insurers a transitional period until 21 December 2012 to adjust their pricing. The ruling was a watershed moment — it showed that the Court would not tolerate legislative opt-outs that undermined the core purpose of a directive, even when explicitly written into the text.

The timing was pivotal. The ruling came during the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis, which had severely damaged the insurance industry's reputation. Public trust in financial institutions was at a low point. The Commission, now under José Manuel Barroso and Commissioner for Justice Viviane Reding (a Luxembourgish conservative who had made gender equality a personal cause), swiftly confirmed that no legislative fix would be introduced — unisex pricing was now EU law. The Commission's 2012 implementation report found that unisex pricing had led to an average premium increase of 12% for women in motor insurance and 8% for men in life insurance, but overall market competition absorbed much of the shock.

## Timeline

- **1957** — Treaty of Rome establishes principle of equal pay for equal work (Article 119)
- **1976** — Equal Treatment Directive (76/207/EEC) covers employment but not goods/services
- **1997** — Amsterdam Treaty (Article 13) gives EU power to act against sex discrimination
- **2000** — Commission proposes Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) and Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC)
- **2001** — European Parliament calls for gender equality directive covering goods and services
- **5 November 2003** — Commission proposes Directive on equal treatment between women and men in access to goods and services
- **13 December 2004** — Council adopts Directive 2004/113/EC with Article 5(2) opt-out for insurance
- **21 December 2007** — Transposition deadline; 14 member states transpose with opt-out; 11 without
- **2008** — Test-Achats files challenge before Belgian Constitutional Court, which refers questions to the CJEU
- **1 March 2011** — CJEU Grand Chamber ruling in Case C-236/09 *Test-Achats* strikes down Article 5(2)
- **21 December 2012** — Deadline for member states to implement unisex pricing in insurance
- **2014** — Commission report finds no significant market disruption from unisex pricing
- **2020** — Commission's "Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025" reaffirms the directive's objectives

## Impact and consequences

The immediate consequence was the end of gender-based pricing in insurance across the EU. As of 21 December 2012, all new insurance contracts — motor, life, health, and annuities — must be priced without regard to the policyholder's sex. The insurance industry initially warned of premium increases of 15-30% for certain groups; in reality, the effect was more modest. A 2014 Commission report found that the impact on average premiums was 5-15%, with most increases absorbed through market competition. The UK's Association of British Insurers (ABI) reported that young female drivers saw their premiums rise by an average of 10% in 2013, while young male drivers' premiums fell by 5%.

The directive's legacy extends beyond insurance. It forced member states to examine and reform a wide range of other gender-discriminatory practices. France amended its civil code on inheritance rights for widows and widowers. Italy banned gender-discriminatory pricing in car rental and hotel booking. Poland removed a provision allowing employers to offer "women-only" discounts on public transport. The 2013 Commission implementation report noted that 8 member states had to change laws on access to credit, where women had historically been charged higher interest rates on loans.

The Test-Achats ruling also had a chilling effect on attempts to introduce actuarial exceptions in other EU legislation. When the Commission proposed the 2014 Insurance Distribution Directive (IDD), the insurance industry's request for a new gender-based pricing exemption was dismissed by the Parliament and Council. The Court had effectively closed the door.

However, enforcement has been uneven. A 2019 European Commission report found that 5 member states still allowed gender-based pricing in health insurance for pre-existing conditions. The Commission opened infringement proceedings against Belgium and Spain in 2020 over failure to ensure unisex pricing in private health insurance. As of 2023, 3 member states — Poland, Hungary, and Austria — have been referred to the CJEU for non-compliance.

## Key questions answered

### Why did European insurers use different prices for men and women?

Insurance companies traditionally priced policies based on statistical differences between male and female customers: men die younger, so life insurance costs them more; women have fewer car accidents, so motor insurance costs them less. Insurers argued this was actuarially sound and reflected real risk. The EU's Test-Achats ruling declared this practice illegal because it treats individual customers based on group stereotypes, violating the fundamental right to equal treatment regardless of sex.

### Does EU law still allow gender discrimination in any other services?

Only in a few narrow exceptions. EU law permits positive action (offering scholarships or training to one gender to address underrepresentation) and there is a broad exemption for "statutory social security systems" — meaning state pensions and state health systems can still use gender-based calculations. Private insurance, financial services, housing, education, and commercial services are all covered by the general ban on sex discrimination in the 2004 directive and the Court's Test-Achats ruling.

### Why did it take so long for the EU to ban gender-based insurance pricing?

The insurance industry fought the measure for nearly a decade, arguing it would raise costs for women by billions and lead to market distortion. Germany and Austria, with their heavily gender-segmented markets, blocked full equal treatment in the Council in 2003-2004, resulting in the compromise Article 5(2) opt-out. The breakthrough came when a Belgian consumer organisation, Test-Achats, brought a legal challenge that the Court used to strike down the opt-out as incompatible with the directive's core principle. The 2011 ruling made further legislative action unnecessary.

### What happened to car insurance prices after the ruling?

The effect was mixed. According to the Commission's 2014 impact assessment, young female drivers (age 17-25) saw average premium increases of 8-12%, while young male drivers saw decreases of 3-6%. Older drivers of both sexes saw minimal changes, typically 1-3% adjustments. The overall market absorbed the shift without major disruption — competition among insurers prevented prices from rising as dramatically as the industry had predicted.
