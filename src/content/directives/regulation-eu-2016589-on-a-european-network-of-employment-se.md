## What is it?

The EURES Regulation (2016/589/EU) modernises and transforms the European Employment Services network — originally created in 1993 — into a single, EU-wide digital job-matching platform and co-operation framework. It requires each Member State to designate a national EURES coordinator, usually the public employment service, to feed vacancy data into a common database, the EURES portal, and to offer targeted mobility support to workers and employers.

The regulation also establishes cross-border partnerships (EURES-T) to address specific labour market challenges in border regions, and lays down quality standards for job placements. It replaced the 2003 EURES Decision and the earlier Council Regulation (EEC) No 1612/68 provisions on clearance of vacancies.

## Why was it introduced?

The 2008 financial crisis cracked Europe’s labour market in two. By 2012, youth unemployment in Greece and Spain exceeded 50%, while Germany and Austria had labour shortages, particularly in skilled trades and care work. The existing EURES network, built on a 1993 legal base, was a loose co‑operation of voluntary national services. It handled barely 1% of cross‑border placements, according to a 2013 Commission impact assessment. Member states' own job portals did not talk to each other; data was not machine-readable or standardised. Workers looking for a job in another EU country had to navigate 28 separate platforms.

The political breakthrough came under the Barroso Commission, with then‑Employment Commissioner László Andor pushing the "Employment Package" (April 2012) and the "Youth Employment Package" (December 2012). The European Council repeatedly called for better labour mobility as part of the response to the sovereign debt crisis. The June 2012 "Compact for Growth and Jobs" singled out EURES reform. But Member States were divided: northern countries with high vacancy rates wanted a mandatory, fully searchable database; southern countries feared a brain drain of their skilled workers and insisted on safeguards against poaching and on respecting national labour law.

The Commission tabled its legislative proposal in January 2014, after a public consultation that drew mostly positive responses from trade unions and employer organisations, but cautious reactions from several public employment services that did not want to cede control of their vacancy data. The proposal stalled briefly during the 2014 European Parliament elections. After the Juncker Commission took office in November 2014, new Commissioner Marianne Thyssen (Employment, Social Affairs, Skills and Labour Mobility) made the file a priority, linking it to the "Investment Plan for Europe" and the "Skills Agenda". The Parliament’s rapporteur, Anne Sander (EPP, France), brokered a compromise: mandatory data exchange but with opt‑outs for “sensitive” vacancies, and a gradual rollout. The Council reached a general approach in June 2015; the final text was adopted in April 2016 by a large majority, with only a handful of MEPs from left‑wing and nationalist groups voting against, arguing that the regulation would facilitate wage dumping and undermine national social systems.

The regulation succeeded because it balanced liberalisation with safeguards: it made data sharing compulsory but did not harmonise national benefit systems, and it preserved the role of public employment services as gatekeepers. It also created a budget line for IT development, co‑financed by the EU (the EURES portal cost roughly €8 million to build, according to the Commission).

## Timeline

- **1993** – EURES is established by Commission Decision, based on Article 49 TFEU, as a voluntary network of national employment services.
- **2008–2012** – Financial crisis leads to extreme labour market divergence; peak youth unemployment in Greece (58.3% in April 2013, per Eurostat). Political calls for mobility increase.
- **April 2012** – Commission Employment Package and "Youth Employment Package" propose modernising EURES.
- **June 2012** – European Council "Compact for Growth and Jobs" endorses EURES reform.
- **13 January 2014** – Commission adopts legislative proposal for a new EURES Regulation (COM(2014) 6 final).
- **2014–2015** – European Parliament and Council negotiations. Main sticking points: mandatory data sharing, quality standards, and the role of private employment agencies.
- **25 June 2015** – Council reaches political agreement (general approach).
- **14 April 2016** – Regulation (EU) 2016/589 adopted by the European Parliament and Council.
- **1 May 2016** – Regulation enters into force (twentieth day after publication in the Official Journal).
- **1 May 2017** – Most provisions become applicable, including the obligation for Member States to transmit vacancy data.
- **2018** – First evaluation report by the Commission notes uneven implementation, with some Member States still failing to provide adequate data.
- **2020** – EURES portal is integrated into the EU's single digital gateway (Regulation 2018/1724).
- **2022** – Commission publishes a report on the functioning of EURES, recommending deeper integration with national social security systems.

## Impact and consequences

The regulation forced public employment services to share vacancy data that many had treated as proprietary. By 2020, the EURES portal listed over 3 million job vacancies from 28 countries, and more than 1.5 million CVs were searchable, according to the Commission's 2020 evaluation. The number of cross-border placements increased, though the absolute figure remains modest – a 2019 Commission study estimated that EURES facilitated around 150,000 placements per year, up from roughly 50,000 before the regulation.

Countries with large border regions, such as Germany, France, Austria, and Benelux, invested heavily in cross-border partnerships (EURES-T). In the Saar-Lor-Lux region, for example, daily cross-border commuting increased by 12% between 2016 and 2019, according to a 2021 study by the German Federal Employment Agency.

Employer organisations such as BusinessEurope broadly welcomed the reform, while trade unions, led by the European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC), remained critical, arguing that EURES facilitated "posted worker" exploitation by making it easier for employers to recruit from low-wage countries without improving enforcement of labour law.

The major weakness exposed post‑adoption is data quality. Several Member States, including Italy, Poland, and Romania, struggled to upload machine‑readable, standardised vacancy data. The Commission opened infringement procedures in 2019–2020 against seven Member States for non‑compliance with data transmission obligations. By 2022, most had corrected the shortcomings, but the portal still has a reputation for incomplete or outdated listings.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU have a single website for finding jobs across Europe?

Yes. The EURES portal (eures.europa.eu) allows jobseekers to search for vacancies posted by national public employment services in all EU countries (plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, and Switzerland). It includes an automatic translation tool for job descriptions and CVs, and provides information on living and working conditions in each country.

### Can anyone use the EURES portal to work in another EU country?

Yes, any EU citizen can register and apply, but the system works best for jobs listed by public employment services. Many private-sector vacancies are not included. The regulation also created a network of "EURES advisers" who can help with practical matters – tax, social security, housing – but their availability varies by country.

### Did the EURES Regulation stop brain drain from poorer EU countries?

No. Critics argue the regulation actually accelerated it. The Commission acknowledged in its 2020 evaluation that the digital matching made it easier for professionals from eastern and southern Europe to find jobs in Germany, the Netherlands, and Scandinavia. The regulation includes a "fair mobility" clause (Article 12) requiring Member States to ensure that recruitment does not harm the labour market of the country of origin, but it has never been enforced.

### Why did some countries resist sharing their job vacancy data?

Public employment services often see vacancy data as a strategic asset – it helps them place local jobseekers and measure their performance. Sharing it with a European platform meant losing control over the user interface and the risk that local vacancies would be filled by workers from other countries, increasing the political pressure on the minister responsible for unemployment. Parliamentary records show that Italy, Poland, and several Baltic states raised these concerns in Council working groups.
