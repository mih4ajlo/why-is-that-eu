## What is it?

Regulation 1/2003 is the procedural backbone of EU antitrust enforcement. It replaced Regulation 17/62 — the very first regulation ever adopted by the European Economic Community — which had required companies to notify any cooperation agreement to the Commission in Brussels and wait for an exemption decision. Under the new regime, firms must themselves judge whether their agreements restrict competition and whether they qualify for the exemption in Article 81(3) (now 101(3) TFEU).

The regulation built the European Competition Network (ECN), a structure in which the Commission and the 27 national competition authorities (NCAs) coordinate cases, share evidence, and allocate investigations. NCAs and national courts gained the power — and the duty — to apply EU competition law directly alongside their national rules.

It also reinforced the Commission's investigative arsenal: dawn raids on business premises and, with judicial authorisation, on private homes; structural remedies; fines of up to 10% of worldwide turnover; and a formal leniency framework for cartel whistleblowers.

## Why was it introduced?

By the late 1990s, the notification system was drowning. DG Competition under Karel Van Miert and then Mario Monti was buried in thousands of pending notifications, most of them benign distribution or licensing agreements that posed no real threat to competition. The Commission's own 1999 White Paper on Modernisation, drafted under Director-General Alexander Schaub, argued that the system absorbed enforcement resources on routine clearances while genuine cartels — secret, hidden, and damaging — escaped detection. The notification monopoly, designed in 1962 for a six-member common market, had become incompatible with a Union about to admit ten new members in 2004.

The accession timing was decisive. Monti, who took over DG COMP in 1999, made it plain that the Commission could not police competition across 25 jurisdictions from Brussels alone. Without decentralisation, enlargement would either paralyse enforcement or force the Commission to spread itself impossibly thin. The political coalition behind reform thus combined Monti's reform agenda, the imminent enlargement deadline, and a growing body of national competition authorities — particularly the German Bundeskartellamt and the UK's Office of Fair Trading — that had matured enough to take on EU-law cases.

Resistance came mainly from Germany. The Bundeskartellamt and parts of German industry were attached to the legal certainty of the old notification system: a comfort letter from Brussels was effectively an insurance policy. German negotiators pushed hard to preserve some form of prior authorisation and to limit the Commission's power to override national authorities. The compromise — Article 11(6), which allows the Commission to take a case away from an NCA, combined with the binding effect of Commission decisions on national courts under Article 16 — preserved a hierarchy that German lawyers still describe as constitutionally awkward.

A second structural tension was private enforcement. The Court of Justice's 2001 *Courage v Crehan* ruling had already established that any individual harmed by an antitrust violation must have a right to claim damages under EU law. Regulation 1/2003 — by ending the Commission's exemption monopoly and allowing national courts to apply Article 81(3) — was the procedural precondition for that right to become operational. The follow-on Damages Directive 2014/104/EU built directly on this foundation.

## Timeline

- **1962** — Regulation 17/62 establishes the notification and centralised authorisation system.
- **1999** — Commission publishes the White Paper on Modernisation of the Rules Implementing Articles 85 and 86 of the EC Treaty.
- **2000** — Commission formally proposes the new regulation.
- **September 2001** — Court of Justice rules in *Courage v Crehan* that victims of antitrust breaches have an EU-law right to damages.
- **December 2002** — Council adopts Regulation 1/2003 after intense negotiations, particularly with Germany.
- **1 May 2004** — Regulation enters into application, the same day ten new Member States join the EU.
- **2005–2008** — European Competition Network handles its first large wave of coordinated cartel cases; Commission imposes record cartel fines on car glass, elevators, and vitamins producers.
- **2014** — Damages Directive 2014/104/EU adopted, completing the private enforcement architecture.

## Impact and consequences

The notification backlog vanished overnight: thousands of pending files were closed without individual decisions, freeing the Commission to redirect resources toward cartels, abuse of dominance, and merger control. The Commission's leniency programme, formalised in parallel, became the single most productive cartel-detection tool in Europe. According to Commission cartel statistics, fines imposed between 2005 and 2014 ran into the tens of billions of euros, with peaks including the 2008 car-glass cartel decision (around €1.4 billion, later reduced on appeal) and the 2016 trucks cartel (€2.93 billion across MAN, Daimler, DAF, Iveco and Volvo/Renault).

National competition authorities became serious enforcers of EU law. The Commission's 2014 Communication on Ten Years of Antitrust Enforcement reported that NCAs had collectively adopted well over a thousand decisions applying Articles 101 and 102 since 2004 — far more than the Commission itself. The Bundeskartellamt, the French Autorité de la concurrence and the Italian AGCM emerged as particularly active. Divergences in NCA powers, however, eventually prompted the 2019 "ECN+" Directive (2019/1) to harmonise sanctions, independence, and investigative tools across the network.

Private damages claims — almost unknown in continental Europe before 2004 — grew into a substantial litigation market, particularly in Germany, the Netherlands, and the UK (before Brexit). Cartel follow-on actions by trucking companies, airlines, and industrial customers have produced multi-billion-euro settlements, with claims vehicles such as those organised around the trucks cartel becoming a fixture of European commercial litigation.

For businesses, the cost was legal uncertainty: without the Brussels comfort letter, in-house counsel had to assess Article 101(3) compliance themselves, increasing reliance on competition lawyers and internal compliance programmes.

## Key questions answered

### Why don't companies have to ask Brussels for permission before signing agreements anymore?

Until 2004, EU rules required firms to notify cooperation agreements to the European Commission and wait for clearance — a system designed for the six-country common market of 1962. By the late 1990s it was overwhelmed and was about to become unworkable across an enlarged Union of 25 Member States. The Commission, under Mario Monti, replaced it with a self-assessment regime: companies and their lawyers now judge for themselves whether an agreement complies with EU competition law, and the Commission concentrates on hunting cartels and abuses of dominance.

### Can a national competition authority enforce EU competition law, or only the Commission?

Since 2004, every national competition authority in the EU has both the power and the obligation to apply Articles 101 and 102 TFEU whenever a case affects trade between Member States. They coordinate through the European Competition Network, share evidence, and allocate cases among themselves. The Commission can still pull a case to Brussels, but in practice the vast majority of European antitrust decisions are now taken at national level.

### Can victims of a cartel sue for damages in Europe?

Yes — and this is a relatively recent development. The Court of Justice's 2001 *Courage* ruling established the principle, the 2003 modernisation made it procedurally workable by ending the Commission's monopoly over exemptions, and the 2014 Damages Directive harmonised the rules. Follow-on actions against cartels in trucks, air cargo, and elevators have produced large settlements, particularly in German, Dutch, and (formerly) UK courts.

### Why was changing EU antitrust enforcement tied to the 2004 enlargement?

DG Competition concluded it simply could not run a centralised notification system across 25 jurisdictions from Brussels. Decentralising enforcement to national competition authorities was both an efficiency measure and a political necessity: the new Member States from Central and Eastern Europe needed competent national enforcers, and the existing ones had to take on more responsibility. The application date — 1 May 2004 — was deliberately aligned with enlargement day.
