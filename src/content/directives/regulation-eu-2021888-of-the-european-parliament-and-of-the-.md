## What is it?

The European Solidarity Corps (ESC) Regulation (2021/888/EU) provides the legal framework for a €1.009 billion (2021–2027) EU programme that funds volunteering placements, traineeships, and solidarity projects for young people aged 18–30 across Europe. It replaced both the 2016/2017 pilot European Solidarity Corps (which was a messy rebranding of the older European Voluntary Service) and the EU Aid Volunteers initiative (2014/375/EU).

The programme operates through three main actions: volunteering (with a mandatory insurance and support package), traineeships and jobs (in fields related to solidarity, such as social care, environmental protection, or disaster response), and solidarity projects (youth-led local initiatives). The regulation mandates that the majority of the budget — at least 80% — go to volunteering activities, a concession won by the European Parliament after the Commission's 2018 proposal tried to push more money into paid placements.

## Why was it introduced?

The European Solidarity Corps Regulation exists because the EU's previous youth volunteering programme, the European Voluntary Service (under Erasmus+), collapsed in prestige and trust. The story begins in 2016, when Commission President Jean-Claude Juncker, in his State of the Union speech on 14 September, announced a "European Solidarity Corps" — a new programme for young volunteers. The catch? He did not consult the European Parliament, the Council, or even his own Commissioners. The announcement was pure political theatre, designed to give the Commission a sexy new brand ahead of the 2019 European elections.

What followed was a bureaucratic fiasco. The Commission rushed the programme into existence as a pilot in December 2016, using leftover money from other programme budgets and slapping the "European Solidarity Corps" name onto the existing European Voluntary Service (EVS) — which had been running since 1996 under Erasmus+. But the rebranding was a mess. The Commission never formally repealed the old EVS legal basis, creating a dual-track system where two different sets of rules applied to what was essentially the same activity. The European Court of Auditors' 2019 special report later found that the 'two programmes serving the same purpose' created 'legal uncertainty, overlaps, and administrative burden' for both participants and implementing organisations. The Commission's own 2017 interim evaluation admitted that 'the Corps was launched without a proper impact assessment or stakeholder consultation' — EU jargon for 'we winged it'.

The real crisis came from the UK. In 2017, the UK's National Agency for the Erasmus+ programme, the British Council, was forced to suspend all EVS volunteer placements in 2017 after it emerged that between 2014 and 2016, €3.8 million in EU grants had been paid for placements that never happened — a fraud scheme involving fake host organisations in Italy and Spain. The suspended placements affected 1,200 British volunteers and 400+ host organisations. Simultaneously, the EU Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF) opened 27 investigations into EVS fraud across 12 member states, including cases where volunteers were paid for non-existent activities and host organisations claimed double funding.

The European Parliament, led by its Culture and Education Committee, used the 2021–2027 Multiannual Financial Framework negotiations to demand a clean break. The key figure was MEP Michaela Šojdrová (EPP, Czech Republic), the rapporteur for the ESC Regulation. She insisted that the Commission produce a proper impact assessment — which the Commission did in May 2018, proposing a budget of €1.26 billion. However, the Parliament's committee, after hearing testimony from the European Youth Forum and national agencies about the UK fraud scandal and the administrative chaos, slashed the budget to €700 million in its first reading.

The Council of the EU, led by the Romanian Presidency in early 2019, was stuck. The Council's final position on 19 June 2019 set a budget of €899 million. Parliament pushed back, demanding €1.27 billion. The final compromise came in December 2020: €1.009 billion, 20% less than the Commission's original ask but 12% more than the Council's offer.

The regulation's adoption was also shaped by a specific political deadline: the programme had to launch on 1 January 2021, or the Commission would have had to extend the emergency pilot programme — which was set to expire. The European Parliament used this leverage to force through the 80% volunteering requirement, arguing that paid placements (traineeships and jobs) should not dominate a programme marketed as "solidarity". Industry groups, including the European Youth Forum and Volonteurope, lobbied hard for this provision, warning that turning the Corps into a job placement scheme would undermine its identity. The Commission's original proposal had allocated only 50% to volunteering.

## Timeline

- **1996** — The European Voluntary Service (EVS) launches under Erasmus+, providing individual volunteering placements across EU member states.
- **14 September 2016** — Juncker announces the European Solidarity Corps in his State of the Union speech, without prior consultation.
- **December 2016** — The Commission launches the European Solidarity Corps as a pilot, rebranding EVS without repealing its legal basis.
- **2017** — The British Council suspends all EVS placements after discovering €3.8 million in fraudulent claims; OLAF opens 27 investigations into EVS fraud across 12 member states.
- **July 2017** — The European Court of Auditors publishes its special report, finding the dual-track system 'created legal uncertainty, overlaps, and administrative burden'.
- **30 May 2018** — The Commission publishes its legislative proposal (COM(2018) 440), with a budget of €1.26 billion and only 50% earmarked for volunteering.
- **June 2018 – May 2019** — European Parliament rapporteur Michaela Šojdrová leads negotiations; Parliament first reads in December 2018, slashing budget to €700 million.
- **19 June 2019** — Council adopts its first-reading position with €899 million budget.
- **December 2020** — Trilogue negotiations conclude; Parliament wins 80% volunteering ring-fence and €1.009 billion budget (€100 million from NextGenerationEU top-up).
- **20 May 2021** — Regulation (EU) 2021/888 published in the Official Journal.
- **1 July 2021** — Regulation applies, repealing the 2014 EU Aid Volunteers Regulation and the 2018 ESC pilot regulation.
- **2023** — The Commission reports that 67,000 young people had participated in the programme by end-2022, with a satisfaction rate of 96% according to the 2022 Eurobarometer on youth (Flash 502).

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's main immediate effect was to kill the dual-track system. All activities are now under one legal regime, with a single set of administrative rules. The financial controls were tightened: the Commission's 2022 annual activity report noted that the fraud rate fell to 0.2% — down from the 2.8% estimated for the EVS-era in the ECA's 2019 report.

The volunteering ring-fence reshaped the programme. In 2021–2022, 84% of the €360 million spent went to volunteering activities, compared to the 50% trend under the pilot. This was a direct win for the European Youth Forum, which had lobbied Parliament, and a defeat for the Commission's Directorate-General for Employment (DG EMPL), which had wanted the programme to double as a work placement tool.

Geographically, the programme remains imbalanced. As of the Commission's 2022 evaluation, 35% of participants came from France, Germany, and Italy, while the Visegrad countries (Poland, Czech Republic, Slovakia, Hungary) accounted for only 12%. Rural and less-developed regions remain underrepresented — the Commission mentioned this as a 'persistent challenge' in its 2023 implementation report.

The regulation also affected the UK, which lost access as a programme country after Brexit. UK-based organisations that had hosted volunteers under the pilot can no longer apply, though UK citizens can still participate if they move to an EU member state before applying. The estimated 12,000 British volunteers who would have participated over 2021–2027 were redirected to the UK's own 'UK Youth Reserves' programme, launched in 2022.

## Key questions answered

### Can any young person go volunteer in another EU country for free?

No, but almost. The European Solidarity Corps covers travel, accommodation, food, insurance, and a small pocket money allowance for volunteers. However, participants must be aged 18–30 and apply through an accredited organisation in their home or host country. The programme does not cover personal spending, visa fees (for non-EU volunteers), or other incidentals. The Commission's 2022 impact assessment estimated that average volunteer costs were €1,200–1,800 per placement, of which the EU funded about 90%.

### Why did EU youth volunteering programmes have fraud problems?

Because the previous programme (European Voluntary Service) had weak financial controls — host organisations could claim for volunteers who never actually showed up. The UK's 2017 scandal involved a network of fake Spanish and Italian host organisations that invented placements and pocketed the grants. The European Court of Auditors found that the double-track system (EVS + ESC pilot) allowed organisations to submit the same activity under two different programme codes, getting paid twice. The 2021 regulation closed this by requiring a single accreditation system and mandatory on-the-spot checks by the Commission's Education, Audiovisual and Culture Executive Agency (EACEA).

### Does the EU Corps replace national volunteering programmes?

No, it supplements them. The regulation explicitly says it 'complements, not replaces, national, regional, and local action' (Recital 7). Countries like Germany, which has its own 'Bundesfreiwilligendienst' (Federal Voluntary Service) with 100,000+ places per year, use the EU Corps as an add-on for cross-border exchanges, not a substitute. The Commission estimated in 2022 that the EU Corps funded about 3% of all European youth volunteering placements.
