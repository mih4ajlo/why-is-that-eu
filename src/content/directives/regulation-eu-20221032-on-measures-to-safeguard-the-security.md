## What is it?

Regulation (EU) 2022/1032 is an emergency measure that obliges EU member states to fill their underground gas storage facilities to at least 80% of capacity by November 1, 2022, and 90% by the same date in subsequent years. It also requires member states to certify all storage operators, impose filling obligations on gas suppliers, and report storage levels to the Commission every month.

The regulation amends the existing Security of Gas Supply Regulation (2017/1938/EU), which had no mandatory storage targets. It applies to all member states with underground storage facilities on their territory and extends to those that share storage capacity through regional cooperation agreements.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to Russia's gradual strangulation of gas supplies to Europe in the months before and after its full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022. By spring 2022, Gazprom had cut flows through the Yamal pipeline to zero, reduced Nord Stream 1 deliveries to 40% of capacity, and shut down transit through Ukraine at the Sokhranivka entry point — actions the Commission described in its May 2022 impact assessment as "a deliberate attempt to create uncertainty and drive up prices."

The structural failure was that the EU had no binding mechanism to ensure storage levels were adequate before winter. Under the 2017 regulation, storage filling was voluntary and left to market forces. In practice, this meant that during the 2021–22 winter, European storage was drawn down to historically low levels — the Commission reported that by March 2022, EU storage was only 25% full, compared to 35% the previous year. When Gazprom began reducing flows in the spring of 2022, member states scrambled to refill storage at the worst possible moment: spot gas prices had already risen fivefold from 2021 levels, and Russia's threats to stop deliveries entirely made the summer refill season a geopolitical race.

The timing was driven by a specific political rupture. German Chancellor Olaf Scholz, whose country was the largest buyer of Russian gas and whose Wirtschaftsminister Robert Habeck had resisted mandatory storage targets in early 2022, changed position after Russia stopped deliveries to Poland and Bulgaria in April 2022. On April 26, Gazprom announced it would cut supplies to those countries for refusing to pay in rubles, as demanded by President Putin's March 23 decree. This was the first direct weaponisation of gas against EU member states. The following week, Scholz publicly supported mandatory storage targets, and the German government introduced its own 80% minimum on April 30.

The coalition that passed the regulation was unusually broad. The European Council, meeting on May 30–31, 2022, explicitly called on the Commission to propose a storage mandate. The Commission tabled the proposal on June 8, and it was adopted by the European Parliament on June 23 — just 15 days later, an exceptionally fast timeline for EU legislation. The only significant opposition came from Hungary, which had continued buying Russian gas and whose Prime Minister Viktor Orbán argued that the regulation would impose unfair costs on landlocked countries without their own storage. Hungary voted against the measure but was outnumbered 26–1 among member states.

The specific figure of 80% for 2022 was chosen based on the Commission's emergency modelling, which showed that EU gas consumption of approximately 360 billion cubic metres per year required at least 30 billion cubic metres in storage to survive a winter with reduced Russian flows. Total EU storage capacity was around 107 billion cubic metres, with the largest facilities in Germany (24 bcm), Italy (18 bcm), France (13 bcm), and the Netherlands (13 bcm). The Commission calculated that 80% would provide a 90-day winter buffer under normal conditions, and 90% would cover a worst-case scenario of a complete Russian cut-off.

The regulation also solved a classic free-rider problem: countries without storage could benefit from their neighbours' storage without contributing. Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Finland, and Ireland had negligible or no underground storage. The regulation forced member states with storage to impose filling obligations on all gas suppliers — including those owned by Gazprom — and allowed countries without storage to negotiate bilateral agreements with neighbours, with the Commission empowered to intervene if negotiations failed.

## Timeline

- **November 2017** — Regulation (EU) 2017/1938 on security of gas supply enters into force, with voluntary storage targets based on risk assessments by each member state
- **October 2021** — European gas storage is only 77% full before winter, the lowest level in a decade; Gazprom does not book additional transit capacity through Ukraine
- **December 2021** — Nord Stream 2 certification is suspended by Germany after Russia's recognition of separatist republics in eastern Ukraine
- **February 24, 2022** — Russia invades Ukraine; EU gas prices spike to €200/MWh, four times the average of 2021
- **March 23, 2022** — Putin signs decree requiring "unfriendly countries" to pay for Russian gas in rubles
- **April 26, 2022** — Gazprom cuts gas supplies to Poland and Bulgaria for refusing the ruble payment mechanism
- **April 30, 2022** — Germany introduces a national 80% storage fill target, binding on storage operators
- **May 18, 2022** — The Commission publishes its REPowerEU plan, pledging to end Russian gas imports by 2027, and announces a legislative proposal for mandatory storage filling
- **June 8, 2022** — Commission proposes Regulation (EU) 2022/1032
- **June 23, 2022** — European Parliament adopts the regulation with 470 votes in favour, 120 against, and 9 abstentions; Council adopts on June 27
- **July 23, 2022** — Regulation enters into force, with immediate effect on member states
- **September 2, 2022** — Gazprom completely shuts down Nord Stream 1, claiming maintenance issues; EU gas prices peak at over €300/MWh
- **November 1, 2022** — First deadline: EU average storage fill reaches 95.6% according to Gas Infrastructure Europe (GIE) data, well above the 80% target
- **November 1, 2023** — Second deadline: EU average storage fill reaches 99.3%
- **September 2024** — The Commission's annual assessment finds that the regulation has successfully prevented supply disruptions during the 2023–24 winter, with storage levels never falling below 55% even during peak demand periods

## Impact and consequences

The regulation's most immediate effect was a dramatic acceleration of gas storage refilling in summer 2022. According to Gas Infrastructure Europe (GIE) data, EU storage was only 39% full on July 1, 2022 — the day after the regulation entered force. By September 1, it had reached 80%, and by the November 1 deadline, it stood at 95.6%, a fill rate approximately 20 percentage points higher than the historical average.

The cost of compliance was enormous and fell disproportionately on member states with large storage capacities. Germany spent approximately €15 billion on gas purchases during the summer refill period, with state-owned Trading Hub Europe (THE) buying gas on the spot market. France, Italy, and the Netherlands each spent billions. The Commission estimated in its October 2022 impact assessment that total EU spending on storage refilling was between €30–50 billion for the 2022–23 winter alone.

The regulation exposed a critical weakness: the largest storage facilities were owned by companies with ties to Russia. Gazprom Germania, the German subsidiary of the Russian state-owned gas monopoly, operated the Rehden storage facility (capacity 4.4 bcm, the largest in Germany) and Astora (4.1 bcm). In April 2022, Germany expropriated Gazprom Germania through a trustee arrangement under the Energy Security Act (EnSiG), and the German regulator Bundesnetzagentur took operational control. Similarly, OMV and Snam (Italy) were forced to buy back gas from Gazprom's storage positions.

The regulation also had unintended consequences for global LNG markets. By forcing European buyers to compete for spot cargoes in summer 2022, it contributed to price spikes that affected developing countries. The International Energy Agency (IEA) noted in its November 2022 Gas Market Report that European LNG imports jumped 65% year-on-year in the second half of 2022, diverting cargoes from price-sensitive markets in South Asia. Pakistan and Bangladesh experienced severe gas shortages and power blackouts during the same period.

One group that benefited unexpectedly were storage operators themselves. The regulation created a guaranteed demand for storage capacity, and many operators — particularly those with salt caverns (which can inject and withdraw gas faster than porous reservoirs) — saw their profits surge. Storengy (owned by Engie) and Naftogaz (Ukraine) reported record revenues from storage services.

The main challenge in implementation was enforcement. The regulation required member states to impose filling obligations on "suppliers" — but defining who counted as a supplier proved contentious. In Germany, domestic gas suppliers argued that the obligation should fall on importers like Uniper and RWE, who had long-term contracts with Gazprom. Uniper, which was losing €40 million per day buying replacement gas after Gazprom cut deliveries, was nationalised by Germany in September 2022, partly because the storage obligations made its losses unsustainable.

## Key questions answered

### Does the EU legally require countries to fill up gas storage tanks before winter?

Yes — since July 2022, EU law requires member states with underground storage to fill them to 80% of capacity by November 1 each year (90% from 2023 onward). Countries without their own storage must sign bilateral agreements with neighbours or pay for storage capacity abroad. The rule was an emergency response to Russia's deliberate cuts in gas supplies after its 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

### Who foots the bill for filling gas storage — taxpayers or energy companies?

The regulation places the obligation on gas suppliers and traders who sell gas to consumers. But in practice, when prices soared in 2022, many companies — especially those like Germany's Uniper that had long-term Russian contracts — could not afford to buy replacement gas on the spot market. Several governments stepped in with loans, guarantees, and ultimately nationalisations: Germany spent €15 billion on gas purchases and took over Uniper, SEFE (formerly Gazprom Germania), and several storage operators.

### Can the EU force Russian gas companies like Gazprom to follow its storage rules?

The regulation applies to any company operating storage on EU territory, including Gazprom's subsidiaries. But enforcement proved nearly impossible in practice. Gazprom Germania simply stopped injecting gas into its Rehden facility in April 2022, and German authorities had to expropriate and re-operationalise the site under the Energy Security Act. The regulation's real enforcement mechanism was that member states had to take direct control of any storage that was being deliberately left empty.

### Why were the storage targets set at 80% or 90% — where did those specific numbers come from?

The Commission's emergency modelling showed that EU gas demand of around 360 billion cubic metres per year requires at least 30 billion cubic metres in storage to survive a winter with less than 50% of normal Russian flows. EU total storage capacity is approximately 107 billion cubic metres, so 80% fill provides about 86 billion cubic metres — enough for roughly 90 days of winter demand at normal consumption rates. The 90% target was added for subsequent years to cover a complete Russian cut-off scenario.
