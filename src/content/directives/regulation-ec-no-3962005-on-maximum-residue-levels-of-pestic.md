## What is it?

Regulation (EC) 396/2005 sets harmonised maximum residue levels (MRLs) for pesticides in food and feed across the European Union. An MRL is the highest legally permissible concentration of a pesticide residue in a product, ensuring consumer safety while allowing farmers to control pests effectively. The regulation applies to 315 types of food and feed, covering raw and processed products.

The regulation established a centralised system: the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) evaluates pesticide residues and proposes MRLs, which the European Commission then adopts into law. Food products exceeding an MRL cannot be placed on the EU market, regardless of their origin. Imported goods must meet the same MRLs as those produced within the EU, creating a level playing field.

## Why was it introduced?

The regulation was a direct response to the fragmentation of national MRL standards that plagued the single market in the 1990s and early 2000s. By 2003, EU member states had wildly divergent MRLs for the same pesticide-crop combinations. For instance, the UK permitted 10 mg/kg of captan on apples while Germany allowed only 2 mg/kg — a fivefold difference that forced food processors to maintain separate supply chains. This divergence created trade barriers and confused consumers.

The trigger for EU action came from a 2002 ruling by the European Court of Justice (ECJ) in *Commission v. Italy* (Case C-227/01), where Italy blocked imports of Spanish peaches because they contained a pesticide residue permitted in Spain but banned in Italy. The ECJ ruled that Italy could not impose stricter national MRLs unless it had proven a specific health risk. This case laid bare the legal chaos: without harmonised MRLs, member states could unilaterally block imports, undermining the single market in agricultural goods.

The Coalition for the Harmonisation of Pesticide MRLs, an industry group led by crop protection giants Bayer CropScience and Syngenta, pushed hard for EU-wide rules. They argued that the patchwork of national standards cost the European food industry an estimated €600 million annually in compliance costs, according to a 2004 Commission impact assessment. The European consumer organisation BEUC supported harmonisation but demanded strict health protections, warning that "consumers need uniform safety guarantees, not a race to the bottom."

Timing was decisive: the 2004 EU enlargement brought 10 new member states with weaker pesticide regulations into the bloc. Without harmonisation, the risk increased that low-MRL countries would become dumping grounds for pesticides banned elsewhere, while high-MRL countries could block imports from new members. The Prodi Commission, under Health Commissioner David Byrne, made MRL harmonisation a priority after the 2003 White Paper on Food Safety called for "a coherent and efficient system for establishing MRLs."

The regulation was adopted under the co-decision procedure. The European Parliament, led by rapporteur Jan Mulder (ELDR, Netherlands), secured amendments requiring that MRLs be set at the lowest level achievable with good agricultural practice, not just the highest tolerable level. The Council voted unanimously in December 2004, with only Denmark raising reservations about losing its strict national standards. The regulation entered into force on 1 September 2005, with application from 2008 to allow industry transition.

- **2002** — ECJ ruling in *Commission v. Italy* blocks Italy's attempt to enforce stricter national MRLs, exposing legal fragmentation
- **2003** — Commission White Paper on Food Safety calls for harmonised MRLs
- **2004** — Commission proposal adopted under Health Commissioner David Byrne; EU enlargement adds 10 states with weaker pesticide rules
- **December 2004** — Council unanimously adopts the regulation after Parliament amendments
- **March 2005** — Regulation (EC) 396/2005 officially adopted
- **1 September 2005** — Regulation enters into force
- **1 September 2008** — Regulation becomes applicable; national MRLs replaced by EU harmonised levels
- **2009** — First review of MRLs completed; EFSA evaluates 1,000+ active substances
- **2010** — Commission issue guidance on MRLs for baby food, setting lower limits
- **2018** — Regulation amended to add provisions for so-called "MRLs of concern" for pesticides not approved in the EU
- **2021** — EU Court of Justice rules that EU MRLs must apply to imported organic food, rejecting calls for stricter rules on products from non-EU organic farms

## Impact and consequences

The harmonisation eliminated the estimated 1,500 national MRLs that had previously existed, replacing them with a single EU database. As of 2023, the regulation covers 1,500+ active substances and generates over 100,000 MRLs. According to EFSA's 2022 Annual Report on Pesticide Residues, 94.5% of food samples tested across the EU fell below the legal MRL limit, with 3.9% exceeding MRLs but still within safe levels, and 1.6% exceeding both MRLs and safety thresholds.

The regulation had a dramatic effect on imported foods. In 2020, the Commission's RASFF (Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed) recorded 2,300 notifications for pesticide residues, with the highest rates of non-compliance from India (21% of samples above MRL), Turkey (12%), and China (8%). The EU's "farm to fork" strategy, adopted in 2020 under the von der Leyen Commission, has pushed for further tightening: by 2023, the Commission proposed reducing MRLs for 49 pesticides linked to environmental and health concerns, including clothianidin and thiamethoxam (neonicotinoids).

The regulation has been criticised by the pesticide industry for being too strict. CropLife Europe argued in a 2022 position paper that "harmonised MRLs based on risk assessment, not hazard, would better support agricultural innovation." Conversely, environmental groups like PAN Europe have argued the MRLs are too permissive, pointing to EFSA data that 22% of food samples contained multiple pesticide residues (the "cocktail effect").

## Key questions answered

### Why does the EU set MRLs on imported food?

To create a level playing field and protect EU consumers. If imports could use pesticides banned in the EU, EU farmers would be at a competitive disadvantage and EU consumers could be exposed to higher risks. The regulation requires all food sold in the EU, regardless of origin, to meet the same MRLs, avoiding a race to the bottom. The 2021 EU Court of Justice ruling (Case C-160/20) confirmed this principle applies even to organic imports.

### Why doesn't the EU ban pesticides outright rather than set MRLs?

MRLs are not direct bans on pesticide use. They set limits on residues that can legally remain in food, but the safety assessment occurs under the separate Plant Protection Products Regulation (1107/2009), which decides whether a pesticide can be used at all. MRLs are a food safety tool, while the product authorisation process controls market availability. This two-tier system ensures that even if a pesticide is authorised, its residues are kept as low as practical through good agricultural practice.

### Why were national MRLs allowed to diverge before 2005?

Before 2005, EU law (Directive 91/414/EEC) allowed member states to set their own MRLs, provided they respected certain minimum standards. This produced a system where the same pesticide-crop combination could have ten different MRLs across the EU. The lack of harmonisation created trade barriers. The regulation replaced this with a centralised system managed by EFSA, which proposes MRLs based on a single EU-wide assessment.
