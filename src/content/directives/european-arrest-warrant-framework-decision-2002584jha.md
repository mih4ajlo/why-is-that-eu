## What is it?

The European Arrest Warrant (EAW) is a simplified, court-to-court procedure for surrendering a suspected or convicted person from one EU member state to another for criminal prosecution or execution of a prison sentence. It replaced the traditional, politically controlled extradition system with a judicial decision enforceable across the Union within a tight timeframe — typically 90 days for a consented surrender, 10 days if the person agrees.

Under the EAW, a national judge in one member state can issue a warrant, and a judge in another member state must execute it unless one of a limited number of mandatory or optional grounds for refusal applies (e.g., double jeopardy, amnesty, or the risk of inhuman treatment). The system is built on the principle of mutual recognition of judicial decisions, meaning that each member state trusts the others’ legal systems enough to enforce their warrants without prior political approval.

## Why was it introduced?

The EAW was a direct response to the 9/11 attacks, which shattered the EU’s slow, cumbersome extradition system. Prior to 2002, extradition between EU states was governed by the 1957 European Convention on Extradition and its protocols, which allowed governments to block surrenders on political grounds and involved months or even years of diplomatic negotiation. The system was notoriously slow: cases like that of the alleged terrorist Zacarias Moussaoui, where extradition from France to the US took over two years, highlighted the system's inadequacies.

The political turning point came on 20 September 2001, just nine days after the attacks. The European Council, led by Belgian Prime Minister and Council President Guy Verhofstadt, declared that "the fight against terrorism is a priority for the Union." The German and Spanish governments — with José María Aznar and Gerhard Schröder — pushed for a fast-track surrender mechanism, arguing that the existing system was too slow to deal with cross-border terrorism networks. The European Commissioner for Justice and Home Affairs, António Vitorino, drafted the Framework Decision in just six months, bypassing the usual lengthy consultation.

The coalition supporting the EAW was broad but fragile. EU interior ministers from all 15 member states voted unanimously for it on 13 June 2002 — a rare moment of post-9/11 unity. However, the European Parliament, which had only a consultative role in the third pillar, voiced strong concerns about due process, fair trial rights, and the absence of a mandatory human rights safeguard clause. Civil liberties NGOs like Statewatch and Amnesty International warned that the system could lead to the surrender of individuals to states with poor prison conditions. Yet the security climate made opposition politically untenable.

The structural tension was clear: EU integration had created free movement of people, goods, and capital, but criminal justice remained national. Criminals and suspects could flee across borders faster than extradition procedures could follow. The EAW solved this asymmetry by making a judge's warrant enforceable across the Union — effectively extending a national arrest warrant’s reach.

What changed? An earlier attempt to harmonise extradition in the 1990s — the 1995 and 1996 EU Conventions on Extradition — had failed to enter into force because too few member states ratified them, as national parliaments were reluctant to lose sovereignty over extradition. The 9/11 shock broke this deadlock by creating an urgency that overwhelmed sovereignty concerns. The 2001 Tampere European Council had already set the goal of mutual recognition of judicial decisions, but it took a terrorist attack to force the political will.

Alternatives considered included a purely administrative surrender system (rejected as too weak) and a European criminal court (rejected as too ambitious). The EAW struck a middle ground: judicial, but fast; EU-wide, but respecting national legal traditions.

## Timeline

- **1997** — Treaty of Amsterdam establishes the EU as an area of freedom, security, and justice, creating the legal basis for mutual recognition of judicial decisions.
- **1999** — Tampere European Council sets the goal of abolishing formal extradition between member states and replacing it with a simplified surrender system.
- **11 September 2001** — Terrorist attacks in the US trigger a security-focused political response across the EU.
- **20 September 2001** — Extraordinary European Council in Brussels declares that the fight against terrorism is an EU priority and calls for the rapid adoption of a European arrest warrant.
- **19 November 2001** — European Commission presents the proposal for a Framework Decision on the EAW, drafted by Commissioner António Vitorino.
- **13 June 2002** — Council of the EU unanimously adopts the Framework Decision (2002/584/JHA).
- **1 January 2004** — EAW enters into force in the 15 member states (with implementation varying by country).
- **2004-2005** — First implementation assessments by the Commission show significant delays and partial transposition in several states.
- **2008** — The European Court of Justice delivers its first judgments on EAW interpretation, including in *Advocaten voor de Wereld* (C-303/05), which upheld the EAW’s legality against constitutional challenges.
- **2015** — Commission reports that over 54,000 EAWs were issued between 2005 and 2014, with about 15,000 persons surrendered.
- **2017** — Eurojust data shows that average surrender time for consented cases is 16 days; for contested cases, 50 days.

## Impact and consequences

The EAW drastically accelerated cross-border surrender. Before the system, extradition took an average of nine months to over a year; under the EAW, the average in 2016-2017 was 16 days for consented cases and 50 days for contested ones, according to Eurojust’s 2017 annual report. This speed transformed law enforcement cooperation — police now routinely issue EAWs for crimes as minor as bicycle theft, a significant shift from the pre-2002 era when only serious crimes justified the cumbersome extradition process.

The system has been heavily used. The European Commission’s 2011 report noted that over 54,000 EAWs were issued between 2005 and 2010, and surrenders averaged around 3,000 per year. By 2020, the total number of EAWs issued had surpassed 200,000, with surrender rates of around 60-70%.

However, the EAW has faced serious criticism. Human rights concerns arose over surrenders to states with poor prison conditions or procedural shortcomings. The 2018 *Aranyosi and Căldăraru* judgment of the European Court of Justice (C-404/15 and C-659/15 PPU) created a two-step test requiring executing authorities to verify prison conditions in the issuing state before surrendering a person. This followed cases like that of Romanian suspect who spent months in overcrowded pre-trial detention after being surrendered from the UK.

Implementation has been uneven. Some member states, like Poland and Hungary, have faced accusations of using the EAW to prosecute political opponents. Poland’s attempt to create a 2018 law that would block EAW execution for people accused of crimes under a political regime that changed after the offence was condemned by the Commission. The UK, before Brexit, was a major user: in 2018 alone, the UK issued 1,150 EAWs and executed 240.

The EAW also created tensions with third countries. Norway and Iceland, as Schengen associates, adopted the system via agreement; while Switzerland and Lichtenstein did so via bilateral treaties. The system’s success led to the expansion of mutual recognition to other judicial decisions, including transfer of prisoners (2008/909/JHA) and probation decisions (2008/947/JHA).

## Key questions answered

### How fast can a country hand over a wanted person to another EU country?

Under the European Arrest Warrant, a person who agrees to surrender must be handed over within 10 days of the judicial decision. If they contest the surrender, the process takes up to 90 days from the date of arrest. In practice, according to Eurojust, the average time is 16 days for agreed surrenders and 50 days for contested ones. This is a dramatic reduction from pre-2002 extradition, which often took 12 to 18 months.

### Could a country refuse to send someone if their prison system is abusive?

Yes, but only since 2018. In the *Aranyosi and Căldăraru* case, the European Court of Justice ruled that an executing judge can postpone surrender if there is evidence of systemic prison overcrowding or inhuman treatment in the requesting country. The judge must first ask the requesting country for guarantees about the person’s treatment. If no adequate guarantee is given, surrender can be refused temporarily. This was tragically tested in 2019 when a UK court refused to surrender a man to Hungary due to prison conditions.

### Why did EU countries give up their right to veto extradition requests?

After the 9/11 attacks, EU leaders feared that terrorists could hide in one country while planning attacks in another. The existing extradition system required a government minister to approve each request, which could take months or years and be blocked for political or diplomatic reasons. The European Arrest Warrant replaced this with a judge-to-judge system, removing politics from the process. The trade-off was speed and efficiency for trust: member states had to trust that each other’s legal systems met minimum standards of justice.

### Who gets surrendered under the European Arrest Warrant?

Anyone — from wanted terrorists to accused shoplifters. The EAW covers all crimes punishable by at least 12 months of imprisonment in the issuing country. For 32 categories of serious crimes (like terrorism, murder, rape, and fraud), the double criminality check — verifying the act is a crime in both countries — is abolished. This means someone can be surrendered for a crime that is illegal in the requesting country but legal in the surrendering country, as long as it falls on this list. Critics argue this violates national sovereignty.
